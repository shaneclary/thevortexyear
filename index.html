The Discoverer of Vortex-Based Mathematics

Marko Rodin is an American inventor, researcher, and teacher best known as the discoverer of Vortex-Based Mathematics (VBM) and the originator of the Rodin Coil — a toroidal electromagnetic geometry that has drawn sustained interest from independent engineers, physicists, and energy researchers around the world. Over more than four decades of self-directed inquiry, Rodin has developed a unified body of work that bridges pure number theory, electromagnetic engineering, sacred geometry, and Bahá'í cosmology, presented today through his ongoing classes and the Decoqubit framework.

To his students and collaborators, Rodin is the modern forefather of a recovered ancient mathematical science — one in which numbers are not symbols but living, geometric, energetic realities. His central claim is straightforward and immense: he has identified the underpinning geometry of the universe, and that geometry can be drawn on a single sheet of paper.

Origins

Rodin was born in Seattle, Washington, and grew up showing the kind of relentless curiosity and pattern-recognition that defines his life's work. He has consistently described himself as self-taught in mathematics — uncredentialed in the conventional sense, but committed from a young age to questioning the inherited assumptions of academic mathematics and physics. Where formal training would have constrained him to linear, quantity-based logic, his autodidactic path left him free to look for what mainstream curricula had overlooked: the qualitative relationships between numbers.

This outsider stance is not incidental to his work. Rodin has long held that the great gaps in modern science — the unexplained non-decaying spin of the electron, the missing source of the universe's continuous motion, the riddle of dark matter, the failure to produce a clean Grand Unified Theory — are not solvable from inside the materialist framework that produced them. They require a return to first principles: numbers themselves, and the geometry they generate when treated as real.

The Seminal Vision

The decisive moment of Rodin's life came at the age of fifteen. In a state of intense, meditative focus, he projected his attention as far across the universe as he could and asked a single question: What is the secret behind intelligence?

What returned to him, in a flash of bodily and cognitive certainty, was an answer he has been unfolding ever since: that all intelligence flows from a name — that the personal name a being is given, and the language in which it is spoken, shape consciousness itself, and that the most powerful names are the names of God. This insight set him on a years-long study of the world's great religious traditions, searching for the system of names from which the architecture of intelligence could be derived.

The Bahá'í Faith and the Number Nine

Rodin found his answer in the Bahá'í Faith, becoming a believer shortly after his first encounter with its teachings. Within days of his conversion, he turned to the Most Great Name — the superlative form Abhá (rendered in the salutation Yá Bahá'u'l-Abhá, “O Glory of the All-Glorious”) — and applied the Abjad system, an ancient one-to-one correspondence between Arabic letters and numerical values used throughout Islamic and Bahá'í scriptural tradition.

The result was decisive. Letter by letter — Alif = 1, Bá = 2, Há = 5, Alif = 1 — the Most Great Name resolved to nine. Rodin recognized the convergence at once: nine is the omni-potent number across every sacred tradition he had studied, the singularity, the axis, the number that always returns to itself under multiplication. He drew a circle, placed the nine at the top, distributed one through eight clockwise around it, and began tracing what would become the foundational diagram of his life's work.

He has since named this diagram the Symbol of Enlightenment — the Mathematical Fingerprint of God.

The Symbol of Enlightenment

The Symbol of Enlightenment encodes the entire grammar of Vortex-Based Mathematics in a single figure. By doubling repeatedly and reducing each result to its digital root, an unbroken pattern emerges: 1, 2, 4, 8, 7, 5 — repeating infinitely in both directions, perfectly preserved under halving as well as doubling. This is the Doubling Circuit: the third-dimensional pathway of energy, traced as an infinity-symbol lemniscate across the face of the circle.

Standing apart from this circuit are the numbers 3, 6, and 9 — the vertical axis. The 3 and 6 oscillate against each other as a fourth-dimensional magnetic polarity (3 doubled is 6; 6 doubled is 12 → 3; 12 doubled is 24 → 6, and so on indefinitely). The 9 sits in the center as the singularity, the linear emanation, the Primal Point of Unity. Where conventional thought sees the universe as a duality, Rodin saw a trinity — yin, yang, and the S-curve between them.

This insight reframed centuries of inherited assumptions: the base-ten decimal system is not a human convention but a natural consequence of how energy flows; numbers possess intrinsic spatial, temporal, and volumetric qualities; and what mathematics has always been describing, without recognizing it, is the surface of a torus.

What is Vortex-Based Mathematics?

VBM studies the repeating patterns created by digital roots, especially the 1-2-4-8-7-5 doubling circuit and the 3-6-9 axis. In Rodin's system, these are not just arithmetic curiosities. They are a map of flow.
