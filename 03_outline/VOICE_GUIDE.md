# Voice Guide

The voice every one of the 1,404 lessons must speak in.

This document is the test of itself. If it reads as the bland, list-fond, vaguely confident style that AI-generated copy slips into, it failed. Read it once. Then read three drafts of your own writing against it.

---

## The bar, in one paragraph

Plain English carries the depth. We don't sand it off; we trust it. Marko Rodin says numbers are alive. The voice that teaches that says it without irony and without inflation. Sentences earn their length. Verbs do the work. The reader does some of the work. Nothing on the page is filler. Nothing on the page is hedge. Where Rodin or Powell speak, they speak — we don't translate them into corporate prose.

---

## Five rules (the Writer's Diet, applied)

**1. Verbs do the work.**
Strong verbs replace strings of abstract nouns. *"Marko discovered the cycle"* — not *"the discovery of the cycle was made by Marko"*. *"The circuit closes"* — not *"the circuit forms a closed configuration"*. Hunt for words ending in *-tion, -ment, -ance, -ity, -ness*. Most are verbs hiding inside nouns. Free them.

**2. Be-verbs (is, was, are, were) are weak — use them only when nothing else fits.**
Compare: *"The 3-6-9 axis is something that is orthogonal to the surface"* (slop) vs. *"The 3-6-9 axis runs orthogonal to the surface"* (alive). Watch for *"is being"*, *"was made"*, *"has been"* — almost always a verb is hiding behind them.

**3. Concrete beats abstract.**
*"The wire"* not *"the conductor element"*. *"Twenty-four columns"* not *"a number of columnar positions"*. *"Marko was fifteen"* not *"during his formative years"*. When you find yourself reaching for a category word, reach for the thing instead.

**4. Cut the prepositions.**
*"Rodin's pattern"* not *"the pattern of Rodin"*. *"The doubling circuit on the torus"* not *"the circuit of the doubling pattern as it appears on the surface of the torus"*. Three prepositions in a row is the smell of slop. Two is usually one too many.

**5. Modifiers earn their place or get cut.**
*"The elegant, beautiful, profound doubling circuit"* — cut all three. The circuit is the circuit. If it earns *one* modifier — *"the closed doubling circuit"* — keep that one. **Adverbs ending in -ly are guilty until proven innocent.** *Quickly. Easily. Simply. Effectively.* Almost always cuttable.

## Five tones to favour

**1. Direct address.** *"You'll trace the circuit."* Not *"Students will be tracing the circuit."* The course speaks to one person. Always.

**2. Specificity over hedge.** *"Twenty-four columns."* Not *"approximately two dozen columns."* If you don't know, say so. If you do know, say it.

**3. Sentence rhythm.** Long sentence, then short one. A sentence with three commas, then a fragment. The cadence carries the listener through the six minutes.

**4. Curiosity, not authority.** The host is a fellow learner who got there a year earlier. Not a guru. *"Here's what I noticed when I drew this for the first time."* Not *"As any serious student will recognise…"*

**5. Trust.** Trust the reader to follow. Don't say *"in other words"* — give the words once, well. Don't say *"as I mentioned earlier"* — they remember. Don't recap unless the recap teaches.

---

## Five moves to cut on sight

**1. Throat-clearing.** *"It's important to note that…"* / *"It's worth remembering that…"* / *"Let's take a moment to consider…"* Cut. Whatever follows is what matters.

**2. False suspense.** *"There are three things you need to know about the Doubling Circuit."* No there aren't — there's *one* thing that matters, surrounded by context. Just say the thing.

**3. List fetish.** Not everything is a list. Prose that flows beats prose that's been chopped into bullets. Reserve lists for items that actually parallel.

**4. AI-isms.** These trigger immediate distrust:
- *"In today's [adjective] world…"*
- *"Whether you're a beginner or an expert…"*
- *"It's no secret that…"*
- *"This isn't just X — it's Y."* (overused contrast frame)
- *"Let's dive in / let's unpack / let's explore."*
- *"At its core…"* / *"At the end of the day…"*
- *"Robust, scalable, comprehensive, holistic, dynamic."*

**5. Faux-empathy.** *"I know this can seem complicated, but…"* / *"Don't worry — we'll take it slow."* The student doesn't need reassurance. They need clarity. The reassurance comes from the clarity.

## Breaking it down and putting it back together

This is the teaching shape every lesson follows.

**Break it down.** Take the concept. Find the *one* dimension this lens illuminates. Pull it loose from the others. Hold it up. Let the student see it isolated. *"Today, only this. The doubling circuit, as a path you trace with your finger. We won't worry about why it closes. We won't worry about what it means. Today: the line."*

**Put it together.** The Bridge section, every time, re-attaches the part to the whole. *"That line you traced — next lesson we count it. The week after, you'll see the same line wrap onto a torus. The cycle closes. The circuit lives."*

The break-down is what gives the student a clean thing to hold. The put-it-together is what keeps the year coherent. **Either alone fails.** A course that only breaks down produces students who know parts but not patterns. A course that only synthesises produces students who can recite but not do.

## Voice in different lenses

The nine lenses share the voice but lean different ways within it.

| Lens | Voice lean |
|------|------------|
| 1 See | Visual-first. Sparse narration. Trust the diagram. |
| 2 Count | Numerical-precise. *"Twenty-four. Six. One-hundred-forty-four."* Owns its specificity. |
| 3 Build | Imperative. Action verbs. *"Pick up the pencil. Place it on the page."* |
| 4 Hear | Mostly silent on the host's part. Frames the clip. Lets Rodin speak. |
| 5 Feel | Slowest. Most pauses. Concrete imagery, no abstraction. *"Wet stone. Held breath."* |
| 6 Apply | Worked example. *"In Marko's first coil, this winding produced…"* |
| 7 Compare | Two voices, one host. *"A topology textbook would call this `S¹ × S¹`. Marko calls it the ABHA Torus. Same surface."* |
| 8 Defend | Calmest of the nine. Doesn't dismiss the critic. Doesn't grovel. *"Yes, and."* |
| 9 Connect | Cleanest of the nine. Names the prior, names the next. Gets out of the way. |

## Worked examples

### Example 1 — Lens 2 (Count) opener

**Slop:**
> "It's important to note that the foundational principles of Vortex-Based Mathematics rest upon a relatively small number of core axiomatic propositions, which we'll be exploring in detail throughout this lesson."

**Voice:**
> "VBM rests on five claims. We'll write them down. That's the lesson."

(35 words → 14 words. Same content. The verb *"rests"* does the work. *"Five"* commits. *"That's the lesson"* respects the student's time.)

### Example 2 — Lens 5 (Feel) middle

**Slop:**
> "It's worth taking a moment to really sit with the profound and somewhat counterintuitive claim that Marko Rodin makes here, which is the rather revolutionary idea that numbers themselves possess a kind of living quality."

**Voice:**
> "Marko says numbers are alive. He doesn't mean it as a metaphor. Sit with that for a moment."

(36 → 18 words. *"Marko says"* is direct. *"He doesn't mean it as a metaphor"* answers the obvious objection in one beat. The pause goes where the pause goes.)

### Example 3 — Lens 8 (Defend) response

**Slop:**
> "While it's certainly true that some critics have raised concerns about the validity of these claims, it's also worth considering that the mathematical structures underlying VBM are well-established and that the broader implications, while perhaps controversial, are nonetheless worthy of serious engagement."

**Voice:**
> "The arithmetic is straightforward modular arithmetic. The geometric and physical claims built on it are the contested part. Both can be true at once."

(45 → 24 words. The slop hedges; the voice commits. Two sentences carry what four buried.)

## The one-pass test

Before any lesson is marked drafted, run this pass:

1. **Read it aloud.** If you stumble, fix it. The student listens. So does the producer.
2. **Cut every sentence that starts with *It is* / *There are*.** Rewrite those subjects.
3. **Strike every adverb ending in *-ly*.** Restore only the ones that survive justification.
4. **Find the longest sentence.** If it has more than two prepositions in a row, break it.
5. **Find every paragraph longer than 5 sentences.** Either it's earning its length or it's not.
6. **Look at the first word of every paragraph.** If three in a row are *"The"*, vary.
7. **Ask: would I read this if I weren't paid to?** If no, fix.

A draft that passes this test is *drafted*. A draft that doesn't isn't, regardless of word count.

## What this voice is not

It's not minimalism. It's not "say less". Lessons can be dense. The body of a Lens 2 lesson can spend three paragraphs on one calculation if the calculation earns it. The constraint isn't *short* — it's *every word working*.

It's not casual. The voice doesn't get folksy. It doesn't say *"y'all"* or *"folks"* or *"buddy"*. It addresses one adult reader, with respect, plainly.

It's not flat. Marko's work has weight. The voice carries the weight without inflating it. *"Numbers are real and alive."* That sentence sits where it needs to. We don't dilute it with apology.

---

## The standard, restated

Every lesson is plain enough that an attentive reader passes through the prose unaware of it, and dense enough that they leave knowing something they didn't.

That's the bar. Now write.
