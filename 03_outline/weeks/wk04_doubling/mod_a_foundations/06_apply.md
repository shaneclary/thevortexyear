## 0. Authoring notes
Apply the Doubling Circuit by doubling natural numbers and watching them cycle. Take any starting number — your house number, your year, the page you're on — and apply the doubling-and-reduce rule. The cycle traces the same six-step path through {1, 2, 4, 5, 7, 8} or one of the side cycles.

## 1. Outcome
By the end of these 6 minutes, the student has applied the doubling rule to three personal numbers and observed the cycle structure.

## 2. Hook (0:00–0:30)
*[On screen: a number — 247 — doubles repeatedly with reduction at each step. After several iterations, it lands in the {1, 2, 4, 8, 7, 5} cycle.]*

> "Take a number from your life. Double. Reduce. Watch where it ends up."

## 3. Setup (0:30–1:00)
> "Lens 6 of 9. Apply. Today: take a number from your life and watch the doubling cycle catch it. Six minutes."

## 4. Body (1:00–4:30)

> "Take any whole number. The first thing to do is reduce it to its digital root. Once you have the digital root — which is in {1, 2, 3, 4, 5, 6, 7, 8, 9} — you can apply the doubling rule and watch where the number cycles.
>
> Example. Take 247. Reduce: 2 + 4 + 7 = 13, then 1 + 3 = 4. The digital root is 4. Now apply the doubling rule starting from 4. Double: 8. Double: 16, reduce: 7. Double: 14, reduce: 5. Double: 10, reduce: 1. Double: 2. Double: 4. Back to 4. The number 247, under doubling, traces the cycle 4 → 8 → 7 → 5 → 1 → 2 → 4. Six steps.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> Take 999. Reduce: 9 + 9 + 9 = 27, then 2 + 7 = 9. Digital root 9. Apply doubling from 9: double to 18, reduce to 9. Stays at 9. Forever. The number 999, under doubling, never moves from vertex 9. It's in the *fixed-point* component of the doubling map.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> Take 36. Reduce: 3 + 6 = 9. Digital root 9. Same fixed point.
>
> Take 144. Reduce: 1 + 4 + 4 = 9. Digital root 9. Same.
>
> All three of these — 999, 36, 144 — are *multiples of 9*. They all have digital root 9. Under doubling they all stay at 9. The fixed point at 9 is where every multiple of 9 lives.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> Take 15. Reduce: 1 + 5 = 6. Digital root 6. Apply doubling: double to 12, reduce to 3. Double to 6. Double to 12, reduce to 3. The number 15 traces the *two-cycle* between 3 and 6 — never touching the main Doubling Circuit. 15 is a multiple of 3 but not of 9, so it lives in the 3-6 component.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> So three components, three behaviours.
>
> Numbers with digital root 1, 2, 4, 5, 7, or 8 trace the Doubling Circuit — six steps, returning to start.
>
> Numbers with digital root 3 or 6 trace the two-cycle, alternating forever.
>
> Numbers with digital root 9 stay at 9, fixed.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> Take a personal number — your year of birth, your house number, your phone number's last four digits. Reduce. Identify which component it lives in. Three numbers, three components. The Symbol partitions every number you'll ever encounter into one of these three classes."

## 5. Try it (4:30–5:30)
