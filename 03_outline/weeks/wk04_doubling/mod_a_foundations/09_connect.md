## 4. Body (1:00–4:30)

> "Module A opened with the trace. Pencil on Symbol. From 1 to 2 to 4 to 8 to 7 to 5 back to 1. Six lines. The figure-eight emerged on your page — the symbol mathematicians have used for infinity since 1655.
>
> From there, computation. The doubling rule — double, reduce if needed, land on the next vertex. The cycle closes after exactly six steps regardless of where you start. Three components on the Symbol: the six-cycle through {1, 2, 4, 5, 7, 8}, a two-cycle between 3 and 6, a fixed point at 9. Three pieces, no leaks.
>
> Then three traces by hand. Three printed Symbols. The figure-eight in your hand by the third drawing.
>
> Then Marko spoke on the discovery moment. The fifteen-year-old, the paper napkin, the line that returned to the beginning. The figure he had spent fifty years describing — first traced in his notebook, the way you've traced it in yours.
>
> Then a contemplative beat. The figure-eight as *emergence*. You drew six lines following instructions; an infinity sign appeared. The system produced the symbol that humans, independently, settled on for the same idea.
>
> Then we applied. Three personal numbers — house, year, phone — reduced to digital roots, then sorted into the three components of the doubling map. Every whole number lives in exactly one of three places: the Doubling Circuit, the 3-6 two-cycle, or the fixed point at 9.
>
> Then the comparison. Doubling cycle vs. arithmetic progression. Multiplicative selectivity vs. additive exhaustion. The cycle's closure is a property of multiplication on `(ℤ/9ℤ)*`; the arithmetic progression's exhaustion is a property of addition on `ℤ/9ℤ`. Different operations, different group structures, different behaviours.
>
> Then the calibrated answer to *why doubling and not some other multiplier?* Forty-three words about doubling and halving as the maximal generators of `(ℤ/9ℤ)*`. The other multiplications either collapse the Symbol or produce shorter cycles.
>
> *[pause — spiral wheel with Wks 1-3 fully lit; Wk 4 Mod A lit]*
>
> Module A done. The Doubling Circuit is no longer abstract — you've traced it three times, watched it cycle, defended its centrality, identified the three components on the Symbol. The figure-eight is in your hand.
>
> Tomorrow Module B opens. Mathematical lens. The formal proof that the multiplicative order of 2 in `(ℤ/9ℤ)*` is exactly 6 — and what that proof depends on. Three lines of algebra; six minutes of unpacking."
