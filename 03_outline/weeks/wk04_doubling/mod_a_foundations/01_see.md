## 0. Authoring notes
The first time the student traces the Doubling Circuit on the Symbol. Three weeks of preparation lead here. The figure-eight emerges. Foundations lens leans visual-intuitive — the trace is the lesson.

## 1. Outcome
By the end of these 6 minutes, the student has traced the Doubling Circuit — 1-2-4-8-7-5 — on the Symbol and seen the figure-eight emerge.

## 2. Hook (0:00–0:30)
*[On screen: the Symbol. A pencil enters. A line draws from 1 to 2, to 4, to 8, to 7, to 5, back to 1. A figure-eight emerges.]*

> "Three weeks led here. Today we trace. Six numbers. One line. The figure-eight."

## 3. Setup (0:30–1:00)
> "Lens 1 of 9, Week 4. The Doubling Circuit. Three weeks of preparation, six minutes for the first trace. Pencil on Symbol. Now."

## 4. Body (1:00–4:30)

> "Open your printed Symbol. A pencil. We trace.
>
> Start at 1. Place your pencil tip on the 1.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> Move to 2. Draw a line — gentle, curved, going right and down to where the 2 sits. The line is the path; don't worry about the shape yet.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> From 2, move to 4. The 4 sits clockwise around the circle from 2. Draw a line.
>
> From 4, move to 8. The 8 sits at roughly the eleven-o'clock position — almost across the circle from where you started. Your line crosses the centre region of the Symbol. Draw it.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> From 8, move to 7. The 7 is across the circle again — at roughly the seven-o'clock position. Another line crossing the centre.
>
> From 7, move to 5. The 5 is at roughly the five-o'clock position — close to the 7 but on the other side of the centre.
>
> From 5, return to 1. The line closes.
>
> *[pause — let the student look at what they've drawn]*
>
> Look at the figure. Six numbers. One closed line. The line crosses itself in the centre. Two lobes — one upper, one lower — connected at the crossing point.
>
> A figure-eight. Lying on its side. The mathematical symbol of infinity.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> That figure is what Marko saw at fifteen years old. The Doubling Circuit traces an infinity sign on the Symbol. The path 1, 2, 4, 8, 7, 5, back to 1 — six steps that form a lemniscate, the formal name for the infinity figure.
>
> Notice three things.
>
> First — the figure closes. Six steps return you to your starting point. There's no eighth step, no ninth — the cycle is exactly six long.
>
> Second — the figure is symmetric. The upper lobe contains 1, 2, 4. The lower lobe contains 5, 7, 8. The two lobes are mirrors across the horizontal axis of the Symbol.
>
> Third — three numbers are *not* on the figure. Look at where the 3, the 6, and the 9 sit. They're nowhere on your figure-eight. The doubling cycle visits 1, 2, 4, 5, 7, 8 — and avoids 3, 6, 9. We'll meet 3-6-9 in Week 8.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> What you've drawn is the heart of vortex-based mathematics. Marko has spent fifty years pointing at this figure. Tomorrow we'll count *why* it closes after exactly six steps. Today, sit with the trace."

## 5. Try it (4:30–5:30)
> "Pause. Trace the figure again on a fresh page. Three more times. Each time, your pencil moves more confidently. The shape becomes familiar. By the end of this lesson the doubling cycle should be in your hand. Sixty seconds."

*[Pause card on screen for 60 seconds.]*

> "Welcome back. The figure-eight is on your page. The Doubling Circuit is in your hand."

## 6. Bridge (5:30–6:00)
> "Next lesson — Count. We've traced. Tomorrow we count: why does the cycle close after six steps? Why those six numbers? Lens 2."

*[Closing card: "Lesson 1 of 9 — See. Next: Count."]*
