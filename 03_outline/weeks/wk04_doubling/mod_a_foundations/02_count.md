## 0. Authoring notes
Compute the cycle from any starting point. Show 1 → 2 → 4 → 8 → 16 (reduce to 7) → 14 (reduce to 5) → 10 (reduce to 1) at intuitive level. Foundations lens, no formal proof yet (Mod B). The student should leave knowing how to compute the cycle without prompting.

## 1. Outcome
By the end of these 6 minutes, the student can compute the next number in the Doubling Circuit from any starting point.

## 2. Hook (0:00–0:30)
*[On screen: the cycle 1, 2, 4, 8, 7, 5 — each number followed by an arrow showing the doubling and reduction.]*

> "One step at a time. From 1, you double, you reduce, you land. Six minutes."

## 3. Setup (0:30–1:00)
> "Lens 2 of 9. Yesterday we traced the cycle. Today we compute it. One number doubles to the next. Reduce if needed. Six minutes."

## 4. Body (1:00–4:30)

> "The rule: take a number, double it, reduce by digit-summing if the result is bigger than 9. The result is the next number in the cycle.
>
> Watch.
>
> Start at 1. Double: 2. Single digit, no reduction. Land on 2.
>
> Start at 2. Double: 4. Land on 4.
>
> Start at 4. Double: 8. Land on 8.
>
> Start at 8. Double: 16. Two digits, reduce: 1 + 6 = 7. Land on 7.
>
> Start at 7. Double: 14. Two digits, reduce: 1 + 4 = 5. Land on 5.
>
> Start at 5. Double: 10. Two digits, reduce: 1 + 0 = 1. Land on 1.
>
> Back to the beginning. The cycle closes.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> So the doubling rule produces a sequence: 1, 2, 4, 8, 7, 5, 1, 2, 4, 8, 7, 5, ... It cycles through six numbers and repeats forever. That's why the trace yesterday closed after six steps.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> Now try it from a different start. Begin at 4. Double: 8. Double: 16, reduce: 7. Double: 14, reduce: 5. Double: 10, reduce: 1. Double: 2. Double: 4. Same cycle, started at a different point. The order is preserved — 4 leads to 8 leads to 7 leads to 5 leads to 1 leads to 2 leads back to 4.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> Try from 7. Double: 14, reduce: 5. Double: 10, reduce: 1. Double: 2. Double: 4. Double: 8. Double: 16, reduce: 7. Same cycle.
>
> Wherever you enter, you stay on the rails. The cycle is *self-contained* — once you're in, you can't leave.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> What about 3, 6, 9? Try doubling each.
>
> Start at 3. Double: 6.
>
> Start at 6. Double: 12, reduce: 3.
>
> 3 and 6 trade places forever. They form their own little cycle of length two.
>
> Start at 9. Double: 18, reduce: 9.
>
> 9 doubles to itself. It's a fixed point.
>
> So the operation has *three components* on the Symbol. The big six-cycle through {1, 2, 4, 5, 7, 8} — the Doubling Circuit. The two-cycle between 3 and 6. The fixed point at 9. Three pieces, no leaks between them.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> The doubling rule respects the structure of the Symbol. The big cycle is what we trace. The 3-6-9 pieces are what we'll meet in Week 8."
