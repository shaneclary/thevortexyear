## 4. Body (1:00–4:30)

*[Camera close on hand filling the table.]*

> "Open your notebook. Draw a six-row table with columns: *element, powers, order*. The six elements of `(ℤ/9ℤ)*` are 1, 2, 4, 5, 7, 8. Fill them down the first column.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> Element 1. Powers: *1¹ = 1*. The order is 1 — *gives identity in one step*. Mark order = 1.
>
> Element 2. Powers: *2¹ = 2, 2² = 4, 2³ = 8, 2⁴ = 16 ≡ 7, 2⁵ ≡ 5, 2⁶ ≡ 1*. Order = 6. Generator.
>
> Element 4. Powers: *4¹ = 4, 4² = 16 ≡ 7, 4³ = 64 ≡ 1*. Order = 3. Subgroup {1, 4, 7}.
>
> Element 5. Powers: *5¹ = 5, 5² = 25 ≡ 7, 5³ ≡ 8, 5⁴ ≡ 4, 5⁵ ≡ 2, 5⁶ ≡ 1*. Order = 6. Another generator.
>
> Element 7. Powers: *7¹ = 7, 7² = 49 ≡ 4, 7³ ≡ 1*. Order = 3. Same subgroup as 4.
>
> Element 8. Powers: *8¹ = 8, 8² = 64 ≡ 1*. Order = 2. Subgroup {1, 8}.
>
> *[pause — show the completed table]*
>
> The structure of `(ℤ/9ℤ)*` becomes visible.
>
> Two generators: 2 and 5. Each visits all six elements in a single cycle. They are *inverses* of each other: 2 × 5 = 10 ≡ 1 (mod 9). So multiplying by 5 is the inverse of multiplying by 2 — *halving*.
>
> One subgroup of order 3: {1, 4, 7}. Generated by 4 or by 7.
>
> One subgroup of order 2: {1, 8}. Generated by 8.
>
> The identity element 1 is in every subgroup.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> The full group structure of `(ℤ/9ℤ)*` is *cyclic of order 6*. It has subgroups of every divisor of 6: orders 1, 2, 3, 6. Elements split by order: one of order 1, one of order 2, two of order 3, two of order 6.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> What the table tells you about VBM. The Doubling Circuit (×2) and the Halving Circuit (×5, next week) are the two operations that visit *every* unit. The ×4 and ×7 cycles visit only three units — the {1, 4, 7} subgroup. The ×8 cycle visits only two — {1, 8}. The Symbol's underlying group structure is captured completely in this six-row table."
