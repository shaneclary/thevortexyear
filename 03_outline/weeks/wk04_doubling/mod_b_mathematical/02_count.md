## 0. Authoring notes
Prove formally that the order of 2 in `(ℤ/9ℤ)*` is exactly 6. Two-line argument: (1) the order divides the group's size by Lagrange's theorem; (2) compute lower powers to rule out smaller orders. The student should leave with the proof in their notebook.

## 1. Outcome

## 4. Body (1:00–4:30)

> "We want to show: the order of 2 in `(ℤ/9ℤ)*` is exactly 6.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> *Step one — the order divides the group's size.* Lagrange's theorem says: for any element *g* in a finite group *G*, the order of *g* divides the order of the group *|G|*. We have *|`(ℤ/9ℤ)*`| = 6* — the group has six elements: {1, 2, 4, 5, 7, 8}. So the order of 2 must be a divisor of 6.
>
> The divisors of 6 are 1, 2, 3, and 6. The order of 2 is one of these.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> *Step two — rule out the smaller divisors.* Compute *2¹, 2², 2³* modulo 9 and check whether any of them equal 1.
>
> *2¹ = 2.* Not 1.
>
> *2² = 4.* Not 1.
>
> *2³ = 8.* Not 1.
>
> None of the smaller powers equals 1 mod 9. So the order of 2 is not 1, 2, or 3.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> *Step three — confirm the remaining possibility.* The only divisor of 6 left is 6 itself. So the order of 2 must be 6.
>
> Verify: *2⁶ = 64 = 7 × 9 + 1 ≡ 1 (mod 9)*. Confirmed. The order of 2 in `(ℤ/9ℤ)*` is exactly 6.
>
> *[pause — show the proof on screen]*
>
> Three steps. One application of Lagrange. Three small computations. Done.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> Implications worth carrying.
>
> Because the order of 2 equals *|G|*, 2 is a *generator* of `(ℤ/9ℤ)*`. Its powers produce all six units. The Doubling Circuit visits every element in the multiplicative group, in a specific order: *2⁰, 2¹, 2², 2³, 2⁴, 2⁵* maps to *1, 2, 4, 8, 7, 5*. Same six elements as the group; specific traversal order from the powers.
>
> One more observation. Lagrange says the order divides 6. It doesn't say *which* divisor. Different elements can have different orders. In `(ℤ/9ℤ)*`, the orders are: 1 has order 1; 8 has order 2 (because *8² = 64 ≡ 1*); 4 and 7 have order 3; 2 and 5 have order 6. Two elements (2 and 5) are generators. Two (4 and 7) generate a subgroup of order 3. One (8) generates a subgroup of order 2. The identity is itself.
>
> The Doubling Circuit's special status comes from being generated by an element of *maximum order*. That's why doubling produces a cycle that visits all six units — and why other multiplications, like ×4, produce shorter cycles."
