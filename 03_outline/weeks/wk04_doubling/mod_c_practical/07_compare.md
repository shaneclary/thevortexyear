## 0. Authoring notes
Place the Rodin Coil winding pattern next to a standard solenoid winding. Both are inductors. Different topologies produce different electromagnetic behaviour. The student leaves able to articulate what's specifically different about a Rodin Coil's geometry vs. a solenoid.

## 1. Outcome
By the end of these 6 minutes, the student can name three differences between Rodin Coil winding and standard solenoid winding.

> "Two coils. Both inductors. Different geometries. Today: what changes."

## 4. Body (1:00–4:30)

> "A standard solenoid. Wire wound in *parallel turns* around a cylindrical core. Each turn is a circle perpendicular to the axis. Adjacent turns are nearly identical. Industry-standard inductor design.
>
> A Rodin Coil. Wire wound on a torus, following the Doubling Circuit's helical path. Each segment runs from one vertex of `(ℤ/9ℤ)*` to the next. Adjacent segments are *not* parallel; they cross the torus surface in different orientations.
>
> Three differences worth carrying.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> *Difference one — geometric form factor.* Solenoid: cylinder. Rodin: torus. Cylindrical inductors have an open axis along which a magnetic field passes; the field couples with the surrounding space. Toroidal inductors confine most of the field within the donut shape; less couples to the surrounding space. Marko's claim is that the *specific* toroidal winding pattern of the Rodin Coil produces field structure that even a generic toroidal winding doesn't.
>
> *Difference two — turn-to-turn relationships.* Solenoid: each turn is geometrically equivalent to its neighbours. Magnetic field of a solenoid is roughly uniform along the axis (except at the ends). Rodin Coil: turns are arranged according to a non-trivial cycle structure; adjacent winding segments are *not* parallel, and the spatial relationship between turns reflects the polar pairs and family groups on the Symbol. The field structure is much more complex.
>
> *Difference three — design constraint.* Solenoid: turn count, turn density, wire gauge, and core material can be varied freely to tune inductance and resistance. Rodin Coil: the *winding pattern* is fixed by the cycle 1-2-4-8-7-5; only the form factor and wire gauge are free parameters. The winding pattern is the spec.
>
> *[pause — show the two coils with their differences highlighted]*
>
> One consequence. A solenoid's behaviour is well-predicted by classical electromagnetic theory — given turn count and dimensions, you can compute its inductance, resistance, and resonant frequency to within a few percent. The Rodin Coil's behaviour is *also* predicted by classical theory, given the geometry. Marko's hypothesis (Lens 6 of Mod B) is that the Rodin Coil's *unique geometry* might produce additional electromagnetic effects beyond the classical prediction. That's the testable claim Q3 examines.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> A working RF engineer would describe the Rodin Coil as *a toroidal inductor with an unusual winding pattern*. They'd predict its measurable properties (inductance, Q factor, self-resonant frequency) using standard theory. Whether the standard prediction captures *all* the observed behaviour is the open question. Q3 — your bench measurements — gets to weigh in."
