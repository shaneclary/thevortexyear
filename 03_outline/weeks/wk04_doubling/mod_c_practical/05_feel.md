## 0. Authoring notes
*The handwork of tracing — repetition as practice.* The contemplative beat for the practical lens. Randy's line — *the math changes when you build it* — opens the contemplative question. What is hand-knowledge, and how does it differ from mind-knowledge?

## 1. Outcome
By the end of these 6 minutes, the student has spent quiet time with the difference between mind-knowledge and hand-knowledge of the cycle.

## 2. Hook (0:00–0:30)
*[On screen: a hand tracing the Doubling Circuit, slowly, repeatedly. The same gesture, fifty times.]*

> *(slow)* "The hand learns by repeating. What does it know that the mind doesn't?"

## 3. Setup (0:30–1:00)
> "Lens 5 of 9. Six minutes."

## 4. Body (1:00–4:30) — *contemplative, paced, sparse*

> *(slow)* "You can know the Doubling Circuit two ways.
>
> *[long pause]*
>
> The first way: you can recite it. *One, two, four, eight, seven, five, back to one.* You can name the cycle, prove its order is six, find its place in `(ℤ/9ℤ)*`. This is *mind-knowledge*. It lives in the parts of you that do articulation.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> The second way: you can trace it without thinking. Pick up a pencil at the 1 vertex. Your hand moves. The figure-eight appears. You don't decide what to do at each step. The hand decides. This is *hand-knowledge*. It lives in the parts of you that do gesture.
>
> *[long pause]*
>
> Most students arrive at mind-knowledge first. The course can deliver mind-knowledge in a few weeks. Hand-knowledge is slower. It requires repetition. The fifty traces of the Symbol over the year, the four-colour string mock-up yesterday, the actual coil winding in Week 33 — these are the practices that build hand-knowledge.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> Randy's claim, the one we heard yesterday, is that *the math changes when you build it*. He doesn't mean the algebra changes. He means the *meaning* of the algebra changes. Reciting *the order of 2 in `(ℤ/9ℤ)*` is six* is a different experience from having traced the cycle a thousand times in copper, in string, in pencil, in air. The same fact carries different weight.
>
> *[long pause — 12 seconds]*
>
> Why does this matter for the course? Because every week from here on, the practical lens — the Mod-C work — is asking you to *do* what Mod-A and Mod-B let you *understand*. The doing is not redundant. It's the second register of knowing.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> A skeptic could object: *isn't this just rote learning? Isn't repetition just for people who can't grasp the concept the first time?* No. Mind-knowledge and hand-knowledge are different kinds of knowing, not different speeds of the same knowing. A pianist who can play a sonata didn't *understand it more times* than the listener — she *built a different relationship to it* through the practice.
>
> *[long pause]*
>
> Sit with that. The math you've spent four weeks understanding is going to change when you spend Q3 building. Not because you'll learn new math. Because the math you already know will become hand-knowledge as well as mind-knowledge.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> That's what Randy's line points at. *The math changes when you build it.*"
