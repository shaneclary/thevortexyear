## 0. Authoring notes
First lesson of Module C. Same theme, third lens. The student has now spent twelve hours on Welcome (Mod A foundations, Mod B mathematical). Mod C reframes everything in terms of *what gets produced*. Lens 1 shows the year-end portfolio: the three artefacts the student will hold up by Week 52. Specific. Photographable. Concrete. No abstract talk of "growth" or "transformation."

## 1. Outcome
By the end of these 6 minutes, the student can describe the three artefacts of a finished year-52 portfolio in concrete physical terms.

## 2. Hook (0:00–0:30)
*[On screen: a wide shot of three objects on a wooden table — a thick notebook, a finished Rodin Coil with leads exposed, a bound printout. No labels yet.]*

> "Three things on a table. By Week 52 these are yours. Six minutes to look at each one."

## 3. Setup (0:30–1:00)
> "Lens 1 of 9, Module C. Module A asked you why. Module B asked you what. Module C asks what you'll make. We start with the end — the table that holds the year's work — and walk backward."

## 4. Body (1:00–4:30)

> "**The notebook.** Hardcover, the one you bought in Module A Lesson 3. By Week 52 it holds, at minimum, fifty-two drawings of the Symbol of Enlightenment, dated and annotated. The Doubling Circuit traced in red. The polar pairs marked in blue. Twelve hand-built reductions you did when stuck on a particular calculation. A scattering of sketched torus diagrams. Phyllotaxis spirals from Week 11. Notes from every Lens 4 — the lessons where Marko or Randy speak — with the lines that landed for you. The notebook ends up roughly two-hundred pages. About half are diagrams. About a quarter are written reflections from the Lens 5 contemplative lessons. The remaining quarter are calculations and computations. The notebook is the record of how you came to know this material.
>
> **The coil.** Wound by you, on a torus former, in Quarter Three. Single-strand for your first build, in Week 33. Possibly a multi-strand or phased variant for your second build in Week 35. Beside the coil, a build log — the spiral-bound section of your notebook, or a separate folder, your choice. The build log holds the bill of materials for each coil, photographs from each step of the winding, the measurements you took on the bench (DC resistance, inductance, self-resonant frequency), and the comparison to your predicted values. Some students stop at one coil. Some students wind three or four by Week 52. The minimum portfolio holds one good coil with full documentation.
>
> **The capstone.** A bound document. Twenty to fifty pages, depending on which capstone path you pick. The capstone could be a deeper build with a research write-up — extended antenna tests, a measurement campaign comparing your coil to a reference dipole. It could be a teaching artefact — a primer on one VBM concept written for someone who's never met it, with your own diagrams. It could be an integration project — connecting VBM to a domain you bring (biology, music, computer science). The capstone is the artefact that says *I'm not just a student of this work. I have made my own contribution to it.*
>
> *[pause — slow pan across the three objects again]*
>
> Notebook. Coil. Capstone. Three artefacts. Each photographable. Each defendable. Each yours. The Rodin and Powell organisation maintains a portfolio of student capstones — the table you're looking at is your Week 52 entry into that portfolio."

## 5. Try it (4:30–5:30)
> "Pause. Think which of the three artefacts you're most drawn to right now. The notebook? The coil? The capstone? Write the name in your notebook on a fresh page, with the date. We'll come back to that page at Week 13, Week 26, Week 39, and Week 52. Sixty seconds."

*[Pause card on screen for 60 seconds.]*

> "Welcome back. Whichever artefact you named — that's your compass for the year."

## 6. Bridge (5:30–6:00)
