## 0. Authoring notes
Mod A defended *is this real math?* Mod B defended *are these axioms or just claims?* Mod C defends *I'm not technical enough to build.* Same calm tone. Same calibrated-answer structure. The honest answer here is *the curriculum scaffolds for non-engineers* — and the lesson lays out exactly how, week by week, so the student can verify the claim rather than trust it.

## 1. Outcome
By the end of these 6 minutes, the student can answer the objection *I'm not technical enough to be a builder* with one calibrated sentence and recognise the scaffolding the curriculum provides.

## 2. Hook (0:00–0:30)
*[On screen: a single line of text.]*

> "*I'm not technical enough to build a coil.* Today: the honest answer."

## 3. Setup (0:30–1:00)
> "Lens 8 of 9. The third Defend lesson of Week One. Mod A answered the math question. Mod B answered the axioms question. Today: the technical-confidence question. Six minutes."

## 4. Body (1:00–4:30)

> "The objection in its strongest form. *Track 3 — the building part — assumes I know how to solder, how to read a wiring diagram, how to use a multimeter. I don't. I'm a writer, a yoga teacher, a financial analyst. By Week 33 when the course expects me to wind a coil, I'll be lost. I should probably stick to the watcher path.*
>
> The objection is reasonable. It's also based on a misreading of the curriculum.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> Here's how the building actually scaffolds.
>
> *Quarter One* requires zero technical equipment. You need a notebook and two coloured pencils. The whole quarter is paper, pencil, and your printed Symbol. If you can write, you can do Quarter One.
>
> *Quarter Two* adds one new tool — paper folding. You'll cut a strip and glue it into a cylinder, then a torus. There's a 3D-printed torus model available as an upgrade if you want one, but a paper torus is sufficient. Total Quarter Two equipment: paper, scissors, glue.
>
> *Quarter Three* is where the building intensifies. But it doesn't intensify suddenly. Week 27 through Week 32 are math-and-paper work — revisiting prior concepts, deriving the coil topology. Week 33 is the first build. Six weeks of preparation precede it. By Week 33 you'll know exactly what you're winding, why each turn goes where it goes, and what to measure when you're done. The build session itself is six hours, broken into chunks. The course supplies a parts list with specific products and vendors. The course supplies a step-by-step winding video. The course supplies a measurement protocol with expected ranges. If your DC resistance comes out wildly different from the expected range, the course supplies a troubleshooting checklist. The build is graded scaffolding, not a sudden cliff.
>
> *Quarter Four* extends the building or pivots to the teaching/integration paths if your capstone goes that way. By Week 50 you choose your capstone — and a non-technical student can pick the teaching capstone or the integration capstone, both of which are described in detail and have past graduates as proof.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> So the calibrated answer to the question *I'm not technical enough to build a coil*:
>
> *The build curriculum is scaffolded across thirty-two weeks of preparation. By the time you wind your first coil, you've been preparing — not jumping in cold. And the capstone is yours to choose, with non-build options available.*
>
> Twenty-eight words. Memorise. The objection is real, the answer is specific. Don't let the objection stop you in Week 1 from a course that anticipates and absorbs it across Q1 and Q2."
