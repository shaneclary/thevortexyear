## 0. Authoring notes
Mod A's Feel asked *why am I here?* Mod B's asked *what is five?* Mod C's asks *what's the difference between learning this and doing this?* The contemplative question for the practical lens. Slow pacing. The student should leave able to articulate which mode they're currently in and why it matters.

## 1. Outcome
By the end of these 6 minutes, the student has spent quiet time with the difference between learning VBM and doing VBM, and can write one sentence about which mode they're currently in.

## 2. Hook (0:00–0:30)
*[On screen: a black field. Two words appear — *learn* and *do*. They sit side by side. They don't move.]*

> *(slowly)* "Learn. Or do."

## 3. Setup (0:30–1:00)
> "Lens 5 of 9. The contemplative question the practical lens raises: which mode are you in? Six minutes. Sit with the difference."

## 4. Body (1:00–4:30) — *contemplative, paced, sparse*

*[Visual: the two words remain. Optional: very subtle motion or breathing of the screen.]*

> *(slow)* "You can spend a year learning about VBM. You can read every diagram. You can know every claim. You can quote Marko and quote Randy. At the end of that year, you know things.
>
> *[long pause — 8 seconds]*
>
> You can spend the same year *doing* VBM. You can draw the Symbol two-hundred times. You can compute digit-roots until they're automatic. You can wind a coil. You can measure it. At the end of that year, you have done things.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> Both are real. Both are valid. The course is built so you can choose either. But know which one you're choosing.
>
> *[long pause — 12 seconds]*
>
> The watcher and the doer have different relationships to the material. The watcher can describe the Doubling Circuit in beautiful sentences. The doer has traced it so often that her hand knows the figure before her mind does. The watcher can tell you about the Rodin Coil. The doer has wound one and measured it and knows what its DC resistance was last Tuesday. Neither is wrong. They produce different results.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> The reason this matters is that VBM is unusually unforgiving of pure watching. Most subjects, if you watch enough lectures, you absorb the material by osmosis. VBM doesn't reward that. The patterns only become visible once you've drawn them yourself. The torus only feels three-dimensional once you've held a paper one. The coil only teaches what it teaches once it's on a bench in front of you, with leads, and a multimeter, and your wrong reading the first time before you discover the loose connection.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> Marko, when he describes how he came to this work at fifteen, doesn't say *I read about it*. He says *I drew it*. The drawing is the discovering. That's not a sentimental claim — it's an empirical one about how this particular material gets learned.
>
> *[long pause — 12 seconds]*
>
> So — sit with the question. Are you here to learn about VBM, or to do VBM? Both are honest answers. Pick the one that's true today."

## 5. Try it (4:30–5:30)
> *(softer)* "In your notebook, on the closing-reflection page for Module C, write one sentence. *Right now, I'm here to ___.* Either *learn about* or *do*. Pick one. We'll come back to it. Sixty seconds."

*[Pause card on screen for 60 seconds.]*

> "Welcome back. The sentence you wrote describes today. It will probably change. The course supports either path."

## 6. Bridge (5:30–6:00)
> *(returning to ordinary tempo)* "Next lesson — Apply. We've sat with the question of mode. Next we look at three students who picked *do*, and what they made. Three graduate stories. Lens 6."

*[Closing card: "Lesson 5 of 9 — Feel. Next: Apply."]*
