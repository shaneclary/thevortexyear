## 0. Authoring notes
First lesson of Module B. Same Symbol Mod A pinned on the wall, presented this time as a mathematical object stripped of the spiritual framing. Show it cold: nine vertices, a cyclic order, two operations. Voice: direct, specific, no metaphors that haven't earned their place. The student should leave knowing the Symbol's full mathematical content fits in two sentences.

## 1. Outcome
By the end of these 6 minutes, the student can describe the Symbol of Enlightenment as a finite mathematical object: nine vertices, a fixed circular order, two defined operations.

## 2. Hook (0:00–0:30)
*[On screen: the Symbol drawn cleanly. No aura, no flourish. Numbered vertices, plain circle.]*

> "The same circle you pinned on the wall last week. Today, no spiritual frame. Just the math. Nine points. One order. Two operations. Six minutes."

## 3. Setup (0:30–1:00)
> "Lens 1 of 9, Module B. Last week we met the Symbol as an artefact to live with. Today we meet it as a mathematical object to work with. Same drawing. Different gaze."

## 4. Body (1:00–4:30)

> "Nine vertices around a circle. Read clockwise from the top: 9, 1, 2, 3, 4, 5, 6, 7, 8, back to 9. That cyclic order is the Symbol's first piece of structure. Move the 4 between the 1 and the 2 and the diagram becomes a different object — different cycles, different math, no longer the Symbol.
>
> Now the operations. Two of them.
>
> The first you already know: double a vertex, reduce the result to one digit by summing if needed, land somewhere on the diagram. Double 4, get 8, land on 8. Double 8, get 16, sum to 7, land on 7. The doubling rule defines a function from the nine vertices to the nine vertices. It will give us the Doubling Circuit in Week 4.
>
> The second operation: halving. On this diagram, halving means multiplying by 5 — same closed-cycle reasoning, different rotation. We'll prove that 2 and 5 act as inverses here in Week 5; for now, take it on the construction.
>
> *[pause — hold the diagram]*
>
> That's the whole object. Nine vertices. One cyclic order. Two operations. Notice what isn't there. No coordinates. No notion of distance. The Symbol doesn't care how far apart the vertices are drawn, only what order they sit in. A topology textbook would call the underlying object a cyclic group of order 9, with two specific group automorphisms. We'll use both languages — Marko's *Symbol of Enlightenment* and the textbook's *cyclic group* — in different lessons. Same object.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> Why this matters. Every claim you'll meet across the year — the Doubling Circuit, the polar pairs, the family groups, the 3-6-9 axis, the wrap to a torus, the coil winding — reads structure off this single diagram. The whole curriculum sits on nine points and two operations. That economy is part of why Marko thinks something deep runs through the decimal system. The math, by itself, doesn't prove him right. The math, by itself, *does* prove the diagram carries more than its size suggests."
