## 0. Authoring notes
Place VBM's claims next to mainstream axiom systems. Specifically: Peano (natural numbers), ZFC (set theory), Euclid (plane geometry). Show the structural difference: mainstream axioms describe what objects *do*, not what they *are*. VBM's claims one and two assert what numbers *are*. That's the real divergence; everything else flows from it. This is the lesson where the student learns the vocabulary to talk about VBM with a working mathematician without either side getting confused.

## 1. Outcome
By the end of these 6 minutes, the student can describe one structural difference between VBM's claims and a mainstream axiom system in a sentence a mathematician would accept.

## 2. Hook (0:00–0:30)
*[On screen: three axiom systems lined up — Peano (5), ZFC (~9), Euclid (5). Then VBM (5) joins the line.]*

> "Mainstream math has axiom systems. VBM has claims. The difference is real. Six minutes to name it."

## 3. Setup (0:30–1:00)
> "Lens 7 of 9. The compare lens places VBM next to its neighbours. Today: Peano. ZFC. Euclid. Three classical systems. Each has five-to-nine premises. Each does specific work. Each leaves something out. We'll show what."

## 4. Body (1:00–4:30)

> "Start with Peano. Five axioms, written by Giuseppe Peano in 1889. They describe the natural numbers: 1, 2, 3, and so on. Axiom one — 1 is a natural number. Axiom two — every natural number has a successor. Axiom three — 1 isn't anyone's successor. Axioms four and five — successor is one-to-one and induction works. From those five, you can derive every theorem about whole numbers.
>
> What's striking: Peano's axioms don't say what a number *is*. They say what a number *does* — has a successor, isn't successor of 1, supports induction. The numbers themselves are whatever satisfies those rules. A formalist mathematician shrugs at the question of whether numbers exist. The axioms don't need an answer.
>
> Now ZFC. The Zermelo-Fraenkel set theory axioms. Roughly nine, depending on how you count Choice and the Axiom Schema of Replacement. They describe what a set is allowed to do — combine, intersect, contain, choose, replace. From sets, you build numbers. From numbers, all the math anyone uses. Same pattern as Peano: the axioms describe behaviour, not essence.
>
> Now Euclid — older, geometric. Five postulates plus five common notions. *A straight line can be drawn from any point to any point.* *Things equal to the same thing are equal to each other.* Operational. Behavioural.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> Now VBM, which we wrote down yesterday. Five claims. *Numbers are real and alive.* *The decimal system is natural law.* *Digit-summing reveals stable structure.* *The doubling cycle is non-decaying.* *3, 6, 9 sit outside the cycle on a separate axis.*
>
> Look at claims one and two. They aren't behavioural. They aren't *if-then* statements about how numbers act. They're *is* statements — claims about what numbers *are* and where the decimal system *comes from*.
>
> That's the real difference. Mainstream axiom systems are silent on essence; they describe behaviour and let philosophy handle the rest. VBM breaks the silence. It says: numbers exist, base 10 is found, the patterns aren't artefacts.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> A formalist mathematician will tell you VBM's claims one and two aren't axioms — they're metaphysical assertions. That's not a dismissal; it's a true description. Claims three, four, five *are* axiom-like — testable statements about specific arithmetic patterns. The course makes that distinction explicit, every time. Mainstream vocabulary: *axioms*. VBM vocabulary: *claims*. We use both, on purpose."
