Wk 01 — Theme: Welcome — Numbers Are Living — Lens 5: Feel — Module B (Mathematical)
Runtime: 6:00. Author: draft. Status: Draft.

## 0. Authoring notes
Slowest lesson of the module. Voice paced 30% slower than Lenses 1-3. Pauses written in. Concrete imagery, no abstraction. The question this lesson sits in: *what's the difference between a symbol and an entity?* Mod A's Feel lesson asked *"why am I here?"* Mod B's asks *"what if the numbers really are?"* Don't preach. Hold the question.

## 1. Outcome
By the end of these 6 minutes, the student has spent quiet time with the difference between a number-as-symbol and a number-as-entity, and can write one sentence about which view they currently hold.

## 2. Hook (0:00–0:30)
*[On screen: a single black field. A small white "5" in the centre. Nothing else.]*

> *(slowly)* "Five."

## 3. Setup (0:30–1:00)
> "Lens 5 of 9. The contemplative beat. Six minutes. Not for new content — for sitting with one question: *is the 5 on the screen a symbol, or is it a thing?*"

## 4. Body (1:00–4:30) — *contemplative, paced, sparse*

*[Visual: the "5" remains. Optional: very slow, almost imperceptible motion — a tiny sway, a shift in luminance.]*

> *(slow)* "Most of the math you've ever done treats a number as a symbol. Five sheep. Five fingers. Five people in a queue. The numeral on the page stands in for a count. Useful. True, in its register. Limited.
>
> *[long pause — 8 seconds]*
>
> Marko asks a different question. Not what does five count. What is five.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> Hold the question for a moment. *What is five.* Not *what does five mean*. Not *what does five count*. Not *how do I use five*. *What is five.*
>
> *[long pause — 12 seconds, just the visual]*
>
> One answer says: nothing. Five doesn't exist. Only fivenesses exist — five sheep, five fingers — and we abstract a label and call it five for convenience. The number is a fiction we agree on. The mathematician Henri Poincaré took roughly this view.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> Another answer says: five exists, in the same sense that triangles exist, the way circles exist. The objects of mathematics are real, in their own register, independent of any sheep or fingers. Plato held this view. Many living mathematicians do, quietly, when they think no one is checking.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> Marko goes one further. Five exists, *and* five does work. The number isn't merely the abstract object Plato pointed at — it's an active particle, taking part in the structure of reality. *Numbers are alive.*
>
> *[long pause — 12 seconds]*
>
> You don't have to take Marko's view today. You don't have to take it ever. The course teaches the math regardless. But if you can hold the question — *what is five* — without rushing to one answer, you'll find that some of what we do this year reads differently than it would have otherwise.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> Take a breath. The number on the screen is whatever you say it is."

## 5. Try it (4:30–5:30)
> *(softer)* "In your notebook, beside the page where you wrote the five claims, write one sentence. *Right now, I think a number is ___.* Don't strive. Don't impress. Whatever's true today. We'll come back to it. Sixty seconds."

*[Pause card on screen for 60 seconds, with the still "5" behind.]*

> "Welcome back. That sentence is yours. It will likely change. Some weeks it will change twice. Come back and rewrite."

## 6. Bridge (5:30–6:00)
> *(returning to ordinary tempo)* "Next lesson — Apply. We've sat with the question. Next we look at where the five claims actually go — what each one buys you across the year. Lens 6."

*[Closing card: "Lesson 5 of 9 — Feel. Next: Apply."]*
