## 0. Authoring notes
The eight-lens recap is the lesson the Mod-A version got wrong (per `04_notes/PRODUCTION_ISSUES.md`). It defaulted to a bulleted list. This version writes the recap as flowing prose — the host walks the student through Module B in a single arc, not eight bullets. The forward-look hands off cleanly to Module C (practical lens — what students will produce by week 52).

## 1. Outcome
By the end of these 6 minutes, the student can describe Module B's arc in three sentences and name what Module C opens with.

## 3. Setup (0:30–1:00)

## 4. Body (1:00–4:30)

*[Visual: a quiet animation tying the week's pages together — a single page-turn through eight notebook spreads, each marked with what was added.]*

> "Module B opened by handing you the Symbol of Enlightenment as a mathematical object. Not the artefact you pinned on the wall last week, but the object itself: nine vertices, one cyclic order, two operations. That economy — the whole curriculum sits on this one diagram — is what we keep returning to.
>
> From there, you counted what the Symbol carries. Five claims. You wrote them in your notebook on a single page that will live with you all year. Numbers are real and alive. The decimal system is natural law, not human invention. Digit-summing reveals stable structure. The doubling cycle 1-2-4-8-7-5 is non-decaying. And 3, 6, 9 sit outside that cycle on a separate axis.
>
> Then you heard claim one in Marko's voice. Three and a half minutes of him saying *numbers are real and alive* in his own words, with subtitles, no host narration. You wrote *(in his own voice)* in the margin. The line and the recording are linked on your page now.
>
> Then a contemplative lesson. We sat with one question — *what is five?* — and you wrote a sentence about which view you currently hold. That sentence is dated. It will likely change. Some weeks it will change twice.
>
> Then you walked claim two forward through the year. Five consequences from one premise. The point wasn't the consequences themselves — those will arrive in their own weeks. The point was to feel how *load-bearing* a single claim can be.
>
> Then you placed VBM next to Peano, ZFC, Euclid. Mainstream axiom systems describe what objects do; VBM's claims, two of them, describe what objects are. That's a real difference and the course is honest about it.
>
> Then we worked the calibrated answer to a working mathematician's question. *Three of the five are axiom-like and we treat them that way. Two are metaphysical claims — we present them as Marko's framework, not as derived results.* Twenty-three words. Yours now.
>
> *[pause — show the spiral wheel, both modules lit]*
>
> That's Module B. Eight lessons. One module of mathematical orientation. Tomorrow Module C begins — same theme, practical lens. *The work students will produce by week 52.* Module A asked you why. Module B asked you what. Module C asks what you'll make."
