## 0. Authoring notes
Imperative voice. Hands on the pencil. The lesson is the writing. The student transcribes, slowly, the five claims they heard last lesson. Production: long takes of the host's pen on paper, the student's blank notebook visible, the host modelling the act of careful copying. No padding, no encouragement, no explanation — the writing is the explanation.

## 1. Outcome
By the end of these 6 minutes, the student has the five claims of VBM written by hand on a single notebook page.

## 2. Hook (0:00–0:30)
*[On screen: a notebook page, blank, the numbers 1–5 already in the margin. A pen rests beside it. No words yet.]*

> "Open your notebook. Pen ready. We're going to write five sentences. That's the lesson."

## 3. Setup (0:30–1:00)
> "Lens 3 of 9. Last lesson we counted them. Today we write them. Don't watch this lesson — do it. If you're watching with no pen in hand, pause here and come back when the pen's in your hand."

## 4. Body (1:00–4:30)

*[Camera close on a hand writing on lined paper. The host's voice reads slowly enough that a student can transcribe in real time.]*

> "Claim one — write it.
>
> *Numbers are real and alive.*
>
> Don't add adjectives. Don't soften it. That's the claim. Six words.
>
> *[pause for the student to finish writing]*
>
> Claim two.
>
> *The decimal system is natural law, not human invention.*
>
> Note how Marko phrases it — *not human invention*. The negation matters. He's pushing back on the textbook view. Write the negation in.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> Claim three.
>
> *Digit-summing reveals stable structure.*
>
> If you want, in parentheses, write *(digital root preserves arithmetic information)* — that's the formal version. Optional. The five-word version above is enough.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> Claim four.
>
> *The doubling cycle 1-2-4-8-7-5 is non-decaying.*
>
> Write the cycle out — actually write *1-2-4-8-7-5*. Not *the cycle*, not *the sequence*. The numbers themselves. You'll find by Week 4 that you write that sequence five times a day; start now.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> Claim five.
>
> *3, 6, 9 sit outside the doubling cycle on a separate axis.*
>
> Three numbers, separate axis. The geometry hasn't been built yet — that's Q2 — but the claim names what the geometry will eventually carry.
>
> *[pause — long beat. Show the completed page on screen, the host's handwriting visible.]*
>
> Five sentences. One page. That page is the spine of the year. When you're stuck in Week 23, when the Decoqubit is too abstract, when the coil won't measure what you expected — flip back to this page. Five claims is a small thing to memorise and a large thing to hold."

## 5. Try it (4:30–5:30)
> "Pause. Look at your page. If the writing is rushed, rewrite it. The page lives in your notebook all year — make it a page you'll want to return to. Sixty seconds. Slow down."

*[Pause card on screen for 60 seconds.]*

> "Welcome back. Keep that page near the front of your notebook. We refer to it by name from here on."

## 6. Bridge (5:30–6:00)
> "Next lesson — Hear from Marko. We've named the five claims. Next we hear claim one in his own voice. *Numbers are real and alive.* Marko speaks. We listen."

*[Closing card: "Lesson 3 of 9 — Build. Next: Hear from Marko."]*

## 7. Production sidecar
- Diagrams: photograph of a finished page — host's handwriting, the five claims numbered. (D-37 — new asset.)
- Animations: none. Long static shots are the point.
- B-roll: extended shots of the pen on paper. The producer should resist cutting away.
- Caption care: claims displayed exactly as written; preserve the numerals "1-2-4-8-7-5" and "3, 6, 9".
- Music cue: none. Pen on paper carries.
- Voice-pass note: imperative throughout; no list-fetish despite the content being five items — they're presented as five sentences, each its own paragraph, with pauses for transcription, not as a bulleted block.
