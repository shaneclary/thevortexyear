## 4. Body (1:00–4:30)

> "**Claim one. Numbers are real and alive.** Not symbols for other things. Themselves things. Marko's word is *elementary particles* — at the level of reality where physics meets arithmetic, numbers are the particles. This claim is the load-bearing one. Without it, everything else in VBM is a curiosity about modular arithmetic. With it, the curiosity becomes a description of how the universe is built.
>
> **Claim two. The decimal system is natural law, not human invention.** Base ten is found, not chosen. Marko grants that humans count in tens because of fingers — but argues the ten-ness was waiting to be counted. The patterns we'll meet only show up cleanly in base ten. In any other base they're different patterns or no pattern at all. Either base ten is privileged, or the patterns are an accident. Marko picks the first.
>
> **Claim three. Digit-summing reveals stable structure.** Take any whole number, sum its digits, repeat until one digit remains. The result — the *digital root* — preserves something about the original under multiplication and addition. We'll prove the formal version next week: digital root equals the number modulo 9. Today: it's the operation that makes every later pattern visible.
>
> **Claim four. The doubling cycle 1-2-4-8-7-5 is non-decaying.** Double 1, reduce, you get 2. Double 2, you get 4. Continue: 8, then 7, then 5, then back to 1. Six steps, closed loop, runs forever. Crucially — runs without losing information. No starting point breaks it. No iteration count weakens it. Marko reads this as evidence that the cycle is structural, not merely arithmetic.
>
> **Claim five. The numbers 3, 6, 9 sit outside the doubling cycle on a separate axis.** Try doubling 3: you get 6, then 3, then 6 — a two-step oscillation. Try doubling 9: you stay at 9 forever. The trinary {3, 6, 9} isn't broken; it's *separate*. Marko reads this as evidence the decimal partition has structure that pure arithmetic doesn't predict.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> Five claims. Two of them — claims one and two — assert that the math reflects something real. Three of them — claims three, four, five — describe specific arithmetic patterns whose internal validity you can check on paper. The course teaches all five. The arithmetic claims we prove. The reality claims we present in Marko's voice and let you decide.
>
> Compare the count. Peano's axioms for the natural numbers: five. Euclid's for plane geometry: five. ZFC for set theory: nine. The size of an axiom set isn't the measure of its depth — but five is small. VBM is doing a lot with very little."
