## 0. Authoring notes
Lens 8 is *Defend*. For Week 1, the objection isn't a specific mathematical claim — it's the meta-objection: *is this real math?* Address it head-on. The lesson must be honest enough that a sceptical friend wouldn't be embarrassed to be quoted in it, *and* clear enough that a committed student doesn't feel demoralised. The lens repeats every week with concept-specific objections; this is the meta-template for how those lessons feel. Tone: respectful, specific, doesn't dismiss.

## 1. Outcome
By the end of these 6 minutes, the student can answer the question "is this real math?" with one calibrated sentence.

## 2. Hook (0:00–0:30)
*[On screen: a single line of text, no other visuals.]*

> "Tell a sceptical friend you're taking a year-long course in vortex-based mathematics. They will ask one question. Let's give you the honest answer."

## 3. Setup (0:30–1:00)
> "Lesson 8 of 9. Defend. Every week from now on, this lesson hands you the strongest objection to that week's concept and the response we recommend. For Week One, the objection is the big one — *is this real math?* — and the response is going to take all six minutes."

## 4. Body (1:00–4:30)

> "Here is the objection in its strongest form. Mainstream mathematicians look at vortex-based mathematics and see *modular arithmetic in base ten*. Reducing numbers by summing their digits — that's the same as taking the number modulo nine. The 1-2-4-8-7-5 cycle is the orbit of two under multiplication mod nine. The polar pairs are pairs that sum to nine. The 3-6-9 are the multiples of three. *None of this is news.* It's freshman discrete mathematics, dressed up in different language.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> That objection is correct as far as it goes. The arithmetic at the centre of this course is modular arithmetic. We don't pretend otherwise. In Lens 2 — Count — we'll do digit reductions and we'll *call them* digit reductions. In Lens 7 — Compare — we'll show you the conventional notation alongside the Rodin notation. We don't hide it.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> But the objection has a hole in it. The objection assumes that *because* the arithmetic is well-known, *therefore* the patterns Marko points to are uninteresting. That doesn't follow. A pattern can be familiar at one level and entirely overlooked at another. The orbit of 2 mod 9 is well-known. The fact that this orbit, embedded on a torus with the polar pairs as antipodes and the 3-6-9 as a perpendicular axis, gives you a coil winding pattern with specific electromagnetic properties — that is *not* well-known. That is what we study in this course."

*[Visual: a split screen — left side, conventional math textbook page. Right side, a Rodin diagram with the same content marked.]*

> "So here's our calibrated answer to the question *is this real math?*
>
> Yes — when we do arithmetic, prove cycle lengths, derive geometric structure, the math is conventional and defensible. A mathematician who watched Lens 2 of any week would not find an error.
>
> Where Marko Rodin extends the work is *what he claims the math is for*. He claims the modular arithmetic of base ten reflects an objective structure of reality — a structure with physical, geometric, and metaphysical implications. *That* claim is much stronger and is not established by the arithmetic alone. The course will teach the math rigorously and present the larger claim as Marko's framework — supported by some evidence, contested by other evidence, and worth your serious attention either way.
>
> The honest one-sentence answer to your sceptical friend: *the arithmetic is straightforward modular arithmetic; what's interesting is the geometric and physical claims built on top of it, which are still being tested.*
>
> That sentence will hold up at any dinner party. Memorise it."

## 5. Try it (4:30–5:30)

Now say it in your own words — not memorised, but sourced from your own understanding of the framing. Sixty seconds."
