## 0. Authoring notes
Lens 6 is *Apply*. For Week 1, the application is: where does a year of this work actually take people? This is the "previously on the future" lesson — show three concrete examples of work students will produce by week 52. The point is to make the year tangible *as outcomes*, not just as time. Three short vignettes work better than one long one. The org should curate the three case studies — current placeholders are illustrative.

## 1. Outcome
By the end of these 6 minutes, the student can describe three concrete artefacts a graduate of this course produces by week 52.

## 2. Hook (0:00–0:30)
*[On screen: three artefacts laid out — a notebook full of hand-drawn diagrams, a built Rodin Coil, a research write-up.]*

> "Three things, by the end of this year. Not vague things — specific things. By week 52 you will own all three. Let me show you what they look like."

## 3. Setup (0:30–1:00)
> "Lesson 6 of 9. Apply. The lens that asks: where does this work *land*? For Week 1, we're previewing the year — three artefacts you'll have produced by week fifty-two. Mileposts. Things you can hold up and say *I made this*."

## 4. Body (1:00–4:30)

*[Cut to artefact 1: a notebook open on a desk, full of hand-drawn diagrams.]*

> "**Artefact one: the practice notebook.** By week 52 your notebook holds — at minimum — fifty-two drawings of the Symbol of Enlightenment, each at a different week, each showing what you were studying that week. The Doubling Circuit traced in red. The polar pairs marked in blue. The torus number map sketched flat, then on a paper torus you folded yourself. Phyllotaxis spirals. Enneagram overlays. Page after page. The notebook is the *visible memory* of the year. Students who keep one say it's the single thing they refer back to most after the course ends.
>
> [`[ORG-REVIEW]` — *insert quote from a real graduate, if available, on what their notebook means to them.*]"

*[Cut to artefact 2: a Rodin Coil sitting on a workbench, multimeter beside it, a build log open showing measurements.]*

> "**Artefact two: a working Rodin Coil and its build log.** Quarter Three, weeks twenty-seven through thirty-five. You'll wind a coil from scratch. You'll measure it — DC resistance, inductance, self-resonant frequency. You'll keep a build log. By week fifty-two you have a physical object you can hand to a sceptical engineer and say: *here are my measurements, here's the construction, here's the geometry that produced it*. That is what 'I learned vortex-based mathematics' looks like to someone who works with their hands. It is incontrovertible evidence that you did the work."

*[Cut to artefact 3: a written research write-up — the capstone document.]*

> "**Artefact three: your capstone write-up.** Weeks fifty and fifty-one. Each student picks one capstone — could be a deeper build, could be a research write-up of a Rodin claim you tested, could be a teaching artefact you can use to bring others to this work. The capstone is *yours*, not ours. It's the artefact that says: *I'm not just a student of this work, I have made my own contribution to it*. The Rodin and Powell organisation maintains a portfolio of student capstones — by week 52 yours is in it."

*[Wide shot returns: all three artefacts side by side.]*

> "Notebook. Coil. Capstone. Three things, fifty-two weeks. The numbers we counted in Lesson 2 turn into objects. That's where this work lands."

## 5. Try it (4:30–5:30)
> "Pause. In your notebook — already starting to fill its job description — write down which of those three artefacts you're most excited about. Some students light up at the notebook. Some can't wait for the build. Some are already drafting the capstone in their heads. Whichever one is yours: write its name. We'll come back to it. One minute."

*[Pause card on screen for 60 seconds.]*

> "Welcome back. That's a kind of compass for you. Whenever a week gets hard — and weeks will — remember which of those three artefacts you're working toward. The hard week is the cost of the artefact."

## 6. Bridge (5:30–6:00)
> "Next lesson — Compare. We've seen what this work produces. The next lens places it on the map of conventional knowledge. How is this course like — and unlike — a math class? A physics class? A meditation course? Lens 7: compare."

*[Closing card: "Lesson 6 of 9 — Apply. Next: Compare."]*
