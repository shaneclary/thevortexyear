## 0. Authoring notes
Lens 7 is *Compare*. For Week 1, the comparison is: how is this course similar to — and different from — three things students might confuse it with: a math class, a physics class, and a meditation course. Don't position the course as superior. Position it accurately. Students who know what kind of object they're holding study it correctly.

## 1. Outcome
By the end of these 6 minutes, the student can name three things this course **shares with** and **departs from** in conventional math, physics, and contemplative practice.

## 2. Hook (0:00–0:30)
*[On screen: three doors, each labelled — "Math class", "Physics class", "Meditation course". A fourth door appears: "This course".]*

> "If you tell a friend you're taking a year-long course in vortex-based mathematics, they'll picture one of three things. None of them is quite right. Let's place this course accurately on the map."

## 3. Setup (0:30–1:00)
> "Lesson 7 of 9. Compare. Six minutes to locate this course among the things students might mistake it for. We're not here to claim superiority. We're here to be specific."

## 4. Body (1:00–4:30)

*[Visual: three columns build, one per comparator.]*

> "**Comparator one: a math class.** Like a math class, this course teaches arithmetic, modular structure, geometry, and proof. We'll do digit reductions, derive cycle lengths, prove polar-pair symmetries, construct quotient surfaces. If a math professor visited Week Four, they'd recognise the math. *Unlike* a math class, this course doesn't stop at 'the pattern is consistent under base-10 reduction'. It goes further: it asks what the pattern *is for* — Marko's claim that the structure is physically instantiated, not just symbolic. A math class won't follow it there. We will. Honestly, with the question marks where they belong."

*[Second column highlights.]*

> "**Comparator two: a physics class.** Like a physics class, this course will eventually talk about energy, fields, antennas, and propulsion. In Quarter Three you'll build a coil and measure its electrical properties. *Unlike* a physics class, the course doesn't begin from established conservation laws and derive forwards. It begins from a number pattern Marko Rodin discovered and asks: *if this pattern is physically instantiated, what would we expect to measure?* Some of those expectations have been tested; some haven't. We'll be specific each time about which is which."

*[Third column highlights.]*

> "**Comparator three: a meditation course.** Like a meditation course, this course returns to the same object — the Symbol of Enlightenment — week after week, with patience, until the object reveals more than it did at first glance. Like meditation, it values slow study over fast consumption. *Unlike* a meditation course, the work isn't introspective. It's outward — the Symbol exists, the math exists, the coil exists. The contemplative register is one of nine; the others are arithmetic, geometric, engineering, comparative. We borrow the *patience* of contemplative practice without confusing the course for one."

*[Fourth column — "This course" — appears bridging the other three.]*

> "**This course** sits at the intersection. It's not pure math, not pure physics, not pure contemplation. Marko Rodin and Randy Powell have been moving between those three rooms for decades, insisting that the work belongs in all three. This course doesn't ask you to take their word for it. It teaches each register on its own terms — and lets you decide, by week fifty-two, whether the rooms connect the way Marko and Randy say they do."

## 5. Try it (4:30–5:30)
> "Pause. In your notebook, sketch the four columns. Math. Physics. Meditation. This course. In each column write *one sentence* — what you expect from a course of that kind. We're testing your map of expectations now so we can revisit it at week twenty-six and again at week fifty-two and see how it changed. Sixty seconds."

*[Pause card on screen for 60 seconds.]*

> "Welcome back. Save those four sentences. We'll ask you to come back to them at the quarter-mark."

## 6. Bridge (5:30–6:00)
> "Next lesson — Defend. We've placed the course accurately on the map. But *is this real math?* That's the question your sceptical friend will ask. The next lens gives you the honest first answer — and prepares you for the longer answer that takes the rest of the year. Lens 8: defend."

*[Closing card: "Lesson 7 of 9 — Compare. Next: Defend."]*
