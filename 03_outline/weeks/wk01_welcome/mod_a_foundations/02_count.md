## 0. Authoring notes
Lens 2 is *Count*. For an orientation theme, "count" means: count your commitment. Make the year tangible in numbers. This is the lesson where students decide whether they're really doing this. Be honest about the time, the work, the rhythm. The student who finishes this lesson knows exactly what they signed up for — and that clarity is what makes them stay.

## 1. Outcome
By the end of these 6 minutes, the student can state their weekly commitment in concrete numbers: ~3 hours per week, 27 lessons per week, one theme per week, for 52 weeks.

## 2. Hook (0:00–0:30)
*[On screen: a clock face. Hour markers transform into "3 hours" / "27 lessons" / "52 weeks".]*

> "If you do this course as designed, you will spend 140 hours with us across one year. Let's make those numbers real before they sneak up on you."

## 3. Setup (0:30–1:00)
> "Lesson 2 of 9. We just *saw* the year. Now we *count* it. This is the lens that turns 'a year-long course' into 'three hours on Tuesday and Thursday'. Boring? A little. Important? Completely. Here we go."

## 4. Body (1:00–4:30)

*[Visual: a stack of cards, each one a number, building up.]*

> "Six minutes. That's a lesson. Read the card.
>
> *[Card: 6 minutes per lesson.]*
>
> Nine lessons make a module. That's fifty-four minutes — about a high-school class period.
>
> *[Card: 9 lessons × 6 min = 54 min per module.]*
>
> Three modules make a week. Two and a half to three hours.
>
> *[Card: 3 modules × 54 min = ~2.7 hours per week.]*
>
> Twenty-seven lessons in a week. That's the number to remember — twenty-seven six-minute conversations with one idea.
>
> *[Card: 27 lessons per week.]*
>
> Fifty-two weeks. One year.
>
> *[Card: 52 weeks.]*
>
> Total: one thousand four hundred and four lessons. About one hundred and forty hours of video. In real time, slightly less than three hours a week — you'll come out the other side fluent."

*[Visual: zoom out — show the same numbers laid out as a typical week's schedule.]*

> "Here's how I'd structure your week if I were you. Three sittings, an hour apiece. One sitting for Module A — the foundations lens. One for Module B — the mathematical lens. One for Module C — the practical lens. Or, if you prefer, three half-hour sittings of an hour, on three different days. The course doesn't care; you do.
>
> Some weeks will run long — the build weeks in Quarter Three, the capstone weeks at the end — and the time will spill into actual project work. We'll signpost those weeks. For ninety percent of the year, plan three hours."

*[Visual: a horizontal bar — 52 weeks long — with a current-week marker at week 1.]*

> "And one more number that matters more than any of the others: **one**. One week at a time. You don't watch ahead. You don't binge. You don't catch up by skipping. The course is a spiral, and the spiral only works if you walk it at the pace it's calibrated for. Three hours a week. Twenty-seven lessons. Fifty-two times. Then you're done."

## 5. Try it (4:30–5:30)
> "Pause the lesson. Open your calendar. Find three hours next week — three one-hour blocks, ideally — and put them on the calendar with the title 'Rodin'. Don't pick weekends if you usually work weekends; pick weekdays. Don't pick after dinner if you fall asleep at nine; pick before lunch. Put yourself in your own way. Come back when you've done it. Sixty seconds."

*[Pause card on screen for 60 seconds.]*

> "Welcome back. If you put it on the calendar, you'll show up. If you didn't, this course will get away from you. That's not a threat — it's the math of attention."

## 6. Bridge (5:30–6:00)
> "Next lesson — Build. We're going to make a list of the few simple things you'll need across the year: a notebook, a pencil, a printed Symbol of Enlightenment. Nothing expensive. Nothing technical yet. The build lens, in its simplest form."

*[Closing card: "Lesson 2 of 9 — Count. Next: Build."]*
