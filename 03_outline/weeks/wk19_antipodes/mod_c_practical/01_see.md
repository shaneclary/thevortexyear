## 4. Body (1:00–4:30)

> "The canonical six-strand Rodin Coil has *six wires* wound on the torus former. Today's lens shows how the six are organised into *three antipodal pairs* with counter-current flow.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> *Six-strand layout.*
>
> *Strand 1.* Passes through value-1 cells. Starts at column 0, row 0. Wound following the doubling helix path.
>
> *Strand 2.* Passes through value-2 cells. Starts at column 0, row 1. Wound following the same helix path but offset by one row.
>
> *Strand 4.* Passes through value-4 cells. Starts at column 0, row 2.
>
> *Strand 8.* Passes through value-8 cells. Starts at column 0, row 3.
>
> *Strand 7.* Passes through value-7 cells. Starts at column 0, row 4.
>
> *Strand 5.* Passes through value-5 cells. Starts at column 0, row 5.
>
> *Six strands wound simultaneously*, each occupying its own row's worth of cells, each following the doubling helix from its starting row.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> *Pairing into antipodal partners.*
>
> *Pair 1: Strands 1 and 8.* Strand 1 (row 0) and strand 8 (row 3). Antipodes (row 0 + 3 = row 3). *Polar pair (1, 8).*
>
> *Pair 2: Strands 2 and 7.* Strand 2 (row 1) and strand 7 (row 4). Antipodes. *Polar pair (2, 7).*
>
> *Pair 3: Strands 4 and 5.* Strand 4 (row 2) and strand 5 (row 5). Antipodes. *Polar pair (4, 5).*
>
> Three pairs. Each pair's strands occupy antipodal positions on the torus through their entire length.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> *Counter-current flow.*
>
> Within each pair, the two strands are connected to power supplies such that *currents flow in opposite directions* through them.
>
> Strand 1: current flows in direction A (e.g., column 0 → column 1 → column 2 → … → column 23 → column 0).
>
> Strand 8: current flows in direction B (the opposite — column 0 → column 23 → column 22 → … → column 1 → column 0).
>
> *Or equivalently*, currents are equal magnitude but opposite signs. *Counter-current flow.*
>
> The same applies to pairs 2-and-7, 4-and-5.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> *Why counter-currents?*
>
> *Because of cancellation.* When two parallel wires carry equal counter-currents, their magnetic fields *partially cancel*. The closer the wires, the more complete the cancellation.
>
> On the Rodin Coil torus, paired strands are *antipodal* — at maximum distance through the surface but *closer through the doughnut hole* than around the surface. *The through-hole distance enables specific field cancellation patterns.*
>
> The result: a coil with *specific magnetic field structure* — the cancellation pattern is what produces the claimed field-shape behaviour.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> Three observations.
>
> *Observation one.* The counter-current pairing is *necessary* for the canonical Rodin Coil pattern. Co-current pairing (currents in the same direction) gives a different coil with different (and worse, by Rodin Foundation conventions) properties.
>
> *Observation two.* The pairing is *implemented at the terminals*, not at the wire. Each strand has two ends; pair partners' ends are connected to opposite poles of the power supply. The wire path is identical for both members of a pair; the *electrical connection* makes them counter-current.
>
> *Observation three.* The counter-current arrangement is *bifilar* in mainstream electronics vocabulary. *The Rodin Coil is, in part, a bifilar arrangement of multiple wires on a toroidal substrate.*
>
> *[pause]*
>
> Practical takeaway. *Plan three antipodal pairs when designing a six-strand build.* Use six wire colours (one per strand) for visual clarity. Mark pair partners by colour scheme — strand 1 red, strand 8 dark red; strand 2 blue, strand 7 dark blue; etc."
