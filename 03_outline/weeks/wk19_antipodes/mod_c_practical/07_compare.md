## 4. Body (1:00–4:30)

> "*Bifilar inductor.* Two wires wound together (like a twisted pair), both following the same path. When energised with opposite-direction currents, fields partially cancel.
>
> Used for: common-mode chokes, low-noise inductors, signal-isolation inductors. Standard in RF and audio engineering.
>
> *Rodin counter-wound coil.* Two wires wound on a torus following antipodal paths (not the same path). When energised with opposite-direction currents, fields partially cancel.
>
> Used for: Rodin Coil engineering, claimed unconventional field-shape applications.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> Three structural similarities.
>
> *Similarity one — counter-current.* Both arrangements use opposite-direction currents through paired wires.
>
> *Similarity two — field cancellation.* Both produce partial magnetic field cancellation. Reduced external field; modified internal field.
>
> *Similarity three — engineering vocabulary.* Both fall under the *bifilar* family in mainstream electronics terminology. Engineers familiar with bifilar can recognise the Rodin variant immediately.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> Three structural differences.
>
> *Difference one — wire path.* Bifilar wires follow *identical paths* (twisted together). Rodin counter-wound wires follow *antipodal paths* on a torus surface.
>
> *Difference two — distance between paired wires.* Bifilar: very close (millimetres). Rodin: across the torus surface (centimetres) but through the doughnut hole (smaller than surface distance).
>
> *Difference three — topological context.* Bifilar arrangements work on any substrate (cylindrical core, planar substrate, etc.). Rodin counter-wound *requires* the torus structure for the antipodal pairing to work.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> *Engineering implications of the differences.*
>
> *Distance affects cancellation.* Cancellation is most complete when paired wires are closest. *Bifilar (close)* gives near-complete cancellation in the bulk; *Rodin counter-wound (further apart on surface)* gives partial cancellation with specific spatial structure.
>
> *Specific structure matters for Rodin.* The Rodin *partial* cancellation — combined with the doubling-cycle wind pattern — produces the specific field structure VBM claims. *Mainstream bifilar produces uniform cancellation; Rodin counter-wound produces structured cancellation.*
>
> *[pause]*
>
> *What an EM engineer would say.*
>
> *Engineer's likely first observation.* 'You're doing bifilar but with the wires on opposite sides of the torus instead of twisted together.'
>
> *Engineer's likely second observation.* 'The distance between paired wires is large compared to a typical bifilar inductor. Cancellation will be less complete.'
>
> *Engineer's likely third observation.* 'The doubling pattern is unusual. What's the design rationale?'
>
> *Your likely response.* The unusual pattern is *the point* — the doubling structure plus antipodal counter-current produces a specific field configuration that uniform bifilar doesn't. *Q3 measurement will tell us how this configuration differs from mainstream predictions.*
>
> *[pause]*
>
> Useful framing. *Rodin counter-stranding is structurally a specialised form of bifilar arrangement, where the wire paths are antipodal on a torus rather than identical-and-twisted. The mainstream bifilar principles apply (counter-current, field cancellation), but the spatial configuration is specific to the doubling structure.*
>
> Practical takeaway. *Use the bifilar framing in conversations with mainstream electrical engineers.* They'll recognise the family of techniques immediately. The Rodin specifics are a *specialisation* within the family.
>
> A note on overclaiming. *Don't claim Rodin counter-stranding is fundamentally novel*. It's a careful application of standard bifilar principles on a specific topology. The novelty is in the *pattern*, not in the *underlying technique*."
