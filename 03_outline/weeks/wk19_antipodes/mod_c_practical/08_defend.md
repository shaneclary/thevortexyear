## 4. Body (1:00–4:30)

> "The objection. *Counter-stranding doubles the complexity of the build. Single-strand coils are easier to wind, easier to measure, easier to compare. Why bother with counter-stranding for the canonical build?*
>
> The objection is fair for *learning builds*. For canonical-tradition builds, counter-stranding is essential.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> *Three reasons counter-stranding matters for canonical builds.*
>
> *Reason one — pair symmetry is structural.* Q1 established polar pairs algebraically. Q2's antipodal correspondence shows polar pairs are antipodes. *A coil that ignores this structure isn't realising the framework's geometry — it's just a wound coil that happens to use the doubling helix.* The counter-stranding is what *implements* the pair structure in hardware.
>
> *Reason two — claimed effects depend on the symmetry.* Marko's and Powell's stronger claims about Rodin Coils — non-Hertzian fields, novel cancellation patterns, etc. — depend on the *full counter-wound symmetric structure*, not just any single-strand or non-counter-wound version. *Without counter-stranding, you're not testing the full claim.*
>
> *Reason three — comparability.* Foundation-published measurements use counter-wound coils. *Single-strand or co-current coils produce different measurements.* If you want to compare your results to the foundation tradition, you need to match the canonical configuration.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> *Why single-strand is still useful.*
>
> *Use 1 — first build.* Q3 Wk 33 starts with single-strand to *learn the wind technique*. Multi-strand builds add complexity that obscures basic winding errors.
>
> *Use 2 — basic measurement.* Single-strand coils are easier to characterise. Resistance, basic inductance, single-strand magnetic field. *Foundational measurements.*
>
> *Use 3 — comparison baseline.* Comparing single-strand and multi-strand results reveals how counter-stranding modifies the field. *Both versions are valuable for evaluation.*
>
> *[pause]*
>
> *Counter-stranding is required for:*
>
> *Required 1 — canonical Rodin Coil identity.* If the coil isn't counter-stranded, it's not the canonical Rodin Coil. *It might be a useful coil for some purpose; it's just not what the framework specifies.*
>
> *Required 2 — testing strong claims.* Energy-density, non-Hertzian, cancellation-pattern claims require the counter-stranded configuration to test.
>
> *Required 3 — foundation-tradition collaboration.* Builders comparing results across the community need matching configurations.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> So the calibrated answer to *why counter-stranding matters*:
>
> *Counter-stranding implements the polar-pair-antipode structure in hardware. Without it, the coil ignores the framework's structural geometry. Foundation-published measurements use counter-wound coils; results are not comparable without matching configuration. Stronger Rodin Coil claims (cancellation patterns, novel field structures) depend on the counter-wound symmetry. Single-strand coils are useful for first builds and baselines but don't realise the canonical Rodin Coil. Counter-stranding is required for canonical builds; recommended for any builder serious about evaluating the framework's claims.*
>
> Around eighty words.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> Practical takeaway. *Wind single-strand first (Wk 33), counter-wound second (Wk 35).* Both have value. The single-strand teaches winding; the counter-wound realises the canonical configuration.
>
> Don't conflate them. *Different coils, different purposes, both useful.*"
