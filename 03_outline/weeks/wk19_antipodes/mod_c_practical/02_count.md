## 4. Body (1:00–4:30)

> "Today we count strands and explain why six is the canonical number.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> *The 6 = 2 × 3 decomposition.*
>
> Three polar pair *types*: (1, 8), (2, 7), (4, 5). Three independent antipode relationships.
>
> Each pair-type needs *two strands* — one for each side of the pair. *Two strands per pair-type × three pair-types = six strands.*
>
> The number 6 is forced if you want *every polar pair represented exactly once* with *both its members carrying current*.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> *Alternative strand counts and what they cover.*
>
> *1 strand.* Single doubling helix. Visits 24 cells through one row's worth of values. *Doesn't cover any pair* directly — only one half of each. Useful for first builds (Wk 33) but doesn't realise the counter-current pattern.
>
> *2 strands.* One pair only. Two strands counter-wound. *One polar pair* of three covered. Partial implementation.
>
> *4 strands.* Two pairs. Two of three polar pair types covered. *Asymmetric* — favours two pair-types over the third.
>
> *6 strands.* Three pairs. All three polar pair types covered. *Symmetric and complete.* The canonical configuration.
>
> *8 or more strands.* Some strand-types repeat. For instance: 12 strands could give two strands per row-position. *Higher density but no new pair structure.* Used in some experimental builds; not canonical.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> *Why 6 is canonical, not other multiples of 2 or 3.*
>
> The Rodin Coil's structure has *exactly three polar pairs* — derived from the six units of (ℤ/9ℤ)*. Six strands give *one strand per unit*; this is *canonical coverage* of the (ℤ/9ℤ)* group.
>
> *Three strands* would cover only one element per pair, breaking the symmetry. *Twelve strands* would double-cover, adding redundancy without new structure. *Six is the natural fit.*
>
> *[pause]*
>
> *Strand-count counts on the wound coil.*
>
> Per strand: 96 turns (24 cells × 4 traversals).
>
> Per coil: 6 × 96 = 576 turns total.
>
> Per coil per traversal: 6 × 24 = 144 turns.
>
> Same numbers as Wk 14 Mod C — the strand count and the cell count are connected.
>
> *Wire length per strand: ~3 m. Total wire: ~18 m.*
>
> *Solder joints per coil: 12* (6 strand starts + 6 strand ends).
>
> *Number of distinct power supply leads: 6* (each strand has its own pair of wires going to the supply, but they share return paths in pair couplings — typical setup uses 6 supply leads with internal pairing).
>
> *[pause]*
>
> Three observations.
>
> *Observation one.* The number 6 is *meaningful structurally*. It's the order of (ℤ/9ℤ)*; the period of the doubling cycle; the count of unit values. The coil's strand-count carries this structure.
>
> *Observation two.* The number 6 is *not arbitrary engineering*. Other strand counts produce different coils with different properties. Six is the *Rodin canonical*.
>
> *Observation three.* The *number 3* (polar pair types) and the *number 2* (members per pair) factor into 6. Both factor counts are independently meaningful — 3 is the number of cosets of family-3 in ℤ/9ℤ; 2 is the order of the antipodal involution.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> Practical takeaway. *Six is the right strand count for the canonical Rodin Coil.* Other counts have their uses but produce non-canonical coils. *When sourcing wire and planning a multi-strand build, plan for six strands.*"
