## 0. Authoring notes
The hand spans the donut for the first time. Contemplative on the embodied experience of bilateral attention required by counter-winding.

> *(slow)* "Both hands at once. Across the donut. Sit with the spanning."

## 3. Setup (0:30–1:00)

## 4. Body (1:00–4:30)

> "Multi-strand winding requires a body-skill that single-strand winding doesn't: *bilateral attention*. The hand at strand A's current cell. The other hand at strand B's antipodal cell. Both attended to simultaneously.
>
> *[long pause — 12 seconds]*
>
> Sit with the prospect.
>
> *[long pause]*
>
> Most ordinary tasks use one hand at a time, with the other hand stabilising or doing something supportive. Knife and fork. Pen and paper.
>
> Multi-strand winding is different. *Two hands, equal attention, opposite sides of an object.* The hands work as *peers*, not as *primary and supporting*.
>
> Some students find this *natural* — they've practised bilateral skills (drumming, juggling, multi-stranded knitting). Others find it *new* — most people don't develop bilateral hand-skills past childhood.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> Three contemplations on bilateral attention.
>
> *Contemplation one — the body learns the geometry.* When you wind both strands simultaneously, your hands *internalise the antipodal map*. Strand A's hand at (0, 0); strand B's hand at (12, 3). Strand A's hand at (1, 1); strand B's hand at (13, 4). *The hands move in coordinated antipodal step.*
>
> Over hours of practice, the antipodal pattern becomes *physical* — the hands know where the partner is without conscious computation.
>
> *Contemplation two — bilateral attention is rare cognition.* Most cognitive tasks are unilateral or sequential. Bilateral simultaneous attention requires the brain to *parallelise*. Some practitioners find this builds new neural connections; others find it tiring.
>
> Either response is honest. The skill is *unusual* enough to be worth practising for its own sake — quite apart from coil winding.
>
> *Contemplation three — the donut spans the body.* The torus former is *small enough to span with two hands*. A coil on a much larger torus (say, 30 cm major) would require both arms. A coil on a much smaller torus (say, 2 cm major) would require one hand and a tool.
>
> The 10 cm canonical size is *deliberately hand-spanning*. Two hands fit comfortably on opposite sides. *The size matches the body.*
>
> *[long pause — 12 seconds]*
>
> What does anticipating bilateral winding feel like?
>
> Some students notice *intrigue*. Bilateral skill is unusual; the prospect of practising one is interesting.
>
> Some students notice *daunted preparation*. The skill is harder than single-stranding; it'll take longer to acquire.
>
> Some students notice *embodied anticipation*. They can already feel their hands forming the bilateral posture, even before any wire is in their fingers. *Imagined practice is itself practice.*
>
> All three are honest.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> A practical observation. *Most builders find single-strand winding easy and multi-strand winding hard*, especially the first few times. The hard part isn't the geometry — the marked former handles that. *The hard part is bilateral attention.*
>
> Q3's Wk 35 (Multi-strand) is where this skill is built. Before then, you can practise on the paper torus by tracing two helices simultaneously with two fingers. *Tracing rehearses the bilateral move without committing wire.*
>
> *[pause]*
>
> One more contemplation. Bilateral hand-skill is *one of the gifts of long-form work*. By the time you've wound your fifth or tenth Rodin Coil, your hands will have the skill that was unfamiliar at the start. *The body learns. The hands acquire vocabulary they didn't have.*
>
> Coil winders, basket weavers, lace makers, and complex-needlework practitioners all share this kind of bilateral hand-skill. *The Rodin tradition joins this lineage of bilateral makers.*
>
> Hold the threaded torus. Feel both sides at once. The bilateral attention is rehearsal."
