## 0. Authoring notes
Antipodes felt physically with your finger. Contemplative on the embodied experience of touching antipodal points simultaneously.

## 1. Outcome
Student has spent quiet time with the embodied experience of touching paired antipodal cells.

## 2. Hook (0:00–0:30)
*[Two fingers touching opposite sides of the threaded torus, the thread visible between.]*

> *(slow)* "Touch the pair. Feel the chord."

## 3. Setup (0:30–1:00)
> "Lens 5 of 9. Six minutes, slower."

## 4. Body (1:00–4:30) — *contemplative*

> *(slow)* "Hold your threaded torus. Place one finger at cell (0, 0) — value 1. Reach with another finger to cell (12, 3) — value 8.
>
> *Both fingers touch the torus simultaneously, on opposite sides.* The thread between them passes through the doughnut hole.
>
> *[long pause — 12 seconds]*
>
> Sit with the gesture.
>
> *[long pause]*
>
> Two fingers, one thread, one pair. The polar pair (1, 8) is *between your fingers*. *Algebra has become geometry, has become embodied gesture.*
>
> *[pause]*
>
> Three contemplations on this gesture.
>
> *Contemplation one — the body knows what the math derives.* You don't need to compute that 1 + 8 = 9 mod 9. *Your fingers feel that the cells are partners through the hole.* The body's spatial intuition recognises antipodes faster than arithmetic does.
>
> Some students are surprised by how *immediate* the body's recognition is. The math takes thought; the gesture takes only attention.
>
> *Contemplation two — the gesture *spans*.* Most touches are local — finger to surface, one point at a time. The antipodal gesture is *bilateral* — two points held simultaneously, with the body bridging them through space.
>
> The gesture is rare in ordinary life. We don't often hold two points of an object simultaneously, especially across a hole. *The torus invites this gesture* in a way most objects don't.
>
> *Contemplation three — the chord between is invisible but real.* The thread you've placed makes the chord visible, but even without the thread, *the chord exists as a mathematical line between the antipodal points*. The thread is a witness; the chord doesn't depend on it.
>
> Some students at this point feel a small *recognition* — *of course there's a line between antipodal points*. The torus's geometry has been present all along; the threaded version just makes it visible.
>
> *[long pause — 12 seconds]*
>
> What does the antipodal gesture feel like?
>
> Some students notice *spaciousness*. Holding two points at once requires a wider posture than holding one. The hands open up.
>
> Some students notice *symmetry*. The two points are mirror images across the torus's centre. The gesture is *symmetric*; the body finds itself in a balanced posture.
>
> Some students notice *connection*. The two cells, distant on the surface, are *close* through the hole. The torus has *short-cuts* that the surface alone doesn't reveal. The body discovers them through the gesture.
>
> All three are honest.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> A practical observation. *Antipodal gestures are rare in mathematics teaching.* Most diagrams ask the eye to scan; few ask the body to span. The torus's specific geometry makes the antipodal gesture available, and the gesture deepens understanding in ways diagram-scanning doesn't.
>
> Some traditions train this. Anatomists palpate paired structures. Sculptors feel the volume of a piece by spanning it. Builders sense structural integrity by holding parts simultaneously. *The torus invites the practitioner into a similar bodily relationship with the math.*
>
> *[pause]*
>
> One more contemplation. *The gesture you make on the paper torus this week is the same gesture you'll make on the wound coil in Q3.* When you wind multi-strand coils, your hands will be on antipodal positions of the former, paying attention to both at once. *The skill of bilateral attention is one of the things this week is teaching.*
>
> Hold the torus. Span it with two fingers. Notice the chord. Notice the gesture. Continue the work."
