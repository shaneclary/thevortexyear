## 4. Body (1:00–4:30)

> "Q1's polar pairs were *algebraic* — pairs of numbers (1, 8), (2, 7), (4, 5) that sum to 9 mod 9. Wk 17's ABHA decoration introduced antipode threads through the torus. Today the threads acquire formal geometric meaning: *Q1's polar pairs are physical antipodes on the torus surface.*
>
> *[pause]*
>
> *Definition of antipode on the torus.* For cell (col, row) on T², the antipode is (col + 12 mod 24, row + 3 mod 6). The antipode is the cell *exactly half-way around in both axes*.
>
> Equivalently: antipodes are *as far apart as possible* on the torus surface in both coordinate directions.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> *Worked examples.*
>
> Cell (0, 0) holds value 1. Antipode: (12, 3) holds value 8 (since 2³ = 8). *Polar pair (1, 8).*
>
> Cell (0, 1) holds value 2. Antipode: (12, 4) holds value 7 (since 2⁴ = 16 mod 9 = 7). *Polar pair (2, 7).*
>
> Cell (0, 2) holds value 4. Antipode: (12, 5) holds value 5 (since 2⁵ = 32 mod 9 = 5). *Polar pair (4, 5).*
>
> *Each polar pair is also an antipodal pair.* The algebraic and geometric structures coincide.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> *Why this is striking.*
>
> Q1 derived polar pairs from arithmetic: a + (9 − a) ≡ 0 mod 9. The result was abstract — pairs of numbers summing to 9.
>
> Wk 14-16 wrapped the strip into a torus. The wrap was a topological operation; it preserved the cells but reorganised their geometry.
>
> *Now*, in Wk 19, Q1's algebraic polar pairs *land at antipodal positions* on the torus. The arithmetic and the geometry agree without our intervention.
>
> *This is one of VBM's most beautiful coincidences — except it's not coincidence.* The doubling operation on (ℤ/9ℤ)* is *order 6*; multiplying by 2³ = 8 is the *involution* (squaring to 1). Geometrically, the involution is exactly the antipodal map. The math forced the alignment.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> *Three observations.*
>
> *Observation one.* The antipode-polar-pair coincidence is *forced by the math*. It's a theorem, not a fluke. Mod B Lens 8 will prove it.
>
> *Observation two.* The antipodal map is *involutive*. Apply it twice and you return to the starting cell. (12 + 12 = 24 ≡ 0; 3 + 3 = 6 ≡ 0.) *Antipodes are partners; pairs are reciprocal.*
>
> *Observation three.* On a *paper* torus the antipode is hard to point to; on a *3D-printed* torus you can put your finger through the doughnut hole and touch both endpoints of an antipodal pair simultaneously.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> *Practical note.* Find the antipode of cell (0, 0) on your paper torus. It's directly across the doughnut hole — about 12 columns around (half the major circumference) and 3 rows down (half the minor circumference). You may have already marked the antipode threads in Wk 17.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> *Looking forward.* Each cell on the torus has *exactly one antipode*. The 144 cells pair up into 72 antipodal pairs. Of these, the 18 pairs lying on doubling-cycle cells (24 cells × 2 endpoints) are *the polar pairs of Q1*. The other 54 antipodal pairs lie on family-3 cells or off-cycle units.
>
> Wait — let me recount. The doubling helix visits 24 cells. The halving helix visits another 24 (the antipodes). That's 48 cells total in the doubling-helix-and-its-antipodes structure. The remaining 144 − 48 = 96 cells are... *off the helices*, but they still have antipodes among themselves.
>
> *Total antipodal pairs on the torus: 144 / 2 = 72.* All cells pair up.
>
> *Polar pairs (in Q1's narrow sense — pairs (a, 9-a) for unit values): 3 distinct pairs.*
>
> *Polar pair occurrences on the torus: 24 × 3 / 2 = 36? need to recount.* Each value-1 cell has its partner value-8 cell. There are 24 value-1 cells; 24 value-8 cells. So 24 (1, 8) pairs at antipodal positions. Plus 24 (2, 7) pairs. Plus 24 (4, 5) pairs. Total: 72 polar pair occurrences, all of them antipodal. ✓
>
> *Conclusion: every antipodal pair on the doubling-and-halving helices is a polar pair.*
>
> Mod B Lens 8 formalises."
