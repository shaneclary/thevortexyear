## 4. Body (1:00–4:30)

> "The nine multiplication series produce five distinct geometric shapes (Wk 10 Mod A). Each shape can be implemented as a *physical coil winding pattern*.
>
> *Figure-eight (×2 / ×5).* The canonical Rodin Coil. The wire follows the doubling cycle 1 → 2 → 4 → 8 → 7 → 5 → 1. Six segments per cycle on the torus. We've covered this build extensively (Wk 4, Wk 9 Mod-C).
>
> *Triangle (×4 / ×7).* A coil whose wire visits only three of the nine vertices: 1, 4, 7. Three segments per cycle. Resulting coil is geometrically simpler than the figure-eight Rodin Coil; visits a subgroup of `(ℤ/9ℤ)*` rather than the full units.
>
> *Line (×8).* A coil whose wire oscillates between vertices 1 and 8. Two segments per cycle. Geometrically just back-and-forth across the torus, much simpler than the others.
>
> *Family-three triangle (×3 / ×6).* A coil whose wire traces 3 → 6 → 9 → 3 (or 6 → 3 → 9 → 6 depending on multiplier). Visits only the non-units. Different geometric region from the doubling family.
>
> *Trivial point (×1).* No traversal — just a single position. Not really a winding pattern.
>
> *[pause — show the four useful winding options]*
>
> Four practical winding patterns plus the trivial. Each has its electromagnetic signature. The figure-eight is the canonical Rodin Coil and the most-studied. The others are *alternative configurations* with different field structures.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> What's specific to each.
>
> *Figure-eight*: full-units coverage, six-segment cycle, antipodal-pair symmetry built in.
>
> *Triangle (1-4-7 or 1-7-4)*: subgroup coverage, three-segment cycle, three-fold rotational symmetry, no antipodal pairs.
>
> *Line (1-8)*: two-segment oscillation, simplest possible cycle, antipodal but no rotation.
>
> *Family-three triangle*: three-fold symmetry on the *non-units* — visiting the 3-6-9 axis vertices.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> Practical takeaway. The canonical Rodin Coil (figure-eight) is the *richest* winding option but not the *only* one. Builders can experiment with the other patterns to test how field structure depends on which polygon is wound."
