> "Cyclic groups have one generator. Non-cyclic groups don't. Different orbit structure."

## 3. Setup (0:30–1:00)
> "Lens 7 of 9. Compare cyclic vs. non-cyclic."

## 4. Body (1:00–4:30)

> "*Cyclic group.* A group generated by a single element. All elements are powers of one generator. Examples: `(ℤ/9ℤ)*`, `(ℤ/7ℤ)*`, `(ℤ/11ℤ)*` — all cyclic.
>
> *Non-cyclic group.* A group with no single generator; multiple elements together generate the group. Examples: `(ℤ/8ℤ)*`, `(ℤ/15ℤ)*`, `(ℤ/24ℤ)*` — non-cyclic.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> Orbit structure differences.
>
> *In a cyclic group of order n.* The maximum element order is exactly *n* (achieved by the generator). The number of generators is *φ(n)*. The subgroup lattice is *totally ordered* — each subgroup contains all subgroups of smaller order.
>
> *In a non-cyclic group of order n.* The maximum element order is *less than n* (typically *lcm of cyclic-component orders*). No single element generates the group. The subgroup lattice is *more complex* — multiple subgroups of the same order, multiple branches.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> Concrete example.
>
> `(ℤ/9ℤ)*` has order 6, cyclic, generated by 2 (or 5). Subgroup lattice: {1} ⊂ {1, 8} ⊂ {1, 4, 7} ⊂ full group. Wait — that's not a *chain*; the order-2 subgroup {1, 8} and the order-3 subgroup {1, 4, 7} are *not* nested. The subgroup lattice has the identity at the bottom, the full group at the top, and *two intermediate subgroups* of orders 2 and 3 that aren't comparable to each other.
>
> So actually `(ℤ/9ℤ)*`'s subgroup lattice is: {1} → {1, 8} → full group, and {1} → {1, 4, 7} → full group. Two parallel paths.
>
> Every cyclic group of order 6 has this same lattice — three subgroups of orders 1, 2, 3, 6, with the order-2 and order-3 subgroups parallel.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> Compare to `(ℤ/8ℤ)*`. Order 4, non-cyclic — isomorphic to `(ℤ/2ℤ) × (ℤ/2ℤ)`. Maximum element order is 2. Subgroup lattice has *three* subgroups of order 2 plus the trivial and full subgroups. More complex.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> Practical significance. The Symbol's structural cleanness — five distinct polygon shapes corresponding to five orbit structures — depends on `(ℤ/9ℤ)*` being cyclic with a clean subgroup lattice. Non-cyclic `(ℤ/nℤ)*` would give *more* polygons but with messier relationships among them.
>
> Mod 9's cyclic structure is part of what makes the Symbol pedagogically clean. Mod 8 (non-cyclic units) would give a different and probably less elegant pedagogy."
