## 4. Body (1:00–4:30)

> "*Star polygons.* In classical geometry, a *star polygon* {*n/k*} is the figure traced by connecting every *k*-th vertex of a regular *n*-gon. {5/2} is the five-pointed star (pentagram). {7/2} and {7/3} are seven-pointed stars (heptagrams). {9/2} is the nine-pointed star, {9/4} another.
>
> The star polygon's structure depends on whether *gcd(n, k) = 1*:
>
> If coprime, you get a *single connected star* visiting all *n* vertices.
>
> If not coprime, you get a *compound* — multiple smaller polygons.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> VBM's multiplication-series polygons are *closely related* but not identical to star polygons.
>
> *VBM ×4 series.* Visits {1, 4, 7} — three of nine vertices, not all nine. Forms a triangle, not a star.
>
> Compare to {9/4} star: connects every 4th vertex of a 9-gon. *0, 4, 8, 3, 7, 2, 6, 1, 5, 0.* Visits all nine vertices in 9 steps. Forms a nine-pointed star, not a triangle.
>
> The difference. VBM's *×n series* iterates *multiplication* by *n* on the digital roots, returning when it cycles. The star polygon *{n/k}* iterates *addition* of *k* on the vertices, visiting all *n* vertices.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> So VBM's polygons are *multiplicative orbits* in `(ℤ/9ℤ)*`. Star polygons are *additive orbits* in ℤ/n. Different operations, similar visual outcomes for some specific cases.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> Where they overlap. The {9/2} star (every other vertex of a 9-gon, additive iteration) is *visually similar* to the figure-eight that VBM's ×2 produces. But the {9/2} star traces *all nine vertices* in alternating fashion; VBM's ×2 visits only six vertices (the units). Visually similar; structurally different.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> Useful framing. *VBM polygons are multiplicative orbits visualised on the Symbol. Classical star polygons are additive orbits on regular n-gons. Both produce striking shapes; they're related but not the same family.*
>
> A geometer reading VBM might initially mistake the polygons for classical stars. The clarification — *these are multiplicative orbits, not additive star polygons* — places the work correctly."
