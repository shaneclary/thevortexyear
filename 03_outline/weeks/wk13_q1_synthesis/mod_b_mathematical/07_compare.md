## 4. Body (1:00–4:30)

> "*A graduate-level survey of modular arithmetic.* Topics typically covered: ring structure of ℤ/nℤ, units and the Euler totient, Chinese remainder theorem, quadratic residues and reciprocity, Hensel's lemma, primitive roots, p-adic numbers, Dirichlet characters, modular forms.
>
> *Q1's modular arithmetic.* Topics covered: units of ℤ/9ℤ, the doubling and halving orbits, polar pairs and family cosets, the 3-6-9 substructure, scale invariance under powers of 10, cyclic numbers, phyllotactic prime distribution.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> Two things both cover.
>
> *Both cover.* The structure of (ℤ/n)*, computation of orbits, basic modular reduction, and the role of cyclic groups in number theory.
>
> *Both demand.* Comfort with elementary group theory, ability to compute residues and orbits, and willingness to draw small examples.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> Five things the *graduate survey* covers that Q1 does not.
>
> *Survey-only one — quadratic reciprocity.* The deepest theorem in elementary number theory. Q1 doesn't touch it.
>
> *Survey-only two — Chinese remainder theorem.* Decomposition of ℤ/nℤ into primes powers' rings. Q1 stays at one fixed modulus (9).
>
> *Survey-only three — Dirichlet characters and L-functions.* Connection to analytic number theory. Q1 stops well before this.
>
> *Survey-only four — modular forms.* The bridge from arithmetic to algebraic geometry. Far beyond Q1.
>
> *Survey-only five — p-adic numbers.* A different completion of ℚ alongside the reals. Not in Q1.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> Five things *Q1* covers that the graduate survey does not.
>
> *Q1-only one — visualisation as a primary tool.* The figure-eight, the polar chords, the family colour-codes. Mainstream surveys present cyclic groups algebraically; Q1 treats the diagram as the primary object.
>
> *Q1-only two — base-10 alignment as a feature.* Q1 *foregrounds* the special relationship between mod 9 and base 10 (the scale invariance). Surveys mention this in passing if at all.
>
> *Q1-only three — phyllotactic and cyclic-number applications.* Q1 connects mod 9 to plant geometry and repeating decimals. Surveys treat these as pedagogical curiosities, not topics in their own right.
>
> *Q1-only four — embodied build practice.* Q1 asks the student to *draw the diagrams by hand* over twelve weeks. The survey expects no physical practice.
>
> *Q1-only five — bridging to engineering.* Q1 treats modular arithmetic as preparation for coil engineering. Surveys make no engineering claims.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> Useful framing. *Q1 covers a narrow slice of modular arithmetic — specifically the slice that aligns with base 10 — and goes deep through visualisation, embodiment, and engineering bridge. A graduate survey covers the breadth of modular arithmetic but flattens visualisation, ignores base-10 alignment, and makes no engineering bridge.*
>
> Practical takeaway. *If you're going on to academic mathematics, supplement Q1 with a graduate survey.* You'll find Q1's content but framed differently. *If you're staying within VBM, you have what you need from Q1 to proceed to Q2.*
>
> A small caution. Reading a graduate survey *while doing Q2* can produce dissonance. The vocabularies diverge. Read in alternation, not in parallel — finish Q2 first, then dip into surveys for breadth."
