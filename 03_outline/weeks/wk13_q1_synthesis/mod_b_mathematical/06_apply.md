## 4. Body (1:00–4:30)

> "Today we apply the Q1 proof tree to a different modulus: 7. The exercise demonstrates that the framework isn't *about* the number 9 — it's a method that works at any modulus, with 9 being one particularly rich case.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> *Setting up.* The integers mod 7 form ℤ/7ℤ, a cyclic group of order 7. Since 7 is prime, every non-zero element is a unit. So (ℤ/7ℤ)* has order 6.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> *Apply step 1 — find the additive structure.* ℤ/7ℤ is itself cyclic of order 7 with generator 1. There are no proper non-trivial subgroups (because 7 is prime). The polar pair structure: every non-zero element a has additive inverse 7-a, giving pairs (1, 6), (2, 5), (3, 4). Three polar pairs that sum to 7.
>
> *Apply step 2 — find the multiplicative structure.* (ℤ/7ℤ)* has order 6. By Lagrange's theorem the orbit of any element divides 6, so possible orbit lengths are 1, 2, 3, 6.
>
> *Apply step 3 — find the doubling orbit.* Compute the orbit of 2 in (ℤ/7ℤ)*: 2, 4, 8 ≡ 1, … The orbit closes after three multiplications. Orbit is (1, 2, 4) — length 3. *Different from mod 9.*
>
> *Apply step 4 — find a generator.* Try 3: 3, 9 ≡ 2, 6, 18 ≡ 4, 12 ≡ 5, 15 ≡ 1. Orbit is (1, 3, 2, 6, 4, 5) — length 6. *3 is a generator* of (ℤ/7ℤ)*.
>
> *Apply step 5 — what does the figure look like?* Plotted on a 7-point circle, the orbit of 3 is a six-pointed star, not a figure-eight. Different visual from mod 9 — but recognisably a multiplicative-orbit visualisation. Same method, different geometry.
>
> *Apply step 6 — base interaction.* In base 7, the analogue of '10ᵏ ≡ 1 mod 9' would be '10ᵏ ≡ 1 mod 7' — but in base 10, we instead have 10 ≡ 3 mod 7. So the digital-root-mod-7 operation isn't trivial; it requires actual modular reduction. Base 10's accidental compatibility with mod 9 is special.
>
> *Apply step 7 — apply to a cyclic number.* The repeating decimal of 1/7 is 0.142857… — same six digits as our familiar cyclic number, but here interpreted in *mod 7* rather than mod 9. The cyclic number's structure is *deeper than mod 9*; it actually emerges from mod 7.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> *Conclusion.* (ℤ/7ℤ)* is a cyclic group of order 6 generated by 3. Polar pairs sum to 7. The doubling orbit is shorter (length 3). The base-10 interaction is non-trivial. The cyclic number 142857 is best understood in mod-7 arithmetic, not mod 9.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> Two takeaways from the application.
>
> *Takeaway one.* The Q1 method is *general*. Same proof structure, different modulus. You could repeat the entire analysis for any modulus n; the answers vary, but the *steps* don't.
>
> *Takeaway two.* Mod 9's special features — the figure-eight visualisation, the base-10 alignment, the family-three structure — are *real* but *modulus-specific*. The course's choice of 9 isn't arbitrary; it's the modulus that *coincides* with our base. Other moduli are mathematically respectable but lose the alignment.
>
> The proof tree generalises. Q2 will lift it into 3D."
