## 4. Body (1:00–4:30)

> "Today's build is a *proof*, not a diagram. You write it by yourself, on a blank page, with no notes available. The proof should take five minutes. The lesson will guide you, but the writing is yours.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> Open your notebook. Title the page *The Doubling Circuit — Proof.*
>
> *State the claim.* In the multiplicative group (ℤ/9ℤ)*, the orbit of 2 under multiplication is (1, 2, 4, 8, 7, 5) and has length 6.
>
> *Set up.* Write: *The multiplicative group (ℤ/9ℤ)* consists of the units of ℤ/9ℤ — the elements coprime to 9. These are {1, 2, 4, 5, 7, 8}. The group has order 6.*
>
> *[pause]*
>
> *Derive.* Write the orbit of 2 by direct computation. Show every step.
>
> *2¹ = 2.*
>
> *2² = 4.*
>
> *2³ = 8.*
>
> *2⁴ = 16 ≡ 7 mod 9.*
>
> *2⁵ = 14 ≡ 5 mod 9.*
>
> *2⁶ = 10 ≡ 1 mod 9.*
>
> *[pause]*
>
> *Note the closure.* The orbit reaches 1 after six multiplications, returning to the identity. It then repeats. The cycle is exactly (1, 2, 4, 8, 7, 5) and has length 6.
>
> *Verify completeness.* The orbit visits every unit: 1, 2, 4, 5, 7, 8. All six units. Therefore *2 is a generator* of (ℤ/9ℤ)*; the group is cyclic of order 6 with 2 as a generator.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> *Conclude.* Write a one-sentence conclusion. Something like: *The doubling circuit is the orbit of 2 in the cyclic group (ℤ/9ℤ)*, which has order 6 and is generated by 2.*
>
> Quod erat demonstrandum, if you like that flourish.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> *What just happened.* You wrote a complete proof. About a hundred words. Three or four lines of arithmetic. A statement of the claim, a derivation, a conclusion.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> The proof is *short*. That's the point. The doubling circuit is not a deep claim; it's a direct computation in a small group. The visualisation as a figure-eight is the *interesting* part, but the proof itself is elementary.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> Three observations.
>
> *Observation one.* If you couldn't write this proof unaided, the foundation isn't yet solid. Re-watch the Wk 4 Build lesson before next week.
>
> *Observation two.* If you wrote it easily, *you have a real piece of mathematical work in your notebook*. Date it. Sign it. It is yours.
>
> *Observation three.* The proof scales. The same structure produces proofs for the halving circuit (orbit of 5), the polar pair sums (a + (9-a) ≡ 0), the family closure ({3, 6, 9} closed under addition). Twelve proofs share *one shape*. That shape is what you've internalised.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> If you have time after writing the proof, attempt one more from the Wk 13 Lens 2 checklist. Each additional proof takes less effort than the first."
