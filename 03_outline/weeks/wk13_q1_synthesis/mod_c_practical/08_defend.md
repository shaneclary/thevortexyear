## 4. Body (1:00–4:30)

> "The objection — and this one comes from *students*, not from sceptics. *Q1 was twelve weeks of arithmetic. Q2 looks more interesting — torus geometry, helical doubling, 3D structure. Why not just skip to Q2 and pick up the foundations as needed? It would be faster.*
>
> The objection is sincere. Some students *would* be faster skipping. Most wouldn't. Here's how to think about it.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> Three reasons Q1 came first.
>
> *Reason one — vocabulary load.* Q2 introduces *new* terms: ABHA Torus, helical doubling, antipodal pairs in 3D, the orthogonal axis. If you're learning these *while also* learning the underlying cyclic group, you'll be carrying double the cognitive load. Q1 has loaded the cyclic-group vocabulary into long-term memory so Q2 can use it as a *given*.
>
> *Reason two — diagram fluency.* Q2's lessons assume you can draw the Symbol from memory in under a minute. They build on it as a layer. If you can't draw the underlying Symbol fluently, the geometric extensions don't have a stable base. Twelve weeks of drawing built that base.
>
> *Reason three — practice cadence.* The build log discipline isn't a Q1 deliverable; it's a *muscle*. Q3's hardware quarter will demand sustained build-log practice across multiple builds simultaneously. The muscle gets built in Q1's twelve weeks and used heavily in Q3.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> A counter-test for the impatient student. *Pause this lesson. Set a one-minute timer. Without looking at notes, draw the Symbol of Enlightenment with the doubling circuit traced on it.*
>
> If you can do this in under a minute, your foundation is loaded. Q2 would proceed smoothly even if you skipped ahead — though you'd miss the synthesis-week reinforcement.
>
> If it takes longer than two minutes, or you hesitate, *the foundation isn't yet automatic*. Q2 would be uphill. Don't skip.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> So the calibrated answer to *why not skip ahead*:
>
> *Q1 builds three things into long-term memory: the cyclic-group vocabulary, automatic diagram drawing, and the build-log practice. These three become invisible substrates for Q2-Q4. Q2 proceeds smoothly only when Q1 is automatic. The pacing of Q1 — twelve weeks at one theme per week — is calibrated for vocabulary acquisition rather than for surface coverage. Going slower deepens the substrate; the substrate is what the rest of the year stands on.*
>
> About eighty words.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> Two follow-up postures.
>
> *Posture one — to a fellow student who's restless.* 'Try the one-minute Symbol test. If you can do it, you can probably skip ahead and pick things up. If you can't, the foundation isn't loaded yet, and skipping costs you more time than it saves.'
>
> *Posture two — to a student who's already taken Q1 once.* 'A second pass through Q1 is usually faster — about half the time. The vocabulary is familiar; you're just deepening the diagrams and proofs. If you've done Q1 once and it didn't stick, redoing it is the right call.'
>
> *[pause]*
>
> Practical takeaway. *The pacing is intentional.* Q2 does not benefit from a slower foundation; it benefits from an *automatic* foundation. Q1's twelve weeks earn the automaticity.
>
> If you're confident in your foundation, proceed to Q2 next week. If you're not, take the next eight weeks to redo the parts of Q1 you skipped. Both are legitimate. The course doesn't grade speed."
