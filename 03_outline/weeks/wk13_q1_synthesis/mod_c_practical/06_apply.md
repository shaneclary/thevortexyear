## 4. Body (1:00–4:30)

> "Today's exercise: pick *one* small physical artefact you'll make using only Q1 vocabulary. The point isn't to pre-empt Q3's coil work. The point is to discover what *embodied* application of the foundation feels like, before Q2's geometric lift complicates the picture.
>
> Three suggestions, choose one or invent your own.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> *Mini-build option 1 — string-art Symbol.* On a foam-board or wooden disc, place nine pins evenly around the edge. Use string of two or three colours to trace the doubling circuit, the halving circuit, and the polar pairs. Time required: thirty to ninety minutes. Materials: pins, string, board. Total cost: under fifteen dollars.
>
> *Mini-build option 2 — beaded bracelet with family colours.* Make a bracelet of nine beads, each coloured by family group: {1, 4, 7} in one colour, {2, 5, 8} in another, {3, 6, 9} in a third. Wear it during Q2. Time: thirty minutes once materials are gathered. Cost: under ten dollars.
>
> *Mini-build option 3 — printed Symbol on cloth.* Use a stencil and fabric paint to print the Symbol of Enlightenment on a t-shirt or canvas bag. Useful for community gatherings or personal reminder. Time: ninety minutes including drying. Cost: under twenty dollars depending on substrate.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> *Or invent your own.* If you have particular skills — woodworking, sewing, ceramics, programming — make something using your skills that *encodes Q1 vocabulary*. The constraint is that the artefact must use at least three Q1 structures (e.g., the Symbol placement plus the doubling circuit plus polar pairs).
>
> *[pause]*
>
> *Why we're doing this.* Three reasons.
>
> *Reason one — embodied vocabulary.* Making a physical artefact with the structures encoded in it makes the vocabulary stick differently than diagrams on paper. Different memory pathway.
>
> *Reason two — sharable artefact.* You can show a Q1 mini-build to someone and they can ask questions. The string-art Symbol is a conversation piece in a way the build log isn't (the build log is for you; the mini-build is for showing).
>
> *Reason three — confidence-building before Q2.* Making something physical, even small, builds confidence for the harder hands-on work in Q3. Some students discover they enjoy hand-making and lean into Q3 with energy. Others discover they don't, and budget Q3 differently. Either is useful intel.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> *Constraints.*
>
> *Use only Q1 vocabulary.* No torus references; that's Q2. No coil shapes; that's Q3. The doubling circuit on a flat circle is the most advanced structure permitted.
>
> *Cap the time.* Don't spend more than ninety minutes on the mini-build. This is not the capstone (that comes at year's end). It's a *scoped check-in*.
>
> *Document it.* Photograph the artefact and tape the photo into your build log with a one-sentence description. The artefact lives at home; the photo lives in the log.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> Practical observation. Most students choose the string-art Symbol. It's the most direct mapping of Q1 onto a physical surface and makes the cleanest conversation piece. If you're undecided, default to the string-art.
>
> Pick. Scope. Make. Document."
