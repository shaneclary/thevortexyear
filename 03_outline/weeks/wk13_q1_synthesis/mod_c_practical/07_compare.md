## 0. Authoring notes
Q1 vs a conventional engineering primer. Honest comparison: what each emphasises, what each leaves out.

## 4. Body (1:00–4:30)

> "*A conventional engineering primer.* Topics: units, dimensional analysis, basic mechanics or circuits, instruments and measurement, lab safety, drawing standards, project documentation, design-process methodology, professional norms.
>
> *Q1's practical track.* Topics: notebook discipline, hand-drawing the Symbol, hand-tracing circuits, making annotated paper plans, scoping a first physical build (Marko's first coil), using build prompts as a sustained practice.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> Two things both cover.
>
> *Both cover.* Notebook discipline as a foundation of practice. Hand-drawing as a way of internalising structures before building them.
>
> *Both demand.* Sustained engagement over time. A few weeks isn't enough; both require months to build the habits.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> Five things the *engineering primer* covers that Q1 does not.
>
> *Primer-only one — units and dimensional analysis.* The fundamental sanity check of all engineering. Q1 doesn't need it because Q1 is dimensionless arithmetic. Q3 will need it.
>
> *Primer-only two — instrumentation.* Multimeters, oscilloscopes, signal generators. Q1 mentions a multimeter once (Wk 12 build); engineering primers cover instruments deeply. Q3 will catch up.
>
> *Primer-only three — lab safety.* Soldering safety, electrical safety, eye protection. Q1 hasn't required these. Q3's hardware work will.
>
> *Primer-only four — drawing standards.* Engineering drawings have conventions: orthographic projection, dimensioning, tolerances, materials callouts. Q1's drawings are diagrammatic, not engineering-standard.
>
> *Primer-only five — design-process methodology.* Requirements gathering, design reviews, iteration cycles, validation. Q1 has used elements of these implicitly (the build-log discipline, the audit weeks) but not explicitly.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> Five things *Q1* covers that the engineering primer does not.
>
> *Q1-only one — visualisation as primary.* Q1 elevates diagram-as-substrate. Engineering primers treat diagrams as documentation of work; Q1 treats them as the work itself.
>
> *Q1-only two — meditation/contemplation paired with build practice.* The Lens 5 (Feel) lessons across Q1 are unusual. Engineering primers don't teach contemplative practice.
>
> *Q1-only three — biographical lineage.* Q1 frames the practice as continuation of Marko's tradition. Engineering primers are tradition-agnostic, presenting techniques as universal.
>
> *Q1-only four — vocabulary tied to a specific structure.* Doubling circuit, polar pairs, family groups — Q1 vocabulary maps to a specific arithmetic. Engineering primer vocabulary is field-general.
>
> *Q1-only five — calibrated answers for sceptics.* Q1 prepares students to defend the framework. Engineering primers don't anticipate scepticism in the same way.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> Useful framing. *An engineering primer prepares you to do generic engineering. Q1's practical track prepares you to do VBM-tradition engineering. The two share the foundational habits — notebook discipline, hand-work, sustained engagement — but diverge on technical content.*
>
> Practical takeaway. *You should read an engineering primer alongside Q3.* Q3 will introduce the technical content (units, instruments, safety) that Q1 deliberately skipped. Knowing where to find general engineering knowledge will make Q3 smoother.
>
> If you're already a working engineer, *Q1's practical track has been the unfamiliar part for you*. The engineering content of Q3 will be more familiar; the *framing* will be the new content.
>
> Q2 lifts the diagrams to 3D. Q3 makes the engineering primer relevant."
