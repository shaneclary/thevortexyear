## 0. Authoring notes
The portfolio as a self-portrait. Contemplative on what your build log says about you.

## 1. Outcome
Student has spent quiet time considering what their build log reveals.

## 2. Hook (0:00–0:30)
*[A pan slowly across the open build log, lingering on individual entries.]*

> *(slow)* "Your build log is a self-portrait. Look at it."

## 3. Setup (0:30–1:00)
> "Lens 5 of 9. Six minutes, slower."

## 4. Body (1:00–4:30) — *contemplative*

> *(slow)* "Open your build log to the first page. Then turn pages slowly.
>
> *[long pause — 12 seconds]*
>
> The handwriting in Wk 1 looks different from the handwriting in Wk 13. The early diagrams are tentative; the later ones are confident. The notes have grown longer or shorter depending on your style. Some pages are dense; some have wide margins.
>
> This is a self-portrait of a particular twelve-week stretch of your life. The portrait is honest because you didn't pose for it.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> What does the portrait show?
>
> Some students see *progress*. The early Symbols were rough; the later ones are precise. The handwriting steadied. The discipline accumulated.
>
> Some students see *gaps*. Weeks where they almost didn't engage. Pages they started and didn't finish. The portrait is honest about absences too.
>
> Some students see *style*. They didn't realise they had a way of doing things; the build log shows it. Their margin notes have a voice. Their diagrams have aesthetic preferences they didn't decide consciously.
>
> All three are real. The portrait shows whatever you actually did, not whatever you planned to do.
>
> *[long pause]*
>
> Sit with what the portrait shows about you.
>
> *[long pause — 12 seconds]*
>
> Three honest reflections.
>
> *Reflection one — am I the kind of person who maintains long records?* Twelve weeks of build-log entries is not a small thing. Many people start journals; few sustain them. If you have most of the entries, that's a finding about you that's worth knowing.
>
> *Reflection two — what changed during the twelve weeks?* Something probably did. Look at Wk 1 versus Wk 12; the difference isn't just diagram quality. It's posture, attention, vocabulary, sometimes self-perception. Notice what changed.
>
> *Reflection three — what's the build log for?* Not for the foundation. Not for me. *For you.* It is a record of your engagement. It exists because you made it. It will sit on a shelf or in a folder, and you can return to it any time. The act of having made it is itself the value.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> The build log will continue to be a self-portrait through Q2, Q3, and Q4. Q3 in particular — the engineering quarter — will record real builds, real measurements, real surprises. That portrait will look different from Q1's, because the underlying work is different.
>
> But the *practice* of keeping the log will be the same. Twelve weeks have established it. The remaining thirty-nine weeks build on the foundation of *you keeping a record*.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> Some students at this point feel *protective* of the build log. They want to keep it private. That's appropriate; the log is yours.
>
> Some students feel *embarrassed* — the early entries are rough, the gaps are obvious. That's also appropriate; first attempts are first attempts.
>
> Some students feel *grateful* that they kept the log at all. The eleven-week-ago version of themselves did them a favour.
>
> All three are honest. The build log doesn't ask for any particular feeling. It just sits there, a record.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> One observation. Twelve weeks ago you were a person who hadn't kept a build log. Today you are a person who has. *That is a real change in identity.* Small, but real. Most identities form this way — by accumulated practice, not by single decisions.
>
> Sit with the change. Continue."

## 5. Try it (4:30–5:30)
> *(softer)* "One sentence. *What does my build log say about me?* Sixty seconds."
