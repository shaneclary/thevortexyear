## 4. Body (1:00–4:30)

> "*Frame.*
>
> The clip you're about to hear is Randy Powell — Marko Rodin's primary collaborator since the early 2000s and the public face of much of the engineering work — addressing builders at a workshop. The clip captures Powell's view of what a student should have practically in hand before serious 3D and engineering work begins.
>
> The clip is approximately fifty seconds. **`[ORG-CLIP]`** — to be supplied by the foundation archive.
>
> What to listen for. Three things.
>
> *One — Powell's emphasis on hands.* Powell almost always returns to the physicality of the work. The math, in his framing, isn't real until you can wind copper. Listen for hand-related vocabulary.
>
> *Two — what Powell considers minimum readiness.* Powell will name a small list of things — drawings completed, vocabulary internalised, a specific build attempted — that count as the threshold for serious work. Note the list.
>
> *Three — Powell's tone toward the audience.* Powell teaches at workshops that include both newcomers and decade-long practitioners. Notice how he balances encouragement and clear-eyed expectation. The mode is generous but not indulgent.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> *[Clip plays — approximately 50 seconds. **`[ORG-CLIP]`** — to be selected from foundation archive.]*
>
> *[pause]*
>
> *Contextualise.*
>
> What you just heard suggests several practical readiness indicators.
>
> *Readiness-indicator one.* The Symbol of Enlightenment, drawn from memory in under a minute, with the doubling circuit overlaid. If you can do this, your hands know the structure.
>
> *Readiness-indicator two.* The doubling circuit recited from memory: *one, two, four, eight, seven, five, one*. Without hesitation. Powell often tests this informally with new collaborators.
>
> *Readiness-indicator three.* A build log that you can show another person without explanation. The diagrams should speak for themselves; if they need narration, they aren't ready.
>
> *Readiness-indicator four.* A clear sense of *what you don't yet know*. Powell often emphasises that good students name their gaps clearly. *I don't yet understand the torus topology* is a better posture than *I think I get most of it*.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> Practical takeaway. *Use Powell's clip as a self-test.* Can you draw the Symbol from memory in a minute? Can you recite the doubling circuit? Can your build log stand on its own?
>
> If yes to all three, you have practical readiness for Q2.
>
> If no to any, fix the gap *this week* before Wk 14 opens. Each gap takes well under an hour to close. Drawing the Symbol from memory is fifteen minutes of practice. Reciting the doubling circuit is five. Build-log clean-up varies.
>
> One more practical note. Powell occasionally gestures that the foundation tradition is *small*. There aren't thousands of practitioners; there are dozens to hundreds. Each new builder matters. The community is thin enough that your work, when good, will be noticed. This isn't pressure; it's an invitation to take your own work seriously.
>
> The clip ends with Powell turning to the torus on the stand. That gesture is the bridge to Q2."
