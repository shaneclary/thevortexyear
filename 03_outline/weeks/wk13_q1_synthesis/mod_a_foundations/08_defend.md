## 4. Body (1:00–4:30)

> "The setting. Coffee with a friend. Smart, sceptical, well-meaning. *What have you been spending your evenings on?*
>
> The temptation. Mention 'sacred geometry' or 'the energy field' or 'Marko's Symbol'. Watch the friend's interest evaporate.
>
> The better answer. Lead with the math.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> Practice paragraph.
>
> *I've been studying a framework called Vortex-Based Mathematics. The first quarter is just modular arithmetic — the same kind of arithmetic clocks use. The course shows that when you reduce numbers mod 9, you get a small set of patterns: a cyclic doubling sequence, three pairs that sum to nine, three families of three numbers each. None of this is controversial — any number-theory textbook has the same content. What's distinctive is that the framework treats those patterns as a deliberate diagram and uses the diagram as the foundation for further structure. The math itself is verifiable; the further structure is what I'm here to evaluate.*
>
> About ninety words. Memorise the shape, not the words.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> Three moves the paragraph makes.
>
> *Move one — name the math.* 'Modular arithmetic' is a recognisable mainstream term. Use it. The friend knows this exists; they have no reason to dismiss it.
>
> *Move two — decouple the verifiable from the speculative.* The doubling circuit, polar pairs, family groups — *verifiable in any textbook*. The further interpretive structure — *what you're here to evaluate*. The framing acknowledges what the friend would otherwise raise themselves.
>
> *Move three — adopt evaluator's stance, not advocate's.* You're studying the framework; you haven't endorsed it. This earns continued conversation. Most things people are scep about are pitched as already-true; positioning yourself as evaluator deflates the scepticism.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> What to do *if the friend pushes back further*.
>
> *Push: 'Isn't this just numerology?'* Answer: 'The arithmetic is standard mod-9 reduction. The patterns are real cyclic-group orbits. Calling it numerology because it uses small numbers conflates *the operations* with *the names of the operations*. A textbook on cyclic groups proves the same patterns.'
>
> *Push: 'The guy isn't a credentialed mathematician.'* Answer: 'True. The math doesn't depend on his credentials — anyone can verify it. The interpretive framework, separately, is open to scepticism for that reason. I'm holding the two apart.'
>
> *Push: 'Where does this lead?'* Answer: 'Q2 lifts the patterns into 3D geometry. Q3 builds an electromagnetic coil based on the geometry. Q4 has me build something of my own. I'll know more in a few months.'
>
> *[pause]*
>
> *[pause]*
>
> Practical takeaway. *Lead with the math; defer the speculative.* Reasonable sceptics will continue talking with you on this footing. Reasonable advocates will recognise the discipline of careful framing.
>
> Memorise the ninety-word paragraph as a working version. Refine your own as you understand more."
