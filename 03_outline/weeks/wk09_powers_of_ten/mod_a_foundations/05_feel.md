## 4. Body (1:00–4:30) — *contemplative*

> *(slow)* "The number 1 has digital root 1.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> The number 10 has digital root 1.
>
> *The number a billion has digital root 1.*
>
> *[long pause]*
>
> Same root. The size of the number disappears under the digital-root operation.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> What this means, contemplatively. The Symbol cares about *something other than size*. The grain of salt and the galaxy occupy the same vertex if their counts have the same digital root. Size is *erased*. What remains is the *position on the Symbol*.
>
> *[long pause — 12 seconds]*
>
> Most things humans care about, we measure by size. Wealth. Distance. Time. Population. Energy. All of these have units; all have *amounts*; all have *more or less*.
>
> The digital root operation throws *the more-or-less* away. The amount disappears. What's left is *only the structure*.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> Sit with that for a moment. *The structure that remains when size is erased.* The Symbol shows it. The vertices hold it. The doubling circuit traces it. The polar pairs partition it.
>
> *[long pause]*
>
> Marko's framing — *the scale is irrelevant* — is unusual in mainstream math. Most mathematics keeps the size; numbers carry their magnitude through every operation. Multiplying 47 by 100 gives 4,700, and the new number is *bigger*. The size matters.
>
> The digital root operation doesn't care. *47 and 4,700 are the same vertex.*
>
> *[pause]*
>
> What does it feel like to spend time with a system that doesn't care about size? Some students notice a relief — the heavy weight of *more-and-bigger* is set down for a moment. Others notice unease — the world's hierarchies of size are how most things get their importance, and erasing size is destabilising.
>
> Both reactions are honest. The math is doing what it does regardless of how it lands.
>
> *[long pause]*
>
> Sit with the size-free structure. The Symbol holds it whether you weigh it or not."
