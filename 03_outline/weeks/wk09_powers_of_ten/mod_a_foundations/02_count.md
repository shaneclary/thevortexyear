## 4. Body (1:00–4:30)

> "Compute the digital root of *10ᵏ* for *k = 0, 1, 2, ..., 15*.
>
> *10⁰ = 1.* Root: 1.
>
> *10¹ = 10.* Digit sum: 1. Root: 1.
>
> *10² = 100.* Digit sum: 1. Root: 1.
>
> *10³ = 1,000.* Root: 1.
>
> *10⁴ = 10,000.* Root: 1.
>
> *10⁵ = 100,000.* Root: 1.
>
> *10⁶ = 1,000,000.* Root: 1.
>
> *10⁷ = 10,000,000.* Root: 1.
>
> *10⁸ = 100,000,000.* Root: 1.
>
> *10⁹ = 1,000,000,000.* Root: 1. (One billion.)
>
> *10¹⁰ = 10¹⁰.* Root: 1. (Ten billion.)
>
> *10¹⁵ = 10¹⁵.* Root: 1. (One quadrillion.)
>
> Sixteen powers, sixteen roots all equal to 1.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> The pattern is *forced* by the structure of digit-sums in base 10. Any positive integer power of ten is *1 followed by some number of zeros*. The digit sum is *1 + 0 + 0 + ... + 0 = 1*. The digital root is 1.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> What about negative powers (decimal fractions)? *10⁻¹ = 0.1*. Strictly the digital root operation is defined for whole numbers, but you can extend it: *0.1* has digit sum *0 + 1 = 1*. Root: 1.
>
> *10⁻² = 0.01.* Digit sum: 1. Root: 1.
>
> Powers of ten in *both directions* — positive and negative — reduce to 1 if you allow the digit sum to operate on decimal fractions. The pattern extends across scales without limit.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> Useful framing. *Multiplication or division by 10 is the identity operation under digital-root reduction.* That's the formal statement.
>
> Consequences for VBM. The Symbol works whether you're counting molecules (10⁻²³ scales) or galaxies (10²⁰ scales). Digital roots erase the scale and preserve the structural digit. The *structure* lives at every scale equally."
