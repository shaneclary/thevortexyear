## 0. Authoring notes
Scale invariance compared to self-similarity (fractals). Show similarities and differences.

## 1. Outcome
Student can articulate the difference between digital-root scale invariance and fractal self-similarity.

## 2. Hook (0:00–0:30)
*[A fractal next to the digital-root scale-invariance demonstration.]*

> "Fractals look the same at every scale. Digital roots stay the same at every scale. Different ideas."

## 3. Setup (0:30–1:00)
> "Lens 7 of 9. Compare."

## 4. Body (1:00–4:30)

> "*Self-similarity (fractals).* A fractal is a geometric object whose detail at one scale resembles the object at another scale. Zoom into the Mandelbrot set; at any zoom level you find structure that looks like the whole set. The *visual appearance* is preserved across scales.
>
> Examples: the Mandelbrot set, the Koch snowflake, fern leaves, lightning, coastlines. Many natural and mathematical objects exhibit fractal self-similarity to varying degrees.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> *Scale invariance under digital-root reduction.* The *digital root* of a number is preserved when the number is multiplied by powers of 10. The *number's identity* (its size, its specific value) changes; the *digital root* doesn't.
>
> So scale invariance is about a *property* — the digital root — being preserved. Self-similarity is about a *visual structure* being preserved.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> Three differences.
>
> *Difference one — what's preserved.* Self-similarity preserves visual/geometric structure. Scale invariance preserves a specific arithmetic invariant (the digital root).
>
> *Difference two — what kinds of objects.* Self-similarity describes geometric objects (fractals, plants, etc.). Scale invariance describes a *function* on numbers (the digital-root operation).
>
> *Difference three — exactness.* Fractal self-similarity is often *statistical* (the Mandelbrot set's small details look like the whole set, but aren't identical to it). Digital-root scale invariance is *exact* (47, 470, 4,700 all have *exactly* the same digital root, not approximately).
>
> *[pause]*
>
> Why does the comparison matter? Because students sometimes hear *scale invariance* and assume *fractal-like behaviour*. The Symbol is not fractal in any standard sense. It's a *number-theoretic* object whose specific arithmetic invariants happen to be scale-free.
>
> Marko occasionally describes the Symbol's structure as *fractal* in lectures. The usage is informal — pointing at the scale-free aspect. A mathematician would say *the digital root is invariant under multiplication by 10*, which is more precise.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> Useful framing. *The Symbol's digital roots are scale-invariant in an exact arithmetic sense; this is structurally different from fractal self-similarity, which is geometric and often statistical.*"
