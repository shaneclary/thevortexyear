## 4. Body (1:00–4:30)

> "Reduce powers of ten to digital roots.
>
> *10.* Digit sum: *1 + 0 = 1.* Reduces to 1.
>
> *100.* Digit sum: *1 + 0 + 0 = 1.* Reduces to 1.
>
> *1,000.* Digit sum: *1 + 0 + 0 + 0 = 1.* Reduces to 1.
>
> *10,000.* Digit sum: *1 + 0 + 0 + 0 + 0 = 1.* Reduces to 1.
>
> *100,000.* Reduces to 1.
>
> *1,000,000.* Reduces to 1.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> Every power of ten reduces to 1. The pattern is exact: 10⁰ = 1, 10¹ = 10 → 1, 10² = 100 → 1, 10³ = 1,000 → 1, ... all reduce to 1.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> What this means. Scaling up by a factor of 10 — adding a zero to a number — *does not change* its digital root. The digital root is *invariant* under multiplication by 10.
>
> So *47 reduces to 2*. *470 reduces to 2*. *4,700 reduces to 2*. *47,000,000 reduces to 2*. The trailing zeros don't contribute to the digit sum (since 0 + anything = anything), and the leading digits stay the same.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> This is *scale invariance*. The Symbol's structure operates the same on numbers of any size. A small number and an enormously larger number with the same digital root sit at the same Symbol vertex.
>
> Two consequences.
>
> *Consequence one — VBM patterns are scale-free.* The Doubling Circuit, the Polar Pairs, the Family Groups all operate on digital roots. Multiplying everything by 10 — or 100, or 10⁹ — doesn't change which patterns appear. The Symbol works at micro- and macro-scales identically.
>
> *Consequence two — the digital root captures something dimensionless.* Most physical quantities have *units* — metres, seconds, grams. The digital root of a measurement is *dimensionless* in a related sense — it doesn't change when the measurement is rescaled. (Caveat: rescaling by exactly 10 is the cleanest case; other rescalings can change the digital root.)
>
> *[pause]*
>
> Marko reads this scale invariance as evidence that the Symbol describes structure that's *fundamental* — operating at every scale, like a fractal but exact rather than statistical. We'll meet the comparison to fractals in Lens 7."
