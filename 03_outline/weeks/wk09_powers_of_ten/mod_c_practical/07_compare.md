## 4. Body (1:00–4:30)

> "Standard engineering has well-developed scaling laws — *similitude* — that describe how physical systems behave when scaled up or down.
>
> *Reynolds number* (fluid dynamics) — characterises flow regimes; preserved across scales when geometric and dynamic similarity holds.
>
> *Mach number* (compressible flow) — characterises compressibility; preserved across scales.
>
> *Strouhal number* (oscillating fluid) — characterises shedding frequencies; preserved across scales.
>
> Engineering similitude lets you build *scale models* and predict full-scale behaviour. Wind-tunnel testing of aircraft. Wave-tank testing of ships. Heat-transfer experiments at lab scale predicting industrial-scale behaviour.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> Rodin Coil scaling resembles engineering similitude in *some* ways and differs in others.
>
> *Where they resemble.* Both preserve *dimensionless ratios* across scales. Engineering preserves Reynolds, Mach, Strouhal. Rodin preserves frequency-ratios, turn counts, polar-pair antipodal relationships.
>
> *Where they differ.* Engineering similitude is *empirical* — derived from observation of physical systems. Rodin Coil scaling is *built into the math* — the digital-root operation gives scale invariance for free; you don't need to measure to know it.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> One specific difference. Engineering similitude often *fails* outside specific regime ranges. Reynolds number invariance breaks down at very high or very low values; the dimensionless laws are *approximations* valid in particular regimes.
>
> Rodin Coil scaling, in its *mathematical* form, never fails. Digital roots are always preserved by multiplication by 10. The *physical* implementation might fail at extreme scales (manufacturing limits, material properties), but the *math* is exact at all scales.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> Useful framing. *Rodin Coil scaling is mathematically exact (the digital-root invariance) and physically approximate (limited by manufacturing and materials). Engineering similitude is empirically derived and physically scoped.* The two are complementary, not redundant.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> Practical takeaway. When you scale a Rodin Coil, you can use *both* sets of rules. The mathematical rules from Mod-A and Mod-B (digital-root invariance) tell you what's preserved structurally. The engineering rules (Reynolds, etc.) tell you which physical regime you're in. Both apply; both are useful.
>
> A working engineer reading your build log will recognise the engineering-similitude language and find the Rodin Coil scaling sensible. They may be impressed that the *mathematical* invariance gives you *exact* preservation rather than approximate."
