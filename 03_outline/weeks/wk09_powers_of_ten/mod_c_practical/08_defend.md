## 4. Body (1:00–4:30)

> "The objection. *A 4-inch Rodin Coil costs $30. It's a toy. Real engineering uses full-scale builds with proper bench equipment. Small builds give imprecise measurements that can't be trusted.*
>
> The objection has merit *for some kinds of measurement*.
>
> *Where small builds give imprecise measurements.* Absolute measurements (specific inductance values, specific frequency in Hz, specific impedance). Small coils have lower absolute values; cheap multimeters have noise floors that swamp small absolute readings.
>
> *Where small builds give precise measurements.* Ratios. The ratio of one resonance to another, the ratio of inductance between two strands, the ratio of harmonic frequencies — these are *preserved across scale*, and they're *much easier to measure* on small coils than absolute values are.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> So the calibrated response. *Small builds are honest measurements for scale-invariant quantities (ratios, patterns) and unreliable for scale-dependent quantities (absolute values).*
>
> If your test is *does the 6f trigger frequency appear?* — that's a ratio question. Small build is fine.
>
> If your test is *what is the absolute base resonance?* — that's an absolute question. Small build is unreliable; use a medium or large build with bench-quality equipment.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> Practical implications.
>
> *Use small builds for hypothesis-testing.* Cheap, fast, scale-invariant ratio measurements. *Does the predicted pattern appear?* Yes/no answer; small build sufficient.
>
> *Use medium builds for quantitative engineering.* When you need actual numbers — inductance in microhenries, resonance in MHz, current capacity in amperes — medium scale gives reliable absolute values.
>
> *Use large builds for power applications and demonstration.* When the coil needs to handle significant power or be visually impressive (for talks, demos, video), large scale matters.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> So the calibrated answer to *aren't small builds toys?*:
>
> *Small builds give honest measurements for scale-invariant quantities (ratios, pattern presence). They're unreliable for scale-dependent quantities (absolute values). Use small builds for hypothesis-testing and pattern-verification; use medium or large builds when absolute numbers matter. Both are real engineering.*
>
> Forty-three words.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> One nuance. Even small builds can give precise absolute measurements if you use proper bench equipment — vector network analyser, calibrated reference, controlled environment. But that equipment costs more than a small coil; few hobbyists have it. The ratio-vs-absolute distinction lets builders match their measurement type to their equipment quality."
