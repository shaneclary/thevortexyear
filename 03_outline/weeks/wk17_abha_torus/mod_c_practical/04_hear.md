> What to listen for. Three things.

> *Contextualise.*
>
> What you just heard tells you several things about the torus's engineering status.
>
> *Engineering-thing one.* The torus is *just plastic* (or wood, or paper) at the engineering level. It has no inherent magic. *The mathematics and the engineering happen at different levels*; the substrate carries the engineering, the structure carries the mathematics, and Powell keeps them clear.
>
> *Engineering-thing two.* The substrate has *practical requirements* that the math doesn't impose. The torus's mathematical correctness doesn't care if it's slippery, brittle, hard to mark, or wrong-coloured. *Engineering does care*. Powell's clips often focus on these practical requirements.
>
> *Engineering-thing three.* The foundation tradition's conventions about substrate (10 cm major × 2.5 cm minor, marked with the 5 essential marks) are *operational standards* that allow builders to compare results. *Standardisation is a community feature*, made possible by Powell's and the foundation's published specifications.
>
> *Engineering-thing four.* Powell's approach is *unromantic in a healthy way*. He doesn't treat the torus former as a sacred object. It's a tool. Tools can be replaced, lost, broken; the work continues with new tools. *The substrate is replaceable*; the *practice* is not.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> Practical takeaway. *Treat the torus former as a tool, not a treasure.* Mark it freely. Replace it if it breaks. Use it heavily. The substrate is meant to support work, and overprotectiveness reduces the work it can support.
>
> One more practical note. Powell occasionally distinguishes *the substrate* from *the coil*. The substrate is the former; the coil is the wound assembly. *They are different objects*, and the substrate is reusable across multiple coils. *One former, many coils* is the typical practice.
>
> When you have your 3D-printed former, you'll have a *durable substrate* that can host many coils across years."
