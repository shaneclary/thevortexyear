## 4. Body (1:00–4:30)

> "The marked torus is now a working tool. Today's apply outlines five ways to use it as a daily reference for the rest of Q2 and into Q3.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> *Use 1 — coordinate lookup.* When you encounter a coordinate (col, row) in a problem or diagram, find it on the torus directly. Pick up the torus, locate column 0 (the triangle), count to the desired column, count down to the desired row.
>
> *Faster than the wall-pinned grid* for embodied learners. The torus is in your hand; the grid requires looking up.
>
> *Use 2 — antipode verification.* When working with polar pairs, use the antipode markers to verify which pair an element belongs to. The threads through the doughnut hole make the verification immediate.
>
> *Use 3 — wind-path tracing.* When studying multi-strand or higher-traversal coils (Wks 18-20 will introduce these), trace the wind path on the torus with a finger. The 3D path is more legible on the surface than on a 2D representation.
>
> *Use 4 — symmetry visualisation.* Rotate the torus to see the rotational symmetry directly. Or, mentally translate cells along (col, row) coordinates to visualise the antipodal map. *Embodied symmetry exploration*.
>
> *Use 5 — Q3 winding rehearsal.* Even before you have a 3D-printed former, use the paper torus as a *winding rehearsal substrate*. Trace the wind path with a coloured pen. Practice the start-position-to-end-position routine. By Q3, the routine will be familiar; only the substrate will change.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> *Three workflow recommendations.*
>
> *Recommendation 1 — keep the torus accessible.* Place it where you can grab it without standing up. A small stand on your desk, a hook within arm's reach, or simply on top of the build log.
>
> *Recommendation 2 — handle it daily.* Even brief contact (10 seconds, picking it up, setting it down) maintains familiarity. Without daily contact, the embodied vocabulary fades.
>
> *Recommendation 3 — re-mark as needed.* Marks fade. Spend a few minutes re-marking when smudging makes the marks unclear. Don't let mark-degradation make the torus useless.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> *What the torus does that the wall grid doesn't.*
>
> *Difference 1 — physical handling.* You can rotate the torus to study symmetries. The grid stays flat.
>
> *Difference 2 — wrap visibility.* The torus *shows* the wraparound; the grid *implies* it. Wraps are easier to follow on the torus.
>
> *Difference 3 — antipode geometry.* Antipodes are *physical opposites* on the torus. On the grid, antipodes are just coordinate pairs.
>
> *Difference 4 — Q3 continuity.* The torus you have now is the *same shape* as the Q3 former. Working with it now is rehearsal for Q3.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> *What the wall grid does that the torus doesn't.*
>
> *Difference A — bulk visibility.* You can see all 144 cells of the grid at once. The torus shows about half its surface at any given moment.
>
> *Difference B — easy annotation.* Writing on the wall grid is easy; writing on the curved torus surface is harder.
>
> *Difference C — comparison with other builders.* Wall grids are easier to photograph and share than 3D objects.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> Practical takeaway. *The torus is your hand-reference; the wall grid is your survey-reference.* Use both. They serve different roles in your study workflow.
>
> By Q3, the torus will become the *bench-reference* (the substrate of actual winding). The wall grid will continue to serve as the *wiring chart* (the design document). Both persist; both have roles."
