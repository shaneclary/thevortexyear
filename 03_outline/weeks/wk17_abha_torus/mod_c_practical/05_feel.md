## 0. Authoring notes
First moment your former feels real. Contemplative on the moment when a teaching artefact crosses the line into being a working tool.

## 1. Outcome
Student has spent quiet time with the experience of an artefact crossing from study-aid to working tool.

## 2. Hook (0:00–0:30)
*[The marked torus on the bench, hands setting it down with care, the angle suggesting it's now part of the workspace.]*

> *(slow)* "The torus has become a tool. Sit with the becoming."

## 3. Setup (0:30–1:00)
> "Lens 5 of 9. Six minutes, slower."

## 4. Body (1:00–4:30) — *contemplative*

> *(slow)* "Two weeks ago the paper torus was a *teaching object* — fragile, approximate, slightly silly. You folded it for a lesson; you photographed it; you set it aside.
>
> Now it's marked, labelled, decorated, and ready to support actual work. Something has shifted.
>
> *[long pause — 12 seconds]*
>
> Hold the marked torus. Notice it now feels *prepared*.
>
> *[long pause]*
>
> *Prepared* is a state different from *complete*. Complete suggests finished — nothing left to do. *Prepared* suggests *ready for what comes next*. The torus isn't finished; it's poised.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> Three contemplations on preparation.
>
> *Contemplation one — preparation is conditional.* The torus is prepared *for winding*. If you weren't going to wind a coil, the preparation would be ornamental. *The preparation has a purpose that hasn't yet happened.* The torus's marked state is meaningful only in the context of the future build.
>
> Most prepared objects are like this. A musical instrument tuned for a concert that hasn't started. A canvas primed for paint that hasn't been applied. A loaf shaped for an oven that hasn't been heated. *Preparation looks toward action.*
>
> *Contemplation two — preparation has weight.* An unprepared object is light. A prepared object carries small accumulated commitments — the time spent marking, the care taken to align angles, the choice of which marks to apply. *The torus is now a record of those commitments.* Putting it down without the future build would let those commitments down.
>
> Some students feel a small *pull forward* at this point. The torus *wants* to be wound. It has been prepared for that. Sitting unwound feels incomplete.
>
> *Contemplation three — preparation creates a path of least resistance.* Once the torus is marked, the next action is *clear*. Not 'what should I build?' but 'wind this'. Decision space narrows. *Preparation reduces uncertainty about the next step.*
>
> This is one reason builders mark before they wind, even though marking adds time. The marked former eliminates pause; the wind flows once started.
>
> *[long pause — 12 seconds]*
>
> What does *crossing into tool-hood* feel like?
>
> Some students feel *responsibility*. The torus is now *for something*. The previous freedom — *I might or might not build* — has narrowed. They feel committed in a way they weren't.
>
> Some students feel *anticipation*. The future build, previously abstract, now has a *specific substrate*. The build is going to happen *on this exact torus*. The specificity makes it real.
>
> Some students feel *settledness*. The decision is made. The torus is the substrate. Q3 will use it. There's nothing left to deliberate.
>
> All three are honest.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> A practical observation. *Most builders find preparation a quietly satisfying activity.* The marking is repetitive, slow, attention-demanding. It produces an artefact that is more capable than the unmarked one. The transformation is small per minute but accumulates.
>
> Powell's workshops often allow extended time for marking — an hour or two — because the practice itself has value. Students who rush through marking tend to rush through winding too, and the resulting coils are less precise.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> One more contemplation. *The torus has crossed into tool-hood.* From here, every interaction with it is in *tool-mode* — what does it tell you, what can you do with it, where does the next mark go.
>
> The crossover happens once and stays. *Once a tool, always a tool* (until it breaks or is retired). The marked torus on your shelf will, in years, still be a tool. The marks may have faded; you may have re-marked them; the practice continues.
>
> Sit with the marked torus. Notice it has crossed. Continue the work it's prepared for, when the time comes."
