## 4. Body (1:00–4:30)

> "The bare topological torus from Wk 16 has acquired ABHA decorations across Wk 17. Today's lens shows what *complete* means at the practical level — what makes a torus former *ready for winding* versus *still in preparation*.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> *Five marks the former needs.*
>
> *Mark 1 — column zero indicator.* A small triangle, arrow, or dot at column 0 (the canonical starting position). Without this, the builder has to choose a starting position arbitrarily, breaking comparability with foundation-tradition coils.
>
> *Mark 2 — column dividers.* Twenty-three light vertical lines at intervals of 15° around the major circumference, dividing the torus into 24 angular sections. *Builders need these to know where each turn lands.*
>
> *Mark 3 — row dividers.* Five circles around the minor circumference, dividing the torus's tube cross-section into 6 row positions. *These are 'rings' that the wire crosses as it advances along the cycle.*
>
> *Mark 4 — antipodal indicators.* Three small dots or markers showing the polar pair antipodes — typically connected by visible threads through the doughnut hole. *These help builders verify the wind preserves antipodal symmetry.*
>
> *Mark 5 — orientation indicator.* An arrow showing the *winding direction* (the direction of the doubling cycle). Some torus formers also mark *up* (the side that will face the viewer when the coil is displayed).
>
> *[pause]*
>
> *Optional but useful marks.*
>
> *Optional 1 — cell labels.* Each of the 144 cells labelled with its digit-root value. Useful for teaching coils; less necessary for working coils.
>
> *Optional 2 — family colours.* Subtle colour-coding of the rows by family. Useful for multi-strand coils (where each strand is one family colour).
>
> *Optional 3 — strand entry/exit points.* Where each strand starts (terminal A) and ends (terminal B). Required for multi-strand coils.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> *What 'finally complete' means.*
>
> The torus is *complete* when:
>
> A builder can pick it up.
>
> Identify the start position (column 0).
>
> Identify the wind direction (the orientation arrow).
>
> Begin winding without consulting external charts.
>
> *That's the test of completeness.*
>
> If you have to consult the wall-pinned grid at every step, the former isn't complete; it's just topologically a torus. *Complete = self-contained.*
>
> *[pause]*
>
> Three observations.
>
> *Observation one.* Most torus formers shipped from foundation-affiliated sources arrive *partially marked* — column dividers and row dividers but not the optional decorations. Builders add the optional marks themselves based on what they're building.
>
> *Observation two.* The 5 essential marks take *fifteen to thirty minutes* to apply once the former is in hand. The optional marks can take additional time depending on detail.
>
> *Observation three.* A *fully decorated former* is *the canonical Q3 starting point*. Wk 32 (coil topology preparation) assumes the former has at least the 5 essential marks before winding begins.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> Practical takeaway. *Plan the marking in two stages.*
>
> *Stage 1 (now, with paper torus).* Practise marking the paper torus from Wk 16 with the 5 essential marks. Use light pencil or coloured pen.
>
> *Stage 2 (Wk 27-32, with 3D-printed former).* Apply the same marks to the actual hardware substrate. The practice in stage 1 makes stage 2 fast and accurate."
