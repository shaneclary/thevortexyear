## 0. Authoring notes
Decorated former vs blank former. Two states of the same hardware; comparison clarifies what marking adds.

## 1. Outcome
Student can articulate what marking the former adds and when an unmarked former might still be useful.

## 2. Hook (0:00–0:30)
*[Two identical torus formers side-by-side: one bare, one fully marked.]*

> "Bare. Marked. Same shape. Six minutes comparing."

## 3. Setup (0:30–1:00)
> "Lens 7 of 9. Compare decorated and blank torus formers."

## 4. Body (1:00–4:30)

> "*Decorated former.* Bare torus + the 5 essential marks (column zero, column dividers, row dividers, antipodal indicators, orientation arrow), often plus optional decorations.
>
> *Blank former.* Bare torus, no marks. Just the topological shape.
>
> Both are 3D-printed plastic (or wood, or paper) tori with the same dimensions and surface. Different states of preparation.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> Three structural similarities.
>
> *Similarity one — same topology.* Both are toroidal surfaces with the same Euler characteristic and homology. Mathematically interchangeable.
>
> *Similarity two — same physical dimensions.* 10 cm major, 2.5 cm minor, same plastic. A wire wound around either would have the same length and same electrical properties.
>
> *Similarity three — same handling.* Both feel the same in the hand. Same weight, same grip, same wear over time.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> Three structural differences.
>
> *Difference one — readability.* The decorated former *tells you where you are* (at column 0, in row 2, etc.) without external reference. The blank former *requires you to keep track* of position separately.
>
> *Difference two — error rate.* Multi-strand winding on a marked former is *less error-prone* because the marks catch position drift early. Same wind on a blank former requires constant external comparison and is more vulnerable to mid-wind errors.
>
> *Difference three — teaching value.* The decorated former is a *teaching artefact* — a new student can pick it up and learn from the marks. The blank former teaches nothing on its own; it requires verbal or written explanation.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> *When to use a blank former.*
>
> *Use case 1 — experimental winds.* If you're trying a non-canonical wind pattern (e.g., a research variation), the blank former lets you mark the *new* pattern without competing with prior marks. *A clean canvas* for new work.
>
> *Use case 2 — workshop teaching.* In a workshop where you want students to *do the marking themselves* as a learning exercise, you start with blank formers. Students mark them as part of the lesson; the act of marking teaches the structure.
>
> *Use case 3 — second formers.* If you have two formers, one marked and one blank, you can dedicate the blank one to experimental builds and the marked one to canonical builds.
>
> *Use case 4 — long-term storage.* Marks fade. A former kept blank for years stays clean indefinitely. If you don't plan to use it for a year, blank may be preferable.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> *When to use a decorated former.*
>
> *Use case 1 — first canonical build.* The marked former is *the right starting point* for Wk 33's canonical six-strand four-traversal coil.
>
> *Use case 2 — student demonstration.* When showing a coil to someone new to VBM, the marks make the structure legible.
>
> *Use case 3 — daily reference.* The marked former is a usable hand-reference for coordinate lookups and symmetry exploration.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> Useful framing. *The decoration is reversible only by re-acquiring the substrate.* Once you've marked a former, it's marked (or you have to print/buy another). This is why the foundation often distributes formers blank and lets builders mark them — it preserves the substrate's flexibility.
>
> Practical takeaway. *Decide what your one former is for.* If it's for canonical builds and reference, mark it. If you're not sure, leave it blank for now and mark it just before Q3's canonical build.
>
> A more nuanced approach. *Acquire two formers* — one to keep blank for experiments, one to mark for canonical work. The cost difference is small if you're 3D-printing your own.
>
> The decoration adds usefulness for the canonical case. Marking is reversible only by replacement. Both are real costs and benefits."
