## 0. Authoring notes
Fifty-first Defend. Why we don't ship a blank former in Q3 — the course's pedagogical choice.

## 1. Outcome
Student can defend the choice to introduce the marked former in Q3 rather than starting with a blank one.

## 3. Setup (0:30–1:00)

## 4. Body (1:00–4:30)

> "The objection. *Q3 introduces the torus former as a marked artefact. Wouldn't it be more authentic to give students blank formers and let them mark their own? Marking is part of the learning; the course is removing it.*
>
> The objection has merit and points to a genuine pedagogical choice. The course's defence:
>
> *[pause]*
>
> *Three reasons the course recommends starting with marked formers.*
>
> *Reason one — Wk 17 already taught marking.* This week's Mod C Lens 3 (Build) is exactly *the marking exercise on the paper torus*. By Wk 17 students have already practised the marking procedure on a forgiving substrate. Repeating it on the 3D-printed former adds modest skill but reduces time available for actual winding.
>
> *Reason two — Q3's time budget is tight.* Q3 has nine engineering weeks. The canonical six-strand four-traversal build (Wk 33) requires substantial time alone. *Adding a marking-from-blank task would push winding later in Q3* and risk students not completing the canonical build before Q3 closes.
>
> *Reason three — repeat marking is acceptable.* Students who *want* marking practice on the 3D-printed former can do it themselves — there's nothing stopping them. The course supplies a *recommended starting point*, not a constraint. *The marked former is a default, not a mandate.*
>
> *[pause]*
>
> *Counterposition: marking from blank as authentic.*
>
> The course acknowledges:
>
> *Counter 1 — marking is part of the tradition.* Marko and the early builders marked their own formers because no commercial formers existed. Marking is part of the original practice.
>
> *Counter 2 — embodied learning.* Marking your own former teaches the position structure in a way that using someone else's marked former doesn't. There's pedagogical value the course's default skips.
>
> *Counter 3 — community variation.* Different practitioners may mark in different conventions. Forcing one standard reduces useful variation.
>
> *Course response.* The course addresses these by:
>
> *Course-response 1.* Wk 17 already includes marking practice on paper.
>
> *Course-response 2.* Q3 explicitly invites students to mark the 3D-printed former themselves if they prefer.
>
> *Course-response 3.* The foundation's marking conventions are recommended but not enforced; students may use alternatives.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> So the calibrated answer to *why marked, not blank*:
>
> *The course's default is to recommend a marked former in Q3 because Wk 17 has already taught the marking procedure on a paper torus, freeing Q3's tight time budget for actual coil winding. Students who prefer to mark their own 3D-printed former are encouraged to do so; the marked default is a suggestion, not a constraint. The pedagogical choice trades repeated marking practice for more winding time, on the assumption that one marking exercise (on paper) is sufficient.*
>
> Around eighty words.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> Practical takeaway. *The course offers a default; you can deviate.* If you'd rather mark your own 3D-printed former for the embodied learning, do that. Q3 will accommodate either approach.
>
> One more practical note. *Standardisation across builders matters more than personal marking practice.* If you mark your own and use non-standard conventions (different angle markers, different colour codes), your coils will be harder to compare with foundation-tradition coils. *Personal marking is fine; non-standard conventions are not.* Mark with the foundation's conventions, even if you mark yourself."
