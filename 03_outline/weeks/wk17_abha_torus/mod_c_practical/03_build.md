## 4. Body (1:00–4:30)

> "Today's build applies the 5 essential marks to your paper torus. The same procedure will apply when you have a 3D-printed former in Q3, but practising on paper now means you'll be fast and accurate then.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> *Materials.*
>
> Your paper torus from Wk 16, with cell labels added in Wk 17 Lens 3 of Mod A.
>
> A fine-tip permanent marker (or pencil for a lighter version).
>
> A ruler.
>
> A protractor.
>
> Optional: coloured threads for antipodal connections.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> *Mark 1 — column zero indicator.* On the *outermost* part of the torus (the surface farthest from the doughnut hole), at column 0, row 0, draw a small triangle pointing toward column 1.
>
> The triangle indicates 'start here, wind in this direction'.
>
> *Mark 2 — column dividers.* Use the protractor to measure 15° intervals around the major circumference. Mark 23 light pencil ticks at each interval (24 total including column 0).
>
> Tip: if measuring on a curved surface is hard, work in segments. Lay one column-section flat (if your torus permits), measure and mark, then re-curve.
>
> Tip: if your torus is fragile, use a soft pencil so you don't pierce the paper.
>
> *Mark 3 — row dividers.* Around the *minor* circumference of the torus (the small circle of the tube cross-section), mark 5 light circles at intervals.
>
> The 6 rows are separated by these 5 circles; row 0 is on one side, row 5 is on the other (which wraps back to row 0 by closure).
>
> Tip: use a thin ribbon or string to mark a constant minor-radius circle, then trace the line with marker.
>
> *Mark 4 — antipodal indicators.* Three pairs of dots:
>
> Pair 1: (col 0, row 0) and (col 12, row 3) — polar pair (1, 8).
>
> Pair 2: (col 0, row 1) and (col 12, row 4) — polar pair (2, 7).
>
> Pair 3: (col 0, row 2) and (col 12, row 5) — polar pair (4, 5).
>
> Mark each endpoint with a small filled dot.
>
> If you have coloured threads, run them through the doughnut hole connecting each pair. Three threads, one per pair.
>
> *Mark 5 — orientation arrow.* On a flat region between cells, draw a small arrow showing the wind direction. The arrow should point from column 0 toward column 1 *and* from row 0 toward row 1 — the diagonal of the doubling helix.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> *Inspection.*
>
> Look at your torus. Verify:
>
> Column 0 triangle is visible. ✓
>
> 24 column ticks evenly spaced. ✓
>
> 5 row circles around the minor circumference. ✓
>
> 3 antipodal pairs marked (or threaded). ✓
>
> 1 orientation arrow visible. ✓
>
> Total marks: about 34-38. The torus now has the canonical position structure.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> *Step 6 — photograph and document.* Take photographs of your marked torus from multiple angles. Tape the best photos into your build log. Caption: *Marked torus former, paper version, with 5 essential marks*.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> What you've done. *The paper torus now has the canonical position structure.* Combined with the cell labels you added earlier in the week, the torus is *fully prepared as a study substrate*.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> Three observations.
>
> *Observation one.* Marking takes longer than you'd expect. *Allow 15-30 minutes* even for the small paper version.
>
> *Observation two.* The marks may *fade or smudge* with handling. Reapply as needed; this is normal.
>
> *Observation three.* When you mark the *3D-printed* former later in Q3, *use this paper version as a reference*. Hold them side-by-side; the marking pattern translates directly.
>
> Practical takeaway. *The paper torus is now your canonical Q2 reference object.* Pin it where it's accessible. Glance at it daily."
