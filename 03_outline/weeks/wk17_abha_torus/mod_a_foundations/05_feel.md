## 0. Authoring notes
The doughnut speaks back. Contemplative on what it means for a labelled, decorated artefact to start communicating its structure to its maker.

## 1. Outcome
Student has spent quiet time with the experience of an object that has acquired structure through the maker's work.

## 2. Hook (0:00–0:30)
*[Hands holding the labelled torus, the eye visibly moving across the cells, picking out patterns.]*

> *(slow)* "The torus speaks back. Sit with the speaking."

## 3. Setup (0:30–1:00)
> "Lens 5 of 9. Six minutes, slower."

## 4. Body (1:00–4:30) — *contemplative*

> *(slow)* "Yesterday the torus was bare. Today the torus is labelled. By the end of this week, the torus will be fully decorated.
>
> *Each labelling adds a small voice.*
>
> *[long pause — 12 seconds]*
>
> Hold the labelled torus. Notice that it now *says something*. Where the bare torus was a pure shape, the labelled torus is a *structured shape* — its parts have names, families, relationships.
>
> The eye picks out patterns: the row of all 1s; the row of all 2s; the alternating colours by family; the diagonals where the doubling cycle traces.
>
> *Patterns weren't there before the labelling.* Or rather, they were always there *in principle*, but invisible. The labelling makes them *legible*.
>
> *[long pause]*
>
> The torus has begun to *speak back*. Not literally — paper doesn't speak — but in the sense that the structure now *communicates with the eye*. The viewer becomes a *reader* rather than just an observer.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> Three contemplations on this transition.
>
> *Contemplation one — labelling is an act of relationship.* You spent time labelling each cell. The torus has been *touched* by you. There's a thin connection between the maker and the made artefact that didn't exist when the torus was bare. *The artefact now carries your hand-work.*
>
> Some traditions emphasise this. Bookbinders, calligraphers, scribes — they speak of *imbuing* the artefact with care during making. The labelled torus has a small portion of you on it.
>
> *Contemplation two — readability is a gift.* Many objects are unreadable — they have structure but the structure isn't apparent. Stones, leaves, walls. They have rich micro-structures but our eyes don't see them at hand-scale.
>
> The labelled torus is *deliberately readable*. The structure has been made apparent through human work. *We have made the doughnut speak.*
>
> *Contemplation three — the torus joins a family of made artefacts.* The integrated Symbol on your wall, the Number Map grid, the bookmark, the labelled torus. Each is a *made thing* that *speaks back* in some way. Together they form a *system of speaking objects* in your workspace. Each says something different about the same underlying mathematics.
>
> *[long pause — 12 seconds]*
>
> What does it feel like to be in conversation with an artefact you made?
>
> Some students notice *gratitude*. The torus saves them work. Without the labels, every reference to the doubling cycle would require recomputation. With the labels, the torus does the work.
>
> Some students notice *responsibility*. The torus they made will be referenced for months. If the labels are sloppy, future-them will struggle. *Care now is a gift to future-self.*
>
> Some students notice *companionship*. The torus is small, made by hand, deeply familiar. It feels like a *desk-mate* — quiet, present, helpful.
>
> All three are honest.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> A practical observation. *The artefacts you make become part of how you think about VBM.* The labelled torus isn't just a reference; it becomes the *first thing your mind reaches for* when you think about cyclic structure on surfaces. *Made artefacts shape thinking* in ways that read-only references don't.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> One more contemplation. By the end of this year you'll have many made artefacts: the integrated Symbol, the bookmark, the grid, the labelled torus, the wound coil, your capstone build. Each one will speak back to you in its own way.
>
> The conversation has begun. Sit with the labelled torus. Listen for what it now says.
>
> Let it speak."
