## 4. Body (1:00–4:30)

> "The objection. *The torus is a generic mathematical object. Calling it 'ABHA Torus' attaches a religious tradition name to mainstream mathematics. The name introduces unnecessary religious baggage and could alienate non-Bahá'í practitioners or students who'd otherwise engage.*
>
> The objection is reasonable; the naming is defendable; the choice is deliberate.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> *Why ABHA.*
>
> *Reason one — biographical authenticity.* Marko Rodin's discovery began with the Bahá'í greeting *Abha* at age 15 (Wk 12). Reducing *Abha* by Abjad numerology gave 9. From 9 came everything else — the doubling cycle, the Number Map, the torus.
>
> The torus is named *after the trigger of its discovery*. The naming honours the path the work actually took, rather than presenting the work as arising from pure mathematics.
>
> *Reason two — community signal.* Calling it 'ABHA Torus' identifies the artefact as belonging to *VBM as a tradition with a particular history*, not as generic mathematics. Practitioners can identify a Rodin Coil from the ABHA-named substrate. The naming is a community marker.
>
> *Reason three — the math is unaffected.* The naming doesn't change the torus's mathematical content. *Plain torus* and *ABHA Torus* are the same object with different names. The math is universal; the name is tradition-specific.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> *Why the naming might raise concerns.*
>
> *Concern one — religious connotation.* *Abha* is a Bahá'í greeting and theological term. Some students and practitioners are uncomfortable importing religious vocabulary into mathematical objects.
>
> *Concern two — alienation of non-Bahá'í learners.* Students from other religious traditions or secular orientations may feel the name presupposes Bahá'í commitments.
>
> *Concern three — accessibility for academic engagement.* Mainstream mathematicians may be reluctant to engage with an object that names itself religiously.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> *How the course handles the naming.*
>
> *Handling one.* The course uses *ABHA Torus* as canonical because that's the foundation tradition.
>
> *Handling two.* The course also accepts *the structured torus*, *the decorated torus*, or *the VBM torus* as alternative names for students or practitioners who prefer non-religious framing. These are equivalent.
>
> *Handling three.* When discussing the object with audiences who would react poorly to the religious name, *use an alternative*. The math doesn't depend on the name.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> So the calibrated answer to *why call it the ABHA*:
>
> *The ABHA Torus is named for the Bahá'í greeting that triggered Marko Rodin's discovery at age 15. The name honours the discovery's biographical origin and signals VBM-tradition belonging. The mathematical object is the same regardless of name; alternative neutral names like 'the structured torus' or 'the VBM torus' refer to the same object. The naming is deliberate, not arbitrary, and respects the framework's history while remaining substitutable for audiences who prefer non-religious vocabulary.*
>
> Around eighty words.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> Practical takeaway. *The name is a courtesy to the tradition; the math is universal.* Use *ABHA Torus* in foundation contexts. Use *the structured torus* in academic or skeptical contexts. The choice is yours; the underlying object is the same.
>
> One more practical note. Marko has occasionally said that *names matter* — that the act of naming an object is part of how its tradition forms around it. The ABHA name is part of how VBM identifies itself. Renaming the torus would, in some sense, fork the tradition. *Respecting the name is respecting the tradition*; it doesn't require accepting all of the tradition's metaphysics."
