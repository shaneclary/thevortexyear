## 4. Body (1:00–4:30)

> "*Plain mathematical torus.* The bare topological surface. Two cyclic dimensions. Continuous parametrisation by two angles. Used in topology, differential geometry, dynamical systems, abstract algebra. *No labelling on the surface.*
>
> *ABHA Torus.* The plain torus *plus* four annotation layers (cell labels, family colours, polar pair antipode threads, 3-6-9 axis). Used in VBM as the canonical geometric object. *Heavily labelled.*
>
> *[pause]*
>
> Three structural similarities.
>
> *Similarity one — same underlying topology.* Both are 2D toroidal surfaces. The plain torus and the ABHA Torus are *topologically identical*. The decorations don't change the topology.
>
> *Similarity two — both admit coordinates.* Each point on either torus has (major-angle, minor-angle) coordinates. Coordinate computations work on both.
>
> *Similarity three — both support continuous fields.* Heat distributions, electromagnetic fields, etc., live equally well on either torus.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> Three structural differences.
>
> *Difference one — discrete cell structure.* The plain torus has *no preferred cell decomposition*. The ABHA Torus has *144 specific cells* arranged in 6 rows × 24 columns. Plain mathematicians would call the decomposition 'a triangulation' or 'a CW-structure' — useful but optional. The ABHA Torus has the decomposition *built in*.
>
> *Difference two — value labelling.* The plain torus has no values on cells. The ABHA Torus has *cell labels* that encode the doubling cycle. This makes the ABHA Torus a *function-on-surface* artefact rather than a pure topological object.
>
> *Difference three — emphasised substructures.* The plain torus has many possible substructures (any pair of intersecting cycles, for instance). The ABHA Torus *foregrounds specific substructures*: family colours, polar pair threads, 3-6-9 axis. These are *attention directors*; they tell you what to look at.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> *What the four decorations add.*
>
> *Add 1 — discoverability.* The plain torus's structure is implicit; you have to *compute* to see anything. The ABHA Torus's structure is *displayed*; you can read it. Beginners benefit hugely.
>
> *Add 2 — engineering relevance.* The decorations are *exactly what a coil-builder needs*. Cell labels = wind sequence. Family colours = strand assignment. Antipode threads = polar pair connectivity. The plain torus would require re-deriving all of this on every build.
>
> *Add 3 — VBM-tradition specificity.* The decorations identify the torus as *the VBM torus* rather than just *a torus*. This matters for community work — when builders share photographs of their tori, the family colours indicate they're working in the same tradition.
>
> *Add 4 — pedagogical accessibility.* A bare torus looks like a topology homework problem. The ABHA Torus looks like a *map of something*. Beginners engage with the ABHA Torus more readily.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> *What the four decorations cost.*
>
> *Cost 1 — narrowed scope.* The plain torus generalises to any application. The ABHA Torus is specific to mod-9 doubling-Pisano structure. *General topology can do more, but doesn't show as much for this specific case.*
>
> *Cost 2 — interpretive overhead.* Beginners must learn what the decorations mean before using the torus. Plain tori require less prior knowledge.
>
> *Cost 3 — risk of over-fitting.* Heavy decoration may obscure that the *underlying torus* is generic. The ABHA Torus might look more special than it is.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> Useful framing. *The ABHA Torus is a plain torus with VBM-specific decorations. The decorations make the torus useful for VBM-specific purposes. Stripped, the underlying topology is unremarkable; decorated, the torus is the central pedagogical and engineering object of the framework.*
>
> Practical takeaway. *Use the decorations.* They're not noise; they're the structure that makes the torus worth looking at for VBM purposes."
