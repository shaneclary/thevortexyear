## 4. Body (1:00–4:30)

> "*Frame.*
>
> The clip you're about to hear is Marko Rodin describing the *symmetries* he observes on the ABHA Torus. These are patterns that map the torus onto itself — rotations, reflections, swaps that preserve the structure. From the math, these are formal symmetry groups; from Marko's framing, they are *the way the torus is alive*.
>
> The clip is approximately fifty seconds. **`[ORG-CLIP]`**.
>
> What to listen for. Three things.
>
> *One — the rotational symmetries.* Rotating the torus by 15° (one column) sends col index c to col c+1. This is a symmetry that permutes cells but preserves the family colour structure. Marko likely emphasises that the torus *looks the same* after such rotations — a key invariant.
>
> *Two — the antipodal symmetry.* Mapping (col, row) → (col + 12, row + 3) sends each cell to its antipode (its polar pair partner). This is a symmetry of order 2 — applying it twice returns the original. The polar pair structure is exactly the antipodal symmetry made visible.
>
> *Three — the family-preservation.* Cells in family 1 stay in family 1 under the symmetries. Cells in family 2 stay in family 2. *The symmetries preserve the colour decomposition.* Marko occasionally describes this as 'the torus respects its families'.

> *Contextualise.*
>
> What you just heard tells you several things about the symmetry structure.
>
> *Symmetry-thing one.* The torus has a *rich symmetry group*. Rotations in either direction (column or row) plus the antipodal involution generate a finite abelian group acting on the torus. The group is approximately ℤ/24ℤ × ℤ/6ℤ together with the antipodal involution.
>
> *Symmetry-thing two.* The symmetries *partition the cells into orbits*. Each cell has 144 / |stabiliser| copies in its orbit. For most cells (with trivial stabiliser), the orbit is the whole torus. For cells with non-trivial stabiliser (some cells on the 3-6-9 axis, perhaps), orbits are smaller.
>
> *Symmetry-thing three.* The symmetries *connect to the engineering*. Rotational symmetry of the torus implies *positional rotation symmetry of the coil*. A coil rotated by 15° (one column) is the *same coil* under the torus's symmetry — measuring it should give the same results as the original. *Symmetry implies measurement repeatability*.
>
> *Symmetry-thing four.* Marko's framing of the symmetries as *aliveness* is metaphorical. Mathematically, the symmetries are *automorphisms* of the discrete torus. *The 'aliveness' framing is Marko's; the automorphism structure is universal.*
>

>
> Practical takeaway. *The torus's symmetries are exploitable for engineering and pedagogical purposes.* When you wind a coil following the torus, the symmetries mean *small construction errors are tolerated* — the coil can be slightly mis-rotated and still be 'the same coil' up to symmetry.
>
> Pedagogically, the symmetries mean *learning one cell's relationships teaches you all 144*. Each cell is in an orbit; understanding the orbit gives you all cells in it.
>
> One more practical note. Marko occasionally extends 'symmetries' to higher-dimensional symmetries that include the 3-6-9 axis. The full symmetry group becomes ℤ/24ℤ × ℤ/6ℤ × ℤ/3ℤ if we include the family-3 axis as a separate dimension. This is anticipatory of Wk 22's Decoqubit; for now, hold the simpler torus symmetries."
