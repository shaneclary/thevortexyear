## 0. Authoring notes
The axis you can put your finger through. Contemplative on the embodied experience of the orthogonal axis.

## 1. Outcome
Student has spent quiet time with the embodied experience of putting a finger through the doughnut hole.

## 2. Hook (0:00–0:30)

> *(slow)* "Put your finger through the centre. Sit with the passage."

## 3. Setup (0:30–1:00)

## 4. Body (1:00–4:30)

> "Hold your threaded torus. *Remove* the stick temporarily. With one finger, slowly enter the doughnut hole, pass through, and emerge on the other side.
>
> *[long pause — 12 seconds]*
>
> Feel the passage.
>
> *[long pause]*
>
> Most contact with objects is *external*. We touch surfaces. We hold edges. We don't usually pass *through* objects.
>
> The torus is unusual. *It has a hole you can pass through.* The 3-6-9 axis is the geometric direction of that passage. Putting your finger through is *enacting the axis*.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> Three contemplations on through-passage.
>
> *Contemplation one — the inside is reached without breaking.* You don't damage the torus to pass through it. The hole is a *built-in feature*; passage is *invited*. *Other objects (a ball, a brick, a flat sheet) don't allow this.* The torus is one of the few everyday objects whose interior is accessible without violation.
>
> Some traditions speak of *thresholds* — places where the inner and outer meet without barrier. The doughnut hole is a literal small threshold.
>
> *Contemplation two — the passage has direction.* You enter from one side; you emerge on the other. The axis has *two ends* (where 3 and 6 sit) and a *centre* (where 9 sits). Even a brief finger-pass enacts the axis's bipolar structure.
>
> Some students notice this *doubling effect*. There's a *coming-in* and a *going-out*; the axis carries both.
>
> *Contemplation three — the axis is geometrically *not the surface*.* When your finger is at the centre of the doughnut hole (at position 9), it isn't on any surface cell. It's *in the air* — in the geometric centre that the surface surrounds.
>
> *Family 3 is structurally outside the surface*; the embodied passage confirms this. You can be at 9 only by being *off* the surface. The axis literally is *not where the cells are*.
>
> *[long pause — 12 seconds]*
>
> What does the through-passage feel like?
>
> Some students notice *quietness*. The centre of the doughnut hole is a *small empty region*. There's nothing there structurally. The finger encounters air. *The empty centre is, in some traditions, the most active region* — but that's interpretive; the immediate feel is just *quiet space*.
>
> Some students notice *transition*. Outside the doughnut hole; at the centre; outside again on the other side. *Three states in one slow motion.* The motion has a rhythm.
>
> Some students notice *centring*. When the finger is at the centre, all the structure (cells, helix, polar threads, surface) is *around* the finger. The finger is at the *origin* of the geometry. Some find this momentarily settling.
>
> All three are honest.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> A practical observation. *The through-passage gesture is unusual in mathematics teaching.* Most diagrams ask the eye to look; the torus's gesture asks the body to *enter*. The passage is *direct contact with the geometry*.
>
> Marko's metaphysical framing of *Spirit through the centre* (Lens 4) draws on this gesture. Hold the framing as Marko's; the *physical gesture* is meaningful regardless of which framing you bring to it.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> One more contemplation. *The 3-6-9 axis is a third direction.* Q2 has used the surface (two cyclic directions). The axis adds a third — perpendicular, linear, bipolar.
>
> Three is a small but meaningful number of dimensions. *Three-axis structures support volumetric reasoning*. Wk 21 onward will exploit the third axis for higher-dimensional VBM constructions.
>
> Hold the threaded torus. Remove the stick. Pass a finger through. Notice the centre. Replace the stick. Continue."
