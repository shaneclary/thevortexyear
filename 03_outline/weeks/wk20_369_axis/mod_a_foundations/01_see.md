## 4. Body (1:00–4:30)

> "Q2 has spent six weeks on the torus surface. The Number Map's cells, the doubling helix, the polar pair antipodes — all *on* the surface. *Family 3 has been notably absent.*
>
> Wk 17 introduced the four ABHA decoration layers, and noted that family 3 ({3, 6, 9}) doesn't appear on the doubling-only grid. Today we resolve where family 3 *does* appear: *on the orthogonal axis through the doughnut hole*.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> *Definition.* The 3-6-9 axis is a *line through the torus's central hole, perpendicular to the plane of the torus*. It passes through the geometric centre of the doughnut, with one end emerging *above* the torus and the other *below*.
>
> Visually: imagine the torus lying flat on a table, doughnut-hole pointing up. The 3-6-9 axis is a *vertical line* through the centre of the hole, extending up and down through the table.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> *Why family 3 lives there and not on the surface.*
>
> Recall Q1: doubling on (ℤ/9ℤ)* visits the units {1, 2, 4, 5, 7, 8}. *Doubling never reaches 3, 6, or 9* — these are the *non-units* of ℤ/9ℤ. They're not in the multiplicative group; doubling can't transform a unit into a non-unit.
>
> So *no surface cell* on the doubling helix, halving helix, or off-helix family-1-and-2 cells holds value 3, 6, or 9.
>
> *Family 3 is structurally outside the multiplicative cycle.*
>
> Geometrically, this means: family 3 cannot be displayed *on* the toroidal surface (which carries the multiplicative orbit of doubling). It must be displayed *off* the surface — on a distinct axis.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> *Why specifically the orthogonal axis through the centre.*
>
> The torus has *two natural cyclic axes* (major and minor circles). Family 3 doesn't live on either — its structure (3 ↔ 6 oscillation, 9 fixed point) doesn't match a cyclic-translation pattern.
>
> The *third axis*, perpendicular to both, is the geometric direction *not* used by the surface cycles. *Family 3's structure fits on this axis.*
>
> *[pause]*
>
> *Family 3's structure on the axis.*
>
> *Position 9 — the fixed point.* Located at the geometric centre of the torus (inside the doughnut hole, at the centre of the central plane). Symbolically: the axis's *origin*.
>
> *Position 3 — one end.* Located *above* the torus, along the positive direction of the axis.
>
> *Position 6 — the other end.* Located *below* the torus, along the negative direction.
>
> Or, with possibly different conventions: 3 and 6 alternating between top and bottom in a *bipolar oscillation*. The exact convention varies between practitioners; the structural point is that 9 is *central* and 3, 6 are *bipolar*.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> Three observations.
>
> *Observation one.* The axis is *truly outside* the torus surface. You can run a stick through the doughnut hole, perpendicular to the plane. *That stick is the axis.*
>
> *Observation two.* The axis is *one-dimensional* — a line. The torus surface is two-dimensional. Family 3 has *fewer dimensions* than the surface; it has only the central + two bipolar directions, not a 2D extent.
>
> *Observation three.* The axis *passes through the doughnut hole* — the same region the polar-pair threads pass through. *Polar pair threads and the 3-6-9 axis are spatially coexistent through the central hole.*
>
> *[pause]*
>
> Practical takeaway. *VBM's geometry is not just the torus surface.* It's *torus surface + orthogonal 3-6-9 axis*. The axis carries the structural content (family 3) that the surface omits. *Together, they form the complete VBM 3D frame.*"
