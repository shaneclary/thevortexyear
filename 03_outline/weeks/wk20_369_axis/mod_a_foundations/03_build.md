## 4. Body (1:00–4:30)

> "Today's build adds a *physical* representation of the 3-6-9 axis to your paper torus. The axis becomes a stick threaded through the doughnut hole, perpendicular to the torus's plane.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> *Materials.*
>
> Your paper torus from Wks 16-19 (with cell labels, helix trace, polar pair threads).
>
> A wooden skewer, chopstick, dowel, or wire — about 15-20 cm long, thin enough to fit through the doughnut hole.
>
> Tape or hot glue to secure if needed.
>
> Optional: small labels (3, 6, 9) and a fine-point marker.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> *Step 1 — orient the torus.* Lay the torus flat with the doughnut hole pointing up. The major circumference is horizontal; the minor circumference loops around the tube.
>
> *Step 2 — insert the stick.* Push the stick through the doughnut hole *perpendicular to the torus's plane*. The stick should pass straight through the centre, with equal lengths protruding above and below.
>
> *Step 3 — centre the stick.* Adjust so the stick's midpoint is at the centre of the doughnut hole. *The midpoint corresponds to position 9 — the axis's origin.*
>
> *Step 4 — secure.* If the stick is loose, use small pieces of tape inside the doughnut hole to hold it in place. Don't over-tape; the stick should look like *one structure passing through the torus*, not like an attachment.
>
> *Step 5 — label.* Using small labels or a marker:
>
> Mark *9* at the centre of the stick (where it passes through the doughnut hole's centre).
>
> Mark *3* at one end of the stick (e.g., the upper end).
>
> Mark *6* at the other end (the lower end).
>
> *[pause]*
>
> *Inspection.*
>
> Look at the threaded torus from multiple angles.
>
> *Top view (looking down through the doughnut hole).* The stick is visible as a single line passing through the centre.
>
> *Side view.* The stick passes vertically through the torus's centre, with the torus body around its midpoint.
>
> *Quarter-rotated view.* Both the torus surface and the orthogonal axis are visible simultaneously. *The full VBM 3D frame.*
>
> *[pause]*
>
> *What you've built.*
>
> *The complete VBM geometric frame in physical form.*
>
> Torus surface — carries (ℤ/9ℤ)* values via cells.
>
> Helix trace — carries the doubling cycle.
>
> Polar pair threads — carry the antipodal structure.
>
> Orthogonal axis (this week's stick) — carries family 3.
>
> *Four physical components, one integrated VBM diagram.*
>
> *[pause]*
>
> Three observations.
>
> *Observation one.* The axis is *literally separate from the surface*. You added a stick that wasn't there before. *The axis is structurally independent of the torus.*
>
> *Observation two.* The stick *passes through the doughnut hole alongside the polar pair threads*. The threads (algebraic polar pairs as antipodes) and the stick (family-3 axis) coexist in the same central region. *The doughnut hole is busier than it looks.*
>
> *Observation three.* The stick is *physically more rigid* than the paper torus around it. Some students notice this; the *axis* feels like the *spine* of the structure, with the *surface* a body around it. The metaphor isn't precise but the felt sense is suggestive.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> Practical takeaway. *Photograph the threaded torus from at least two angles; tape into your build log.* The threaded version is the *complete Q2 reference object*. Q3's revisits (★ Wks 27-29) will return to it."
