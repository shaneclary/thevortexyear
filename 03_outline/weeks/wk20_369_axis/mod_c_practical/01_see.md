## 4. Body (1:00–4:30)

> "The 3-6-9 axis becomes practical hardware as an *axial wire* — a wire passing along the torus's central axis, through the doughnut hole, perpendicular to the toroidal plane.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> *Configuration.*
>
> Imagine the canonical Rodin Coil — six strands wound on a torus former. Add: *a separate wire passing straight through the central hole, top to bottom*.
>
> The toroidal windings carry current around the surface. The axial wire carries current straight through the axis.
>
> *Two electrically separate components in one rig.*
>
> *[pause]*
>
> *Why the axial wire matters.*
>
> *Reason 1 — physical realisation of family 3.* In Wk 20's geometry, family 3 lives on the orthogonal axis. The axial wire *physically realises this axis* in hardware.
>
> *Reason 2 — independent current source.* The axial wire can carry its own current, independent of the surface windings. *This adds a second electrical degree of freedom* to the coil.
>
> *Reason 3 — interaction with surface fields.* When both the toroidal windings and the axial wire are energised, their fields interact. *This interaction is one of the central engineering interests of Q3's later weeks.*
>
> *[pause]*
>
> *Three configurations.*
>
> *Config 1 — toroidal-only.* Standard Rodin Coil. No axial wire. The torus carries current; family 3 is geometric placeholder only. *Most builds, including the Wk 33 single-strand and Wk 35 canonical, use this configuration.*
>
> *Config 2 — toroidal + axial driven separately.* Both components present. Each driven by its own current source. *The interaction is studied by varying one current independently.*
>
> *Config 3 — axial-only (no toroidal).* Just the axial wire, no surface coil. *Least common; isolates the axial contribution.*
>
> *[pause]*
>
> *Hardware components.*
>
> *Toroidal substrate.* The 3D-printed torus former from Wk 17.
>
> *Surface windings.* The doubling helix wound on the surface, in single-strand or six-strand configuration.
>
> *Axial wire.* A separate insulated wire passing through the central hole. Typically AWG 18-22 (slightly thicker than surface wire, since it can carry larger currents without interaction).
>
> *Axial supports.* Small clamps or non-conductive supports to hold the axial wire centred and perpendicular to the torus's plane.
>
> *Two power supplies.* One for the toroidal windings, one for the axial wire. Each adjustable independently.
>
> *Measurement equipment.* Multimeters for current; oscilloscope for waveforms; magnetic probe for field mapping.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> Three observations.
>
> *Observation one.* The axial wire is *additional*, not a replacement for surface windings. The full rig has both.
>
> *Observation two.* The axial wire *passes through the doughnut hole alongside the polar pair threads* (from your paper torus). In hardware, the polar pair threads aren't physical (they're conceptual); the axial wire takes their place as the physical structure through the hole.
>
> *Observation three.* The axial wire is *introduced in Q3, not Q2*. Today's Mod C is preview material. Wk 30 onward will engage axial drive seriously.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> Practical takeaway. *The axial wire is a planned future component.* Don't build it yet — Q3's Wk 30 will introduce it formally. *Today is preview.* Knowing it's coming helps frame Q2's geometry as preparation for Q3's electronics."
