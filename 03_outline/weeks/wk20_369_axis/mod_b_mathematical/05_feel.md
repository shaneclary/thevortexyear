## 4. Body (1:00–4:30)

> "Orthogonality is one of mathematics' simpler relationships. *Two directions are orthogonal if they are perpendicular* — they meet at right angles. The torus's surface and the 3-6-9 axis are perpendicular.
>
> *[long pause — 12 seconds]*
>
> Sit with perpendicularity.
>
> *[long pause]*
>
> Most relationships in mathematics involve *direct interaction*: addition, multiplication, composition. Orthogonality is *the absence of interaction*. Two perpendicular directions can vary independently; changing one doesn't affect the other.
>
> *Independence as a feature.*
>
> *[pause]*
>
> Three contemplations on orthogonality.
>
> *Contemplation one — orthogonality enables decomposition.* When two directions are perpendicular, you can decompose any vector into components along each. The components can be measured, computed, manipulated *separately*. *The decomposition is the gift orthogonality gives.*
>
> The torus's two cyclic axes are orthogonal to each other (within the surface). The axis adds a third orthogonal direction. *Three perpendicular directions; full 3D decomposition available.*
>
> *Contemplation two — orthogonality is rare.* Most directions in space are *not* perpendicular to each other. Picking two random directions, they almost certainly meet at some non-right angle. *Perpendicularity is the special case*; non-perpendicularity is the generic.
>
> When two structures *are* perpendicular, it usually means *something has selected them* — a symmetry, a constraint, an explicit construction. The torus surface and the 3-6-9 axis are perpendicular *because* the torus has rotational symmetry about the axis.
>
> *Contemplation three — orthogonality scales.* Two perpendicular directions in 2D. Three in 3D. Higher dimensions allow more orthogonal directions simultaneously. *Mathematics often generalises orthogonality* upward.
>
> The Decoqubit (Wk 22) will introduce *higher-dimensional orthogonalities* in VBM. Today's lesson — perpendicular axis to a 2D surface — is a 3D instance of a more general pattern.
>
> *[long pause — 12 seconds]*
>
> What does orthogonality feel like?
>
> Some students notice *clarity*. Perpendicular directions don't interfere. Each can be examined separately. *The math becomes manageable.*
>
> Some students notice *separateness*. The torus surface and the axis are *not in contact*. They share a centre (in 3D), but their content doesn't blend. *Two structures, one space, no overlap.*
>
> Some students notice *completeness*. Three perpendicular directions in 3D *exhaust* the possible directions. *Nothing is missing*; the geometric frame is complete.
>
> All three are honest.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> A practical observation. *Mathematicians and physicists often default to orthogonal coordinate systems.* Cartesian (x, y, z); polar; cylindrical; spherical. Each uses orthogonality as a foundation. *The torus + axis is one specific orthogonal system suited to VBM's needs.*
>
> *[pause]*
>
> One more contemplation. *Orthogonality is mathematical neighbouring without entanglement.* The torus surface and the axis are *next to each other in space* but don't *interfere with each other's structure*. Each can be studied as if the other weren't there.
>
> Until they need to interact. Counter-stranding from Wk 19 used the through-hole connection between paired strands; Wk 30 onward will use the axis as a *driver* through which current flows independently of the surface windings. *When orthogonal structures interact, their interaction is *clean* — no muddled middle ground.*
>
> Hold the threaded torus. Feel the axis perpendicular to the surface. Notice the cleanness."
