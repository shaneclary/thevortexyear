## 0. Authoring notes
Orthogonal axis in S¹ × S¹ vs spherical surfaces. Compare the role of the orthogonal axis on a torus with corresponding structures on a sphere.

## 1. Outcome
Student can articulate why the orthogonal axis works on a torus and what would change on a sphere.

## 2. Hook (0:00–0:30)
*[A torus with z-axis through hole; a sphere with imagined z-axis through poles.]*

> "Torus axis. Sphere axis. Same coord, different role. Six minutes."

## 3. Setup (0:30–1:00)
> "Lens 7 of 9. Compare orthogonal axes for two surfaces."

## 4. Body (1:00–4:30)

> "*Torus T² = S¹ × S¹.* The torus has *no privileged points* on its surface — every cell is geometrically equivalent (up to symmetry). The *z-axis through the central hole* is the natural orthogonal axis.
>
> *Sphere S².* The sphere has *two privileged points* — the *poles* — which are antipodal on the surface. The *z-axis through the poles* would be the natural orthogonal axis.
>
> Both surfaces *can* have a z-axis. The role differs.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> Three structural similarities.
>
> *Similarity one — both have a z-axis option.* For both surfaces, you can pick a privileged direction in 3D space.
>
> *Similarity two — both have rotational symmetry about the z-axis.* The torus rotates around the z-axis through its central hole; the sphere rotates around the z-axis through its poles.
>
> *Similarity three — both surfaces are 2D in 3D space.* The orthogonal axis adds a third dimension to the framework.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> Three structural differences.
>
> *Difference one — relationship to the surface.* Torus z-axis: *passes through the central hole, never touching the surface*. Sphere z-axis: *passes through two surface points (the poles)*. Different topological relationships.
>
> *Difference two — what the axis carries.* On the torus, the axis carries *the family-3 algebraic content* — the non-units of (ℤ/9ℤ). On a sphere, an analogous axis would carry... the points along the z-axis. But what algebraic content? The sphere doesn't have a natural mod-9 structure to partition.
>
> *Difference three — symmetries.* Torus: rotational symmetry about z-axis (continuous). Sphere: rotational symmetry about z-axis (continuous) *plus* full SO(3) symmetry (rotations in any direction). The sphere has *more* symmetries than the torus.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> *Why VBM's structure suits the torus, not the sphere.*
>
> *Reason 1 — two cyclic dimensions.* The Number Map's two periodicities (24 columns, 6 rows) require two cyclic dimensions. The torus has them; the sphere doesn't.
>
> *Reason 2 — orthogonal axis through hole.* The torus's hole *naturally* provides a 'space' for the orthogonal axis to pass through without touching the surface. The sphere lacks a hole; an orthogonal axis must pass through surface points (poles).
>
> *Reason 3 — non-units placement.* On the torus, family 3 lives on the axis (off-surface). On a sphere, where would family 3 go? Possibly the poles, but that's only 2 points, not 3 — and 9 should be *between* 3 and 6, not at one of them. *The sphere doesn't have the right geometry for the family-3 partition.*
>
> *[pause]*
>
> *Could VBM use a sphere?*
>
> *Theoretically yes; practically no.* You could try to embed (ℤ/9ℤ)* on a sphere's surface. The two cyclic axes would have to be the longitude (one cyclic axis) and latitude (not naturally cyclic, since the poles break the symmetry). *The sphere's geometry forces awkward choices*; the torus's geometry naturally supports VBM's structure.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> Useful framing. *The torus is the right surface for VBM because its geometry naturally accommodates two cyclic dimensions plus a perpendicular axis through a hole. The sphere doesn't share this structure — too few cyclic dimensions, no hole for the perpendicular axis.*
>
> Practical takeaway. *VBM is *not* on a sphere because it can't be.* The geometry has been chosen to fit the structure. *If the structure were different, the surface would be different.* For the (24, 6) Number Map, the torus is the unique fit.
>
> A small note. Some practitioners conflate the torus and the sphere informally — both are 'closed surfaces in 3D'. The conflation is *imprecise* and obscures the structural reasons VBM is on a torus. *Be careful not to do this in technical contexts.*"
