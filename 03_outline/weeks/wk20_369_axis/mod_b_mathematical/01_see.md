## 4. Body (1:00–4:30)

> "Mod A introduced the 3-6-9 axis geometrically. Mod B writes it precisely.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> *Setup.* Place the torus in 3D space. Use Cartesian coordinates (x, y, z). Convention: torus lies in the *xy-plane*, with central hole symmetric around the z-axis.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> *Formal description of the orthogonal axis.*
>
> *Axis = {(0, 0, z) | z ∈ ℝ} ⊂ ℝ³.*
>
> Read: the axis is the set of points where x = 0 and y = 0 — the *z-axis* of standard 3D coordinates.
>
> The axis is a *1D subspace* of ℝ³, parametrised by a single real number z.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> *Three special points on the axis.*
>
> *Origin (0, 0, 0).* Corresponds to family-3 element 9 (the additive identity in mod 9).
>
> *Positive end (0, 0, +∞).* Corresponds to family-3 element 3 (one direction).
>
> *Negative end (0, 0, −∞).* Corresponds to family-3 element 6 (other direction).
>
> *Discrete version.* For a finite-extent representation: positions z = +1 (corresponding to 3), z = 0 (corresponding to 9), and z = −1 (corresponding to 6). The axis has *three preferred points* — origin and two endpoints.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> *Relationship to the torus.*
>
> The torus T² has parametrisation:
>
> torus(θ, φ) = ((R + r cos θ) cos φ, (R + r cos θ) sin φ, r sin θ)
>
> where θ is the minor angle (around the tube), φ is the major angle (around the central hole), R is the major radius, and r is the minor radius.
>
> *Notice.* Every point on the torus has z-coordinate r sin θ, which ranges in [-r, +r]. *No torus point has |z| > r.*
>
> The axis (0, 0, z) extends to *all* z ∈ ℝ. *The axis passes through the torus's interior void* (the region not on the torus surface) including the central hole at (0, 0, 0).
>
> *[pause]*
>
> *Three observations.*
>
> *Observation one — orthogonality.* The axis is *perpendicular* to the torus's plane (the xy-plane). At any point on the torus surface, the *tangent plane* to the surface is *not* perpendicular to the z-axis — but the *axis through the central hole* is. *The axis is orthogonal to the plane of the torus.*
>
> *Observation two — the axis isn't on the torus surface.* For every (0, 0, z) ∈ axis, this point is in the central void if |z| < small threshold, and far outside the torus if |z| is large. *No part of the axis touches the surface* — they are disjoint.
>
> *Observation three — the axis has its own coordinate system.* Just one parameter (z) versus the torus's two (θ, φ). *Lower-dimensional structure.*
>
> *[pause]*
>
> *Formal direct sum.*
>
> The 3D Euclidean space ℝ³ decomposes as:
>
> ℝ³ = (xy-plane) ⊕ (z-axis)
>
> The torus lives in the xy-plane (more precisely, *near* the xy-plane — the torus surface dips slightly above and below). The 3-6-9 axis is the z-axis.
>
> *Two perpendicular pieces; together they span all of 3D.*
>
> *[pause]*
>
> Practical takeaway. *The axis is the z-axis when the torus lies in the xy-plane.* Standard Cartesian geometry. No new mathematics needed; just a recognition of how VBM's structure fits into 3D space.
>
> Pin the formal description: *axis = {(0, 0, z) | z ∈ ℝ}*. The simplest mathematical object so far this week."
