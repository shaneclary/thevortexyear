## 4. Body (1:00–4:30)

> "The objection. *VBM places family 3 on a perpendicular axis. But the placement looks arbitrary — couldn't family 3 go anywhere? Couldn't it be on a different surface, or just left out? Why specifically a perpendicular axis?*
>
> The answer is a structural theorem.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> *Theorem (Axis Partition).* For VBM's structure on the (6, 24) torus with cell-value function v(c) = 2^row mod 9, the disjoint partition ℤ/9ℤ = (ℤ/9ℤ)* ⊔ 3ℤ/9ℤ requires a *geometric realisation as 2D + 1D*. The 2D part (the torus surface) carries (ℤ/9ℤ)*; the 1D part (the orthogonal axis) carries 3ℤ/9ℤ.
>
> *Proof.*
>
> *Step 1 — partition.* (ℤ/9ℤ) decomposes as a disjoint union of (ℤ/9ℤ)* (the units, 6 elements) and 3ℤ/9ℤ (the non-units, 3 elements).
>
> *Step 2 — units' geometric structure.* The units (ℤ/9ℤ)* form a cyclic group of order 6 under multiplication. Geometrically realised as a *6-cycle* on the doubling helix. The helix lives on a 2D surface (the torus).
>
> *Step 3 — non-units' geometric structure.* The non-units 3ℤ/9ℤ = {0, 3, 6} form a cyclic group of order 3 under addition. Geometrically realisable as a *3-element line* — a 1D structure with three preferred points (origin and two endpoints).
>
> *Step 4 — dimension counts.* The units' 6-cycle requires 1 dimension on a 2D surface (it's a 1D path *embedded in* a 2D space). The non-units' 3-element line requires 1 dimension. *Total: 2D + 1D = 3D.*
>
> *Step 5 — separation.* The 6-cycle and the 3-line *can't share a dimension* — they have different periods (6 vs 3) and different operations (multiplicative vs additive). They must occupy *different* dimensions.
>
> *Step 6 — orthogonality.* The cleanest separation in 3D space is *orthogonal*. The torus surface (2D) and the perpendicular axis (1D, the z-direction) are orthogonal. *This is the structural realisation forced by the partition.*
>
> *Q.E.D.*
>
> *[pause]*
>
> *Three corollaries.*
>
> *Corollary 1.* The axis isn't arbitrary; it's *forced* by the partition + dimension counting.
>
> *Corollary 2.* The orthogonal placement is *necessary* — non-orthogonal placements (e.g., the axis at some angle to the torus's plane) would break the rotational symmetry of the torus and create asymmetries that the algebra doesn't support.
>
> *Corollary 3.* The axis's content (3ℤ/9ℤ) is *determined* — not an arbitrary choice but the algebraic complement of (ℤ/9ℤ)*.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> *Why the proof is structural.*
>
> The proof doesn't depend on aesthetic choices, tradition, or convenience. It depends on:
>
> The mathematical fact that ℤ/9ℤ partitions into units and non-units.
>
> The dimension-count fact that the unit 6-cycle uses 1D and the non-unit 3-line uses 1D.
>
> The geometric fact that 2D + 1D in 3D space orthogonally is the cleanest realisation.
>
> Each fact is independent of VBM. *VBM combines them.*
>
> *[pause]*
>
> So the calibrated answer to *the axis is forced*:
>
> *The 3-6-9 axis is forced by the partition of ℤ/9ℤ into units and non-units. The units form a 6-cycle realised on the torus surface (2D). The non-units form a 3-element line realised on the orthogonal axis (1D). The two structures cannot share a dimension because they have different periods and operations; orthogonal placement in 3D space gives the cleanest realisation. The axis isn't a design choice; it's the geometric realisation forced by the algebra and dimension counting.*
>
> Around eighty words.
