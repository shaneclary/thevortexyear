## 4. Body (1:00–4:30) — *contemplative*

> *(slow)* "When you wound the doubling-direction string yesterday, your hand learned a specific spiral motion. Clockwise around the torus, six vertices in order. The motion settled into your hand as *the doubling gesture*.
>
> *[long pause]*
>
> Today the hand learns the reverse. *Counter-clockwise. Different vertex sequence. Mirror gesture.*
>
> *[pause]*
>
> Most students find the reverse harder. The hand wants to repeat what it learned yesterday. The new direction feels *unnatural* until you've done it five or six times. Then it settles in alongside the original.
>
> *[long pause]*
>
> What's happening is bilateral practice — the kind of practice musicians and athletes know well. Each hand, each direction, learns its own version of the same gesture. The two versions are mirror images, but the *learning* of each is independent. A pianist knows: scales going up the keyboard are not the same skill as scales coming down. They share structure but require separate practice.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> Marko's claim — *one operation, two directions* — is mathematically correct. Doubling and halving are inverse automorphisms on the same group. But your *hand* doesn't know that abstraction. Your hand has two separate skills to develop: the doubling gesture and the halving gesture. Each takes its own repetition.
>
> *[long pause — 12 seconds]*
>
> By Wk 35, when you wind a real reverse-direction Rodin Coil, your hand should already have done the gesture in string a few times. The first physical wind is harder than the third. Anything you can do now to install the bilateral skill makes Wk 35 easier.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> Sit with the hand. The right-handed gesture is in the right hand from yesterday. The left-handed gesture is the new one. Two skills, one pair, one symmetric structure underneath.
>
> *[long pause]*
>
> The reversibility of the cycle, mathematically, mirrors the bilaterality of your body. Same closed pair. The math doesn't tell you why the body has two hands; the body doesn't tell you why the math has two generators. They're parallel, not derived from each other. The parallel is striking once you notice it."
