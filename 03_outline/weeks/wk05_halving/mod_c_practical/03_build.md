## 4. Body (1:00–4:30)

*[Camera follows hand on torus former.]*

> "Take your torus former from Wk 4 — the one that already has the doubling-direction string mock-up. Have a different-coloured string ready. Blue, if doubling was red.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> Step one — locate vertex 1 on the former. Tape one end of the new string at vertex 1.
>
> Step two — wrap the string from vertex 1 to vertex 5. Notice: this is the *first move* of the Halving Circuit. From vertex 1 to vertex 5 wraps the string in the *opposite direction* around the torus from yesterday's vertex-1-to-vertex-2 wrap.
>
> Step three — from 5 to 7.
>
> Step four — from 7 to 8.
>
> Step five — from 8 to 4.
>
> Step six — from 4 to 2.
>
> Step seven — from 2 back to 1.
>
> *[pause — show one full halving traversal in blue, atop the doubling traversal in red]*
>
> Look at what you've made. The torus former now has two strings on it — the doubling-direction in red, the halving-direction in blue. Both close. Both visit the same six vertices. But the wire paths are *opposite spirals*.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> Three things to notice with your hands.
>
> First — the *handedness* of each spiral. The red string spirals one way; the blue spirals the other. If you trace each with a finger, the finger moves clockwise on one and counter-clockwise on the other.
>
> Second — the *crossings*. Where the doubling string crosses itself (in 3D, it doesn't *quite* cross — the wraps are slightly offset), the halving string crosses at a different position. The two strings interweave.
>
> Third — the *symmetry*. If you look at the torus from one side, the two strings together form a symmetric pattern. If you look from the other side, the same symmetry holds. The pair is more visually balanced than either string alone.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> When you wind two real coils on two real torus formers in Wk 35, this is what you'll be making. Today is preparation."
