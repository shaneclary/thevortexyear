## 4. Body (1:00–4:30)

> "The doubling-halving pair isn't a Rodin-specific invention. Counter-wound coils are standard practice in RF and electromagnetic engineering, used for several decades.
>
> *Application one — bifilar coils.* A bifilar coil winds two parallel wires together — one in each direction — around a core. The two wires' fields oppose each other in *some* directions and add in others. Used in switching power supplies, audio transformers, and common-mode chokes. The opposing-direction principle is exactly what doubling-halving captures, with two wires instead of one.
>
> *Application two — common-mode chokes.* In RF circuits where common-mode noise (signal that's the same on both wires of a pair) needs to be suppressed while differential signal passes through, a common-mode choke uses opposite-direction windings on a shared core. The opposite directions cancel the common-mode component but preserve the differential. Same opposing-direction principle, packaged for noise suppression.
>
> *Application three — balanced transformers (baluns).* Used to convert between balanced and unbalanced transmission lines (for example, between a coaxial cable and a twin-lead antenna). Baluns frequently use counter-wound primary and secondary windings to maintain balance. The doubling-halving pair, again.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> So the technique — wind opposite directions to produce specific cancellation effects — is well-established. Marko's contribution isn't the technique; it's the *specific winding pattern* (the doubling cycle on a torus) that he applies the technique to.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> What this means for your build. When you wind a halving-direction Rodin Coil and stack it with a doubling-direction one, the resulting structure is *both* a Rodin Coil pair (in Marko's framework) *and* a counter-wound toroidal arrangement (in mainstream RF). The two framings describe the same physical object. The behaviour you'll measure is determined by the geometry, regardless of which framing you use to describe it.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> Useful framing for talking to an RF engineer. *The Rodin Coil is a toroidal coil with a specific cycle-following winding pattern; a Rodin Coil pair is a counter-wound toroidal arrangement using mirror-direction Rodin windings.* That sentence places the Rodin Coil in mainstream vocabulary. An RF engineer will recognise the structure and may have specific suggestions about what to measure."
