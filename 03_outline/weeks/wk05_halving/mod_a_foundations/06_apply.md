## 0. Authoring notes

## 4. Body (1:00–4:30)

> "When you halve a quantity in everyday life, the Halving Circuit places the result on the Symbol. Three examples.
>
> *Example one — recipe.* You're halving a recipe that calls for 200 grams of flour. Halve: 100 grams. Reduce 100: *1 + 0 + 0 = 1*. Reduce the original 200: *2 + 0 + 0 = 2*. So *2 halved is 1* on the Symbol. Trace from vertex 2 to vertex 1 — that's one step on the Halving Circuit.
>
> *Example two — distance.* You're halving a distance: 100 km becomes 50 km. Reduce 100: 1. Reduce 50: 5. So *1 halved is 5* on the Symbol — wait, that's not what we computed. Let me re-check.
>
> *[pause — important moment]*
>
> Actually, halving works on the *digital roots*. Reduce 100 km to its digital root first: 1. Halve 1: in standard arithmetic, 0.5 — but on the Symbol, 1 × 5 = 5. So *halving 1 lands on 5*. Verify: 50 km has digital root 5. Match.
>
> *Example three — time.* 60 minutes halved is 30 minutes. Reduce 60: 6. Reduce 30: 3. *Halving 6 lands on 3* — and indeed, *6 × 5 = 30 → 3*. Match.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> So the rule is: reduce both the original and the halved quantity to digital roots. The halving operation on the Symbol takes the original's digital root to the halved digital root via *× 5*.
>
> One nuance. In ordinary arithmetic, halving an odd number gives a non-integer. On the Symbol, this isn't a problem — *halving = × 5*, and 5 × any integer is an integer. So the Symbol's halving is always defined, even where ordinary halving would yield fractions.
>
> *[pause — show the three examples again]*
>
> Three real-world halvings, three Symbol traces. The operation lives in your kitchen, on your map, on your clock.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> One use case. When you split a check at a restaurant — half each — the Symbol's halving traces the check's digital root to the half-share's digital root. Useful as a mental check: if your half-share's digital root doesn't match what you'd expect from the original, you've miscalculated. Same trick as digit-summing for verification, applied to halving."
