## 0. Authoring notes
First Mod-A lesson on the Halving Circuit. The student traces the same six vertices as the Doubling Circuit but in reverse: 1, 5, 7, 8, 4, 2, back to 1. Different colour pencil so the two traces are visually distinguishable on the same Symbol.

## 1. Outcome
By the end of these 6 minutes, the student has traced the Halving Circuit on the Symbol in a different colour from yesterday's doubling trace.

## 2. Hook (0:00–0:30)
*[On screen: the Symbol with the Doubling Circuit (red, from last week) and the Halving Circuit being traced in blue going the other way.]*

> "Same six numbers. Different direction. Different colour. Six minutes."

## 3. Setup (0:30–1:00)
> "Lens 1 of 9, Week 5. The Halving Circuit. The doubling cycle traversed in reverse."

## 4. Body (1:00–4:30)

> "Open your printed Symbol. Pick a different-coloured pencil from yesterday's. Blue, if doubling was red. Or whatever pair of colours you have.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> Place the pencil at 1.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> Move to 5. Notice — yesterday from 1 we went to 2. Today from 1 we go to 5. The 5 sits at the five-o'clock position, low and to the right.
>
> From 5, move to 7. The 7 is to the left and across.
>
> From 7, move to 8. The 8 is at the upper left.
>
> From 8, move to 4. The 4 is across, low and to the right.
>
> From 4, move to 2. The 2 is at the upper right.
>
> From 2, return to 1. Closed.
>
> *[pause — show both traces on the Symbol]*
>
> Look at what you've drawn. Two figure-eights on the same Symbol. The doubling figure (red) traces *1 → 2 → 4 → 8 → 7 → 5 → 1*. The halving figure (blue) traces *1 → 5 → 7 → 8 → 4 → 2 → 1*.
>
> The two figures are *the same shape* — the same lemniscate, the same crossing, the same two lobes. Drawn in opposite directions. The doubling traverses one way; the halving traverses the other.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> The same six vertices appear in both circuits — *1, 2, 4, 5, 7, 8*. The same six skipped vertices — *3, 6, 9*. The doubling and halving aren't different patterns; they're the same pattern, two directions.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> Why call it *halving*? Yesterday, doubling was the rule *take a number, double it, reduce if needed, land on the next vertex*. Today, halving is the rule *take a number, halve it, reduce if needed, land on the next vertex*. Halving 1 gives 0.5 — but we don't allow fractions on the Symbol. So we use a trick we'll prove formally tomorrow: *halving on the Symbol is the same as multiplying by 5*. Five times one is five. Five times five is twenty-five, reduce to seven. Five times seven is thirty-five, reduce to eight. Five times eight is forty, reduce to four. Five times four is twenty, reduce to two. Five times two is ten, reduce to one. The cycle closes.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> So *halving* and *multiplying by 5* are the same operation on the Symbol. Two names, one thing. Tomorrow Mod B proves the equivalence in two lines."
