## 4. Body (1:00–4:30)

> "The doubling-halving pair is one instance of a pattern that runs throughout mathematics. Three more:
>
> *Inverse pair one — multiplicative inverses in real numbers.* Every nonzero real number *x* has an inverse *1/x* such that *x · (1/x) = 1*. The pair is the *operation and its undoing*. Without inverses, division wouldn't work.
>
> *Inverse pair two — additive inverses.* Every real number *x* has an additive inverse *−x* such that *x + (−x) = 0*. Same pattern: an element and its undoing under the operation. Subtraction is *adding the additive inverse*; division is *multiplying by the multiplicative inverse*.
>
> *Inverse pair three — matrix inverses.* For a square matrix *A* (when one exists), there's a matrix *A⁻¹* such that *A · A⁻¹ = I*, the identity matrix. Used everywhere from solving linear equations to rotating coordinate systems.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> The pattern: *operation, inverse operation, identity*. Find an operation. Find its inverse. Their composition gives back the identity. Three pieces. They appear together because they make the operation *invertible* — a closed, well-defined undoing.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> Marko's claim, applied here: *an operation with an inverse is more fundamental than one without*. The doubling cycle on the Symbol has an inverse (halving). That makes it a *good candidate* for describing a fundamental physical process. Operations that can't be inverted — like burning, like dissolving — describe non-fundamental, second-law-of-thermodynamics-affected processes. Operations that *can* be inverted describe what physics calls *time-reversible* dynamics — the regime of conservation.
>
> So when you wind a Rodin Coil in Q3 and observe its electromagnetic behaviour, you're measuring a system whose underlying mathematical structure is reversible. The reversibility isn't a small fact; it's the core of why Marko thinks the cycle is fundamental.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> One useful exercise. Identify three reversible operations in your daily life — folding/unfolding, opening/closing, packing/unpacking. Each one has an inverse. Each one represents a conservative pair. Mathematics' inverses are the abstract version of this everyday pattern."
