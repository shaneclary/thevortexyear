## 4. Body (1:00–4:30)

> "The Doubling Circuit and the Halving Circuit aren't two cycles. They're one cycle, traversed in two directions. The mathematical structure that makes this true is *inverse group automorphisms*.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> An automorphism of a group is a function from the group to itself that preserves the group operation. Multiplication-by-2 in `(ℤ/9ℤ)*` is one. Multiplication-by-5 is another. Both move every element to another element while preserving the multiplicative structure.
>
> Two automorphisms are *inverses* if applying them in succession returns every element to itself. *(× 2) ∘ (× 5) = × 10 ≡ × 1 = identity (mod 9)*. So multiplication-by-2 and multiplication-by-5 are inverse automorphisms.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> The visual consequence. If you trace 2's orbit forwards from 1, you walk through 2, 4, 8, 7, 5, back to 1. If you trace 5's orbit forwards from 1, you walk through 5, 7, 8, 4, 2, back to 1. The second sequence is the first read backwards. Inverse automorphisms produce mirror traversals of the same cycle.
>
> *[pause — show both cycles as one bidirectional path]*
>
> The Doubling Circuit and the Halving Circuit are the same cycle. The doubling automorphism walks it one way; the halving automorphism walks it the other. The cycle's underlying structure — the six-element multiplicative group `(ℤ/9ℤ)*` — doesn't have a *forward* or *backward* direction; either traversal is equally valid.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> One observation worth carrying. In a cyclic group of order *n*, an element *g* and its inverse *g⁻¹* always generate the group in opposite directions if either generates it at all. So the doubling-halving relationship isn't unique to mod 9; it's a general property of cyclic groups. What's specific to mod 9 is *which* multipliers play the role of generators (2 and 5)."
