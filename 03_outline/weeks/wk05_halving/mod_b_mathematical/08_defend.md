## 0. Authoring notes
Fourteenth Defend lesson. The objection: *the proof is one line. Why does the Halving Circuit deserve a separate week of treatment if the math is just multiplicative inverses?* Honest answer: the same applies to most of the Symbol's structure — the underlying algebra is short to state, but the visualisation, application, and cross-domain implications take time to build out. The week's worth isn't the proof; it's everything around the proof.

## 4. Body (1:00–4:30)

> "The objection. *The proof that halving = ×5 is one equation: 2 × 5 = 10 ≡ 1 (mod 9). The Halving Circuit is just multiplication by the inverse of 2. Why does this content deserve a full week — three modules, twenty-seven lessons — when the algebra fits on a single line?*
>
> The objection is fair. The algebraic content of the week is small.
>
> Here's why the week is well-spent anyway.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> *First — the visualisation isn't algebra.* The Halving Circuit traced on the Symbol — the mirror lemniscate from yesterday's Mod-A trace — is a *visual* fact about the inverse pair. The algebra tells you the pair exists; the trace tells you what the pair *looks like*. By Wk 33 your hand will have traced both circuits a hundred times in copper. The visualisation pays off in coil winding.
>
> *Second — the inverse-as-completing-pair framing isn't algebra.* Lens 5 of this module sat with the difference between *opposite* and *inverse*. That's a conceptual distinction with implications across the year — the reversibility of the cycle, the conservation framing, the time-symmetry connection. Algebra doesn't unpack the framing; spending a contemplative beat on it does.
>
> *Third — the cross-context applications.* Lens 6 placed the doubling-halving pair next to additive inverses, function composition inverses, and matrix inverses. The student leaves seeing the doubling-halving pair as one instance of a universal pattern. That perspective doesn't fall out of the proof.
>
> *Fourth — the engineering use cases.* Mod-C will cover halving in coil testing — using the inverse property to verify measurements, to design counter-wound coils, to interpret bench data. The applications are practical and don't follow from the proof alone.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> So the calibrated answer to *the math is one line, why a week?*:
>
> *The proof is one line. The visualisation, the framing as completing-pair vs. opposite, the cross-context generalisation, and the engineering applications are not. The week makes a small piece of algebra into a tool you can use; that's the trade.*
>
> Forty-three words.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> One nuance. The course's structure is deliberately spiral — small algebraic facts get multiple weeks of attention because the *uses* of the facts compound across the year. The Halving Circuit's one-line proof is leveraged in Wk 6 (polar pairs as antipodes), Wk 8 (the 3-6-9 axis), Wk 18 (helical doubling on the torus), and Wk 27 (★ revisit). Investing a week now pays in nine weeks later."
