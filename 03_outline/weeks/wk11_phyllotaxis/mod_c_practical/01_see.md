## 4. Body (1:00–4:30)

> "Phyllotactic patterns are abundant in nature. A short tour of where to find them.
>
> *Sunflowers.* The seed head is the canonical example. Two interlocking sets of spirals at Fibonacci ratios.
>
> *Pinecones.* Open or closed, the scales follow phyllotactic spirals. Closed cones are easier to count; the spirals are tightly packed.
>
> *Pineapples.* The hexagonal scales follow three sets of spirals: 5, 8, and 13.
>
> *Romanesco broccoli.* The fractal-like green vegetable; each cone has 13 visible spirals at every scale.
>
> *Succulents and cacti.* Aloe, agave, barrel cactus — leaves and ribs in phyllotactic arrangement.
>
> *Tree branches.* Many trees have leaves at 137.5° divergence; the trunk's branching follows similar patterns.
>
> *Ferns.* The fronds and the unfurling crozier (fiddlehead) have phyllotactic structure.
>
> *Flower heads.* Daisies, asters, dandelions — composite flowers all show phyllotactic arrangement.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> Most of these are easy to find. Many supermarkets sell pineapples and romanesco; gardens have sunflowers; parks have pinecones; succulents grow as houseplants.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> Photograph one this week. Count the spirals. Note the count. Reduce to digital roots. Place on the Symbol.
>
> Build a small *photo archive* of phyllotactic plants you've encountered. Each photo is a data point — a real-world instance of the math you've been studying.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> One observation. Once you've trained your eye to see phyllotaxis, you'll see it *everywhere*. Most students who do this exercise report that natural pattern-recognition shifts; they can no longer look at a sunflower without counting spirals.
>
> The world is full of Fibonacci. The Symbol's structure isn't an exotic mathematical object; it's the everyday geometry of growth."
