## 0. Authoring notes
Plant identification by spiral counts.

## 1. Outcome
Student can identify a plant species or growth stage by counting its phyllotactic spirals.

## 2. Hook (0:00–0:30)
*[Three plants with their spiral counts: 5/8 pinecone, 21/34 sunflower, 34/55 large sunflower.]*

> "Count the spirals. Identify the plant. Six minutes."

## 3. Setup (0:30–1:00)
> "Lens 6 of 9. Apply phyllotaxis."

## 4. Body (1:00–4:30)

> "Plants have characteristic *spiral counts* — the number of clockwise and counter-clockwise spirals visible. The counts identify the plant species or maturity stage.
>
> *Pinecones.* Most species: 8 clockwise spirals and 13 counter-clockwise (or 5/8, depending on size). The spiral counts let you confirm species or note unusual growth patterns.
>
> *Sunflowers.* Standard varieties: 21/34. Larger seed heads (taller, well-fed plants): 34/55 or even 55/89. Smaller heads (younger or stressed plants): 13/21. Counting tells you the plant's maturity and growth conditions.
>
> *Pineapples.* Three sets of spirals: 5, 8, 13. All Fibonacci.
>
> *Cacti and succulents.* Variable. Aloe vera: 5 spirals. Larger barrel cacti: 13 or 21.
>
> *Romanesco broccoli.* Famously: 13 visible spirals at any zoom level (fractal-like).
>
> *[pause]*
>
> Practical pattern recognition. When you encounter a spiral plant, count both directions of spirals. The counts will be consecutive Fibonacci numbers. The numbers tell you the plant's maturity and species.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> Use case for VBM students. Take a photograph of any spiral plant. Count the spirals. Note the digital roots of the counts.
>
> *21 → 3. 34 → 7.* Pair: 3 and 7.
>
> *13 → 4. 21 → 3.* Pair: 4 and 3.
>
> *34 → 7. 55 → 1.* Pair: 7 and 1.
>
> The pair of digital roots traces a *position on the Symbol* — typically two of the family-1 vertices ({1, 4, 7}) or pairs spanning families.
>
> Marko's framing reads these pair-positions as *encoding the plant's specific geometry on the Symbol*. The plant is at *that* position; the Symbol's mathematics applies *here* in this specific way.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> Practical observation. Most students who try this find the digital-root-pairs of plant phyllotaxis cluster around family-1 vertices. Whether this clustering is meaningful or an artefact of Fibonacci's digital-root structure is contested. The clustering is real; the interpretation is open.
>
> The next time you see a sunflower, count its spirals. Reduce. Place the pair on the Symbol. The plant is at that vertex."
