## 4. Body (1:00–4:30) — *contemplative*

> *(slow)* "A sunflower grows. Each new seed appears at the centre of the seed head. The plant rotates the position by about 137.5° before placing the next.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> The plant doesn't *calculate* this angle. It doesn't have a notebook. It doesn't know what 137.5° is.
>
> *[long pause]*
>
> But it does it. Reliably. Across thousands of generations. The angle is built into the genetic and developmental machinery of the plant.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> What does that *mean*?
>
> *[long pause — 12 seconds]*
>
> One reading: the angle is *useful for the plant*. Optimal packing means more seeds, more sunlight, more reproductive success. Evolution selected for the angle. The plant has it because plants without it lost out over geological time.
>
> Another reading: the angle is *physically forced*. The genetics are merely an implementation; the geometry is what produces the optimum, and any successful packing system would converge on the same angle. The plant is *expressing* a mathematical fact about packing optimisation, not *computing* it.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> Marko's framing — *plants are natural Rodin coils* — pushes the second reading. The plant isn't doing math; it's *being math*. The spiral is the plant's way of fulfilling a geometric optimum that exists independently of biology.
>
> *[long pause]*
>
> Sit with that for a moment. The mathematics you've been studying is also the structure of the plant in the garden. The same Fibonacci numbers. The same 137.5°. The same digital-root 24-cycle. The same optimum.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> Plants have been doing the math for half a billion years. Marko has drawn it. The plant has been *living* it.
>
> *[long pause]*
>
> The Symbol is in your notebook. The Symbol is also in the sunflower. The Symbol is in the pinecone. The Symbol is in the succulent on your windowsill, if you have one.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> The math doesn't stay in the notebook. It moves through everything that grows."
