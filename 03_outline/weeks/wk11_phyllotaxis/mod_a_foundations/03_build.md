## 4. Body (1:00–4:30)

*[Camera close on hand drawing.]*

> "Open notebook. Draw a circle, perhaps 80 mm diameter. We'll fill it with a phyllotactic spiral.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> Step 1 — mark the centre. Place a small dot.
>
> Step 2 — first seed. Draw a small dot a short distance from centre.
>
> Step 3 — second seed. Rotate from the first by ≈137.5° around the centre. Move slightly further from centre. Draw a dot.
>
> Step 4 — third seed. Rotate another 137.5°. Move slightly further out. Draw a dot.
>
> Continue. Each dot is rotated 137.5° from the previous and slightly further from centre.
>
> *[long pause for the student to draw 20-30 seeds]*
>
> Look at what's emerging. As you place more seeds, you'll see *spirals* form — sets of dots that curve in one direction, and another set curving in the other direction. The spirals appeared without your drawing them; they're an *emergent* property of the rotation-and-distance rule.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> The number of spirals visible in each direction will be Fibonacci numbers — typically 5 and 8 for a 20-30 seed sketch, or 8 and 13 for 50-100 seeds.
>
> The spirals' counts depend on the exact divergence angle. *137.5°* is the golden-ratio-derived angle: *360° × (1 - 1/φ) ≈ 137.5°*.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> Why does this work? Because *137.5°* is *irrational with respect to 360°* — successive seeds never land at exactly the same angular position. They distribute as evenly as possible around the circle, producing tight packing.
>
> Marko's claim links this *optimal-packing rotation* to the digital-root structure of Fibonacci, which connects to the Symbol's 24-cycle. The plant's growth pattern and the Symbol's structure share a period.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> Date the page. *Phyllotactic spiral sketched.* Build log."
