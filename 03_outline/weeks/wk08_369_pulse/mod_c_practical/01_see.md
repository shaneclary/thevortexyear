## 4. Body (1:00–4:30)

> "The 3-6-9 vertices on the Symbol — the third family — sit on the torus surface at three specific positions. When you wind a Rodin Coil's six-strand pair-build, those three vertices are *not visited* by any of the six strands (which trace the doubling cycle through {1, 2, 4, 5, 7, 8}).
>
> But you can add a *control wire* that passes through the 3, 6, and 9 positions on the form. This wire isn't part of the doubling cycle's helix; it's a separate physical element threading through the spaces the doubling cycle leaves alone.
>
> Marko calls this wire the *trigger axis*. Powell uses the same term in builder contexts.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> What does the trigger axis do? Two practical roles.
>
> *Role one — secondary winding for control signals.* Drive the trigger axis with a different signal than the main coil. The two interact electromagnetically. Some build configurations use the trigger axis to *modulate* the main coil's behaviour at family-three frequencies (multiples of 3).
>
> *Role two — pickup for measurement.* The trigger axis can be configured as a *sensor* rather than a driver — a separate winding that picks up specific field components without disturbing the main coil. Useful for non-invasive measurement during a build's operation.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> The trigger axis is *optional* for most builds. The Wk-33 single-strand and Wk-35 polar-pair builds don't include it. It becomes relevant in advanced builds that explore the 3-6-9 family's role explicitly — typically Wk-35's optional family-phased build or research-scale rigs.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> One observation. The trigger axis sits *along* the family-three triangle on the torus surface, but its physical implementation is a single wire passing through three points. The wire isn't a triangle; it's a polygon-path or a series of three independent terminations, depending on the build.
>
> Builders typically implement the trigger axis as three short copper rods or a thin wire stitched through the form at the three positions. The geometry is straightforward; the engineering use depends on what you're testing."
