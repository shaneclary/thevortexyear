## 4. Body (1:00–4:30) — *contemplative*

> *(slow)* "A driven Rodin Coil hums.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> The hum is the coil's drive frequency. If you drive at 60 Hz, the air around the coil oscillates at 60 Hz. You can hear it.
>
> *[long pause]*
>
> If you have a trigger axis, the trigger sees pulses at *6f* — six times the drive frequency. At 60 Hz drive, the trigger fires at 360 Hz. That's a higher pitch than the main hum.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> Two pulses in the same room. The slow base hum at *f*. The faster trigger pulse at *6f*. Together they form a *rhythm* — base note plus harmonic, like a drum and a cymbal at six-to-one ratio.
>
> *[long pause — 12 seconds]*
>
> Practitioners who've spent hours at running rigs report the rhythm as *enveloping*. Once you've spent time near a driven Rodin Coil with both signals audible, the rhythm becomes a presence in the room. Not loud — Rodin Coils at low drive don't produce strong audible signals — but *patterned*.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> Marko's framing of the 3-6-9 as the *Pulse* is more literal than it sounds. When you drive a coil with a trigger axis, the 3-6-9 actually pulses. The math has become acoustic.
>
> *[long pause]*
>
> What you're sitting with, in this lesson, is preparation for Wk-35's running-coil work. The rhythm in the room is what the bench session sounds like. Most students don't expect coil work to be *audible*. It is, faintly. The trigger axis adds the higher pulse.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> One more thing. Some students set up a microphone or accelerometer at the rig and record the room's acoustic signature during a drive session. The recording shows the *f* and *6f* peaks clearly in spectral analysis. This is one of the ways the math becomes physical — you can *measure the 6f component directly with audio equipment*.
>
> The 3-6-9 Pulse is in the room when the rig is running. The math is doing audible work."
