## 4. Body (1:00–4:30)

> "The 3-6-9 structure maps onto traditional meditation rhythms — three states with a stillness phase. Use it as a focus aid.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> *Application one — breath rhythm.* Inhale for a count of 3. Exhale for a count of 6. Pause for a count of 9. Repeat.
>
> The ratio is 1:2:3 — inhale:exhale:pause. The pause is the longest phase. The breath itself is the shorter; the stillness between breaths is the longer. This is the *physiological* mapping of 3-6-9 onto a breath cycle.
>
> Practitioners report the long pause produces a deeper meditative state than equal-phase breathing. The mapping isn't required for VBM study; it's offered as one way to embody the trinary structure if you find it useful.
>
> *Application two — attention rhythm.* Three minutes of focus on a problem or object. Six minutes of related work or thought. Nine minutes of pause — letting the mind rest, sitting with the stillness.
>
> Some students use this rhythm for studying VBM material itself. Three minutes reviewing a lesson. Six minutes drawing or computing. Nine minutes sitting with what was learned without pushing forward. The rhythm prevents over-effort and gives integration time.
>
> *Application three — counting practice.* When learning to draw the Symbol or trace the doubling cycle, pace your practice in 3-6-9 ratios. Three slow traces. Six fast traces. Nine seconds (or longer) of stillness between sets. The rhythm becomes part of the embodied practice.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> Important note. *None of these applications is required for the math.* The 3-6-9 is mathematically real regardless of whether you use it contemplatively. These applications are offered for students who find the contemplative dimension of the work useful — not imposed on those who don't.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> Marko's framing of the 3-6-9 as the *Pulse* and the 9 as *Spirit* aligns with these applications. He treats the math and the contemplative practice as continuous — the same structure operates in both registers. Other practitioners separate them. Both approaches are valid; the course teaches the math whether or not you engage the contemplative side."
