## 4. Body (1:00–4:30)

> "Compute the action of doubling on each member of the 3-6-9 family.
>
> *Element 3.* *3 × 2 = 6.* Single digit. Doubling sends 3 to 6.
>
> *Element 6.* *6 × 2 = 12.* Reduce: *1 + 2 = 3.* Doubling sends 6 to 3.
>
> *Element 9.* *9 × 2 = 18.* Reduce: *1 + 8 = 9.* Doubling sends 9 to 9.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> Three results. Two patterns.
>
> *3 → 6 → 3 → 6 → ...* The 3-6 oscillation. A two-cycle of length 2.
>
> *9 → 9 → 9 → ...* The 9 fixed point. A self-loop of length 1.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> Try iterated doublings.
>
> *3, then 2·3 = 6, then 2·6 = 3, then 2·3 = 6, then 2·6 = 3, ...* The sequence alternates 3, 6, 3, 6, 3, 6, ...
>
> *9, then 9, then 9, ...* Constant.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> What's distinctive about this behaviour? Compared to the doubling circuit's six-cycle (six elements, period 6), the 3-6-9 family has *short cycle lengths*: period 2 for {3, 6}, period 1 for {9}. The 3-6-9 family is *kinematically simple* — the motion under doubling is minimal.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> Why does this happen? Algebraically, 3, 6, 9 are *non-units* in `(ℤ/9ℤ)*` because they share factor 3 with 9. Multiplying any of them by 2 keeps them as non-units. So they stay within the non-unit set {0, 3, 6}. Among non-units, doubling produces only the small cycles we observed.
>
> The math is forced. Non-units have shorter cycles than units under multiplicative action.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> One more observation. *The two cycles in the 3-6-9 family have a structural relationship*. The two-cycle's elements (3, 6) sum to 9. So the 3-6 oscillation is *between additive complements* in mod 9. Each pulse from 3 to 6 takes you from 3 to its complement. Then back. The pulse is *additive-inverse-pair oscillation*."
