## 4. Body (1:00–4:30)

> "The set {3, 6, 9} consists of the *non-units* of ℤ/9ℤ — elements that share a factor with 9 (namely 3). They form the *complement* of `(ℤ/9ℤ)*` in `ℤ/9ℤ`.
>
> Doubling acts on {3, 6, 9}. The orbit:
>
> *3 → 6 → 3 → 6 → ...* (period 2)
>
> *9 → 9 → 9 → ...* (period 1)
>
> The set decomposes into two orbits: a *2-cycle* on {3, 6} and a *fixed point* at {9}.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> Why does doubling stay within the non-units? Because the non-units form an *ideal* of ℤ/9ℤ — closed under multiplication by any element. Specifically, {0, 3, 6} = 3ℤ/9ℤ is the *principal ideal* generated by 3. Multiplying anything in this ideal by anything in ℤ/9ℤ gives another element of the ideal.
>
> So 2 × 3 = 6 (still in {0, 3, 6}). 2 × 6 = 12 ≡ 3 (still). 2 × 0 = 0 (still). The doubling map preserves the non-unit set because the set is an ideal.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> Visual orbit. Picture the Symbol with three components highlighted: the six-cycle on the units (Doubling Circuit), the two-cycle on {3, 6}, and the fixed point at {9}. Three components, no inter-component motion. The doubling map is *block-diagonal* — it acts independently within each component.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> One observation worth carrying. The orbit *length* of an element under doubling depends on its membership:
>
> - Units: orbit of 1 (the identity) has length 1; other units have orbit of length 6 (full cycle), 3, or 2.
> - Non-units: orbit of 0 (= 9) has length 1; orbits of 3, 6 have length 2.
>
> The non-unit orbits are *short*. The unit orbits include longer cycles. This is a general phenomenon: multiplicative orbits on ideals tend to be shorter than orbits on units.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> The 3-6-9 Pulse, mathematically, is the *block-diagonal action of doubling on the non-unit ideal of ℤ/9ℤ*. Two orbits: a 2-cycle and a fixed point. Standard finite-group theory."
