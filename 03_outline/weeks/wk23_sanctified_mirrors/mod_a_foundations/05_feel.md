## 4. Body (1:00–4:30) — *contemplative*

> *(slow)* "Some images reward sustained attention. Most don't. *A photograph of a coffee cup doesn't change much when you look at it for ten minutes.* Other images do — *fractals, mandalas, certain artworks, certain mathematical diagrams*.
>
> The Sanctified Mirrors, as Marko frames them, are *images that reward sustained attention*. The structure is rich enough that the eye keeps finding new details over time.
>
> *[long pause — 12 seconds]*
>
> Sit with the practice.
>
> *[long pause]*
>
> *Sustained attention to structured images is an ancient practice.* Tibetan Buddhist mandala meditation. Hindu yantra contemplation. Christian icon prayer. Islamic geometric-pattern study. *Many traditions use structured images as contemplative aids.*
>
> Marko's *Sanctified Mirrors* tradition draws on this lineage — applying contemplative-image practice to VBM's specific structures.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> Three contemplations on contemplative-image practice.
>
> *Contemplation one — the eye as instrument.* In contemplative-image practice, *the eye becomes an instrument of attention*. Slow, careful looking. Following lines. Noticing relationships. *The eye does work that the analytical mind can't do alone.*
>
> Some practitioners find this surprising. *Looking carefully at a structured image is more demanding than reading a text.* The eye gets tired, then settles, then gets tired again. *Cycles of attention*.
>
> *Contemplation two — the image teaches.* Sitting with a Sanctified Mirror, you notice things you didn't notice at first. *The image's structure teaches itself to the patient viewer.* You don't have to extract; the image gives.
>
> Some students find this *trustworthy*. They don't have to *make* the contemplation produce results; they just *attend*, and the image responds.
>
> *Contemplation three — practice changes the practitioner.* Over weeks of contemplating the same Mirror, *the practitioner changes*. Not the Mirror. Memory of the Mirror's structure deepens. Sensitivity to its features sharpens. *The image is the same; the relationship deepens.*
>
> *[long pause — 12 seconds]*
>
> What does sitting with a Sanctified Mirror feel like?
>
> Some students notice *quiet*. The image doesn't demand response. *Sustained attention is restful*, not effortful, when the image rewards it.
>
> Some students notice *productivity-bias-uncomfortable*. *Just looking* feels like wasted time when used to deliverables. *Contemplative practice is non-productive by design*; that's part of what makes it valuable.
>
> Some students notice *integration*. The math you've learned (Q1, Q2 to date) starts to *show up in the image*. The doubling cycle is recognisable; the family-3 structure is recognisable; the antipodes are recognisable. *Math becomes visual; visual becomes felt.*
>
> All three are honest.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> A practical observation. *Contemplative-image practice has a learning curve.* Initial sessions feel awkward. After 20-30 sessions over weeks, the practice settles. *Don't judge by the first session.*
>
> If you find the practice congenial, *establish a regular Sanctified-Mirror practice*. Five to fifteen minutes per session. *Daily or several times a week.*
>
> If you don't find it congenial, *engage the math content without committing to the contemplative practice.* The math stays the same; the contemplative practice is one of several engagement modes.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> One more contemplation. *Marko's framing of mirrors as portals* (Lens 4) emphasises that the practice can be *transformative*. Whether transformation occurs depends on practitioner and practice depth. *The framework allows for it; doesn't require it.*
>
> Hold your sketched Mirror from Lens 3. Look at it for sixty seconds. Notice what you notice."
