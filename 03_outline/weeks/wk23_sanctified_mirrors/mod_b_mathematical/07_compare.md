## 4. Body (1:00–4:30)

> "Shadows are *familiar everyday projections*. Mathematical projections are *formal versions of the same concept*. Today's compare connects them.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> *Shadow (everyday).*
>
> A 3D object blocks light from a source; the unblocked region around it forms a shadow on a 2D surface. *The shadow is the 2D outline of the 3D object as seen from the light source's perspective.*
>
> Common features:
>
> *Light source determines the projection direction.*
>
> *2D shadow is some kind of silhouette of the 3D object.*
>
> *Information is lost in the shadow* (depth, internal structure, surface details).
>
> *[pause]*
>
> *Mathematical projection (formal).*
>
> A map π: X → Y from a higher-dim space X to a lower-dim space Y. The projection direction is *specified by the choice of map*; multiple maps give multiple projections.
>
> Common features:
>
> *Map specifies the projection.*
>
> *Lower-dim image is some kind of summary of higher-dim source.*
>
> *Information is lost; the loss pattern is determined by the map.*
>
> *[pause]*
>
> Three structural similarities.
>
> *Similarity one — both are higher-to-lower-dim.* Both shadows and formal projections reduce dimensions.
>
> *Similarity two — both lose information.* Each forgets some aspects of the source.
>
> *Similarity three — multiple shadows / projections from one source.* Move the light; get a different shadow. Pick a different projection map; get a different image.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> Three structural differences.
>
> *Difference one — formal precision.* Mathematical projections specify *exactly* which coordinates are kept and which forgotten. Shadows are *approximate* — the silhouette is determined by the source's geometry plus the light direction, not by a coordinate map.
>
> *Difference two — generality.* Mathematical projections work for *any* dimensions and *any* spaces. Shadows are typically 3D-to-2D. *Formal projections generalise; shadows don't (without abstraction).*
>
> *Difference three — invertibility.* Mathematical projections are typically *not invertible* (you can't reconstruct the source from the projection). Shadows have similar non-invertibility but with extra ambiguity from light-physics. *Both are lossy; the formal version is precise about what's lost.*
>
> *[pause]*
>
> *How shadows generalise to higher-dim.*
>
> A *5D object* casting a *4D shadow* on a *4D screen* generalises the everyday concept. *We can't visualise this directly* (we don't have 4D vision), but the math is well-defined.
>
> Sanctified Mirrors, in this framing, are *2D shadows of higher-dim VBM structures cast onto 2D screens*. The shadow metaphor maps directly.
>
> *Useful pedagogical bridge.* Shadows are familiar; explaining Sanctified Mirrors as *higher-dim shadows* leverages the familiarity.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> *What this comparison gives us.*
>
> *Gives 1.* A familiar metaphor for explaining Sanctified Mirrors to students.
>
> *Gives 2.* A reminder that information loss is *expected*, not a failure of the framework. *Every projection loses information; choosing what to lose is what makes projections useful.*
>
> *Gives 3.* A connection to mainstream geometry — projections are studied extensively in mathematical research. *VBM's Mirror practice connects to a substantial mathematical literature.*
>
> *[pause]*
>
> Useful framing. *Sanctified Mirrors are formal mathematical projections that function like 'shadows' of higher-dim VBM structures. The shadow metaphor is pedagogically useful; the formal projection theorem is mathematically precise. Both framings refer to the same underlying mathematical concept.*
>
> Practical takeaway. *Use the shadow metaphor when teaching Sanctified Mirrors to non-mathematicians.* Use the projection theorem when teaching mathematicians. *Match vocabulary to audience; the underlying concept is the same.*"
