## 4. Body (1:00–4:30)

> "Today we examine *how Sanctified Mirrors function in teaching contexts*. The Mirror's pedagogical role is one of its strongest defendable contributions to VBM practice.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> *Three teaching contexts.*
>
> *Context 1 — workshop (group teaching).* A teacher (Marko, Powell, or other) leads 5-30 students through VBM material. Mirrors printed on large posters; pinned to walls or projected onto screens. Teacher points at specific Mirror features while explaining concepts.
>
> *Mirror role in workshop.* Visual anchor. *All eyes on one image.* Reduces ambiguity about what's being discussed.
>
> *Context 2 — one-on-one teaching.* A teacher works with one student. Mirrors printed at desk-size. Both consult the Mirror together; teacher annotates with pen as concepts are introduced.
>
> *Mirror role in one-on-one.* Working surface. Builds shared visual vocabulary specific to that teacher-student pair.
>
> *Context 3 — self-teaching.* Solo practitioner studies VBM via books, videos, and Mirrors. Mirrors printed personally; placed in workspace.
>
> *Mirror role in self-teaching.* Memory anchor and self-orientation. *Returning to the same image* across study sessions builds familiarity.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> *What the Mirror enables in each context.*
>
> *Enable 1 — concept anchoring.* When a new concept is introduced, *finding it on the Mirror* gives students a memory hook. *Concept + visual location = better retention.*
>
> *Enable 2 — relationship visibility.* The Mirror shows *relationships between concepts* visually. Antipodes are physically opposite; family colours are adjacent; the doubling helix traces a specific path. *Visual relationships supplement verbal description.*
>
> *Enable 3 — review and re-engagement.* Returning to the same Mirror across sessions reinforces material. *The familiar image becomes a reliable reference point.*
>
> *Enable 4 — community shared vocabulary.* Multiple practitioners using the same canonical Mirrors share a visual language. *Discussions about the framework become more efficient.*
>
> *[pause]*
>
> *Five practical teaching uses.*
>
> *Use 1 — conceptual introduction.* Show the Mirror at the beginning of a lesson. 'Today we'll discuss this — see the central triangle here.'
>
> *Use 2 — interactive pointing.* During discussion, point at specific Mirror features. 'Notice this cycle here, this antipode there.'
>
> *Use 3 — student exercise.* Ask students to find specific features on the Mirror. 'Where's the doubling cycle on this Mirror?'
>
> *Use 4 — comparison.* Show two Mirrors side-by-side. 'Compare these two projections; what differs?'
>
> *Use 5 — synthesis.* At the end of a lesson, return to the Mirror to summarise what was covered.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> Three observations.
>
> *Observation one.* The Mirror's pedagogical use is *recognisable as standard teaching practice*. Visual aids in technical teaching are widespread. *VBM's specific practice fits this lineage.*
>
> *Observation two.* The Mirror is *more useful when carefully designed*. Sloppy or poorly-printed Mirrors don't support the same kinds of pointing and pattern recognition. *Quality matters.*
>
> *Observation three.* Mirrors are *especially useful for visual learners*. Some students engage VBM more readily through visual content than through verbal or mathematical content. *Mirror practice is one of VBM's accommodations to learner diversity.*
>
> *[pause]*
>
> Practical takeaway. *If you teach VBM (formally or informally), use Mirrors as visual aids.* The pedagogical benefits are real and well-established."
