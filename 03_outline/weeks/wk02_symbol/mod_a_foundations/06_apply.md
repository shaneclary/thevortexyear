## 0. Authoring notes
The Symbol as a memory aid. Specifically: every concept in VBM has a placement on the Symbol, and once the student has the placements memorised, the Symbol becomes a navigation tool for the whole year. Walk through three concrete applications: the doubling circuit's path, the polar pairs' axes, the family groups' triads. Each one previewed; each one anchored to specific Symbol vertices.

## 1. Outcome
By the end of these 6 minutes, the student can name three ways the Symbol functions as a memory aid for VBM concepts.

## 2. Hook (0:00–0:30)
*[On screen: the Symbol, with three different overlays appearing in succession — a curve through 1-2-4-8-7-5; three coloured pairs; three coloured triads. Each overlay flashes briefly then disappears.]*

> "One drawing. Three overlays. Today: how the Symbol stores the year for you."

## 3. Setup (0:30–1:00)
> "Lens 6 of 9. The Apply lens shows what the concept *does*. Today: the Symbol as a memory aid. Three concrete uses — preview of the year — anchored to the drawing on your wall."

## 4. Body (1:00–4:30)

> "**Use one — the Doubling Circuit, traced.** Week 4 introduces the cycle 1-2-4-8-7-5. Once you've drawn it on the Symbol — a single curve from 1 down to 2, across to 4, around to 8, back across to 7, down to 5, returning to 1 — your hand has it forever. The Doubling Circuit isn't six numbers; it's a *figure* on the Symbol. After Week 4, when someone asks you what the Doubling Circuit is, you don't recite — you trace. The Symbol is the storage.
>
> **Use two — the Polar Pairs, marked.** Week 6 introduces the pairs (1, 8), (2, 7), (4, 5). Each pair sums to 9. Each pair sits on opposite sides of the Symbol, drawing a line through the centre. Once you've coloured the three pair-lines on your Symbol — three diameters, three colours — the structure is in your hand. Pairs are a fact about the *geometry* of the Symbol, not a fact you memorise from a list.
>
> **Use three — the Family Number Groups, tiled.** Week 7 introduces three triads — {1, 4, 7}, {2, 5, 8}, {3, 6, 9} — each spaced 120° apart on the Symbol. Once you've coloured the three triads, the partition becomes visible: three colours tile the Symbol cleanly. The Family Groups are also a geometric fact, not a memorised partition.
>
> *[pause — show the fully annotated Symbol with all three overlays]*
>
> Three uses. Three weeks of content, all stored on one drawing. By Week 26 your wall-hung Symbol could be annotated with: the Doubling Circuit traced, the polar pairs marked, the family groups coloured, the digit-root reduction direction noted, the placement of zero at 9 underlined. One drawing. Most of the first half of the curriculum, on one page.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> Marko, when he teaches in person, draws the Symbol once at the start of a session and refers back to it every few minutes. He doesn't draw a new diagram for each concept. He draws *on* the same one. The Symbol is the canvas. The curriculum is what you mark on it.
>
> Some students keep one *blank* Symbol on the wall and draw a fresh one each week with the new annotation. Others keep one Symbol and accumulate annotations on it across the year — by Week 52 it's covered. Both approaches work. The point is to use the drawing as a *map*, not as decoration."

## 5. Try it (4:30–5:30)
> "Pause. On your printed Symbol — the one on your wall — sketch lightly with pencil where you think the Doubling Circuit will go. From 1, to 2, to 4, to 8, to 7, to 5, back to 1. Don't worry about getting the line shape right. Just trace the path with your finger first, then with the pencil. We'll formalise it in Week 4. Sixty seconds."

*[Pause card on screen for 60 seconds.]*

> "Welcome back. The first overlay is on your wall now. Two more come over the next month."
