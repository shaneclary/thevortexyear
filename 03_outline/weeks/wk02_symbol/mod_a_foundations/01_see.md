Wk 02 — Theme: The Symbol of Enlightenment — Lens 1: See — Module A (Foundations)
Runtime: 6:00. Author: draft. Status: Draft.

## 0. Authoring notes
First lesson of Week 2. The student has been pinning the printed Symbol on a wall for a full week. Today they draw it themselves for the first time. Hands on the page. The Foundations lens for this theme leans visual-intuitive — no proof, no formal claims, just the act of drawing the diagram and watching it appear under your hand. Voice: direct, imperative where appropriate, no abstraction.

## 1. Outcome
By the end of these 6 minutes, the student has drawn the Symbol of Enlightenment by hand for the first time and can describe the placement convention from memory.

## 2. Hook (0:00–0:30)
*[On screen: a blank page. A pencil enters the frame. A circle is drawn. The numbers begin to appear, one at a time, slowly.]*

> "Notebook open. Pencil in hand. Today we draw the Symbol you've been looking at all week."

## 3. Setup (0:30–1:00)
> "Lens 1 of 9, Week 2. The first piece of math we open in your hand. Six minutes. By the end, the Symbol is no longer something on your wall — it's something you've drawn."

## 4. Body (1:00–4:30)

*[Camera close on a hand drawing. Slow enough that the student can keep up.]*

> "Open your notebook. Find a fresh page. We're going to draw the Symbol of Enlightenment from scratch.
>
> Step one. Draw a circle. Don't worry about perfect. A coin or the bottom of a mug works fine. The size matters less than the centre being clean.
>
> *[pause for the student to draw]*
>
> Step two. Find the top of the circle. Mark the number 9 there. Marko places 9 at the top, always. The Symbol has a fixed orientation — 9 sits at the highest point. Some traditions place numbers differently around a clock face; the Symbol is unusual in this convention. Hold to it.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> Step three. Imagine the circle as a clock. 9 sits where 12 would be. Now place 1, 2, 3, 4, 5, 6, 7, 8 around the circle, equally spaced, going clockwise. The 1 sits where the clock's 1 would be — a little to the right of 9. Then 2 to its right and down. Then 3 at roughly the clock's 3 position. Continue around. The 8 ends up just to the left of 9 at the top.
>
> *[pause for the student to fill in the numbers]*
>
> Step four. Look at your drawing. You should have a circle with nine numbers around it: 9 at the top, 1 through 8 spaced clockwise. That's the Symbol of Enlightenment. The most-drawn diagram in VBM. The diagram everything else hangs from.
>
> *[pause — show a finished example beside the student's drawing]*
>
> Notice what you've made. It looks like a clock with one number short — no 0, no 10, just 1 through 9. We'll talk about why in the next lesson; the short version is that VBM treats 9 the way arithmetic treats 0. The Symbol's placement isn't a stylistic choice; it's the consequence of how digit-root reduction works.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> One more thing. Date your drawing. Write today's date small in a corner. By Week 52 you'll have at least fifty-two of these dated drawings. Looking back at the first one — the one you're looking at right now — is part of the curriculum. Marko, when he describes his first drawing of the Symbol, says he was fifteen years old and drew it on a paper napkin. Yours is in your notebook. Same gesture, different paper."

## 5. Try it (4:30–5:30)
> "Pause. Look at your drawing. If the placement is rough — if 1 is too far from 9, if the spacing is uneven — don't fix it. The first one stays. Date it. Below it, write *first drawing*. We come back to it. Sixty seconds."

*[Pause card on screen for 60 seconds.]*

> "Welcome back. The first drawing is on your page. The hand has begun."

## 6. Bridge (5:30–6:00)
> "Next lesson — Count. We've drawn the Symbol. The next lesson asks the obvious question: why nine numbers and not ten? Where is 0? The answer turns the Symbol from a stylistic choice into a structural one."

*[Closing card: "Lesson 1 of 9 — See. Next: Count."]*
