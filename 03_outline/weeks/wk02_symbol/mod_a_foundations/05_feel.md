## 0. Authoring notes
The contemplative beat for Week 2. The question: *what does it mean to live with this drawing?* The Symbol now hangs on the student's wall (since Mod A wk 1) and lives in the notebook (since this week's Build). The lesson asks the student to notice their relationship to the drawing — without prescribing one. Slow pacing. No instruction.

## 1. Outcome
By the end of these 6 minutes, the student has spent quiet time with the question of how the Symbol now sits in their daily attention, and can write one sentence describing the relationship.

## 2. Hook (0:00–0:30)
*[On screen: a black field. The Symbol fades in slowly, small, centre. It stays there for the whole lesson.]*

> *(slowly)* "It's on your wall. It's in your notebook. You drew it three times yesterday. What is it becoming?"

## 3. Setup (0:30–1:00)
> "Lens 5 of 9. The contemplative beat. Six minutes. No new content. One question, held."

## 4. Body (1:00–4:30) — *contemplative, paced, sparse*

> *(slow)* "A week ago, the Symbol was a curiosity. Something you'd seen on the internet, maybe. A diagram associated with a name — Marko Rodin — that you'd encountered.
>
> *[long pause]*
>
> Now it's on your wall. You walk past it on the way to make coffee. It's in the corner of your eye when you sit at your desk. It's in your notebook on the page from yesterday and the day before.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> Notice. Just notice. Without judging.
>
> *[long pause — 12 seconds]*
>
> The Symbol is becoming part of your peripheral attention. Not the centre — you have other concerns. But it's *present*. The way a clock on a wall is present. The way a window facing east is present.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> Some things, when they enter peripheral attention, stay decorative. They don't change anything. They become invisible after a week.
>
> Some things, when they enter peripheral attention, start to *teach*. They wait. The mind, in idle moments, returns to them. Patterns the mind hadn't noticed begin to surface. The thing on the wall becomes a quiet co-thinker.
>
> *[long pause — 12 seconds]*
>
> The Symbol can do either. It depends on you. Marko, when he describes his fifteen-year-old self, says the Symbol *had* him for the rest of his life. Most people who walk past it don't experience that. The difference is attention given.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> Don't try to give it more attention than is honest right now. The course will earn the attention slowly. Today, just notice that the drawing has entered your space. A week ago it was elsewhere.
>
> *[long pause]*
>
> Take a breath. The drawing waits."

## 5. Try it (4:30–5:30)
> *(softer)* "In your notebook, on the page after the three Symbols, write one sentence. *Right now, the Symbol on my wall is ___.* Whatever's true. Decoration. Curiosity. Co-thinker. Companion. Mystery. Something else. Sixty seconds."

*[Pause card on screen for 60 seconds.]*

> "Welcome back. The sentence is yours. It will likely change. Mark the date."

## 6. Bridge (5:30–6:00)
> *(returning to ordinary tempo)* "Next lesson — Apply. We've sat with the question of what the Symbol is becoming. Next we look at what it does — specifically, how it works as a memory aid for the rest of the year's content. Lens 6."

*[Closing card: "Lesson 5 of 9 — Feel. Next: Apply."]*
