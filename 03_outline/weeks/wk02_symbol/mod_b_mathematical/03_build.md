## 1. Outcome

## 2. Hook (0:00–0:30)

> "A regular nine-sided polygon. Add labels. That's the Symbol, geometrically. Today we construct it."

## 3. Setup (0:30–1:00)
> "Lens 3 of 9. Mod A had us draw the Symbol freehand. Mod B has us construct it. Compass, straightedge, or graph paper. The vertex placement is no longer eyeball — it's geometry."

## 4. Body (1:00–4:30)

> "Open your notebook to a fresh page. You need a circle and a way to mark nine equal arcs around it. Three options:
>
> *Option one — compass and straightedge.* Draw a circle. Find its centre. Draw a vertical diameter. The intersection at the top is vertex 9. To get the other eight vertices, divide the circle's circumference into nine equal arcs. The classical construction for a regular nonagon is famously *not* possible with compass and straightedge alone — the nonagon is non-constructible. So we approximate, or use a protractor.
>
> *Option two — protractor.* Draw a circle. Mark the top — that's 9. From the centre, measure 40° clockwise. That marks the next vertex — 1. Continue at 40° intervals: 80° marks 2, 120° marks 3, and so on around. Nine vertices, equally spaced.
>
> *Option three — graph paper template.* Use the printed Symbol from your course materials as a template. Trace the vertex positions onto your notebook page.
>
> *[pause for the student to choose and execute]*
>
> Once the vertices are placed, label them. 9 at the top. 1 at 40° clockwise. 2 at 80°. 3 at 120°. 4 at 160°. 5 at 200°. 6 at 240°. 7 at 280°. 8 at 320°. Back to 9 at 360°.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> Connect the vertices around the rim if you want — that gives you the regular nonagon. Don't draw any internal connections yet. The Symbol's action diagrams — the doubling arrows, the polar pair lines, the family-group triangles — get added in later weeks. Today: just the labelled nonagon.
>
> *[pause — show a finished construction beside the student's]*
>
> The construction matters because it makes the placement *non-arbitrary*. You didn't put 1 there because it looked right; you put 1 there because the angle between 9 and 1 is 40° — which is 360° divided by 9, the natural angle for a regular nonagon. Every vertex's position is determined by geometry. If anyone asks why 1 sits where it sits, the answer is: it's the vertex 40° clockwise from 9 on a regular nine-gon. That's a mathematical placement, not a stylistic one."

## 5. Try it (4:30–5:30)
> "Pause. Look at your construction. If your nonagon's spacing is uneven — if some arcs are larger than others — redo it with a protractor. Mathematical construction tolerates a few degrees of error; it doesn't tolerate eyeball-only. Sixty seconds."
