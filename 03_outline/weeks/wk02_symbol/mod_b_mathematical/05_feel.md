## 0. Authoring notes
Contemplative beat on the Symbol as compressed encoding. The mathematical lens has shown the Symbol is a finite group action diagram, holding the entire infinite family of digit-root reductions in nine vertices. The contemplative question: *what does it mean for an infinite arithmetic to fit on this small drawing?* Slow pacing. Concrete imagery. No jargon.

## 1. Outcome
By the end of these 6 minutes, the student has spent quiet time with the compression of infinite arithmetic into nine points, and can write one sentence about what they noticed.

## 2. Hook (0:00–0:30)
*[On screen: a black field. The Symbol fades in, very small. Then numbers begin to flow into it from off-screen — 47, 999, 1,000,000, 314,159,265 — each disappearing into its position. The Symbol stays the same size.]*

> *(slowly)* "Every number ever, on nine points. Hold the size of that for a moment."

## 3. Setup (0:30–1:00)
> "Lens 5 of 9. The contemplative beat, mathematical edition. Six minutes. One image, held."

## 4. Body (1:00–4:30) — *contemplative, paced, sparse*

> *(slow)* "There are infinitely many whole numbers. You know this. You learned it in grade school: count up, you don't run out.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> The Symbol you constructed yesterday has nine positions. Just nine.
>
> *[long pause]*
>
> Every number — every one of the infinitely many — reduces to one of those nine positions under digit-summing. Forty-seven reduces to two. A million reduces to one. A trillion reduces to four. Pi's first hundred digits reduce to something specific. Every count anyone has ever made on this planet, every number that will ever be written, finds its home on one of nine vertices.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> Sit with that for a moment. An infinite collection — *all whole numbers* — partitions into nine parts. Not approximately. Exactly. And those nine parts are visible, on this drawing, on your page.
>
> *[long pause]*
>
> Mathematicians call this *compression*. A small structure encodes a large one. The map from the integers to the Symbol is one of the most efficient compressions in elementary number theory.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> Marko reads more into this than mainstream mathematics does. He sees the compression not as a computational convenience but as evidence — the numbers are *organised*, and we have found their organisation. The Symbol isn't a useful summary; it's a *picture of the organisation itself*. That reading is contested; we'll handle it in Lens 8 across the year. The compression — that the Symbol holds all of arithmetic in nine vertices — is not contested. It's a fact.
>
> *[long pause]*
>
> When you look at your constructed Symbol now, hold both pictures. The image of nine quiet points on a circle. And the image of every number that has ever been thinking about it, falling toward those nine points.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> The drawing is small. What it carries is not."

## 5. Try it (4:30–5:30)
> *(softer)* "In your notebook, on the closing-reflection page for Module B, write one sentence about what you noticed in this lesson. Not what the lesson said. What you, sitting here, noticed. Sixty seconds."

*[Pause card on screen for 60 seconds.]*

> "Welcome back. The sentence is yours."

## 6. Bridge (5:30–6:00)
> *(returning to ordinary tempo)* "Next lesson — Apply. We've sat with the compression. Next we look at how the Symbol works as a *reference* — every later derivation in this course points back to positions on the Symbol. Lens 6."
