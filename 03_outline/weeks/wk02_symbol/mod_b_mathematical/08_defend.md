## 0. Authoring notes
Fifth Defend lesson. Mod-B objection on the Symbol: *the placement is arbitrary; you could put 1-9 in any order and it'd be the same group.* The honest answer: the *abstract group* is unchanged by relabelling, but the *visual figures* depend on the placement, and those figures encode information the abstract group doesn't carry on its own. Calibrated answer: the placement carries information about the action's geometry, not just the group's structure.

## 1. Outcome
By the end of these 6 minutes, the student can answer the objection *the placement of 1-9 on the Symbol is arbitrary* with one calibrated-answer sentence.

## 3. Setup (0:30–1:00)

## 4. Body (1:00–4:30)

> "The objection in its strongest form. *The integers modulo 9 form an abelian group of order 9. The group is the same regardless of how you draw the elements. Putting 9 at the top and 1 through 8 clockwise is no more correct than putting them in any other order. The placement is a stylistic choice; treating it as if it carries mathematical content is a confusion.*
>
> The objection is correct about the *group*. The abstract group `ℤ/9ℤ` is unchanged by relabelling. A textbook can draw the diagram with vertices in any positions and the algebra is the same.
>
> But the Symbol of Enlightenment is not the abstract group. The Symbol is the abstract group *plus a specific visualisation of an action*. And visualisations depend on placement.
>
> Here's why this matters concretely.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> Take the Doubling Circuit — the orbit (1, 2, 4, 8, 7, 5) under multiplication-by-2. As an abstract sequence of group elements, this orbit is the same regardless of how you draw it. *As a figure on a circle*, it depends entirely on where the elements are placed. Put 1 through 9 in numerical order around the circle, with 9 at the top, and the orbit traces a figure-eight on its side — the symbol of infinity. Move any vertex and the figure deforms. Put the elements in random positions and the orbit traces a chaotic squiggle that carries no visual information.
>
> So the placement *does* carry mathematical content — specifically, content about the *geometry of the action*, not the *structure of the group*. The Symbol's value as a teaching tool, and its value to Marko, comes from this geometry. Move the placement and the geometry collapses.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> The calibrated answer to *the placement is arbitrary*:
>
> *The abstract group is invariant under relabelling, but the geometric figures the action draws on the diagram — the figure-eight of the doubling circuit, the antipodal lines of the polar pairs — depend on the placement. The Symbol carries information about the action's geometry, not just the group's structure.*
>
> Forty words. Longer than the previous calibrated answers, because the question demands more careful unpacking. Memorise the structure if not the exact wording.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> One nuance worth carrying. *Other* placements could also produce useful geometric figures — there's nothing uniquely sacred about 9-at-top-clockwise. Marko picks this placement because the figures it produces are the most legible and the most aligned with reading conventions. A different placement would produce different figures and a different aesthetic. The Symbol is *one* useful visualisation of the action, not the *only* one. That qualification is part of the honest answer."
