## 0. Authoring notes
Place the Symbol next to the standard Cayley diagram of `ℤ/9ℤ` (often shown in algebra textbooks as a circle of dots labelled 0-8 with arrows). Show what's the same — finite cyclic group, nine elements. Show what's different — Marko's diagram replaces 0 with 9, fixes the orientation (9-at-top, clockwise), and pre-loads the figure with named structures (polar pairs, family groups) that aren't in the textbook diagram. The student should leave able to translate between Marko's notation and the textbook's.

## 4. Body (1:00–4:30)

> "**Difference one — labelling.** A standard textbook Cayley diagram for `ℤ/9ℤ` labels the residue classes 0 through 8. Most authors place 0 at the top; some at the bottom; orientation varies. Marko's Symbol labels them 1 through 9, with 9 in the position the textbook would place 0. As we saw in Lens 2, this is a *representation choice* — same set, different labels. The Symbol's choice is motivated by the role 9 plays in the doubling map and by the aesthetic preference for non-zero labels. The textbook's choice is motivated by convention and by the additive identity sitting at index 0. Both are correct; they describe the same underlying group.
>
> **Difference two — orientation.** A textbook Cayley diagram doesn't usually fix an orientation. Vertices can be drawn anywhere on the circle as long as the arrows preserve the structure. The Symbol fixes the orientation — 9 at top, 1 through 8 clockwise — because the *visual figures* that emerge from the action (the figure-eight of the doubling circuit, the antipode lines of the polar pairs) only emerge cleanly in this specific orientation. Rotate the Symbol and the figures rotate with it. The textbook diagram doesn't care about visual figures; the Symbol does. This is one of the places Marko's notation does work the textbook doesn't.
>
> **Difference three — pre-loaded structure.** A textbook Cayley diagram, when first introduced in a chapter, is bare — just vertices and the arrows for the chosen generator. Marko's Symbol comes pre-equipped with named structures the textbook doesn't name: the Doubling Circuit, the Halving Circuit, the Polar Number Pairs, the Family Number Groups, the 3-6-9 axis. These aren't different objects from what a textbook would derive — they *are* the substructures the textbook would prove exist — but Marko names them upfront and uses the names throughout. The Symbol functions less like a clean blackboard diagram and more like a *named atlas*. Each named structure is a region of the atlas the student can refer to.
>
> *[pause — show both diagrams with the three differences highlighted]*
>
> Three differences: labelling, orientation, pre-loaded structure. None of the three is a *mathematical* disagreement with mainstream algebra — every Symbol fact has a matching textbook fact. The differences are notational and pedagogical. The Symbol works *as* a teaching tool for VBM in a way the bare Cayley diagram doesn't, because it gives names to the substructures students will be working with for fifty-two weeks.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> Useful translation: when you see a textbook write `(ℤ/9ℤ)* under multiplication`, that's the same object Marko's Doubling Circuit lives in. When you see a textbook write `the cosets of 3ℤ in ℤ/9ℤ`, that's the same object as the Family Number Groups. The vocabulary is different; the objects are not."
