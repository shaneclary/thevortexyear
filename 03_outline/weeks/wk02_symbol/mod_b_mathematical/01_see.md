## 0. Authoring notes
Mod A's See showed the Symbol as a circle with nine numbers. Mod B's See shows the Symbol as a *group action diagram* — vertices, an operation, the arrows that the operation draws between them. Same drawing, more annotated. The student should leave knowing that the Symbol carries information about an action, not just a set of points.

## 1. Outcome
By the end of these 6 minutes, the student can describe the Symbol of Enlightenment as a group action diagram — vertices, doubling action, arrows.

## 2. Hook (0:00–0:30)
*[On screen: the Symbol from Mod A. Then arrows appear, one at a time, drawing the doubling map: 1 → 2 → 4 → 8 → 7 → 5 → 1.]*

> "Yesterday, a circle of numbers. Today, the same circle with arrows. The arrows are the math."

## 3. Setup (0:30–1:00)
> "Lens 1 of 9, Module B. Mathematical lens. The Symbol carries more than nine vertices. It carries an *action* — a function from the vertices to themselves. Today we draw that action."

## 4. Body (1:00–4:30)

> "A *group action diagram* — sometimes called a *Cayley diagram* in textbooks — shows two pieces of information at once: a set of points, and a function from the set to itself, drawn as arrows. The Symbol of Enlightenment is exactly this kind of diagram, with one specific action.
>
> The set: nine vertices, labelled 1 through 9, placed on a circle with 9 at the top.
>
> The action: *doubling, then digit-summing*. Take any vertex, double it, sum the digits if needed. The result is another vertex on the circle. Draw an arrow from the input to the output.
>
> *[pause — show the Symbol with the doubling map arrows revealed]*
>
> Trace the arrows. From 1, double to get 2 — arrow from 1 to 2. From 2, double to get 4 — arrow from 2 to 4. From 4, double to get 8 — arrow from 4 to 8. From 8, double to get 16, sum to 7 — arrow from 8 to 7. From 7, double to get 14, sum to 5 — arrow from 7 to 5. From 5, double to get 10, sum to 1 — arrow from 5 to 1. The cycle closes.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> What about 3, 6, 9? Trace those arrows. From 3, double to 6 — arrow from 3 to 6. From 6, double to 12, sum to 3 — arrow from 6 to 3. A two-step oscillation. From 9, double to 18, sum to 9 — arrow from 9 to itself. A self-loop.
>
> So the Symbol's action diagram has three components — a six-cycle through {1, 2, 4, 8, 7, 5}, a two-cycle through {3, 6}, and a fixed point at 9.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> This decomposition isn't arbitrary. It's forced by the structure of the multiplicative group `(ℤ/9ℤ)*` — the units modulo 9 — under the action of doubling. The six elements coprime to 9 — {1, 2, 4, 5, 7, 8} — form the Doubling Circuit. The non-coprime elements — {3, 6, 9} — sit elsewhere because they aren't in the group of units. This is exactly the kind of structural fact a textbook on algebra would derive in a chapter on cyclic groups.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> So when you look at the Symbol now, see two layers. The outer layer is what Mod A saw — nine numbers around a circle. The inner layer is the action — the arrows between them. Most VBM diagrams across the year add additional arrows to this base — the halving action, multiplication-by-3, multiplication-by-7. Same Symbol, different overlays."

## 5. Try it (4:30–5:30)
> "Pause. On a fresh page, draw the Symbol. Then, with arrows, draw the doubling action — 1 to 2, 2 to 4, 4 to 8, 8 to 7, 7 to 5, 5 to 1. Then 3 to 6 and 6 to 3, with a small two-headed arrow. Then a self-loop at 9. The action diagram is in your hand. Sixty seconds."

*[Pause card on screen for 60 seconds.]*

> "Welcome back. The Symbol now carries an action. Tomorrow we count what's in the action's structure."
