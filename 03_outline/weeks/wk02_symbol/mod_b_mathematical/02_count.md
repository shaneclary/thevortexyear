## 0. Authoring notes
Mod A's Count answered "why nine, not ten" intuitively. Mod B's Count answers it formally. The set ℤ/9ℤ has nine elements. The Symbol uses 9 in place of 0 because 0 and 9 occupy the same residue class — but more carefully, *9 represents the additive identity in this representation*. Walk the student through the formal vocabulary: residue class, modular equivalence, additive identity. Don't drown them in notation. Land each term once, cleanly.

## 1. Outcome
By the end of these 6 minutes, the student can state |ℤ/9ℤ| = 9 and explain the convention of representing 0 as 9 on the Symbol.

## 4. Body (1:00–4:30)

> "Start with the set. The integers modulo 9, written `ℤ/9ℤ`, are the nine equivalence classes of integers under the relation *differ by a multiple of 9*. Standard convention represents these classes with the integers 0 through 8: zero, one, two, three, four, five, six, seven, eight. Nine elements. The cardinality is 9 — that's what `|ℤ/9ℤ| = 9` means.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> Now the Symbol's representation. Marko writes the elements as 1 through 9 — replacing 0 with 9. This is a *representation choice*, not a different set. The element at the top of the Symbol — labelled 9 — is the same residue class that a textbook labels 0. Both labels point to the same thing: the equivalence class of integers divisible by 9.
>
> Why the relabelling? Because 9 is more useful than 0 for the structure Marko wants to highlight. Specifically: in the multiplicative structure on the Symbol, the value 9 is the *fixed point* of the doubling map. Doubling 9 gives 18, which reduces to 9. Doubling 0 also gives 0. Both are fixed. But 9 — written as 9 rather than 0 — sits naturally as the *capstone* of the diagram, the position that all multiples-of-9 reduce to. The text *9* is more legible than *0* in the role of *the value that holds the rest together*. Marko picks the more legible label.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> Quick check on the count. The Symbol shows nine numbered positions: 1, 2, 3, 4, 5, 6, 7, 8, 9. That matches `|ℤ/9ℤ| = 9`. Every integer reduces to one of these nine via the digit-root operation. Every position on the Symbol corresponds to exactly one residue class. The match is exact.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> One more piece. The action diagram you drew yesterday — the doubling map — is a *group action* on the underlying set. The set ℤ/9ℤ is an *abelian group* under addition; the subset of *units* — elements coprime to 9 — is a multiplicative group `(ℤ/9ℤ)*` of order 6. The orbit of 2 under the doubling map fills `(ℤ/9ℤ)*`, which is why the Doubling Circuit has six positions. The non-units {0, 3, 6} — written {9, 3, 6} on the Symbol — sit outside that group structure, which is why 3-6-9 lives separately.
>
> Notation lesson: `(ℤ/9ℤ)*` is read *the multiplicative group of units modulo 9*. The asterisk means *units only*. The order — meaning size — is 6. We'll prove the orbit structure formally in Week 4."
