## 0. Authoring notes
Place the Symbol next to a Rodin Coil winding template directly. Both encode the same structural information in different forms. The Symbol is 2D and abstract; the winding template is 3D and physical. Each carries information the other doesn't — the Symbol carries the polar pairs as a visible diameter line, the winding template carries the helical path as a physical curve. The student should leave able to translate between the two diagrams.

## 1. Outcome
By the end of these 6 minutes, the student can describe one piece of information the Symbol carries that the coil winding template doesn't, and vice versa.

## 2. Hook (0:00–0:30)
*[On screen: the Symbol on the left, a coil winding template (a flat unfolded torus showing the helical path) on the right.]*

> "Two diagrams. Both encode the same coil. Each shows what the other hides."

## 3. Setup (0:30–1:00)
> "Lens 7 of 9. The Compare lens for the practical module. Today: Symbol next to winding template — what each carries, what each loses."

## 4. Body (1:00–4:30)

> "Both diagrams describe the same Rodin Coil. The Symbol of Enlightenment is a 2D abstract; the winding template is a 2D unfolded representation of the 3D coil surface. Different surfaces, different conventions, same underlying coil.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> **What the Symbol shows that the winding template hides.**
>
> The Symbol shows the *polar pairs* — (1,8), (2,7), (4,5) — as visible diameters across the circle. You can see at a glance that 1 and 8 sit opposite, 2 and 7 sit opposite, 4 and 5 sit opposite. The relationship is one *look*.
>
> On the winding template — which shows the unfolded torus surface — these pairs aren't visually adjacent. They sit on opposite sides of the unfolded grid, and their *antipodal* relationship is preserved by the wrap but not visible on the flat template until you mentally fold the template back into 3D.
>
> The Symbol also shows the *3-6-9 axis* as numbers off the doubling cycle — visually distinguishable from {1, 2, 4, 5, 7, 8} as positions on the circle that don't get visited by the doubling helix. The winding template doesn't show 3, 6, 9 at all, because they don't appear on the toroidal surface — they live on the central axis through the torus, which the unfolded template can't represent.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> **What the winding template shows that the Symbol hides.**
>
> The winding template shows the *physical helix* — the actual path the wire takes through 3D space. The helix has direction, pitch, and a specific number of full revolutions per traversal. The Symbol gives you the cycle order but not the helix shape. You can't read winding pitch off the Symbol; you can read it off the template.
>
> The winding template also shows *cell adjacency* on the torus surface — which positions are physically next to which others. On the Symbol, positions on opposite sides of the circle are far apart. On the torus surface, those same positions can be adjacent — the antipodal relationship is *physical* on the torus, not just a number-summing fact. The template makes this geometric.
>
> *[pause — show both diagrams with their distinguishing info highlighted]*
>
> Two views of the same coil. The Symbol is your tool for reading off algebraic structure — pairs, families, cycles. The winding template is your tool for reading off physical geometry — helix, adjacency, surface flow. Workshop practice keeps both diagrams visible during a build. We'll teach the winding template formally in Q2; today, just notice that it's *a different view*, not a substitute."

## 5. Try it (4:30–5:30)
