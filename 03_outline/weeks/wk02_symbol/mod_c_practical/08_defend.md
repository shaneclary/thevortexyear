## 0. Authoring notes
Sixth Defend lesson. Mod-C objection: *do I really have to draw the Symbol every week? Can't I just understand it once and move on?* Honest answer: yes, you can — and you'll lose something specific. Don't moralise. Don't shame. State what's lost, with specifics about which weeks the loss shows up.

## 1. Outcome
By the end of these 6 minutes, the student can answer the objection *do I really need to draw the Symbol every week?* with one calibrated-answer sentence.

## 2. Hook (0:00–0:30)
*[On screen: one line.]*

> "*Do I really need to draw the Symbol every week?* Today: the honest answer, with specifics."

## 3. Setup (0:30–1:00)
> "Lens 8 of 9. The sixth Defend of the course. Today's objection is a fair one — the course asks for fifty-two drawings, and a busy student wonders if that's necessary. Six minutes."

## 4. Body (1:00–4:30)

> "The objection in its strongest form. *I get the Symbol. I've drawn it three times. I understand the placement, the doubling map, the polar pairs. Why do I need to keep drawing it every week? The diagram doesn't change. The repetition feels like make-work for a course that doesn't trust me.*
>
> The objection is reasonable. The course does ask for fifty-two drawings. They look identical. The repetition seems like a signal to a slow student.
>
> Here's why it matters anyway, with specifics.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> The drawings *don't* change in their basic content — but they *accumulate annotations*. Week 4 adds the Doubling Circuit traced on the Symbol. Week 5 adds the Halving Circuit. Week 6 adds the polar pair lines. Week 7 adds the family group colours. Week 9 adds powers-of-ten markers. Week 10 adds the multiplication-series polygons. Week 11 adds phyllotaxis annotations. By Week 13, your stack of weekly Symbols isn't fifty-two repetitions of the same drawing — it's a record of your growing annotation vocabulary, drawing by drawing, week by week.
>
> Students who skip the weekly drawing don't lose understanding immediately. They lose *fluency*. By Week 17, when the course wraps the Number Map onto a torus, the students who've drawn weekly can *visualise* the wrap because the Symbol's structure is in their hand. The students who haven't drawn struggle to picture the wrap because they're working from a mental image of the Symbol that their hand doesn't fully trust. The math is the same. The fluency isn't.
>
> By Week 33, when the build happens, the gap is starkest. Students who've drawn the Symbol forty-five times know — without effort — where each vertex sits on a torus former. They wind the coil with the Symbol's structure in their hands. Students who've skipped the weekly drawing wind the same coil but consult the diagram repeatedly, slowing the build, introducing winding errors, doubting their placements. Same coil, different relationships to making it.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> So the calibrated answer to *do I really need to draw the Symbol every week?*:
>
> *You don't, technically. But the weekly drawing builds a fluency the course will draw on heavily by Week 17 and again by Week 33. Skipping it doesn't lose understanding; it loses speed and confidence at later milestones.*
>
> Thirty-three words. Memorise the structure. The honest answer respects the question and gives specifics.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> One nuance. *Some* students don't need the fifty-two drawings — students who already have strong visual mathematical intuition, who internalise diagrams quickly. Those students may draw fifteen times and feel done. The course doesn't require fifty-two drawings as a hurdle. It requires whatever number gets *you* to fluency. For most students that's between twenty-five and fifty. Watch your own ease — when you can draw the Symbol with annotations from any week without thinking, you're there."
