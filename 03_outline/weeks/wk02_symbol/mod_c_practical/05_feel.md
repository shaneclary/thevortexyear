Wk 02 — Theme: The Symbol of Enlightenment — Lens 5: Feel — Module C (Practical)
Runtime: 6:00. Author: draft. Status: Draft.

## 0. Authoring notes
Mod A's Feel was *what is the Symbol becoming?* Mod B's was *infinity compressed to nine vertices.* Mod C's Feel is *the Symbol as a posture* — the way you stand or sit when you work with it, not just the diagram itself. Concrete-physical contemplative — the body's relationship to the wall, the desk, the Symbol's gaze. Slow pacing.

## 1. Outcome
By the end of these 6 minutes, the student has spent quiet time with the bodily relationship to the Symbol on their wall, and can write one sentence about it.

## 2. Hook (0:00–0:30)
*[On screen: a wide, slow shot of someone sitting at a desk, the Symbol on the wall behind them. The person is not looking at the Symbol. They are working.]*

> *(slow)* "The Symbol on the wall behind you. You can't see it. It's still there."

## 3. Setup (0:30–1:00)
> "Lens 5 of 9. The contemplative beat for the practical lens. Six minutes. Sit with what your body knows about the Symbol now that it's on your wall."

## 4. Body (1:00–4:30) — *contemplative, paced, sparse*

> *(slow)* "Three days ago, the wall behind you was blank.
>
> *[long pause]*
>
> Now, in the space behind your shoulder or above your desk, there's a circle with nine numbers. You don't have to look at it. Your body knows it's there.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> Most things on a wall are decoration. They become invisible. The eye stops registering them within a week.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> The Symbol can become invisible too. If you let it.
>
> *[long pause — 12 seconds]*
>
> Or — and this is the thing the practitioner from the last lesson described — the Symbol can become a *gaze partner*. Something that doesn't look at you, but isn't quite passive either. A still presence. The way a window onto a garden is a still presence — not interactive, not demanding, but altering what kind of room you're in.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> What the body learns, when the Symbol is on the wall, is a small posture shift. The eye, in idle moments, drifts toward it. Not seeking — just resting on it. The way an eye rests on a window or on a familiar object. The mind, when it drifts there, does no work. It just looks. Ten seconds. Then back to whatever you were doing.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> Over weeks, those ten-second drifts add up. The mind, without effort, becomes more familiar with the Symbol than it would by trying. A face on a wall you walked past every day for a year — you'd know it well, even though you never *studied* it.
>
> *[long pause]*
>
> Marko, when he describes how he came to know the Symbol, says he didn't memorise it. He says it was on his desk for years and the diagram became part of his thinking. That's not mystical — it's how the brain handles things in stable peripheral attention.
>
> *[long pause — 12 seconds]*
>
> Now, sit for ten seconds without looking at the Symbol on your wall. Notice what your body knows about its presence. The angle. The distance. The colour.
>
> *[ten seconds of silence]*
>
> Some students discover, when they try this, that they can describe the Symbol's location and rough appearance without looking. Some can't yet. Either is fine. The week is young."

## 5. Try it (4:30–5:30)
> *(softer)* "In your notebook, write one sentence about the Symbol's location relative to your body right now. Not the Symbol's content — its *position*. Above and to the left? Behind your back? Three feet away? Whatever's true. Sixty seconds."

*[Pause card on screen for 60 seconds.]*

> "Welcome back. The body has its own relationship to the Symbol now. The mind has noticed."

## 6. Bridge (5:30–6:00)
> *(returning to ordinary tempo)* "Next lesson — Apply. We've sat with the Symbol's presence. Next we look at what it *does* — specifically, how the Symbol becomes the canonical face of the Rodin Coil. The practical bridge to Q3. Lens 6."

*[Closing card: "Lesson 5 of 9 — Feel. Next: Apply."]*
