## 4. Body (1:00–4:30)

*[Camera close on hand drawing.]*

> "The Doubling Circuit is the cycle *1 → 2 → 4 → 8 → 7 → 5 → 1*. Six positions. We label them *position 0* through *position 5* as we walk the cycle: position 0 is 1, position 1 is 2, position 2 is 4, position 3 is 8, position 4 is 7, position 5 is 5.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> The Polar Pairs correspond to *cycle positions that are diametrically opposite* — *position k* paired with *position k+3 (mod 6)*.
>
> *Position 0 ↔ position 3.* Vertices 1 and 8. Pair: (1, 8).
>
> *Position 1 ↔ position 4.* Vertices 2 and 7. Pair: (2, 7).
>
> *Position 2 ↔ position 5.* Vertices 4 and 5. Pair: (4, 5).
>
> Three pairs. Same as the additive-inverse pairs from yesterday's lesson. The pairs are the cycle's *three diameters* under the cycle-position-to-vertex map.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> Why does this work?
>
> The Doubling Circuit's six-step closure means *applying doubling three times* maps any element to its antipodal element on the cycle. Specifically: *2³ = 8*, and multiplying by 8 in `(ℤ/9ℤ)*` is the same as multiplying by *−1* (since *8 ≡ −1 mod 9*).
>
> So *applying the doubling map three times sends each element to its additive inverse*. The cycle's three-step structure encodes the additive-inverse relationship.
>
> *[pause — show the connection]*
>
> Build the pair-table. Three rows, three columns: *cycle start*, *three doublings*, *result*.
>
> Row 1: start at 1, double three times — 2, 4, 8 — landing on 8. Pair: (1, 8).
>
> Row 2: start at 2, double three times — 4, 8, 7 — landing on 7. Pair: (2, 7).
>
> Row 3: start at 4, double three times — 8, 7, 5 — landing on 5. Pair: (4, 5).
>
> *[pause]*
>
> The pair-table connects two structures we've spent four weeks on: the Doubling Circuit (Wk 4) and the Polar Pairs (Wk 6). The pairs are *what doubling produces after three steps*. Multiplication-by-8 in `(ℤ/9ℤ)*` is multiplication-by-(−1), and that operation is exactly what pairs each element with its additive inverse.
>
> Two structures, one underlying algebra."
