## 4. Body (1:00–4:30)

> "The objection. *The Polar Pairs are pairs of digits summing to 9. But you could just as easily pair them by summing to 10, or by some other rule. The choice of sum-to-9 looks arbitrary.*
>
> Three reasons it isn't.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> *First — sum-to-9 is the additive-inverse pairing in `ℤ/9ℤ`.* The pair structure follows directly from the group's algebra. *a* and *b* are additive inverses mod 9 iff *a + b ≡ 0 (mod 9)*, which means *a + b* is a multiple of 9 — equivalently, *a + b = 9* when both are positive integers less than 9. The pairing isn't a chosen sum target; it's determined by what additive inverses *are* in this group.
>
> *Second — the pairs are picked out by the Doubling Circuit.* In yesterday's build lesson, we showed that applying the doubling map three times (×2³ = ×8 ≡ ×(−1) mod 9) maps each unit to its additive inverse. So the pairs aren't a choice in pairing; they're *what the cycle's three-step structure produces*. Walking the Doubling Circuit three steps lands you at your additive inverse, period.
>
> *Third — the pairs are unique among useful pairings.* You could try to pair the units by *multiplicative* inverses instead: 2↔5, 4↔7. Two pairs of multiplicative inverses, plus the self-inverses {1, 8}. But that pairing produces a *different structure* — the Halving Circuit pair (Wk 5). Both pairings are real and both have uses, but they're *not the same pairing*. They serve different purposes:
>
> Additive-inverse pairing (Polar Pairs): three pairs, geometric antipodes, summing to 9.
>
> Multiplicative-inverse pairing (Doubling-Halving): two pairs (2-5, 4-7) and two self-inverses (1, 8), used for cycle reversal.
>
> The Polar Pairs are specifically the *additive* version. They're forced by additive structure, not chosen.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> So the calibrated answer to *the pairing is arbitrary?*:
>
> *No. The Polar Pairs are pairs of additive inverses in `ℤ/9ℤ`, which is forced by the group's algebra. The Doubling Circuit's three-step structure produces them: applying the doubling map three times sends each unit to its additive inverse. The pairing is derived, not chosen.*
>
> Forty-eight words.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> One nuance. The mathematical naming — *Polar Pairs* — emphasises the additive-inverse aspect. A different naming convention could foreground the multiplicative-inverse aspect (the Halving Circuit). Marko has chosen the additive naming because the geometric antipodality is more visually salient and ties more directly to the Symbol's symmetry. The choice of naming is conventional; the underlying mathematics gives both pairings equal status."
