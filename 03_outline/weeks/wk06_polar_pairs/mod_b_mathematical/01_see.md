## 4. Body (1:00–4:30)

> "Place 1 through 9 on a regular nonagon's vertices, in order. The vertex labelled *k* sits at angle *(k − 9) × 40°* from the top — that is, at the position you'd reach by stepping clockwise *k − 9* positions from 9.
>
> Two vertices are *antipodal* if the line connecting them passes through the centre of the nonagon. Antipodal pairs in a regular nonagon are pairs whose label angles differ by 180°.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> Compute. Vertex 1 sits at angle 40° from 9. Vertex 8 sits at angle 320° from 9. The angular difference between them is 280° — *not* exactly 180°. So 1 and 8 are *not exactly antipodal* on a regular nonagon.
>
> However, they are *closest to antipodal* among the integer vertices. The exact 180° antipode of vertex 1 — at angle 220° from 9 — would fall between vertices 5 and 6. Since neither 5 nor 6 sits at exactly the antipodal point, 8 is the nearest integer vertex to 1's antipode.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> So a precise statement: the Polar Pairs are *near-antipodal* on the regular nonagon. They become *exactly antipodal* under a different placement convention — for example, on a *complete* circular layout where 0 (= 9) is at the top and 1 through 8 are placed at angles *k × 40°* for *k = 1, ..., 8*, with the polar pairs falling at exactly opposite points modulo a 20° rotation.
>
> Different conventions give different exact placements; in all standard layouts, the polar pairs are *visually* on opposite sides of the figure, even when not at exactly 180° apart on a nonagon.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> One cleaner way to say this. *In `(ℤ/9ℤ)*` viewed as a cyclic group of order 6, the polar pairs are the three pairs of elements that are additive inverses modulo 9.* The pair (1, 8) consists of two numbers that sum to 9 ≡ 0 mod 9 — so each is the additive inverse of the other in the cyclic group `ℤ/9ℤ`. The geometric *antipodality* on the diagram is a *visual consequence* of placing additive inverses at opposite positions when arranging by additive order.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> The key geometric fact: in any group whose elements sit on a circle in additive order, *additive inverses sit antipodally*. This isn't a property of the Symbol's specific placement; it's a property of how additive-cyclic structure embeds in a circular geometric layout. The Symbol inherits the antipodality from the underlying group structure."
