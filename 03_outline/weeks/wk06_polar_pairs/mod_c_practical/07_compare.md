## 0. Authoring notes
Counter-wound vs. bifilar coil design — closely related but distinct techniques. The Rodin Coil's pair structure relates to both but isn't identical to either.

## 4. Body (1:00–4:30)

> "Three pair-related coil designs in mainstream and VBM engineering. Each has specific properties.
>
> *Counter-wound coil.* Single wire, single core, but the wire reverses direction halfway through the winding. The two halves carry current in opposite directions. Used for impedance matching and as a simpler version of common-mode chokes. *One pair, one wire.*
>
> *Bifilar coil.* Two parallel wires wound together as a single bundle, with one wire reversed in direction. The two wires sit beside each other throughout the winding. Used for tightly-coupled mutual inductance applications and balanced-line transformers. *One pair, two wires, side by side.*
>
> *Rodin Polar-Pair coil.* Multiple wires, each starting from a different vertex of the multiplicative group. Pairs of wires sit on opposite sides of the torus throughout the winding. Used for three-axis field cancellation in advanced Rodin builds. *Three pairs, six wires, antipodally placed.*
>
> *[pause — show the three designs with their geometries]*
>
> Three differences worth carrying.
>
> *Difference one — number of pairs.* Counter-wound: one. Bifilar: one. Rodin: three.
>
> *Difference two — strand placement.* Counter-wound: same wire's two halves. Bifilar: side-by-side. Rodin: antipodal.
>
> *Difference three — cancellation directions.* Counter-wound: one direction (the wire's axis). Bifilar: one direction. Rodin: three orthogonal directions.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> One useful framing. The Rodin Polar-Pair coil is *three independent bifilar coils, antipodally arranged on a shared torus*. That description places it in standard vocabulary while specifying what's distinctive — the antipodal placement and the three-pair structure.
>
> An RF engineer hearing this description would understand the geometry and could predict its measurable properties using standard transmission-line and antenna theory. They might disagree with claims about its electromagnetic novelty, but they wouldn't be confused about what's being built.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> Practical takeaway. When you wind a Wk-35 multi-strand Rodin Coil, you can describe it to anyone in standard engineering terms: *three antipodally-placed bifilar pairs on a shared toroidal core, wound according to the doubling-cycle pattern*. The description is precise and tells engineers something useful."
