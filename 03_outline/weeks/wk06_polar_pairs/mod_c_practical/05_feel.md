## 0. Authoring notes
*The handwork of dual winding.* What does it feel like to wind two strands in parallel? More complex than single-strand. Bilateral attention. Both hands working.

## 1. Outcome
Student has spent quiet time with the bilateral attention required for pair-winding.

## 2. Hook (0:00–0:30)
*[Two hands winding two strands simultaneously.]*

> *(slow)* "Two hands. Two strands. Sit with that."

## 3. Setup (0:30–1:00)
> "Lens 5 of 9. Six minutes."

## 4. Body (1:00–4:30) — *contemplative*

> *(slow)* "Single-strand winding is one hand on the wire, one hand on the form. The dominant hand does the placement; the supporting hand stabilises.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> Pair-strand winding is *both hands on wires*. One strand in each hand. Both wires placed simultaneously. Both hands tracking position.
>
> *[long pause]*
>
> The bilateral attention is harder than it sounds. Most people, asked to do two complementary tasks at once, find their attention strobing — focus on one, drop the other for a moment, return. Bilateral simultaneity isn't natural for most adults; it's a learned skill, like piano playing.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> Wk-35's six-strand build is even harder. Six strands in flight at once. You can't hold six wires at once with two hands, so you arrange them on spool stands and advance each strand in turn. The hand moves between strands; the *attention* must hold all six positions in mind simultaneously.
>
> *[long pause — 12 seconds]*
>
> Practitioners who've built six-strand coils describe it as a flow state. After the first hour, the strand-tracking becomes background; the rest of the attention rests on the form. The hand and the eye and the multiple wires settle into a coordinated motion that wasn't present at the start.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> What this means for your preparation. Single-strand mock-ups (Wk 4) gave your hand the doubling gesture. Pair-strand mock-ups (this week) give your hand the *paired* gesture — two strands tracked together. Wk-35's six-strand mock-up will give your hand the *flock* gesture — many strands, distributed attention.
>
> Each layer of complexity is its own skill. Marko, when describing his teaching practice, says students who skip the mock-ups arrive at the multi-strand build with the right knowledge in their heads and the wrong knowledge in their hands. The hands haven't *practiced* the bilateral motion. They struggle.
>
> *[long pause]*
>
> The pair-strand mock-up you did today is your first introduction to bilateral attention applied to coil work. By Wk 35 you'll have built up to six. The hand learns layer by layer.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> Hold both hands now. Notice their independence. Notice their coordination. The Polar Pairs structure asks both hands to participate equally. The math doesn't care about handedness; the build session does."
