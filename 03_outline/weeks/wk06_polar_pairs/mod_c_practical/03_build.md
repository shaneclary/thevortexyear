## 4. Body (1:00–4:30)

*[Camera follows hands.]*

> "Take your torus former from Wk 4. It has the doubling-direction string mock-up. Today we add a second string — different colour — starting at vertex 8 instead of vertex 1.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> Step one — tape the new string at vertex 8.
>
> Step two — wrap the string from 8 to 7. (Following the doubling cycle from 8 — *8 doubles to 16 → 7*.)
>
> Step three — from 7 to 5. (*7 doubles to 14 → 5*.)
>
> Step four — from 5 to 1.
>
> Step five — from 1 to 2.
>
> Step six — from 2 to 4.
>
> Step seven — from 4 back to 8.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> Look at the former. Three strings now: the original doubling-direction (red, starting at 1), the halving-direction from Wk 5 (blue, starting at 1, going other way), and the new strand starting at vertex 8 (green, starting at 8, going same direction as red).
>
> *[pause]*
>
> The green strand and the red strand are a *Polar Pair* — they're at antipodal positions throughout. When red is at vertex 1, green is at vertex 8. When red is at vertex 2, green is at vertex 7 (its pair partner). When red is at vertex 4, green is at vertex 5.
>
> Notice: the two strands maintain antipodal positioning *throughout the entire winding*. They never get closer than antipodal; they never get farther. The pair structure is preserved by the doubling cycle's three-step antipode property.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> One observation about the mock-up. With two pair-strands, the four corners of any cell on the torus surface are bracketed by the two strands. The strands together form a *closed pair-helix* that you can trace around the torus's body. This is hard to see from a single-strand winding; it's the essential thing to see in a paired winding.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> When you wind a real six-strand Wk-35 coil, this is the relationship that holds for *each pair*. Three pairs, each tracing a closed pair-helix. The full coil is three nested pair-helices on the same form."
