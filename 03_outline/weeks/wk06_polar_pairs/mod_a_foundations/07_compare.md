## 4. Body (1:00–4:30)

> "*Complementary pairs* in mathematics generally are pairs that sum to a fixed value. Different fields use different fixed values:
>
> *Decimal complement to 9.* This is what VBM uses. (1,8), (2,7), (4,5). Pairs of digits summing to 9.
>
> *Decimal complement to 10.* Standard for arithmetic teaching. (1,9), (2,8), (3,7), (4,6), and 5+5. Used to teach mental subtraction.
>
> *Hundred-complement.* Used in financial calculations and in some architectures of computer arithmetic. Pairs summing to 100.
>
> *Two's complement.* Used in computer arithmetic for representing negative numbers in binary.
>
> All of these are *complementary pair* structures. They differ in the *fixed sum*. The fixed sum determines the structure of the pairing.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> What's specific about complement-to-9 (Polar Pairs)?
>
> *Specificity one — the unit group of mod 9.* Pairs that sum to 9 in {1, ..., 8} are exactly the pairs of multiplicative inverses in `(ℤ/9ℤ)*`. Specifically: 1 and 8 are not multiplicative inverses (1 × 8 = 8 ≠ 1 mod 9), but they are *additive complements* mod 9. The complementary structure is *additive*, not multiplicative.
>
> Wait, let me be more careful. *Multiplicative inverses* in `(ℤ/9ℤ)*` are pairs (a, b) with a·b ≡ 1 (mod 9). For the six units, those are: 1↔1, 2↔5, 4↔7, 8↔8. So 2 and 5 are inverses (we proved this in Week 5). But 1 and 8 are *not* multiplicative inverses; they're additive complements (their sum is 9, equivalently 0 mod 9).
>
> So the Polar Pairs are *additive* complements mod 9, not multiplicative ones. This is a different relationship from the doubling-halving inverse pair from Week 5.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> *Specificity two — diametric placement.* On the Symbol — with the standard 9-at-top, clockwise placement — additive complements happen to sit *diametrically opposite*. The 1 (one o'clock) and 8 (eleven o'clock) are nearly opposite. The 2 (two o'clock) and 7 (ten o'clock) are nearly opposite. This is a property of how the Symbol places the digits, combined with the additive complement structure.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> Useful framing. *Polar pairs are additive complements modulo 9, geometrically antipodal on the Symbol.* That's a precise mathematical description. A working number theorist would recognise the structure as *the pairs (k, 9-k) for k = 1, 2, 4* — a standard additive-complement family in modular arithmetic.
>
> The novelty isn't the pairing — that's standard. The novelty is the *visualisation* and the *use* — Marko makes the antipodal-pair structure central to coil winding (Wk 19, Wk 28 ★) where standard treatment wouldn't."
