> "The objection. *The Polar Pairs are additive complements modulo 9. That's an obvious arithmetic fact — pairs summing to 9 in {1, ..., 8}, of which there are exactly three. Why is this called out as a special structure? It's not even surprising. Why do the pairs deserve a week of attention?*
>
> The objection is fair. The arithmetic fact is trivial. Three pairs summing to 9 is one line of mental arithmetic.
>
> Here's why the pairs earn their week.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> *First — antipodal placement isn't trivial.* The pairs that sum to 9 in {1,...,8} happen to sit *diametrically opposite* on the Symbol of Enlightenment, because the Symbol places digits in numerical order around a circle. This *coincidence of arithmetic and geometry* — additive complements being antipodal — is what makes the pair structure visible without requiring computation. You read it off the diagram, not from a table. That's a specific property of the Symbol's placement convention.
>
> *Second — the antipodal property propagates to the torus.* When the Symbol wraps onto the torus in Q2 (Wk 16), the polar pairs become *antipodal points on the torus surface*. Antipodes on a torus are non-trivial: they're not just opposite points across the donut, but specific topologically-related positions. The pair structure that's geometric on the flat Symbol becomes topological on the torus. Wk 19 explores this.
>
> *Third — the pairs constrain coil topology.* The Rodin Coil winding pattern (the doubling cycle on the torus) visits the polar pairs in a specific order. Wk 28 ★ revisits the polar pairs explicitly as *coil-strand antipodes*. The pair structure isn't decoration; it's a structural fact that determines coil-winding constraints.

> *The arithmetic of summing-to-9 is trivial. What earns the pairs their week is that additive complements happen to sit antipodally on the Symbol, propagate to topological antipodes on the torus, and constrain coil-winding patterns. The pair structure is a geometric and topological fact, not just an arithmetic one.*
