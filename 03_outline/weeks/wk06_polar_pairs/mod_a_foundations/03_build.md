## 0. Authoring notes
Colour the three Polar Pairs on the printed Symbol. Three pairs, three colours. The student leaves with an annotated Symbol on the wall — pairs visible at a glance.

## 1. Outcome
Student has coloured the three Polar Pairs on their printed Symbol with three distinct colours.

## 2. Hook (0:00–0:30)
*[Three coloured pencils — red, blue, green. A printed Symbol below.]*

> "Three colours. Three pairs. Six minutes."

## 3. Setup (0:30–1:00)
> "Lens 3 of 9. Today: colour your Symbol. The pairs become visible at a glance."

## 4. Body (1:00–4:30)

*[Camera close on hand colouring printed Symbol.]*

> "Take your printed Symbol — wall print, notebook insert, or any clean copy. Pick three different-coloured pencils or pens. Red, blue, green — or whatever you have, three colors that contrast.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> First colour — red. Use it to mark the (1, 8) pair. Draw a small red dot beside the 1. Draw another red dot beside the 8. Connect them with a thin red line through the centre. The (1, 8) pair is now colour-coded.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> Second colour — blue. Mark the (2, 7) pair. Dot beside 2, dot beside 7, line connecting them. Second diameter, second colour.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> Third colour — green. Mark the (4, 5) pair. Dot beside 4, dot beside 5, line connecting them. Third diameter, third colour.
>
> *[pause — show the fully coloured Symbol]*
>
> Three colours. Three diameters. The Symbol's symmetry is now visible at a glance. From across the room you can read the polar pair structure.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> One annotation. In a small key at the bottom of the page, write the colour-to-pair mapping. *Red: 1–8. Blue: 2–7. Green: 4–5.* The key makes the colour code interpretable to anyone else who picks up your Symbol.
>
> Date the page. *Polar pairs coloured, today's date.* The page goes in your build log under *Diagrams*.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> Why this matters. Many VBM diagrams from here on use colour to denote polar pairs. Your colour choice today becomes your *personal convention* for reading other diagrams. If your red is the (1,8) pair, every diagram using red to denote a pair will probably be marking (1,8) — and you'll read it correctly without consulting the legend. Consistency from the start saves cognitive load later."

## 5. Try it (4:30–5:30)
