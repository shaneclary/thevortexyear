## 4. Body (1:00–4:30) — *contemplative*

> *(slow)* "Look at the Symbol with the three diameters drawn.
>
> *[long pause]*
>
> The diagram has *bilateral symmetry*. Fold the page along any of the three diameters and the figure maps onto itself. Three different folds, three different mirror axes, all valid for the same diagram.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> This isn't unusual for circular diagrams. Most circles have rotational symmetry. The Symbol has *reflection* symmetry along three specific axes — the polar pair lines.
>
> *[long pause]*
>
> What does symmetry *feel* like, perceptually?
>
> *[pause]*
>
> Most people, looking at a symmetric figure, experience a sense of *rest*. The eye doesn't have to work hard to process the figure; one half tells you the other. The mind relaxes. Symmetric figures appear in calm spaces — meditation halls, churches, sacred geometry traditions — partly because the eye *settles* on them.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> Asymmetric figures pull the eye. Symmetric figures release it.
>
> *[long pause — 12 seconds]*
>
> The Symbol's three-fold reflection symmetry is part of why it functions as a contemplative artefact. The eye, resting on it, finds three independent axes of folding — and behind those, the rotational symmetry of the underlying nine-fold figure. Layers of symmetry, all reinforcing.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> Marko's framing — *the pairs are the Symbol's bones* — points at symmetry as the deep structural fact. Not the cycles, not the multiplications, not the eventual torus wrap. The *symmetry* is the bone. Cycles and groups are the muscles around the bones.
>
> *[long pause]*
>
> Hold the Symbol in your peripheral attention right now. Notice the three diameters you've coloured. Notice the symmetry. Notice what your eye does.
>
> *[ten seconds of silence]*
>
> The figure rests. You rest with it. That's symmetry as a felt property.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> Mathematicians measure symmetry by the size of its symmetry group — the set of transformations that leave a figure unchanged. The Symbol has a symmetry group of size 6 — three reflections and three rotations (including identity), forming the *dihedral group D₃*. The size measures how many ways the figure folds onto itself.
>
> Six is small enough to hold in the mind, large enough to give the eye somewhere to rest. The Symbol's symmetry is *contemplative-sized* — not so simple it bores the eye, not so complex it tires the eye.
>
> *[long pause]*
>
> Sit with the symmetry. Three pairs. Three diameters. Three folds. One quiet figure."
