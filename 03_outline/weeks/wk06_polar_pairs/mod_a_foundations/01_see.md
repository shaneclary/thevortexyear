## 0. Authoring notes
First Mod-A on Polar Pairs. The three pairs (1,8), (2,7), (4,5) revealed as three diameters across the Symbol. Visual-first. The student colours the pairs in three colours.

## 1. Outcome
Student can identify the three Polar Number Pairs on the Symbol and trace each as a diameter.

## 2. Hook (0:00–0:30)
*[Symbol with three coloured lines drawn across — 1↔8 (red), 2↔7 (blue), 4↔5 (green). All pass through the centre.]*

> "Three pairs. Three lines through the centre. Six minutes."

## 3. Setup (0:30–1:00)
> "Lens 1 of 9, Week 6. Polar Number Pairs. The symmetry of the Symbol made visible."

## 4. Body (1:00–4:30)

> "Look at your Symbol. Find the 1 at the one-o'clock position. Find the 8 at the eleven-o'clock position. Draw a line connecting them.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> The line passes through the centre of the Symbol. It's a *diameter*.
>
> Find the 2 (two-o'clock). Find the 7 (ten-o'clock). Draw a line. Another diameter, in a different direction.
>
> Find the 4 (four-o'clock). Find the 5 (five-o'clock). Draw a line. Third diameter.
>
> *[pause — show all three diameters on the Symbol]*
>
> Three lines. Three diameters. Three pairs of vertices that sit *opposite each other* through the centre.
>
> *Polar Number Pairs.* The three pairs are *(1, 8), (2, 7), (4, 5)*. Each pair connects two vertices on opposite sides of the Symbol.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> One arithmetic fact. *Each pair sums to 9.*
>
> *1 + 8 = 9.*
>
> *2 + 7 = 9.*
>
> *4 + 5 = 9.*
>
> Tomorrow's Lens 2 will let you verify this systematically. For now: the three pairs are characterised both *geometrically* (opposite vertices on the Symbol) and *arithmetically* (sum to 9).
>
> *[pause]*
>
> What about 3, 6, 9? They're not in any polar pair. The 9 sits alone at the top — it's its own opposite, in a sense, since 9 + 0 = 9 (and 0 collapses to 9 on the Symbol). The 3 and 6 sit at the four-o'clock-and-eight-o'clock and ten-o'clock-and-two-o'clock positions — not directly opposite each other in the Symbol's standard placement. The polar-pair structure picks out exactly the six elements of `(ℤ/9ℤ)*` — the same six elements as the Doubling and Halving Circuits.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> What you're seeing. The pairs identify *the six units of mod 9*, partitioned into *three pairs of mutually-opposite elements*. The structure is geometric — visible as diameters — and algebraic — verified by sum-to-9. Same six elements you've been working with for two weeks; new lens.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> Three pairs colour-code most VBM diagrams from here on. By Week 13 you'll see the polar pairs in coil winding diagrams, in torus colour schemes, in cross-section drawings. The colour code becomes part of how you read VBM imagery."
