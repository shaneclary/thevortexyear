## 4. Body (1:00–4:30)

> "Module B opened with the picture of why digit-summing equals modulo-9 reduction. Every power of ten reduces to one mod nine, so when you write a number as a base-10 expansion and reduce, the powers of ten *vanish* — leaving just the digit-sum.
>
> From there, the formal proof. Two lines. *n equals the sum of digits times powers of ten*; *modulo 9, the powers of ten become 1, leaving the digit-sum*. The proof is short; the unpacking carried the lesson.
>
> Then the table. A 10×10 grid of digital roots for the integers 0 through 99. Each row a shifted cycle of 1 through 9. The proof's first concrete artefact, in your build log under *Calculations*.
>
> Then Randy spoke on the universality of mod-9. ISBNs, banking, codons, coil winding — the operation appears across many domains. Marko's claim isn't that he invented the math; it's that the math points at something physical.
>
> Then a contemplative beat. The digital root is a *projection* — it preserves the mod-9 residue, discards everything else. Sit with what survives the projection and what doesn't.
>
> Then we applied. Three engineering examples — ISBN checksums, banking routing numbers, the Luhn algorithm for credit cards. The math underneath modern infrastructure. The Doubling Circuit is the heart of every credit card validation in the world.
>
> Then we compared. Mod-9 versus mod-7 versus mod-11. Mod-9 is special *given base 10* because of the digit-summing trick. Other moduli are equally valid; they just don't have the trick. The specialness is base-dependent.
>
> Then the calibrated answer to *base 10 is just our fingers*. Marko's three-part argument — ten-fingers as evolutionary structure, base 10 at the positional-notation threshold, the structural richness of mod-9. Forty-eight words. Respects both Marko's case and the mainstream pushback.
