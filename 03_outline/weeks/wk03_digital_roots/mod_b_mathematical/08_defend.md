## 4. Body (1:00–4:30)

> "The standard view, which most mathematicians hold: base 10 is an accident of human anatomy. We have ten fingers, so we count in tens. Aliens with eight fingers would count in eights. There's nothing privileged about ten beyond that biological coincidence.
>
> Marko's view, briefly: base 10 is *non-arbitrary* in three specific ways.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> *First — the ten-fingers fact isn't arbitrary either.* Vertebrate evolution converged on five-digit limbs across many lineages. Two arms, two times five, equals ten fingers. The five-fingered hand isn't a coincidence; it's a stable evolutionary attractor with deep mathematical structure (the pentadactyl pattern). The biological choice of ten reflects something about the geometry of life itself. So *ten fingers* isn't a free parameter — it's a constrained outcome.
>
> *Second — base 10 is the smallest integer above the threshold where digit-positions become useful.* Numbers below 10 don't need positional notation; they fit in one symbol. Numbers from 10 upward require place value. So 10 is the *first* point in the integer sequence at which positional notation becomes necessary. Marko reads this as another sign of natural selection — base 10 sits at the boundary where compact notation begins.
>
> *Third — and this is the specifically VBM argument — the structural properties of mod-9 that arise *from* base 10 are unusually rich. The Doubling Circuit has period 6, which is highly composite. The polar pairs sum to 9, the highest single digit. The 3-6-9 trinary structure aligns with multiple spiritual and mathematical traditions. Marko argues these properties wouldn't all coincide so cleanly in an arbitrary base — and the fact that they do, in base 10, suggests something about base 10's selection.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> The argument is suggestive, not conclusive. A skeptical mathematician will accept the biological observations and reject the structural-richness argument as confirmation bias — *of course* we find rich structure in the base we use; we have all our examples there. The bias point is fair. Marko's response is that the structural richness is mathematically *unusual*, not just familiar — but that response is hard to make rigorous, and most mathematicians don't accept it.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> The calibrated answer to *base 10 is just our fingers*:
>
> *Marko argues base 10 is non-arbitrary three ways: the ten-fingers fact reflects deep evolutionary structure; base 10 sits at the threshold where positional notation begins; the modular structure under base 10 is unusually rich. The argument is suggestive, not conclusive. Mainstream math accepts the biology, rejects the rest as confirmation bias.*
>
> Forty-eight words. The calibrated answer respects both Marko's case and the mainstream pushback. Memorise the structure if not the wording.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> Note what the answer is *not* doing. It's not asserting that Marko is right. It's not asserting that he's wrong. It's giving you the case as he makes it, and the case as the mainstream rejects it. Your job is to listen to both and decide. The course teaches the math regardless of which side of the question you land on."
