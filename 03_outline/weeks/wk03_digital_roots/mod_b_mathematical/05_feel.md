## 0. Authoring notes
Contemplative beat: *the compression as a window onto structure*. We've shown that all whole numbers reduce to nine residues. The operation throws away most of the information about a number and keeps something specific — its mod-9 residue. What does it mean to call that residue *structure*? Slow pacing.

## 1. Outcome
By the end of these 6 minutes, the student has spent quiet time with what the digital-root operation *preserves* — and what it *discards*.

## 2. Hook (0:00–0:30)
*[On screen: a black field. The number 8,927,365 appears — large. Then it slowly shrinks toward a small "4" at the centre. Around the "4", many other large numbers appear and shrink to other digits. The Symbol fades in, faintly, behind.]*

> *(slow)* "Most of the number disappears. What stays?"

## 3. Setup (0:30–1:00)
> "Lens 5 of 9. The contemplative beat. Six minutes. The operation takes a number, throws away almost everything about it, keeps something. What did it keep?"

## 4. Body (1:00–4:30) — *contemplative, paced, sparse*

> *(slow)* "Take 8,927,365. The number has a lot in it. Its size — about nine million. Its specific digits — eight, nine, two, seven, three, six, five. Whether it's prime or composite. Whether it's odd or even. Its prime factorisation. Its position in the number line.
>
> *[long pause]*
>
> Reduce it. Digital root: four.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> Most of what was in 8,927,365 is now gone. We don't know its size. We don't know its parity. We don't know its factors. We know one thing — *its residue modulo nine is four.*
>
> *[long pause — 12 seconds]*
>
> What is that residue, *exactly*? It's the answer to one specific question: *what is left over when this number is divided by nine?* That question, and only that question, is what the digital root preserves.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> In information theory, an operation that preserves some properties and discards others is called *projection*. Digital-root reduction is a projection — from the infinite world of whole numbers to the nine-element world of `ℤ/9ℤ`. The projection is *lossy*; you can't recover the original number from its digital root. But the projection isn't *random*. It preserves something specific — the mod-9 residue.
>
> *[long pause]*
>
> Mainstream mathematics calls what survives *the algebraic structure* of the original. Two numbers with the same digital root behave identically *under any operation that respects mod-9*. They're additively interchangeable, multiplicatively related, structurally aligned. They differ in size, in factorisation, in position — but in this one specific structural sense, they're the same.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> Sit with that. The Symbol shows you nine *equivalence classes*. Every number falls into one. The class membership is what survives the projection. The infinite world of integers, *projected through the digit-root lens*, becomes nine.
>
> *[long pause]*
>
> Marko reads this projection as *a window onto fundamental structure*. He argues the residue mod 9 isn't an arbitrary projection chosen by mathematicians — it's a *natural* one, given the structure of base 10. If he's right, the nine vertices of the Symbol aren't *one of many* possible projections; they're *the* projection, the one that preserves what's worth preserving.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> Most mathematicians don't accept the *the* — they'd argue projections are choices, plural and equivalent. But even under the milder reading, the projection is real, the structure it preserves is real, and the nine vertices are a genuine compression of the infinite into the finite.
>
> *[long pause]*
>
> What gets to survive? The mod-9 residue. What disappears? Everything else.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> The Symbol is the picture of what gets to survive."
