## 4. Body (1:00–4:30)

> "Module A opened with the cascade. A large number — eight million nine hundred twenty-seven thousand three hundred sixty-five — reduces by digit-summing to a single digit, four. Every whole number has exactly one digital root. The Symbol's nine vertices are the nine possible shadows.
>
> From there, four worked examples. Forty-seven, one hundred forty-four, nine hundred ninety-nine, two thousand twenty-six — reduced by hand, slowly, with the digital roots placed at their vertices. You learned that 144 and 999 share a vertex, that 2026 lives at 1, that the operation has *destinations*, not *paths*.
>
> Then the worksheet. Fifty consecutive integers, reduced. The pattern of digital roots cycled 1-2-3-4-5-6-7-8-9 over and over. Your hand learned the operation through repetition. By the end the cascade ran without thinking.
>
> Then Marko spoke on 9. The number that returns itself under multiplication. The still point. *Spirit*, in his framing — not as mystical assertion but as a name for the unchanging element. You tested the claim by multiplying nine by something arbitrary; the reduction came back to nine.
>
> Then a contemplative beat. *The numbers don't really get bigger.* Under digital-root reduction, 1 and a billion share a vertex. The arithmetic is scale-invariant. The familiar idea that bigger numbers carry more meaning meets a different reading.
>
> Then we applied. Birthday, house number, phone digits, the year — four personal numbers reduced to digital roots and marked on your Symbol. The diagram became an address book.
>
> Then the comparison. Digit-summing is the operation; *modulo 9* is its formal name; *casting out nines* is its historical name. Three vocabularies, one arithmetic. You can speak any of them.
>
> Then the calibrated answer to the strongest mainstream objection. *This only works because base 10. Marko's stronger claim is that base 10 is non-arbitrary; that's contested. The patterns are real either way.* Forty-six words. The longest calibrated answer of the course so far, because the question demands it.
>
> *[pause — show the spiral wheel with Weeks 1, 2 fully lit and Week 3 Mod A lit]*
>
> Module A done. The cascade is in your hand, the operation has its three vocabularies, the strongest objection has its answer. Tomorrow Module B opens with the formal proof: digital root equals n modulo 9, derived in three lines. The arithmetic foundation made rigorous."
