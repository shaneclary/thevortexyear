## 0. Authoring notes
First lesson of Week 3. The student has met digit-summing twice already (Mod-A wk 2 informally, Mod-B wk 2 formally). This lesson formalises it as a *cascade* — large number, sum its digits, repeat until one digit remains. Visual-first treatment. The student should leave knowing that every whole number has a single-digit shadow, and that the Symbol's nine vertices are the nine possible shadows.

## 1. Outcome
By the end of these 6 minutes, the student can describe the digital-root cascade and identify which Symbol vertex any given whole number reduces to.

## 2. Hook (0:00–0:30)
*[On screen: a large number — 8,927,365 — with arrows leading down to its digit-sum, then again, and again, until 4 remains. The 4 then highlights on the Symbol.]*

> "Eight million nine hundred twenty-seven thousand three hundred sixty-five. Reduces to four. Today: how, and why."

## 3. Setup (0:30–1:00)
> "Lens 1 of 9, Week 3. The first piece of formal arithmetic. We've been digit-summing since Week 1. Today we name it, watch it work, and place every whole number on the Symbol."

## 4. Body (1:00–4:30)

> "The operation is simple. Take any whole number. Sum its digits. If the result has more than one digit, sum again. Continue until one digit remains. That single digit is the *digital root* of the original number.
>
> Watch it on the screen. Eight million nine hundred twenty-seven thousand three hundred sixty-five. Sum: 8 + 9 + 2 + 7 + 3 + 6 + 5. That's 40. Two digits, so reduce again: 4 + 0 = 4. The digital root of 8,927,365 is 4.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> Try another. One hundred forty-four. Sum: 1 + 4 + 4 = 9. Single digit, done. Digital root of 144 is 9.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> Another. Two thousand twenty-six. Sum: 2 + 0 + 2 + 6 = 10. Two digits, reduce: 1 + 0 = 1. Digital root of 2026 is 1.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> Three numbers, three digital roots: 4, 9, 1. Each one corresponds to a vertex on the Symbol. 4 sits at roughly the four-o'clock position. 9 sits at the top. 1 sits at the one-o'clock position. The big number disappears. Its single-digit shadow lives on the Symbol.
>
> *[pause — show the Symbol with 4, 9, 1 highlighted]*
>
> Two facts to carry from this lesson.
>
> First: every whole number has *exactly one* digital root. The cascade always terminates. There's no number that reduces ambiguously to two different vertices. The map from whole numbers to Symbol vertices is well-defined.
>
> Second: the digital root captures *something* about the original number that survives across all the digits. Two different big numbers can have the same digital root — 47 and 92 both reduce to 2 — but their digital roots are not arbitrary. They reflect a property of the numbers in their relationship to 9. We'll formalise the property next week. For now: the cascade shows you the property exists.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> Look at your printed Symbol. The nine vertices aren't just labels. They're the *nine residue classes* of all whole numbers under digit-summing. Every number in existence — past, present, future — reduces to one of nine spots. The Symbol is a *complete map* of how whole numbers live."

## 5. Try it (4:30–5:30)
> "Pause. Pick a four- or five-digit number. Today's date written as a number — 20260508 — works. Reduce it by hand. Cascade until you have one digit. Place a finger on that vertex of the Symbol on your wall. Sixty seconds."

*[Pause card on screen for 60 seconds.]*

> "Welcome back. Today's date now has its position on the Symbol. So does every other number you'll write this year."
