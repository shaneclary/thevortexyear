## 0. Authoring notes
The contemplative beat for this theme is the strange fact that *the numbers don't really get bigger*. Under digit-root reduction, a billion and a one are the same vertex if their digital roots match. The reduction collapses scale to structure. Slow pacing.

## 1. Outcome
By the end of these 6 minutes, the student has spent quiet time with the structural collapse of scale under digit-root reduction.

## 2. Hook (0:00–0:30)
*[On screen: a black field. The number 1 in the centre. Then 10, 100, 1,000, 10,000... appearing in succession, all reducing visually to the same 1.]*

> *(slow)* "One. Ten. A hundred. A thousand. All the same vertex."

## 3. Setup (0:30–1:00)
> "Lens 5 of 9. The contemplative beat. Six minutes. Sit with the strangeness of what we just learned."

## 4. Body (1:00–4:30) — *contemplative, paced, sparse*

> *(slow)* "We count. We have always counted. From the first marks on a tally bone to the trillions in a national budget, we have organised the world by counting up.
>
> *[long pause]*
>
> The Symbol of Enlightenment treats counting differently. Under digit-root reduction, the cascade we did yesterday, the *value* of the number — its ordinary size — is replaced by a single digit. The number's *structure*, in some specific sense, survives. The number's *scale* doesn't.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> Look at it concretely. The number 1 has digital root 1. The number 1,000,000 has digital root 1. The number 1,000,000,000 has digital root 1. Every power of ten, reduced, is 1. Three trillion and a single sheep land at the same vertex on the Symbol.
>
> *[long pause]*
>
> This isn't a coincidence. It's a property of base-ten arithmetic. The digit-root operation is exactly *modulo nine*, and ten is congruent to one modulo nine. So multiplying by ten — moving up one decimal place — leaves the digital root unchanged. Scale up by a billion, and the structure is preserved.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> Sit with that for a moment. Whatever the digital root captures about a number — and it captures something specific — it's a property *invariant under multiplication by ten*. The arithmetic property that survives is *the number's relationship to nine*.
>
> *[long pause — 12 seconds]*
>
> Marko reads this as evidence that *scale doesn't matter* in some deep sense. The same structure that operates on small numbers operates on enormous ones. The Doubling Circuit you'll trace next week works the same on integers in the millions as it does on the integer 1. The polar pairs hold. The family groups hold. The math is *scale-invariant*.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> Some students hear *scale-invariant* and feel the floor open beneath them. The familiar idea that bigger numbers carry bigger meaning — the budget is bigger than the bake sale, the city is bigger than the village — turns out to be one kind of meaning. The Symbol points at another kind. Two billion and one share a vertex. They are the same, in this reading, in a specific way.
>
> *[long pause]*
>
> What would it mean to take that seriously? Not as a metaphysical claim. As a *mathematical* one. The structure of base-ten arithmetic is genuinely scale-invariant under digital-root reduction. The numbers really don't get bigger, in the sense the Symbol cares about.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> The numbers in your bank account, the populations of cities you'll never visit, the count of stars in galaxies — all reduce to nine vertices. The scale of the world is, in this one specific reading, a kind of decoration on top of nine fundamental positions."
