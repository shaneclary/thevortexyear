## 0. Authoring notes
Apply digital-root reduction to numbers from the student's life. Concrete: birthday, address, phone number, year. The student walks away with several small marks on their printed Symbol — vertices that *belong* to them. Practical, specific, useful.

## 1. Outcome
By the end of these 6 minutes, the student has reduced at least four personally meaningful numbers and marked their positions on the printed Symbol.

## 2. Hook (0:00–0:30)
*[On screen: a printed Symbol with several small coloured dots placed at various vertices. Each dot is labelled with what it represents — birthday, address, phone, year.]*

> "Reduce a number from your life. Mark its vertex. The Symbol becomes personal."

## 3. Setup (0:30–1:00)
> "Lens 6 of 9. Apply. Today: digital-root reduction on numbers that matter to you. The Symbol becomes a map of where you live."

## 4. Body (1:00–4:30)

> "The operation we've been doing — reduce by digit-summing — works on any whole number. Most students learn it on abstract examples and then move on. But the real grip the operation has on your attention comes from applying it to *your* numbers.
>
> Pick four. The course suggests these starters:
>
> **Your birth date.** Written as a single number. If you were born on August 14, 1989, write 19890814 and reduce. One plus nine plus eight plus nine plus zero plus eight plus one plus four. Forty. Reduce: four plus zero. Four. Birth date reduces to four. Mark a vertex.
>
> **Your house number.** If you live at 1247 Main Street, reduce 1247. One plus two plus four plus seven. Fourteen. Reduce: one plus four. Five. House reduces to five. Mark another vertex.
>
> **Your phone number's last four digits.** Take only the last four — the part that's most personal. Reduce. Mark.
>
> **The current year.** 2026 reduces to 1, as we showed in Lesson 2. Mark.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> Look at your Symbol. Four marks. Four vertices that belong to your life. The Symbol is no longer abstract — it's annotated with your geography, your time, your contact. The reduction collapses the personal numbers into Symbol positions.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> Marko, when he teaches in person, asks students to do this on the first day of a workshop. He says students who have *their* numbers on the Symbol look at the Symbol differently afterward. The diagram has become a kind of address book. The vertex that holds your birthday isn't more important than the others — but you know where it is, and you remember it.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> Practical use. By Week 13, you'll find yourself reducing numbers as a habit. The check at a restaurant arrives with a total — you reduce it without thinking. A friend gives you a phone number — you notice its digital root. The book you're reading has 247 pages — you reduce 247 to 4 and notice. This isn't superstition or numerology — it's the operation becoming part of how you read the world.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> The point isn't that numbers from your life have *meaning* via their digital roots. The point is that the operation, once habituated, gives you another *register* for reading numbers. A long phone number, otherwise undifferentiated, becomes a string of vertices on the Symbol you've memorised. A page count becomes a vertex. The world stays the same; your reading of it gets richer."

## 5. Try it (4:30–5:30)
