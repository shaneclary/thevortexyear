## 0. Authoring notes
*Reducing a phone number — what does your day reduce to?* Apply the *seeing* frame from Lens 4 to the student's own life. Slow pacing. Concrete-personal contemplative beat.

## 1. Outcome
By the end of these 6 minutes, the student has reduced a personal piece of data — a phone number, a date, a sequence of times — and sat with the act of seeing it placed.

## 2. Hook (0:00–0:30)
*[On screen: a phone number — partially obscured, just example digits — slowly transforming into its digital root and landing on a Symbol vertex.]*

> *(slow)* "Reduce your phone number. What do you see?"

## 3. Setup (0:30–1:00)
> "Lens 5 of 9. The contemplative beat for the practical module. Six minutes. Reduce a number from your life and sit with it."

## 4. Body (1:00–4:30) — *contemplative, paced, sparse*

> *(slow)* "We have spent the week proving that every whole number reduces to a single digit. The proof is sound. The operation is automatic now. The spreadsheet does it in one cell.
>
> *[long pause]*
>
> But the proof and the tool are means to an end. The end is what Marko named yesterday. *Seeing*. The number, after reduction, lives in a place. A vertex on the Symbol. A position with neighbours.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> Take your phone number. Or the year you were born. Or today's date written as a number. Reduce it.
>
> *[long pause — give the student time]*
>
> What digit did you arrive at?
>
> *[pause]*
>
> One. Two. Three. Four. Five. Six. Seven. Eight. Nine. Whatever your digit, your number now sits at that vertex of the Symbol on your wall.
>
> *[long pause]*
>
> Notice what your eye does, looking at the vertex. The vertex hasn't changed. It's the same spot on the same diagram. But your phone number is now *there*. The Symbol you've been looking at all week now holds something specific from your life.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> Marko's claim, the one we heard yesterday, is that *seeing* is what the operation produces. The number was abstract; now it's placed. The reduction is the act of placement. And once a number is placed, the diagram is no longer abstract — it carries a specific piece of *you*.
>
> *[long pause — 12 seconds]*
>
> Some students, when they first reduce a personal number, feel something quiet. The Symbol becomes — just slightly — a thing about them. Not loudly. Not dramatically. The way a name on a building feels different from a name on someone else's building.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> Marko, when he describes the practitioner's relationship to the Symbol, says it eventually becomes a place where your numbers live. *Your house. Your year. Your phone. Your daughter's birthday. Your grandfather's address.* Each one reduces to a vertex. Over time, the Symbol's vertices become populated by your life.
>
> *[long pause]*
>
> What does *your* day reduce to today? Take the seconds since midnight, or the date, or the sum of meaningful numbers from your morning. Reduce it. The digit you arrive at is — for today — your day's vertex.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> Tomorrow it will be different. Days reduce to different digits depending on how you count them. The point isn't permanence. The point is that the Symbol can hold *today*. That's what the operation does, finally. It places days."

## 5. Try it (4:30–5:30)
> *(softer)* "In your notebook, write today's date as a number — for example, 20260508 — and reduce it. The single digit at the end is today's vertex. Mark it on your printed Symbol with a small dot. Date the dot. Sixty seconds."

*[Pause card on screen for 60 seconds.]*

> "Welcome back. Today has a vertex. Tomorrow will have another."

## 6. Bridge (5:30–6:00)
> *(returning to ordinary tempo)* "Next lesson — Apply. We've sat with the personal placement. Next we look at where reduction lands in a real Rodin Coil build — the practical use of the operation in counting turns. Lens 6."
