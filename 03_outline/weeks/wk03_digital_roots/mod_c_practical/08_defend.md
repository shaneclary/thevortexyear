## 0. Authoring notes
Ninth Defend lesson. The objection: *I'm a builder. Why does this whole digit-root week matter for me? I just want to wind a coil.* Honest answer: digit-root reduction is the underlying check that distinguishes a build with sound counts from a build that drifts. Without the operation, your turn-counts and measurements have no error-detection layer. With it, you have a fast verification tool. Concrete answer about engineering reproducibility.

## 1. Outcome
By the end of these 6 minutes, the student can answer *why does digit-root reduction matter for engineering reproducibility?* with one calibrated-answer sentence.

## 4. Body (1:00–4:30)

> "The objection in its strongest form. *I'm taking this course to build a Rodin Coil. I expected coil topology, winding mechanics, measurement protocols. Instead I just spent a week on digit-summing — an arithmetic operation that's been in textbooks for a thousand years. Why is this in the course? Why not skip to the build?*
>
> The objection is fair. A first-time builder *can* wind a coil without any of this week's material. The course doesn't gatekeep the build behind digit-roots; if you skipped Week 3 you could still complete the Week 33 build session.
>
> Here's why we put it in the course anyway, with specifics.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> The first reason — *turn-count verification*. We covered this in Lesson 6. As you wind, the digital-root milestone check at every 24 turns — *should be at vertex 6 / 3 / 9 / 6* — catches off-by-two errors that would otherwise propagate through the build. A builder who skipped this week would still wind a coil; they'd be more likely to wind a coil with a counting error and not notice until measurement time.
>
> The second reason — *cross-build comparison*. When you wind a second coil and a third coil, you'll want to compare them. *Was the second build longer? Faster? More uniform?* Digital-root reductions of timestamps and measurements give you compressed signatures for each build session. Without the operation, build comparison requires raw-number examination. With it, you have a one-glance signature.
>
> The third reason — *foundation for everything that follows*. The Doubling Circuit (next week), the polar pairs (Week 6), the family groups (Week 7), the torus wrap (Week 16) — all of these rest on digital-root structure. A builder who skipped Week 3 would meet the Doubling Circuit in Week 4 and have to backfill the proof of why digit-summing equals mod-9. Better to settle the foundation now.
>
> The fourth reason — *engineering culture*. Builders who measure and verify outperform builders who eyeball. Digit-summing is one of the cheapest verification tools available — sixty seconds with a spreadsheet macro replaces minutes of error-checking by hand. A build culture that uses checksums catches errors a build culture that doesn't, misses. This week installs the culture before the builds start.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> The calibrated answer to *why this week for a builder?*:
>
> *Digital-root reduction is the cheapest verification tool in the course. It catches turn-count errors during winding, gives you cross-build comparison signatures, founds every later concept the builds rely on, and installs a measurement culture before the actual builds begin.*
>
> Forty-three words. The structure is concrete: four uses, each tied to a specific build context.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> One more thing. Most builders, by Week 33, are grateful for this week. The students who wind their first coil with the operation in their hand catch their own errors and finish faster. The students who skipped come back and ask if they can review Week 3 between Week 33's first attempt and Week 33's second attempt. The course recommends not skipping the foundation."
