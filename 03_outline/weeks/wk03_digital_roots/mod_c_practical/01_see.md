## 0. Authoring notes
The first practical lesson on digit-root reduction. The student watches the operation applied to *measurement values* — millimetres, seconds, grams, turn-counts — the kinds of numbers a builder works with. The Symbol stops being abstract and becomes a tool for reading workshop data.

## 1. Outcome
By the end of these 6 minutes, the student can apply digit-root reduction to typical measurement values and identify the Symbol vertex of any reading.

## 2. Hook (0:00–0:30)
*[On screen: a workbench. A ruler reading 247 mm. A stopwatch reading 1:23.46. A scale reading 318 g. Each value reduces to a single digit and lands on the Symbol.]*

> "247 millimetres. 83 seconds. 318 grams. Three measurements, three vertices. Today: the Symbol becomes a workshop tool."

## 3. Setup (0:30–1:00)
> "Lens 1 of 9, Module C. Practical lens. We've proved the operation; today we use it. Six minutes on measurements."

## 4. Body (1:00–4:30)

> "A practitioner working on a Rodin Coil reads numbers all day. Wire length in millimetres. Stopwatch readings during a winding session. Resistance values in ohms. Inductance in microhenries. Turn counts. Most of these numbers are larger than nine. Each one has a single-digit shadow on the Symbol.
>
> Watch.
>
> *[show a measurement: ruler reading 247 mm]*
>
> 247. Reduce: 2 + 4 + 7 = 13. Reduce: 1 + 3 = 4. Vertex 4. The wire length, on the Symbol, sits at the 4 position.
>
> *[show: stopwatch reading 83 seconds]*
>
> 83. Reduce: 8 + 3 = 11. Reduce: 1 + 1 = 2. Vertex 2. The winding session's duration sits at the 2 position.
>
> *[show: scale reading 318 grams]*
>
> 318. Reduce: 3 + 1 + 8 = 12. Reduce: 1 + 2 = 3. Vertex 3. The wire's mass at the 3 position.
>
> *[pause — show all three measurements with their vertex placements on the Symbol]*
>
> Three workshop numbers. Three vertices. The reduction takes the unwieldy measurement and gives you a single-digit summary that places it on the Symbol. Why does this matter for a builder?
>
> Two reasons.
>
> First — *quick verification*. When you've measured the same wire twice and the readings differ by more than expected, reducing both readings to digit-roots gives you an immediate flag. Same digit-root means the readings are *consistent mod 9*, which catches typos and transcription errors. Different digit-root means there's a substantive difference. Faster than re-comparing the full numbers.
>
> Second — *Symbol-anchored thinking*. Once you've reduced enough measurements, the Symbol becomes the surface you read your data on. A wire that reduces to 4 lives at the same vertex as the year 22 and the page count 49. They're not *the same* — but they're at the same *place* on the Symbol. The space of measurements partitions onto nine vertices.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> Practical reality. Not every workshop reading needs reducing. You don't reduce every voltage you measure during a coil test — that would slow the work. You reduce *intentionally*, when the question is whether two readings agree, or when you want to see which vertex a session's results cluster around. The reduction is a tool; you use it when it earns its space."

## 5. Try it (4:30–5:30)
> "Pause. Pick three numbers from your build log so far — page counts, line counts, the day's date as a number. Reduce each. Place a small dot on your printed Symbol at each vertex. Sixty seconds."

*[Pause card on screen for 60 seconds.]*

> "Welcome back. Three workshop numbers, three vertices marked. The Symbol is now your data surface."
