## 0. Authoring notes
Casting out nines as the historical accounting check. The student should leave knowing the operation has been used by clerks and bookkeepers for over a thousand years, and that VBM's digit-summing is the same technique with new names and uses.

## 4. Body (1:00–4:30)

> "*Casting out nines* is the name historical accountants gave to digit-root reduction used as an arithmetic check. The technique appears in arithmetic textbooks from at least the twelfth century — the Indian mathematician Bhāskara II describes it in *Lilavati* around 1150 — and was standard in European accounting books through the early twentieth century.
>
> The accounting use case. A clerk adds a long column of numbers. Say five thousand line items, each one a few digits, totalling several million. The clerk writes down a total. How does the clerk verify the total is correct without re-doing the entire addition?
>
> *Cast out nines.* Reduce each line item to its digit-root. Sum the digit-roots. Reduce the result. Compare to the digit-root of the claimed total. If they match, the addition is *probably* correct. If they don't match, there's an error somewhere, and the clerk re-checks.
>
> The technique catches most arithmetic mistakes — single-digit errors, transposition errors, missed line items — at a fraction of the cost of re-adding the full column. *Probably correct* because some errors slip through (any error that preserves the digit-root mod 9), but those are rare in practice. The technique is fast and effective.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> Now place that next to what we did this week.
>
> Same operation. Reduce a number by digit-summing until one digit remains. Identical procedure.
>
> Different use cases. The clerk casts out nines to verify a total. We use the operation to *position* numbers on the Symbol — to read structure off the digital roots, not just to check arithmetic.
>
> Two specific differences worth carrying.
>
> First — *direction of attention*. The clerk treats the digit-root as a disposable byproduct, useful only for the verification step. Once the addition is verified, the digit-roots are forgotten. We treat the digit-roots as *first-class objects* — vertices on the Symbol, positions, classes that group numbers together. The reduction isn't a means to an end; it's the end.
>
> Second — *use of the result*. The clerk uses the digit-root only for matching (verify equality). We use it for *placement* (which vertex), *grouping* (what other numbers reduce here), *sequence analysis* (what's the pattern of digit-roots over time). VBM treats the operation as a generator of structure; accounting treats it as a checksum.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> Same operation. Eight hundred years of use. Different traditions of attention to what it produces.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> When a working accountant asks what you're doing — and they sometimes do — *casting out nines* is the right name to give them. It places the operation in a tradition they recognise. *Digit-root reduction in vortex-based mathematics* is correct but loses them. *I'm casting out nines, then doing something more with the result* is the bridge."
