## 4. Body (1:00–4:30)

*[Camera follows hands.]*

> "Take your torus former from Wk 4. Add three new strings — three different shades of one colour, or three different patterns. We're winding family 1: vertices 1, 4, 7 each becoming the start of a doubling-cycle traversal.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> Strand A — start at vertex 1. Wind 1 → 2 → 4 → 8 → 7 → 5 → back to 1. Six segments.
>
> Strand B — start at vertex 4. Wind 4 → 8 → 7 → 5 → 1 → 2 → back to 4. Six segments.
>
> Strand C — start at vertex 7. Wind 7 → 5 → 1 → 2 → 4 → 8 → back to 7. Six segments.
>
> *[pause — show the three strands on the former]*
>
> Look at what you've made. Three strands, all winding the same doubling cycle, but each starting at a different family-1 vertex. The strands trace the same helix, offset from each other by *two cycle steps* (since 1 → 4 → 7 are spaced 2 doubling-steps apart in the cycle 1, 2, 4, 8, 7, 5).
>
> *[pause]*
>
> The three strands together form the *family-1 phase* of a three-phase coil. Phase 2 (family 2) would add three more strands starting at 2, 5, 8 — also offset by 2 cycle steps from each other, but offset by 1 step from family 1.
>
> Phase 3 (family 3) would add the {3, 6, 9} strands — but those don't trace the doubling cycle. They oscillate or stay fixed.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> The full three-phase build would have all nine strands on one form. Today's mock-up is just family 1 — enough to see the *triadic* structure of the phase.
>
> One observation. The three strands of a single phase are *equally spaced* in the doubling cycle. They trace the same helix but at three different *phases* (in the temporal sense — like three sine waves shifted in time). This is exactly the structure of three-phase electrical power: same waveform, three temporal offsets.
>
> The family-1 mock-up shows you, in your hands, why the families are sometimes called *phases* in coil-building contexts."
