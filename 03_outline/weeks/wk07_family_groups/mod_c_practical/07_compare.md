## 0. Authoring notes
Three-phase electrical systems vs. Rodin family phasing. Standard three-phase has been used in power and motors for over a century; Rodin's family-phasing maps onto it directly but with cycle-following winding.

## 4. Body (1:00–4:30)

> "Standard three-phase electrical engineering and the Rodin Coil's family-phased configuration share the *same drive principle* — three currents at 120° offset — but use different *winding geometries*.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> Standard three-phase. Used in:
> - Power transmission (national grid voltages are typically three-phase).
> - Industrial motors (induction motors, synchronous motors).
> - Generators (alternators in cars, power-plant turbines).
>
> Standard three-phase windings are *evenly-spaced in angle* — three windings 120° apart on a stator (motor's fixed core). The drive currents are 120° offset in time. The combination produces a rotating magnetic field.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> Rodin family-phased. The three phases are *evenly-spaced in cycle position* — each family triangle is 120° rotated from the others on the Symbol, which translates to a specific spacing on the toroidal surface. The drive currents are 120° offset in time, same as standard. The combination produces a rotating field, but the *path* of rotation follows the cycle structure rather than purely angular positions.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> Three differences worth carrying.
>
> *Difference one — geometry.* Standard: cylindrical stator with windings on the inside surface. Rodin: toroidal core with windings following the doubling cycle.
>
> *Difference two — symmetry.* Standard: continuous rotational symmetry around the stator axis. Rodin: discrete nine-fold rotational symmetry (the Symbol's nine vertices) plus the cycle's specific traversal pattern.
>
> *Difference three — current paths.* Standard: each phase's current flows through one continuous winding. Rodin: each phase's current flows through three separate strands (one per family-element start position) that together cover the doubling cycle.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> Useful framing. The Rodin family-phased coil is *a three-phase configuration on a doubly-decorated torus* — decorated by the doubling cycle and the family triangles. An EE engineer reading this description would understand the geometry and could analyse it using standard three-phase theory plus toroidal-core corrections.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> What this means for testing. The Rodin family-phased coil's behaviour can be compared to a standard three-phase coil's behaviour by *driving both at the same configuration* and *measuring identical response curves*. Differences in the response curves attribute to the *geometry* — the family-cycle vs. standard winding. That's a clean experimental comparison."
