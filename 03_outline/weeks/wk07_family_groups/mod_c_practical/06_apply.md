## 4. Body (1:00–4:30)

> "A three-phase Rodin Coil's electromagnetic behaviour depends on how the three phases are *driven* — at what relative timings the three currents flow through the three families' strands.
>
> Three standard drive configurations.
>
> *Configuration one — same-phase drive.* All three families driven at the same timing — same waveform, no temporal offset. The three families' fields add directly. Result: a static field structure with three-fold symmetry but no rotation.
>
> *Configuration two — 120° offset drive.* Standard three-phase configuration, borrowed from electrical power. Family 1 leads; family 2 lags by 120°; family 3 lags by 240° (or leads by 120° in the opposite direction). Result: a *rotating field* — the field structure rotates around the torus's central axis at the drive frequency.
>
> *Configuration three — 60° offset drive.* Family 1 leads; family 2 lags by 60°; family 3 lags by 120°. Half the offset of standard three-phase. Result: a different rotating field with different temporal characteristics.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> Which one to use depends on what you're testing.
>
> Same-phase drive isolates the family geometry's effect — the rotating component is removed. Useful as a baseline.
>
> 120° offset drive is the standard three-phase configuration; comparing the Rodin Coil's response to a standard three-phase test gives you a direct comparison to mainstream three-phase electrical engineering.
>
> 60° offset is *VBM-specific*. Some practitioners argue it produces field structures that 120° doesn't. Whether that's true is a measurable question.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> Practical setup. To drive three phases with controlled timing, you need a *three-phase signal generator* — either a piece of bench equipment or three function generators synchronised by a common clock. Modern digital signal generators can do all three configurations programmatically; older analog setups require mechanical phase-shifters.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> One observation. Three-phase electrical motors use 120° offset drive. The Rodin Coil's family-phased configuration *with 120° drive* would behave electromagnetically similar to a three-phase motor's stator — but with the cycle-following winding rather than parallel-turn winding. Whether the cycle-following geometry produces different motor-like behaviour from a standard stator is exactly the kind of question Q3 testing addresses."
