## 4. Body (1:00–4:30)

> "The objection. *The Wk-35 build uses Polar Pairs (six strands, three pairs). The advanced family-phased build uses Family Groups (nine strands, three triads). Why have both? One should be enough. Are we engineering complexity for its own sake?*
>
> The objection is fair. Both partitions seem to do similar things — give you a structured multi-strand winding. Why two structures?
>
> Because they enable different engineering goals.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> *Polar-pair builds enable field-cancellation engineering.* Three antipodal pairs give three orthogonal cancellation axes. Useful when you want *zero net field* in chosen directions — for shielding, for noise-cancellation, for isolation experiments.
>
> *Family-phased builds enable rotating-field engineering.* Three families with 120° offset drive give a rotating field that follows the family triangles. Useful when you want *rotation* — for motor-like behaviour, for spatial scanning, for time-varying field experiments.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> The two builds aren't redundant. They're tuned to different engineering questions.
>
> Cancellation experiments need pair-pair antipodes. Rotation experiments need family-cycle phases. A serious researcher might build *both* — the Wk-35 polar-pair coil for cancellation tests and the Wk-35 advanced family-phased coil for rotation tests. The two builds together let you ask both kinds of questions.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> So the calibrated answer to *why both partitions matter for builds?*:
>
> *Polar Pairs enable field-cancellation engineering (three orthogonal cancellation axes); Family Groups enable rotating-field engineering (three-phase rotation following the cycle structure). Different engineering goals require different builds. A complete experimental setup uses both.*
>
> Forty-two words.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> One nuance. Most students never build both. The single-strand Wk-33 build is the canonical first build; the polar-pair Wk-35 build is the standard advancement. The family-phased build is *advanced* — it requires three-phase drive equipment, more wire, more time. Students who pursue it tend to be either dedicated researchers or builders specifically interested in rotating-field experiments. The course supports the family-phased build but doesn't require it. The two-tier ambition (single + polar-pair) covers most students; the three-tier ambition (single + polar-pair + family-phased) covers the dedicated few."
