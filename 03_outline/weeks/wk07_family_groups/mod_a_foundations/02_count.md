## 4. Body (1:00–4:30)

> "Compute each Family Number Group's sum.
>
> *Family one — {1, 4, 7}.* Sum: *1 + 4 + 7 = 12*. Reduce: *1 + 2 = 3*. Digital root 3.
>
> *Family two — {2, 5, 8}.* Sum: *2 + 5 + 8 = 15*. Reduce: *1 + 5 = 6*. Digital root 6.
>
> *Family three — {3, 6, 9}.* Sum: *3 + 6 + 9 = 18*. Reduce: *1 + 8 = 9*. Digital root 9.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> Notice the pattern. The three family sums reduce to *3, 6, 9* — which is *the third family itself*. The family sums *are* the third family. There's a self-referential structure embedded in the partition.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> Why does this happen? Each family contains three elements; each element is congruent to a fixed residue mod 3 (the family's residue). Sum of three elements with residue *r* mod 3 is congruent to *3r mod 9*. For *r = 1*: *3 × 1 = 3*. For *r = 2*: *3 × 2 = 6*. For *r = 0*: *3 × 0 = 0 ≡ 9*. The sums are the multiples of 3 mod 9 — which is exactly family three.
>
> The math is forced. Three families sum to 3, 6, 9 because of the residue-mod-3 partition. Marko reads this as another *coincidence* of structure — the partition's classes sum to the elements of one of the classes.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> One more observation. The three sums (3, 6, 9) are *the elements of the third family*. So the partition contains a kind of *self-pointer* — family three holds the *labels* of the partition itself. This isn't a triviality; it's a recursive property of the partition.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> What this gives us. *The third family is special* — it's both a coset (the multiples of 3 mod 9) *and* the label set for the partition. The first two families have no analogous role; they're just the non-multiples-of-3 residue classes."
