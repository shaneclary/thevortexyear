## 0. Authoring notes
First Mod-A on Family Number Groups. Three triads coloured on the Symbol: {1,4,7}, {2,5,8}, {3,6,9}. Each triad steps by 3. Visual-first.

## 1. Outcome
Student can identify the three Family Number Groups on the Symbol and trace each as a triangle.

## 2. Hook (0:00–0:30)
*[Symbol with three coloured triangles overlaid: red {1,4,7}, blue {2,5,8}, green {3,6,9}.]*

> "Three triads. Three triangles. Six minutes."

## 3. Setup (0:30–1:00)
> "Lens 1 of 9, Week 7. Family Number Groups. The Symbol's other partition."

## 4. Body (1:00–4:30)

> "Look at your Symbol. Find vertex 1. Step three positions clockwise — you land on 4. Step three more — you land on 7. Step three more — you're back at 1. Three vertices form a triangle on the Symbol.
>
> The triangle is the *first Family Number Group*: {1, 4, 7}.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> Find vertex 2. Step three: 5. Step three: 8. Step three: back to 2. Second triangle. *Second Family Number Group*: {2, 5, 8}.
>
> Find vertex 3. Step three: 6. Step three: 9. Step three: back to 3. Third triangle. *Third Family Number Group*: {3, 6, 9}.
>
> *[pause — show all three triangles overlaid]*
>
> Three triangles. Three families. Together they tile every vertex of the Symbol. Every digit from 1 to 9 belongs to exactly one family.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> Compare to last week's Polar Pairs. Pairs partitioned the six units {1, 2, 4, 5, 7, 8} into three pairs of additive inverses. Families partition *all nine vertices* {1, 2, 3, 4, 5, 6, 7, 8, 9} into three triads of step-by-3 elements. Different partitions, different structures, both real.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> One observation. Each family has *one element* in {3, 6, 9} — that is, one element divisible by 3. The first family {1, 4, 7} has no such element... wait, that's wrong. Let me re-state. Family one: {1, 4, 7} — none of these is divisible by 3. Family two: {2, 5, 8} — none divisible by 3. Family three: {3, 6, 9} — *all three divisible by 3*.
>
> So the third family is *the multiples of 3 modulo 9*. Families one and two are the *non-multiples of 3* — split by residue modulo 3.
>
> Specifically: family one is digits with *residue 1* modulo 3 (since 1, 4, 7 all ≡ 1 mod 3). Family two is digits with *residue 2* modulo 3 (2, 5, 8 ≡ 2 mod 3). Family three is digits with *residue 0* modulo 3 (3, 6, 9 ≡ 0 mod 3).
>
> The three families are the three *residue classes mod 3*. We're nesting one modular structure inside another.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> Today is the visual. Tomorrow Mod B will give the formal cosets-of-3 structure."
