## 0. Authoring notes
Compare Family Number Groups (cosets of 3 in ℤ/9ℤ) to Polar Pairs (additive inverse pairs). Two different partitions of the same Symbol.

## 1. Outcome
Student can articulate the two different partitions of the Symbol — pairs and families — and what each captures.

## 2. Hook (0:00–0:30)
*[Symbol with both pair-diameters and family-triangles overlaid.]*

> "Two partitions. One Symbol. Six minutes."

## 3. Setup (0:30–1:00)
> "Lens 7 of 9. Compare. Pairs vs. families."

## 4. Body (1:00–4:30)

> "The Symbol has two well-defined partitions of its vertices into smaller groups:
>
> *Polar Pairs (Wk 6).* Three pairs of additive inverses among the units of `(ℤ/9ℤ)*`. Pairs: (1, 8), (2, 7), (4, 5). Excludes {3, 6, 9}. *Six elements partitioned into three pairs.*
>
> *Family Number Groups (Wk 7).* Three triads of step-by-3 elements. Triads: {1, 4, 7}, {2, 5, 8}, {3, 6, 9}. Includes all nine vertices. *Nine elements partitioned into three triads.*
>
> Two different partitions, two different sizes, two different relationships to the underlying algebra.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> Three differences worth carrying.
>
> *Difference one — what's included.* Pairs cover only the units (six of nine vertices). Families cover all nine vertices. The pairs leave out {3, 6, 9}; the third family explicitly contains them.
>
> *Difference two — algebraic content.* Pairs are *additive-inverse pairs* in `ℤ/9ℤ`. Families are *cosets of the subgroup 3ℤ/9ℤ*. Different group-theoretic objects, both real.
>
> *Difference three — geometric shape.* Pairs are diameters (lines through the centre). Families are triangles (equilateral, 120° apart). Different polygons.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> Despite the differences, the two partitions interact. Each Family Number Group contains *one element from each Polar Pair* — but offset:
>
> Family 1 = {1, 4, 7}. From pair (1,8): contains 1. From pair (4,5): contains 4. From pair (2,7): contains 7. So family 1 has exactly one element from each polar pair.
>
> Same for family 2 = {2, 5, 8}. From pair (2,7): 2. From pair (4,5): 5. From pair (1,8): 8. One element from each pair.
>
> Family 3 = {3, 6, 9}. None of the polar pairs intersect with family 3 (since {3,6,9} is disjoint from the polar-pair members).
>
> *[pause]*
>
> So the families and pairs *cross-cut* in a specific way: families 1 and 2 each contain a *complete set of representatives* of the three polar pairs. Family 3 is *outside* the polar-pair structure.
>
> This cross-cutting is what makes the Symbol structurally rich. The two partitions reinforce each other rather than overlapping trivially.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> Useful framing. *Polar Pairs partition the units; Family Groups partition all vertices including the non-units. The two partitions are independent and interact in the cross-cut structure.*"
