## 4. Body (1:00–4:30)

> "Claim: the three Family Number Groups *F₁ = {1,4,7}*, *F₂ = {2,5,8}*, *F₃ = {3,6,9}* are exactly the three cosets of *H = 3ℤ/9ℤ = {0, 3, 6}* in *G = ℤ/9ℤ*.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> *Step one — show each family is a coset.*
>
> A coset *a + H* is generated by adding *H*'s elements to a representative *a*. Show each *Fᵢ* equals *aᵢ + H* for some representative.
>
> For *F₁*: pick representative *a₁ = 1*. Compute *1 + H = {1+0, 1+3, 1+6} = {1, 4, 7}*. Match.
>
> For *F₂*: pick *a₂ = 2*. Compute *2 + H = {2, 5, 8}*. Match.
>
> For *F₃*: pick *a₃ = 0* (which is 9 in our representation). Compute *0 + H = {0, 3, 6} = {3, 6, 9}*. Match.
>
> Each family is exactly one coset.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> *Step two — show these three cosets are all the cosets.*
>
> *G* has 9 elements. *|H| = 3*. By Lagrange's theorem, the number of cosets is *|G| / |H| = 9 / 3 = 3*. So *exactly three cosets exist*.
>
> We've identified three cosets: *F₁, F₂, F₃*. There can't be more (Lagrange caps the count at 3). They are pairwise disjoint (cosets are always disjoint by basic group theory). Their union covers *G* (cosets always partition the group).
>
> So *F₁, F₂, F₃* are exactly the three cosets of *H* in *G*. Done.
>
> *[pause — show the proof on screen]*
>
> Two steps. The proof relies on Lagrange's theorem (subgroup order divides group order, gives coset count) and basic coset properties (cosets partition the group).
>
> *[pause]*
>
> One useful generalisation. The same technique gives the cosets of *kℤ/nℤ* in *ℤ/nℤ* for any *k* dividing *n*. For *k = 3, n = 9*: three cosets of size 3, our case. For *k = 3, n = 12*: four cosets of size 3 in ℤ/12ℤ. For *k = 4, n = 12*: three cosets of size 4. The structure is general; the family-group case is the *k=3, n=9* instance.
>
> The Symbol of Enlightenment, treated mathematically, is just the additive group ℤ/9ℤ with its subgroup 3ℤ/9ℤ. The Family Number Groups are the cosets. Standard quotient group theory."
