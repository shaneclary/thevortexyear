## 0. Authoring notes
Cosets vs. equivalence classes — same partition, different vocabularies. Show that the Family Number Groups can be described both ways and the descriptions are equivalent.

## 1. Outcome
Student can describe the Family Number Groups in coset vocabulary and equivalence-class vocabulary.

> "Two vocabularies. Same partition."

## 3. Setup (0:30–1:00)

## 4. Body (1:00–4:30)

> "The Family Number Groups can be described in two equivalent vocabularies: as *cosets* of a subgroup, or as *equivalence classes* of an equivalence relation.
>
> *Coset vocabulary.* The families are cosets of *3ℤ/9ℤ* in *ℤ/9ℤ*. Specifically: *F₁ = 1 + 3ℤ*, *F₂ = 2 + 3ℤ*, *F₃ = 0 + 3ℤ*. Coset notation makes the additive structure explicit.
>
> *Equivalence-class vocabulary.* Define the relation *a ~ b* iff *a − b ≡ 0 (mod 3)*. This is an equivalence relation (reflexive, symmetric, transitive). The equivalence classes under *~* are the families. *F₁ = [1] = {x : x ~ 1} = {x : x ≡ 1 (mod 3)}*, etc.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> The two vocabularies are equivalent. Cosets of *H* in *G* under addition are exactly the equivalence classes of the relation *a ~ b ⟺ a − b ∈ H*. Same partition, two languages.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> When to use which.
>
> *Coset vocabulary* is preferred when:
> - You want to emphasise the *quotient group* structure (the families form a smaller group ℤ/3ℤ).
> - You want to use additive notation (*1 + 3ℤ*) rather than set-builder notation.
> - You're working with arbitrary subgroups and want to track which subgroup is being quotiented out.
>
> *Equivalence-class vocabulary* is preferred when:
> - You want to emphasise the *partition* itself rather than the quotient structure.
> - You're working in a context where the equivalence relation is the central object (e.g., topology, or set theory generally).
> - The underlying group structure isn't the focus.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> Both vocabularies recover the same three families. A working algebraist would recognise either description and translate as needed. For VBM-internal use, coset vocabulary is cleaner because the additive structure is explicit. For talking to non-algebraists, equivalence-class vocabulary may be more familiar.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> Useful framing. *The Family Number Groups are the equivalence classes of digit-sum-modulo-3.* That sentence avoids both technical vocabularies — *coset* and *equivalence class* — while being mathematically precise. Useful when you don't know your audience's algebra background."
