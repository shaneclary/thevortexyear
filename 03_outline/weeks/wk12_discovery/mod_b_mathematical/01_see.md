## 4. Body (1:00–4:30)

> "The first diagram Marko drew at fifteen contained, mathematically:
>
> *Element 1 — A finite cyclic group.* The integers 1 through 9 placed around a circle in cyclic order. Equivalent to the elements of `ℤ/9ℤ` (with 0 represented as 9).
>
> *Element 2 — A specific group action.* Multiplication by 2, applied to each vertex with reduction modulo 9. The action permutes the units {1, 2, 4, 5, 7, 8} as a 6-cycle and fixes the non-units (well, the 3↔6 oscillation isn't a fixed point exactly).
>
> *Element 3 — The orbit of 1 under this action.* The cycle 1 → 2 → 4 → 8 → 7 → 5 → 1.
>
> *Element 4 — The visual figure of this orbit.* When the cycle is drawn on the placed circle, with lines connecting consecutive elements, the figure is a *figure-eight* (lemniscate).
>
> *Element 5 — Implicit higher structure.* The non-visited vertices {3, 6, 9} hint at additional structure (the 3-6-9 family). The visited units suggest polar pairs (sum-to-9 elements).
>
> *[pause]*
>
> Marko didn't articulate this content in formal group-theoretic vocabulary at fifteen. He saw the *figure*. The formal vocabulary came later (decades later, or via others reading his work).
>
> What he had at fifteen was the *visual fact*: doubling on a 9-circle traces a figure-eight. The math was in his hand even if not yet in formal notation.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> Mathematical reproducibility. Anyone can reproduce Marko's first diagram. Nine numbers on a circle, doubling, reducing — the figure-eight emerges. The discovery isn't private; it's *forced* by the operation.
>
> What's *not* reproducible without further work: the *interpretation*. Marko's reading of the figure-eight as the *infinity sign of natural number flow* requires more than the diagram itself. The diagram gives you the figure; the interpretation is added.
>
> Useful framing. *Marko's first diagram is reproducible mathematics; his interpretation of the diagram is his framework. The course teaches both — the math first, the framework as Marko's contribution.*"
