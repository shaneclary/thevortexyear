## 4. Body (1:00–4:30)

> "Marko's discovery method, distilled from his biography, can be applied to other research questions. Five components.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> *Component 1 — start from a non-mainstream question.* Marko started from Bahá'í numerology. Mainstream mathematicians wouldn't have considered Abjad reductions as a starting point. The question's *non-mainstream-ness* allowed Marko to encounter what the mainstream had missed.
>
> Application: pick research questions from outside your formal domain. Combinations of fields often produce novel results.
>
> *Component 2 — apply simple operations carefully.* Marko didn't use advanced techniques. He used arithmetic and geometric placement. The simple operations were enough to produce a non-trivial result.
>
> Application: don't dismiss simple operations. Apply them carefully and observe what they produce. Many real discoveries come from elementary moves applied with attention.
>
> *Component 3 — visualise the results.* Marko placed numbers on a circle and *traced* the operation as a path. The visualisation revealed the figure-eight that the algebra alone would have hidden.
>
> Application: visualise everything. If the algebra is invisible, the visualisation is part of the result.
>
> *Component 4 — name what you find.* Marko named the figure-eight as *the doubling cycle*. The name made the figure communicable. Without naming, the result stays private.
>
> Application: name your findings clearly. Naming is part of making them public.
>
> *Component 5 — sustain attention over decades.* Marko has refined VBM for fifty years. The discovery moment was brief; the *development* was sustained. Most insights are abandoned before they mature.
>
> Application: don't drop your findings. Refine them. Test them. Build a body of work around them.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> Practical use case for students. *Apply the five components to a question you're curious about.* Pick a non-mainstream question. Apply simple operations. Visualise the results. Name what you find. Sustain attention.
>
> If you find something, it might be a *legitimate discovery*. If you don't, the practice itself is valuable — you've engaged with the world the way authentic discoverers do.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> One observation. The discovery model isn't a *guarantee* of finding something significant. It's a *protocol*. Applying it gives you better odds than randomly trying things, and gives you the structure to recognise authentic findings when they appear.
>
> Marko's biography is suggestive, not normative. *His* method worked for *him*. Whether your application of the method produces meaningful results depends on the specific question and the depth of attention."
