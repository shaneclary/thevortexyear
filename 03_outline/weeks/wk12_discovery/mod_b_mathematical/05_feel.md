## 4. Body (1:00–4:30) — *contemplative*

> *(slow)* "Authentic discovery has specific characteristics. Sit with them.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> *Unsought.* The discoverer didn't go looking for *this specific result*. They were following a different question and the result appeared.
>
> Marko was studying Bahá'í scripture. He wasn't trying to find the figure-eight. The figure emerged.
>
> *[long pause]*
>
> *Forced.* The result wasn't *invented* by the discoverer. It was *forced* by the operation. Anyone repeating the procedure gets the same result.
>
> Marko's figure-eight isn't *his*. It's what the math gives anyone who does the procedure.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> *Surprising in retrospect.* Once the result is seen, the discoverer often wonders why nobody noticed before. The result feels *retrospectively obvious*, but it required someone to actually do the procedure.
>
> Marko has said variations of this for fifty years. *I didn't invent it; I drew it; the drawing is so simple anyone could have done it; nobody had.*
>
> *[long pause — 12 seconds]*
>
> *Followed.* The discoverer doesn't drop the result after the initial moment. They follow it. Refine it. Test it. Build a body of work around it.
>
> Marko has refined VBM for fifty years. The framework continues to develop.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> What does this *feel* like, contemplatively? Some students experience a kind of *awe* at the structure of authentic discovery. The discoverer is more *witness* than *creator*; they noticed something that was already there.
>
> Other students experience *discomfort* — if discovery is forced rather than created, what's the role of human creativity?
>
> Both responses are honest. The structure of discovery raises real questions about agency and originality.
>
> *[long pause]*
>
> Marko's framing — *I drew what was already there* — is humble. He doesn't claim originality in the sense of *invented from nothing*. He claims attention — *I noticed*.
>
> The math was waiting. He attended. The figure-eight appeared.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> What's waiting that you might attend to? The structure of authentic discovery suggests answers are out there, *available* to whoever attends carefully.
>
> Marko's biography, contemplated, is encouraging. Discovery is accessible. It requires attention more than it requires genius."
