## 0. Authoring notes
Marko's path vs. conventional industrial R&D pipelines.

## 1. Outcome
Student can articulate the difference between conventional R&D and Marko's individual-builder path, and judge what each is good for.

## 2. Hook (0:00–0:30)
*[Two side-by-side images: a corporate R&D lab — clean, fluorescent, multi-team — versus a single workbench with one person and a wound coil.]*

> "Two paths. Same problem space. Different tools. Six minutes."

## 3. Setup (0:30–1:00)
> "Lens 7 of 9. Compare."

## 4. Body (1:00–4:30)

> "*Conventional R&D pipeline.* A funded research organisation. Twenty to two hundred staff. A defined research question, often dictated by funding. A project plan with milestones, deliverables, and review cycles. Specialised equipment — clean rooms, network analysers, electromagnetic chambers, machinist support, in-house simulation. Publication targets in peer-reviewed journals. Patents filed by the institution. Lifecycle of a project: typically three to seven years. Result distribution: papers, patents, sometimes products.
>
> *Marko's path.* One person. No funding. A research question chosen freely. No project plan. No milestones. Hardware-store equipment. Notebooks rather than journal submissions. Patents are individually filed, occasionally. Lifecycle: forty-plus years and ongoing. Result distribution: lectures, books, foundation-distributed coil kits, and a community of practitioners.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> Five differences worth being explicit about.
>
> *Difference one — funding model.* Conventional R&D is *grant-funded* or *corporate-funded*; the funder shapes the question. Marko's path is *self-funded*; he shapes the question.
>
> *Difference two — speed.* Conventional R&D is *fast within a cycle*. A three-year project produces deliverables on schedule. Marko's path is *slow per build* but *long across decades*. Each year may yield modest progress; forty years yield substantial bodies of work.
>
> *Difference three — equipment.* Conventional R&D has *specialist instrumentation*. Marko's path uses *general-purpose tools*. The work is therefore reproducible by other amateurs in a way that lab-dependent work isn't.
>
> *Difference four — peer review.* Conventional R&D is gated by *peer review*. Marko's path is gated by *community uptake* — whether other practitioners build on the work. Different gating mechanism, different incentives.
>
> *Difference five — failure cost.* Conventional R&D faces *career consequences* for failure. Marko's path has no such consequences; the only cost is his own time. He can take risks an institutional researcher couldn't.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> What conventional R&D is *good at.* Cumulative refinement of established frameworks. Making a known thing better, faster, cheaper. Operating at scales requiring expensive equipment.
>
> What conventional R&D is *less good at.* Pursuing genuinely heterodox starting points. Funding mechanisms reward extending the mainstream, not departing from it.
>
> What individual-builder path is *good at.* Pursuing heterodox questions over decades. Working at low cost. Maintaining independence from institutional incentives.
>
> What individual-builder path is *less good at.* Cumulative refinement requiring expensive instrumentation. Cross-checking with parallel teams. Producing replicable peer-reviewed measurements.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> Useful framing. *Marko's path is suited to the early generative phase of an unconventional inquiry. Conventional R&D is suited to the cumulative refinement phase of an established framework. Different stages need different infrastructures.*
>
> Marko's work is now at a phase where some conventional R&D involvement could be productive — instrumented bench measurements at engineering labs, simulation by professionals — but the *origin* required the individual-builder mode. Mainstream R&D wouldn't have funded the first prototype.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> Practical takeaway for you. You're not choosing one path or the other for your career. You're noticing that *both modes* exist. The course teaches the individual-builder mode because it matches the originating tradition. If you later go on to professional engineering work, you'll know how the two modes complement each other."
