## 4. Body (1:00–4:30)

> "Marko's *mathematical* method — the discovery model — was the subject of Module B's Apply lesson. Today is the *engineering* counterpart. How Marko works at the bench, distilled into five habits.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> *Habit 1 — start cheap.* Marko's first coil cost under fifty dollars in 1980s-equivalent parts. He didn't begin with a fully-funded lab. He began with hardware-store wire and a hand-turned wooden form. The cheapness wasn't a constraint to overcome; it was a *feature*.
>
> Application for you. Build your first version cheap. Optimise the topology before you optimise the materials. A working ten-dollar coil teaches you more than a stalled thousand-dollar one.
>
> *Habit 2 — document as you go.* Marko's notebooks from each prototype period are dense with sketches, dimensions, observations, and second thoughts. The documentation is contemporaneous, not retrospective.
>
> Application for you. Make the build-log entry *while you build*, not after. Even brief notes — wire gauge, winding tension, soldering temperature, surprises — compound across builds.
>
> *Habit 3 — iterate on a single design.* Marko refined one coil concept across decades. He didn't pivot every two years to a fundamentally different topology. He stayed with the doubling-circuit toroidal coil and refined the *implementation*.
>
> Application for you. Resist the urge to switch designs early. Build the canonical Rodin Coil. Wind it again. Wind it differently. *Then* consider variants. Most learning happens in the second and third build, not in the first.
>
> *Habit 4 — measure when possible, even crudely.* Even with no lab equipment, Marko took resistance and continuity readings. He didn't wait for state-of-the-art instrumentation. Crude measurement is informative.
>
> Application for you. A multimeter is enough for the first builds. A signal generator and oscilloscope come later. Don't postpone measurement waiting for ideal equipment.
>
> *Habit 5 — share what works.* Marko gave talks throughout development, not after. He didn't wait until the engineering was *finished* before showing it. Public exposure forced clarification of explanations and surfaced failure modes he hadn't seen.
>
> Application for you. Show your build to others as it progresses. The Rodin Foundation community, your local maker space, an online forum — surface the work. The feedback is corrective, and the act of explaining sharpens the build.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> Practical use case. Pick one habit you don't currently practice. *Just one.* Note it in your build log as a goal for your Q3 hardware build.
>
> If you don't yet have any builds in progress, the habits still apply. They scale to *any* hands-on practice — woodworking, gardening, software, music. The five habits aren't Rodin-specific. They're how anyone who works with their hands for decades gets work done.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> One observation. The habits are *cheap to adopt*. None of them require special equipment, special funding, or special talent. They require *consistency*. Most engineering competence comes not from intelligence but from consistent practice of these five habits, or others like them, sustained for years."
