## 4. Body (1:00–4:30)

> "Today is a *planning* build, not a winding session. We're not asking you to make the coil this week — we're asking you to scope it so you can.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> The simplest reproducible version of Marko's first coil is what we'll call the *paper-and-pencil precursor*: a flat 2D winding diagram drawn at full size, ready to be lifted onto a wooden form. The actual hardware build comes in Q3, week 33. Today: the diagram on paper.
>
> Open your build log. Date it. Title the page *Marko's First Coil — Paper Plan*.
>
> Then write three blocks.
>
> *Block 1 — the form.* On the page, sketch a circle of about ten centimetres diameter to represent a flat top-down view of the wooden torus. Label nine evenly-spaced points around the circle, 1 through 9 clockwise from the top. *This is the same Symbol you've been drawing for eleven weeks.* The coil winding sits on top of it.
>
> *Block 2 — the doubling path.* On the same circle, draw the path 1 → 2 → 4 → 8 → 7 → 5 → 1 with a single continuous line. Use a coloured pen. This is the doubling circuit you traced in Week 4.
>
> *Block 3 — the parts list.* Write the parts you would need to actually build this in copper:
>
> Wooden torus form, hand-turned or 3D-printed, ten-centimetre outer diameter.
>
> Magnet wire, AWG 22, copper, single conductor, polyurethane insulated. About three metres for one full traversal.
>
> Two terminal posts.
>
> Soldering iron, flux, rosin-core solder.
>
> Wire strippers, fine-point pliers.
>
> Optional: a multimeter for resistance check post-build.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> Three deliberate constraints.
>
> *Constraint 1.* This is the *minimum* coil. One traversal, one strand, no electronics. Not the six-strand, four-traversal canonical Rodin Coil. We're starting where Marko started.
>
> *Constraint 2.* You're drawing on paper today. The hardware build is Q3. The intervening twenty weeks build the geometric and electromagnetic vocabulary you'll need to interpret what you measure.
>
> *Constraint 3.* The parts list is intentionally cheap. Total bill of materials: under thirty dollars. The Rodin coil tradition has always been low-cost; the *first* coil should be too.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> One pause to be honest. *Why are we drawing instead of building?* Because building without first understanding the geometry of the toroidal lift, the interaction with antipodal pairs, and the canonical coil topology produces *frustration*. Students who build at week 12 often dismantle and rebuild three times by week 33 anyway. Drawing now, building once, is faster.
>
> If you can't wait — if you have the tools and you want to wind your own version this weekend — go ahead. You won't break anything. Just keep your *paper plan* as the canonical entry; mark any improvised build as a sketch."
