## 4. Body (1:00–4:30)

> "Marko's discovery has a specific shape. *Pattern recognition. Simple operations. A teenager's curiosity. Sustained attention over decades.*
>
> Your discovery — if you have one — might have a different shape. But the model is worth examining.
>
> *Pattern recognition.* Marko noticed something. He didn't compute it; he saw it. Numbers around a circle, doubled and reduced, produced a figure-eight. The pattern was *visible* once the placement was chosen.
>
> What patterns do *you* notice that others might miss? In your work, your hobbies, your daily life. Pattern recognition is a learnable skill. Most students who develop it report finding patterns in unexpected places.
>
> *Simple operations.* Marko's discovery used arithmetic, addition, multiplication, reduction. No advanced techniques. The operations were available to any literate person.
>
> What simple operations could be applied to your work? Sometimes the deepest insights come from the most basic moves. Don't dismiss the simple.
>
> *Curiosity.* Marko was *curious* about Bahá'í numerology. He didn't have to be. He chose to follow the curiosity to its conclusion — drawing the diagram, doubling, reducing, watching what happened. Curiosity is a *practice*, not a talent.
>
> What are you curious about? Following one's curiosity is itself an investment.
>
> *Sustained attention.* The discovery moment took an afternoon. The development took fifty years. Marko didn't drop the work after the initial pattern; he kept refining, kept building, kept teaching.
>
> Sustained attention to a single line of inquiry is rare. Most people abandon insights before they mature. Marko didn't.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> Use case for VBM students. *Don't try to reproduce Marko's specific discovery* — the Symbol is already drawn. *Do try to develop the same skills.* Pattern recognition. Simple-operation thinking. Sustained curiosity.
>
> The course gives you 52 weeks of opportunity to develop these skills, applied to VBM specifically. Whether you find your own original pattern or just deepen your relationship to Marko's, the practice transfers.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> One observation. Marko's framing of his own discovery as *a path the math forced him to walk* is encouraging for students. He doesn't claim genius. He claims attention. The figure-eight was there; he traced it. You can trace things too.
>
> What's there for you to trace?"
