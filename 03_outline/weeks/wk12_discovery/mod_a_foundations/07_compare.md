## 4. Body (1:00–4:30)

> "Three classic mathematical-scientific discovery stories.
>
> *Newton and gravity (~1666).* According to legend, Newton was sitting under an apple tree when an apple fell. The fall prompted him to wonder why the apple fell down rather than sideways or up. From this, eventually, the law of universal gravitation. The story is partly mythologised — Newton's actual development was longer and more deliberate — but the *legend* has the fall happening in his early twenties.
>
> *Einstein and the elevator (~1907).* Einstein, age 28, was working as a patent clerk when he had what he later called *the happiest thought of my life*: that an observer in a freely falling elevator would feel weightless, equivalent to floating in space far from any gravity. From this thought experiment, eventually, general relativity (1915). Einstein had no academic position when he had the insight.
>
> *Marko and the circle (~1973).* Marko, age fifteen, drew a circle with nine numbers, doubled and reduced, and watched the figure-eight emerge. From this, eventually, Vortex-Based Mathematics (1990s and onward).
>
> *[pause]*
>
> Three commonalities.
>
> *Young age.* All three discoverers were in their teens or twenties at the breakthrough moment. Big shifts often happen before institutional habit settles in.
>
> *Outside-the-mainstream context.* Newton was at home during a plague-driven university closure. Einstein was a patent clerk, not a professor. Marko was a teenager, not a researcher. Established institutional positions weren't where these insights happened.
>
> *Simple operations.* Apple falls. Elevator. Doubling on a circle. Each insight came from a small, accessible observation, not from advanced technique.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> Three differences.
>
> *Subsequent reception.* Newton was eventually celebrated; Einstein eventually celebrated; Marko's framework remains *outside* mainstream academic recognition.
>
> *Domain.* Newton-Einstein were physics; Marko is closer to number theory + geometry + electromagnetic engineering.
>
> *Pace of refinement.* Newton's *Principia* came twenty years after the apple. Einstein's general relativity came eight years after the elevator. Marko's first major publications came two-plus decades after the discovery.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> Useful framing. *Marko's discovery story has the same shape as Newton's and Einstein's — young age, outside-the-mainstream context, simple operations — but the subsequent reception is different. Whether the difference reflects the work's quality or the politics of academic acceptance is debated.*
>
> Practical takeaway. The *shape* of discovery — young, outside-the-mainstream, simple — recurs across history. Marko's biography fits the pattern. Whether the framework matures into recognised mathematics is an open question."
