## 4. Body (1:00–4:30)

> "Marko Rodin was fifteen years old when he first drew the Symbol of Enlightenment. The year was approximately 1973. He was studying Bahá'í scripture and noticed that the name Abha — the Most Great Name in the Bahá'í tradition — reduced to nine via Abjad numerology.
>
> He took a piece of paper. He drew nine numbers around a circle. He started doubling and reducing. The figure-eight emerged.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> The setting wasn't a university laboratory or a research institute. It was a teenager's notebook. The discovery wasn't peer-reviewed; it wasn't funded; it wasn't published in a mainstream journal at the time. It was simply *seen*.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> What Marko found that day became the foundation of Vortex-Based Mathematics. The Symbol. The doubling cycle. The polar pairs. The family groups. All visible — once the right placement was chosen — to a fifteen-year-old's pencil.
>
> Marko has spent the fifty years since refining and explaining what he saw that day. He has built coils. He has given lectures. He has published papers. He has accumulated followers and critics. The work has grown.
>
> But the *foundational moment* was a young person and a piece of paper.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> Why does this matter for VBM students? Because it tells you something about *how the Symbol is encountered*. The Symbol isn't a sophisticated mathematical object that requires advanced training to appreciate. It's a diagram a teenager can draw. The diagram is *accessible*; the implications are vast.
>
> Marko's biography reverses the usual academic-discovery narrative. *Most* mathematical discoveries require years of training, access to research literature, peer review. *This* discovery happened with simple tools and basic arithmetic.
>
> Whether you find that compelling — that discovery can come from outside academia — or sceptical — that real mathematics requires institutional rigour — is your call. The biographical fact stands.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> Today: see the setting. The simple tools. The young age. The Bahá'í context. The piece of paper. *Most VBM material assumes the reader already knows this story; this lesson lays it out for those who don't.*"
