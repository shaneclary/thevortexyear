## 4. Body (1:00–4:30)

> "Today we apply the graph-isomorphism test to three other 9-fold structures, asking: are they isomorphic to the (extended) Rodin Symbol?
>
> *[pause]*
>
> *Test 1 — 9-pointed star polygon {9/2}.*
>
> A 9-pointed star formed by connecting every second vertex on a 9-vertex regular polygon: 1-3-5-7-9-2-4-6-8-1.
>
> *Vertices: 9.* Same as Rodin.
>
> *Edges: 9.* (One 9-cycle visiting every vertex once.)
>
> *Cycles: 1.* (One cycle of length 9.)
>
> *Comparison.* Rodin Symbol (extended): 9 vertices, 9 edges, *2 cycles* (6-cycle + 3-cycle).
>
> *Different cycle structure.* Not graph-isomorphic. ❌
>
> *[pause]*
>
> *Test 2 — Hindu navagraha (nine planets).*
>
> The navagraha are 9 cosmic bodies traditionally arranged in a 3×3 grid:
>
> Sun, Moon, Mars, Mercury, Jupiter, Venus, Saturn, Rahu, Ketu.
>
> Various traditional connections form a graph. The most common diagram has each planet connected to its *immediate neighbours in the grid* (4 connections per inner planet, fewer at edges).
>
> *Vertices: 9.* Same.
>
> *Edges: depends on convention.* The 3×3 grid graph has 12 edges (4 horizontal, 4 vertical, 4 diagonal in some versions). Different from Rodin's 9.
>
> *Comparison.* Different edge count. *Not graph-isomorphic.* ❌
>
> *[pause]*
>
> *Test 3 — Lo Shu / 3×3 magic square graph.*
>
> The Lo Shu is the unique 3×3 magic square: arrangement of 1-9 such that all rows, columns, and diagonals sum to 15.
>
> *Standard arrangement:*
>
> 4 9 2
>
> 3 5 7
>
> 8 1 6
>
> *Graph from row/column/diagonal connections.* Rows: (4,9,2), (3,5,7), (8,1,6). Columns: (4,3,8), (9,5,1), (2,7,6). Diagonals: (4,5,6), (2,5,8).
>
> *Vertices: 9.* Same.
>
> *Edges: 8 lines × pairs in each.* Each line of 3 contributes 3 edges (the three pairs). 8 lines × 3 = 24 edges total, but with overlap counting, distinct edges might be fewer.
>
> Let me count distinct edges: 4-9, 9-2, 4-2 (row 1); 3-5, 5-7, 3-7 (row 2); 8-1, 1-6, 8-6 (row 3); 4-3, 3-8, 4-8 (col 1); 9-5, 5-1, 9-1 (col 2); 2-7, 7-6, 2-6 (col 3); 4-5, 5-6, 4-6 (diag); 2-5, 5-8, 2-8 (anti-diag).
>
> Distinct edges: count carefully... 24 candidate edges; many distinct. Definitely far more than Rodin's 9.
>
> *Different edge count.* Not graph-isomorphic. ❌
>
> *[pause]*
>
> *None of the three are isomorphic to the Rodin Symbol.*
>
> Why? The Rodin Symbol has a *specific* graph structure: a 6-cycle on the units of (ℤ/9ℤ)* + a 3-cycle on the non-units. *Most 9-fold structures don't have this specific topology.*
>
> The classical enneagram *does* — which is why it's isomorphic. *Most other 9-fold material isn't.*
>
> *[pause]*
>
> *Three observations.*
>
> *Observation one.* Not every 9-fold structure is graph-isomorphic to the Rodin Symbol. *Most aren't.*
>
> *Observation two.* The classical enneagram is *unusual* in being isomorphic. The match isn't coincidence — both traditions emerged from related-but-different mathematical sources.
>
> *Observation three.* Cross-tradition correspondence is *specific*. *VBM doesn't share structure with all 9-fold material*; it shares structure with the *enneagram lineage* specifically.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> Practical takeaway. *When encountering a 9-fold structure in another tradition, do the graph-isomorphism check before claiming correspondence.* Vertex count, edge count, cycle structure. If they match Rodin's, you've found a cousin. If not, the 9-fold-ness is a surface-level coincidence."
