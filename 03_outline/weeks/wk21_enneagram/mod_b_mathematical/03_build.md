## 4. Body (1:00–4:30)

> "Today's build connects the *combinatorial* relabelling from Lens 1-2 to a *geometric* operation — a rotation or reflection on the circle.
>
> Open your notebook. Title the page *Geometric alignment of the figures*.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> *Setup.* The 9 vertices are on a circle, equally spaced at 40° intervals. Standard placement: 9 at top, 1 to 8 clockwise from top.
>
> *Geometric question.* Is there a *rotation* of the circle that takes the Rodin doubling cycle's edge set to the classical enneagram's edge set?
>
> *[pause]*
>
> *Step 1 — try a rotation.* Rotate the figure by some angle θ. Each vertex moves to a new position; edges follow.
>
> Rotation by 40° (one position): vertex 1 → 2; 2 → 3; 3 → 4; etc.
>
> Apply to Rodin doubling cycle 1-2-4-8-7-5-1: rotated edge set is 2-3-5-9-8-6-2.
>
> Compare to classical enneagram edges 1-4-2-8-5-7-1: *not the same*.
>
> *Pure rotation doesn't produce the enneagram from the Rodin Symbol.* The two cycles aren't rotations of each other.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> *Step 2 — try a reflection.* Reflect across some axis. The vertex permutation is determined by the axis.
>
> Reflection across the *vertical axis* (through 9 and the centre): swaps left-right pairs. 1 ↔ 8; 2 ↔ 7; 3 ↔ 6; 4 ↔ 5; fixes 9.
>
> Apply to Rodin doubling 1-2-4-8-7-5-1: reflected edges are 8-7-5-1-2-4-8.
>
> *Reading: 8 → 7 → 5 → 1 → 2 → 4 → 8.* This is the *halving* cycle (the doubling cycle traversed in reverse direction).
>
> Compare to classical enneagram 1-4-2-8-5-7-1: *not the same*.
>
> *Reflection across the vertical axis gives halving, not the classical enneagram.*
>
> *[pause]*
>
> *Step 3 — try a reflection across a different axis.* Reflect across the axis through *vertex 1 and the centre* (so 1 is fixed; vertices opposite to 1 swap).
>
> This reflection: 1 ↔ 1 (fixed); 2 ↔ 8 (already wait — let me recompute). Adjacent vertices to 1 are 2 and 9 (clockwise/counterclockwise). Reflection through the 1-axis fixes 1 and 5 (the vertex opposite to 1, at 180°), and swaps the others.
>
> Wait — the vertex opposite to 1 isn't on the standard 9-vertex circle in a clean way (9 is odd). The opposite would be between 5 and 6.
>
> *Hmm — there's no clean axis through both 1 and another vertex.* The reflection axis through 1 passes through the midpoint of the arc between 5 and 6, not through a vertex.
>
> *Reflections preserving vertices.* For an odd number of vertices, axes pass through one vertex and the midpoint of the opposite arc. *9 such axes* (one per vertex).
>
> Let's try axis through 1: fixes 1; swaps 2 ↔ 9; 3 ↔ 8; 4 ↔ 7; 5 ↔ 6.
>
> Apply to Rodin cycle 1-2-4-8-7-5-1: reflected edges 1-9-7-3-4-6-1.
>
> Reading: 1 → 9 → 7 → 3 → 4 → 6 → 1. *Includes family-3 elements.* Not the enneagram cycle (which stays on units).
>
> *[pause]*
>
> *Step 4 — recognise the alignment is non-geometric.* The Rodin doubling cycle and the classical enneagram cycle *aren't related by a Euclidean rotation or reflection of the circle*. They're related by the relabelling ψ from Lens 1, which is a *combinatorial* permutation, not a geometric isometry.
>
> *Specifically.* ψ swaps 2 ↔ 4 and 5 ↔ 7 — non-adjacent pairs. *No geometric rotation or reflection produces these specific swaps* on the standard 9-vertex circle.
>
> *Conclusion.* The graph isomorphism is *combinatorial only*; *not geometric*.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> *Three observations.*
>
> *Observation one.* The combinatorial isomorphism is *real and provable* (Lens 2). The two figures share graph structure.
>
> *Observation two.* The geometric isomorphism is *not present* on the standard circle. The two figures are *visually distinct* on the same circle.
>
> *Observation three.* This is *typical*: graph isomorphism doesn't entail geometric equivalence. *Two graphs can be combinatorially identical but visually different.*
>
> *[pause]*
>
> Practical takeaway. *The Rodin Symbol and classical enneagram are graph-isomorphic but visually distinct.* On the same circle, they look different. Mathematically, they're cousins; visually, they're different drawings.
>
> Pin this in your notebook. *Combinatorial isomorphism without geometric equivalence is what we have.*"
