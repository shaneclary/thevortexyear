## 4. Body (1:00–4:30)

> "The classical *enneagram* — sometimes called the Gurdjieff enneagram, after the early-20th-century teacher who introduced it to Western audiences — is a *9-pointed figure* that has appeared in spiritual and psychological traditions for over a century. *VBM's Symbol of Enlightenment is also a 9-pointed figure.*
>
> Today's lesson examines whether these are the *same* figure or *different* figures, and what the relationship is.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> *Visual comparison.*
>
> *Symbol of Enlightenment.* Nine numbered positions on a circle, with the doubling cycle traced as a figure-eight: 1 → 2 → 4 → 8 → 7 → 5 → 1. *No internal triangle*; the family-3 elements (3, 6, 9) sit on the circle but aren't connected to the doubling cycle.
>
> *Classical enneagram.* Nine numbered positions on a circle (1 through 9, conventionally clockwise from top). *Inner triangle* connecting positions 3, 6, and 9. *Outer hexagon-like path* connecting positions 1, 4, 2, 8, 5, 7 in that cyclic order.
>
> Wait. Read the outer path again: 1 → 4 → 2 → 8 → 5 → 7 → 1. *Compare to the Rodin doubling cycle:* 1 → 2 → 4 → 8 → 7 → 5 → 1.
>
> *Same set of vertices visited; different traversal order.* Both visit {1, 2, 4, 5, 7, 8} as a 6-cycle. The classical enneagram traverses *1 → 4 → 2 → 8 → 5 → 7*; the Rodin traverses *1 → 2 → 4 → 8 → 7 → 5*.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> *The relationship.*
>
> The two cycles are *related by reflection* (or by inverse-direction traversal). Reading the Rodin cycle backward gives 1, 5, 7, 8, 4, 2 — the *halving* cycle. The classical enneagram's outer path is *similar but not identical* — it visits the same six vertices but with a different ordering.
>
> Specifically: classical enneagram outer path 1 → 4 → 2 → 8 → 5 → 7 corresponds to multiplication by 4 on (ℤ/9ℤ)* (which gives 1, 4, 7, 1, 4, 7, …; that's order 3, not 6). So *that's not quite multiplication-by-4 either.*
>
> *Hmm.* Let me reconsider. The classical enneagram outer path comes from the *decimal expansion of 1/7*: 1/7 = 0.142857142857... The repeating digits 1, 4, 2, 8, 5, 7 form the classical enneagram's outer cycle.
>
> *Source structure.* The Rodin doubling cycle comes from *2ⁿ mod 9*. The classical enneagram outer cycle comes from *the digits of 1/7*. Different mathematical sources, same vertex set, different traversal orders.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> *What they share.*
>
> *Share 1 — the vertex set.* Both use {1, 2, 4, 5, 7, 8} = (ℤ/9ℤ)*.
>
> *Share 2 — family-3 separateness.* Both treat {3, 6, 9} differently from the other six. Classical: inner triangle. Rodin: separate from the doubling cycle.
>
> *Share 3 — the 9-fold structure.* Both place 9 vertices on a circle.
>
> *What they don't share.*
>
> *Don't share 1 — vertex traversal order.* Different cycles through the six units.
>
> *Don't share 2 — mathematical source.* Different operations produce them.
>
> *Don't share 3 — interpretive tradition.* The classical enneagram is from Gurdjieff and later Sufi-influenced psychology; the Rodin Symbol is from VBM's algebraic structure.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> Three observations.
>
> *Observation one.* The two figures are *cousins*, not twins. They share genetic material (the 6-cycle on units, the family-3 separation) but differ in expression.
>
> *Observation two.* The classical enneagram is *older* (early 1900s) than VBM (1973). VBM didn't invent the 9-pointed structure; it found it independently from a different mathematical starting point.
>
> *Observation three.* Practitioners sometimes claim the two figures are *the same*. *Mathematically, they're not exactly* — the traversal orders differ. *Conceptually, they share the genus*. Be careful with the level of identification.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> Practical takeaway. *Hold the two figures as related but distinct.* The classical enneagram is a separate tradition; VBM's Symbol is its own object. *Both deserve respect on their own terms; neither should be reduced to the other.*"
