## 4. Body (1:00–4:30)

> "Some experiences have a *click*. The moment when a key turns. The moment when a misaligned mechanism settles into place. The moment when a confused thought becomes clear.
>
> Aligning the overlay onto the base has this quality. *When the registration is right, you can feel it lock.* The figures coincide; the alignment is achieved.
>
> *[long pause — 12 seconds]*
>
> Sit with the click.
>
> *[long pause]*
>
> Most clicks have specific characteristics.
>
> *Discreteness.* Either aligned or not. Not partly. The click marks the transition from misaligned to aligned.
>
> *Felt-sense.* Even without measuring, the alignment is recognisable. The eye and hand know.
>
> *Satisfaction.* The click is mildly satisfying. Mechanism settled; ambiguity resolved; structure visible.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> Three contemplations on the click.
>
> *Contemplation one — the click reveals structure.* When the overlay is misaligned, the figures are confused — overlapping where they shouldn't, separated where they should connect. *The misaligned state contains all the wrong relationships.*
>
> When alignment locks in, the *correct* relationships emerge. *Same vertices coincide; the family-3 triangle on the overlay sits on the family-3 triangle on the base; the cycles cross at vertices, not at random points.*
>
> The click is the *moment when correctness becomes apparent*.
>
> *Contemplation two — clicks are pedagogically useful.* When teaching cross-tradition correspondence, the *click* moment in the demonstration is when students *see* the relationship. *Words can describe; the click shows.*
>
> Some students get it from the words. *Most get it more strongly when the alignment locks in.* The combination of math (Mod B) plus visceral demonstration (this Mod C) reaches different learners through different channels.
>
> *Contemplation three — clicks generalise.* The click of overlay alignment is one instance of a broader pattern. *Insight has a click-quality.* Mathematical proofs click when they finally make sense. Music clicks when a discordant note resolves. Physical assembly clicks when parts seat correctly.
>
> *VBM's framework offers many click moments.* The doubling cycle clicks when first traced. The grid clicks when first filled. The torus clicks when first held. The cross-tradition correspondence clicks here, with the overlay.
>
> *[long pause — 12 seconds]*
>
> What does the click feel like?
>
> Some students notice *physical satisfaction*. Hand-eye coordination achieved an alignment; the body registers success.
>
> Some students notice *cognitive recognition*. The relationship between figures shifts from *abstract claim* to *visible fact*.
>
> Some students notice *integration*. Multiple lessons (Mod A's introduction, Mod B's proof, Mod C's tool) culminate in the click. *Layered learning becoming whole.*
>
> All three are honest.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> A practical observation. *Clicks are worth waiting for.* If the alignment isn't quite right, *don't force it*. Adjust the registration; the click will happen when alignment is true. *Don't accept a partial click as full alignment.*
>
> Some teachers force the alignment for time pressure (a class is ending; a demonstration must conclude). *Better to let the click happen authentically* even if it takes longer.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> One more contemplation. *The click is small but real.* It's not a transcendent insight; it's a small moment of structural recognition. *Many such small clicks accumulate into deeper understanding* — but each click is its own quiet event.
>
> Hold the overlay tool. Lower the transparent layer onto the base. Adjust until the registration locks. Notice the click.
>
> *That moment is the lesson.*"
