## 4. Body (1:00–4:30)

> "Today's apply gives you a *script* for using the overlay tool when teaching the cross-tradition correspondence to others.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> *Setup.* Sit with the colleague at a table. Have the assembled overlay tool visible. Allow about 5-10 minutes for the demonstration.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> *Step 1 — show the base.* Show the Rodin Symbol on the base layer (without the overlay).
>
> Say: 'This is the Rodin Symbol of Enlightenment. Nine numbered positions on a circle. The doubling cycle — 1 to 2 to 4 to 8 to 7 to 5 — traces a figure-eight. Family 3 — the elements 3, 6, 9 — forms an inner triangle.'
>
> Wait for acknowledgement. Don't rush.
>
> *Step 2 — show the overlay alone.* Lift the overlay; show it separately.
>
> Say: 'This is the classical enneagram from the Gurdjieff tradition. Same nine positions on a circle. Different cycle: 1 to 4 to 2 to 8 to 5 to 7. Same inner triangle on family 3.'
>
> Wait.
>
> *Step 3 — overlay them.* Place the overlay on the base, alignment locking in.
>
> Say: 'When we overlay them — same nine vertices, same family-3 triangle — they share the underlying graph structure. Same nodes; different paths through the unit nodes; shared family-3 cycle.'
>
> Wait. Allow the visual to register.
>
> *Step 4 — answer questions.*
>
> Common question: 'Are they the same?'
>
> Answer: 'Graph-isomorphic. Same abstract structure. Different traversal orders. Different traditions. Different interpretations.'
>
> Common question: 'Did one come from the other?'
>
> Answer: 'No. They emerged independently. Gurdjieff in early 20th century; Marko Rodin in 1973. The shared structure is mathematical, not historical.'
>
> Common question: 'Does the math from one tradition apply to the other?'
>
> Answer: 'The graph-theoretic results apply to both. The interpretive content (Gurdjieff's psychology; VBM's algebra) doesn't transfer freely. Match structure, not meaning.'
>
> *[pause]*
>
> *Three demonstration variants.*
>
> *Variant 1 — for math-fluent audiences.* Skip Step 1-3 quickly. Spend more time on the algebraic structure: 'The Rodin doubling cycle is the orbit of 2 in (ℤ/9ℤ)*. The classical enneagram cycle comes from the digits of 1/7 in base 10.' Math fluency lets the conversation skip ahead.
>
> *Variant 2 — for spiritual-tradition audiences.* Acknowledge their tradition first: 'I know the enneagram from Gurdjieff / Sufism / personality theory. VBM has a different starting point — algebra — but arrives at related geometry.' Connection-first; mathematics second.
>
> *Variant 3 — for sceptical audiences.* Lead with rigour: 'Graph isomorphism is a precise mathematical relation. Both figures are graph-isomorphic. The relationship can be checked by anyone who reads graph theory.' Rigour-first; lay framing later.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> Three observations.
>
> *Observation one.* The overlay tool is *flexible*. Different scripts suit different audiences.
>
> *Observation two.* The demonstration is *quick*. 5-10 minutes is sufficient for most audiences.
>
> *Observation three.* The demonstration *opens conversations*. Most listeners have follow-up questions; the tool is a starting point, not an endpoint.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> Practical takeaway. *Practise the script before using it.* Talk through the three steps with a friend or to yourself. The first few demonstrations may feel awkward; *practice smooths the delivery*.
>
> The overlay tool, plus a clear script, makes you an *effective communicator* of the cross-tradition correspondence."
