## 4. Body (1:00–4:30)

> "Today we count the alignment requirements for the overlay teaching tool. Three alignments matter; each has a tolerance.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> *Alignment 1 — centres aligned.* The centres of the two 9-vertex circles must coincide. If the centres are off, the figures shift relative to each other.
>
> *Tolerance.* About ±0.5 mm for a 10 cm diameter circle. Beyond this, the misalignment is visible to most viewers.
>
> *Alignment 2 — rotational alignment.* The rotational orientation must match. If the transparent overlay is rotated by, say, 5°, vertex 1 of the overlay sits 5° clockwise of vertex 1 of the base.
>
> *Tolerance.* About ±2°. (One vertex spans 40° of arc; ±2° is 5% of vertex spacing — within the acceptable visual range.)
>
> *Alignment 3 — scale match.* The two layers must have *the same circle diameter*. If they differ by more than ~5%, the overlay vertices fall on the wrong base vertices.
>
> *Tolerance.* About ±2% scale difference.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> *How to achieve each alignment.*
>
> *For centre alignment.* Use a pin or screw through both layers' centres. The pin acts as a pivot.
>
> *For rotational alignment.* Use a fiducial mark — a small notch or dot at vertex 9 (top) on both layers. Align the marks visually.
>
> *For scale alignment.* Print both layers at the *same intended scale*. If you're sketching by hand, use a compass set to the same radius for both.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> *Verification protocol.*
>
> *Step 1.* Place layers on a flat surface; align centres via pin.
>
> *Step 2.* Rotate overlay until top fiducial marks coincide.
>
> *Step 3.* Visually inspect: do all 9 base vertices coincide with all 9 overlay vertex labels?
>
> *Step 4.* If yes, alignment achieved. If no, identify which alignment is off (centre / rotation / scale) and adjust.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> *Three observations on alignment.*
>
> *Observation one.* The alignment is *precise enough that small errors are visible*. Misalignment of more than 5° rotation is obvious to viewers; less than 2° is imperceptible. *In between is fuzzy.*
>
> *Observation two.* The 3-6-9 triangle *helps verify alignment*. If the triangle on the overlay coincides exactly with the triangle on the base, alignment is correct. *The triangle is the visual anchor.*
>
> *Observation three.* Some teaching tools use *adhesive registration* — the overlay is taped to the base after alignment. This makes the tool a *fixed* artefact rather than an *interactive* one. *Both versions are useful*; pick based on whether you want students to manipulate the alignment themselves.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> Practical takeaway. *Build with care.* The teaching tool's value depends on precise alignment. Sloppy registration produces a confusing tool that doesn't demonstrate the correspondence.
>
> If your skills with manual alignment are limited, *use a printer with both layers in the same file* — the printer handles centre/rotation/scale automatically. *Print on transparency film for the overlay.*"
