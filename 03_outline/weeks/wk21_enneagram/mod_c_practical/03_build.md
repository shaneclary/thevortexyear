## 4. Body (1:00–4:30)

> "Today's build produces the transparent overlay teaching tool. About 30 minutes of work; reusable for years.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> *Materials.*
>
> Heavy paper or card (110+ gsm). White preferred. A4 / letter size.
>
> Transparency film (printer-compatible) OR thin clear plastic sheet (acetate).
>
> Coloured pens or markers (two contrasting colours, e.g., red and blue).
>
> Compass for circle drawing.
>
> Ruler.
>
> Protractor.
>
> Pencil.
>
> Optional: small bolt and nut (M3 or M4) for centre pivot.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> *Step 1 — design the base layer.* On the heavy paper:
>
> Draw a circle of 8-10 cm diameter (centre on the page).
>
> Mark 9 evenly-spaced vertices at 40° intervals. Number them 9 at top, 1-8 clockwise.
>
> Draw the Rodin doubling cycle (1-2-4-8-7-5-1) using one colour (e.g., red). Use line segments connecting consecutive vertices.
>
> Draw the family-3 triangle (3-6-9) in a third colour (e.g., black) or leave for both layers.
>
> Add a fiducial mark at vertex 9 — a small filled circle or notch.
>
> *Step 2 — design the overlay layer.* On the transparency film:
>
> Mark a circle of the *same diameter* (use the same compass setting). Position the centre to match the base when overlaid.
>
> Mark 9 evenly-spaced vertices at the same 40° intervals. Number them 9 at top, 1-8 clockwise (matching the base).
>
> Draw the classical enneagram cycle (1-4-2-8-5-7-1) using a different colour (e.g., blue).
>
> Draw the family-3 triangle (3-6-9) in the same third colour as the base — this triangle is *shared* between figures.
>
> Add a fiducial mark at vertex 9 — same as base.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> *Step 3 — alignment system.* Two options.
>
> *Option A — pivoting pin.* Drill a small hole at the centre of both layers. Insert a small bolt through both. The bolt is the pivot; you can rotate the overlay around the centre.
>
> *Option B — fixed assembly.* Once aligned, tape the overlay to the base permanently. The tool becomes a single fixed artefact.
>
> *Option C — print as one file.* For digital builders: prepare both layers in a single image file with transparency support; print the base on paper and the overlay on transparency film. Both come out aligned by default.
>
> *Step 4 — verify.* Test the overlay:
>
> Centres aligned? *Yes / no.*
>
> Rotational alignment correct? Vertex 1 on overlay sits over vertex 1 on base? *Yes / no.*
>
> All 9 vertices line up? *Yes / no.*
>
> If any check fails, adjust accordingly.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> *Step 5 — label.*
>
> Add small text labels:
>
> Base: *Rodin Symbol — doubling cycle (red).*
>
> Overlay: *Classical enneagram — outer cycle (blue).*
>
> Both: *Family-3 triangle (shared).*
>
> *[pause]*
>
> *Step 6 — photograph.* Take photos of:
>
> The base alone.
>
> The overlay alone.
>
> Both together (the assembled tool).
>
> Tape into your build log. Caption: *Cross-tradition overlay teaching tool*.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> What you've built. *A reusable visual demonstration of the Symbol-enneagram correspondence.* Use it when teaching VBM to others, particularly to show the cross-tradition graph isomorphism without requiring the full mathematical proof.
>
> Three observations.
>
> *Observation one.* The tool is *self-explanatory* once built. Hand someone the assembled artefact; they see the relationship without verbal explanation.
>
> *Observation two.* The tool is *low-cost*. Total materials cost: under $5.
>
> *Observation three.* The tool *complements* the graph-isomorphism proof — it makes the abstract result tangible.
>
> Pin the tool somewhere visible. It's now part of your VBM teaching kit."
