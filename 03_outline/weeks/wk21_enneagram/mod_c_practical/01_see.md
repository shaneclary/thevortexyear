## 4. Body (1:00–4:30)

> "Today's Mod C lens introduces a *teaching tool*: a printed Rodin Symbol with a transparent enneagram overlay. *When the two figures are visible together*, the relationship is immediately apparent.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> *Why a physical overlay.*
>
> Mod B established the graph isomorphism mathematically. The proof is rigorous but *abstract*. For students new to graph theory, *seeing the figures on top of each other* makes the relationship visceral.
>
> The transparent overlay tool shows:
>
> Same 9 vertices.
>
> Different cycle paths.
>
> Same family-3 triangle.
>
> Different vertex-traversal orders despite shared graph structure.
>
> *Visual intuition follows mathematical proof but doesn't replace it.*
>
> *[pause]*
>
> *Components of the tool.*
>
> *Component 1 — base layer (printed paper).* The Rodin Symbol drawn on heavy paper or card. 9 vertices on a circle, doubling cycle traced as a figure-eight, family-3 triangle (3-6-9) drawn.
>
> *Component 2 — transparent overlay.* The classical enneagram printed on a clear plastic sheet. Same 9-vertex circle (same scale and centre), classical outer cycle (1-4-2-8-5-7) traced, family-3 triangle drawn (the triangle is shared between both figures).
>
> *Component 3 — alignment marks.* Small fiducial marks on both layers ensuring the 9-vertex circles align precisely. Without alignment, the overlay shifts and the demonstration fails.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> *How it's used.*
>
> *Use 1 — overlay reveals shared vertices.* When the transparent enneagram is placed on top, the 9 vertices line up. *Same circle, same vertex set.* Visually obvious.
>
> *Use 2 — different colours show different cycles.* The Rodin doubling cycle (one colour, on the base) and the classical enneagram cycle (different colour, on the overlay) are *both visible at once*. *They don't overlap on the same edges*; they trace different paths through the same vertices.
>
> *Use 3 — shared triangle.* The family-3 triangle (3-6-9) is on both layers. *It's the only shared edge structure between the two figures.* The overlay shows this directly.
>
> *Use 4 — slide the overlay slightly.* If you intentionally misalign by 5°, the figures decouple and the structure becomes confused. *Alignment matters.* The teaching tool's value depends on precision.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> *What students take away.*
>
> *Takeaway 1.* The two figures *are different* (different cycles).
>
> *Takeaway 2.* The two figures *share structure* (same vertex set, same family-3 triangle).
>
> *Takeaway 3.* The graph isomorphism — abstract and provable in Mod B — *has a visual analogue* that students can manipulate.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> Three observations.
>
> *Observation one.* The overlay tool is *low-tech*. Paper plus transparency plus alignment marks. Anyone can build one.
>
> *Observation two.* The tool is *durable*. Once made, it can be reused indefinitely for teaching.
>
> *Observation three.* The tool *reveals the limits of visual demonstration*. The graph isomorphism is *more general* than the visual overlap (which only shows specific shared edges, not the full bijection). *The overlay is suggestive, not proof.*
>
> *[pause]*
>
> Practical takeaway. *Build the overlay tool if you'll be teaching VBM to others.* It's a fast, visceral way to communicate the cross-tradition correspondence. Mathematical rigour comes from Mod B; *visual intuition comes from the overlay*."
