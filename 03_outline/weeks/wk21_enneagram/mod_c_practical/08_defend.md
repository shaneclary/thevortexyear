## 4. Body (1:00–4:30)

> "The objection. *VBM has its own Symbol. The classical enneagram is from a different tradition. Teaching VBM with reference to the classical enneagram seems unnecessary at best, conflating-traditions at worst. Why bother?*
>
> The objection has weight. The answer is that pedagogical use is genuinely useful for some audiences.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> *Three reasons builders use the enneagram in VBM teaching.*
>
> *Reason one — recognisability.* For many Western audiences, the classical enneagram is *recognisable*. Enneagram typology is widely known; many adults have encountered it through books, workshops, or therapy. *Starting from a recognisable shape* lowers the barrier to engaging with VBM.
>
> *Reason two — graph-isomorphism is real.* The two figures *do* share graph structure (provably). Teaching from a recognisable figure to its math-rooted cousin is *not conflation* — it's structural translation.
>
> *Reason three — cross-tradition respect.* Acknowledging the enneagram tradition *respects practitioners of that tradition* who may engage with VBM. *VBM didn't invent the 9-pointed structure*; recognising prior arrivals at the same structure is honest.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> *When to use the enneagram in teaching.*
>
> *Use case 1 — audience already knows the enneagram.* Bridge from familiar to unfamiliar. *Significant time-saver.*
>
> *Use case 2 — student asks about cross-tradition correspondence.* The teaching opportunity is direct. Show the overlay; explain the isomorphism.
>
> *Use case 3 — emphasising VBM's structural depth.* Reference to the enneagram tradition shows VBM's structure has *cross-cultural recognition*. *Multiple paths to the same structure suggest the structure is real.*
>
> *[pause]*
>
> *When NOT to use the enneagram in teaching.*
>
> *Don't use 1 — audience unfamiliar with enneagram.* No bridge benefit. Adds confusion.
>
> *Don't use 2 — discussion is mathematical-only.* Stay in mathematical vocabulary. The enneagram tradition is interpretive; doesn't add to math discussions.
>
> *Don't use 3 — risk of conflation.* If the audience is likely to import enneagram personality types into VBM, the cross-reference does more harm than good. *Explicit boundaries needed*; sometimes the cross-reference isn't worth the explanation overhead.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> *The teaching pattern.*
>
> *Pattern.* Reference the enneagram if it helps recognition. Distinguish structure from interpretation. Show the overlay tool when useful. Stay close to VBM's specific mathematical content.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> So the calibrated answer to *why use the enneagram*:
>
> *VBM teachers reference the classical enneagram to leverage its recognisability for Western audiences, to demonstrate the genuine graph isomorphism, and to acknowledge cross-tradition arrival at the same structure. Use is conditional: only when the audience benefits from the bridge, and always with explicit distinction between graph structure (mathematical) and interpretation (different in each tradition). When the audience is unfamiliar with the enneagram or when discussion is purely mathematical, the enneagram reference adds nothing and is omitted.*
>
> Around eighty words.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> Practical takeaway. *Use the enneagram as a pedagogical bridge when it helps; omit when it doesn't.* The judgement is contextual; the criteria are clear.
>
> One more practical note. *In foundation-tradition contexts, enneagram references are routine.* Marko, Powell, and other foundation teachers use them. *In academic mathematical contexts, references are typically omitted* — the math doesn't depend on them.
>
> Match your context. The tool is available; using it is your choice."
