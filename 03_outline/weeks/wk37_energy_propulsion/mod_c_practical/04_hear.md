> *Powell's central framing.*
>
> Powell, on replication: *'Replicating someone else's claim is engineering work, not advocacy. You build to their specification as exactly as you can; you measure as carefully as your bench allows; you report what you find with full attribution to the source. Whether your result confirms or disconfirms, the replication is a contribution. Replication is how engineering advances.'*
>
> *Layer-1 — replication as engineering practice*. *Defendable; mainstream engineering principle*.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> *Powell on the four pillars of responsible replication.*
>
> *Pillar 1 — fidelity to source.* Powell: *'Build to the source's specification. If you must deviate (because you don't have an exact wire diameter, for instance), document the deviation explicitly. Don't substitute a version 'you think is better' — your job is to test their claim, not your improvement.'*
>
> *Pillar 2 — attribution.* Powell: *'Cite the source. Include the demonstration video reference, article reference, lecture date and venue. Replication without attribution is plagiarism; replication with attribution is community contribution.'*
>
> *Pillar 3 — methodology equal-or-better.* Powell: *'Match the source's measurement methodology or improve on it. If they used a multimeter, you can use a multimeter or a calibrated wattmeter; you can't use less rigorous instrumentation. Better instrumentation makes the result more credible; less rigorous instrumentation invalidates the comparison.'*
>
> *Pillar 4 — honest reporting.* Powell: *'Report what you find. Confirming, disconfirming, or ambiguous — all reports are engineering. The temptation to soften disconfirming results out of respect for the source is real; resist it. Honest reports respect the source by treating their work as engineering subject to engineering verification.'*
>
> *Four pillars; responsible replication framework*.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> *Powell on common replication missteps.*
>
> Powell, on missteps: *'Common replication missteps: skipping calibration ('the meter is recent enough'); using only single measurements ('it took only a minute, but I checked'); treating one-off favourable readings as confirming and unfavourable readings as outliers; not documenting environment; not attributing source. All these compromise the replication. Avoid them.'*
>
> *Layer-1 — replication quality discipline*. *Defendable*.

> *Powell on attribution etiquette in disconfirming cases.*
>
> Powell, on attribution: *'When disconfirming a source's claim, attribution remains important. Frame the disconfirmation as 'I attempted to replicate [source]'s claim of [specific result] under [specific conditions]; I measured [different result]; possible explanations include [methodology differences, environment differences, build differences]'. The source has the right to respond, refine the claim, or address the discrepancy. Engineering is dialogue; attribution preserves dialogue.'*

> *Layer-1 — attribution-in-disconfirmation ethics*. *Defendable*.
