## 4. Body (1:00–4:30)

> "Lens 6 used the name as anchor. Today: *compare VBM's naming choice with broader scientific naming traditions*. Where does 'ABHA Torus' sit?
>
> *[pause]*
>
> *Five scientific naming traditions.*
>
> *Tradition 1 — eponymy (named after persons).* Hubble telescope; Pythagorean theorem; Avogadro's number; Hodgkin-Huxley equations; Mandelbrot set. *Most common scientific naming.*
>
> *Tradition 2 — descriptive naming.* Quark; black hole; gravitational wave; double helix. *Names describing properties.*
>
> *Tradition 3 — mythological / classical naming.* Trojan asteroids; Pandora's cluster; Ariadne (asteroid); Olympic Mons. *Names from mythology applied to scientific objects.*
>
> *Tradition 4 — institutional / programme naming.* Apollo missions; Voyager spacecraft; LIGO; Hubble Space Telescope. *Names from cultural programmes.*
>
> *Tradition 5 — religious / sacred naming (rare in modern science but not absent).* The Higgs boson is sometimes called the 'God particle' (informally). The Eightfold Way (Gell-Mann) draws on Buddhist tradition. *Some objects bear sacred-tradition names.*
>
> *VBM's 'ABHA Torus' fits in Tradition 5 — sacred-tradition naming.*
>
> *[pause]*
>
> *Three observations on Tradition 5.*
>
> *Observation one — relatively rare in modern science.* Most mainstream scientific objects are named eponymously, descriptively, or mythologically. *Sacred-tradition naming is unusual but not unprecedented.*
>
> *Observation two — Eightfold Way as parallel.* Murray Gell-Mann's *Eightfold Way* (the SU(3) classification of hadrons) draws on the Buddhist Noble Eightfold Path. *Gell-Mann acknowledged the parallel; mainstream physics adopted the name.* This is a precedent for sacred-tradition naming in mainstream science.
>
> *Observation three — controversy varies.* Sacred-tradition naming sometimes generates discussion (the 'God particle' nickname is contested). *VBM's ABHA Torus naming is similarly discussion-prompting.* *Reception varies; the naming itself is legitimate.*
>
> *[pause]*
>
> *Where 'ABHA Torus' sits compared to alternatives.*
>
> *Alternative 1 — eponymy: 'Rodin Torus'.* Names after Marko. Standard practice. *Loses Bahá'í-tradition information; personalises.*
>
> *Alternative 2 — descriptive: 'Modular Torus' or 'Helical Torus'.* Names by structural property. Standard practice. *Loses biographical-cultural context.*
>
> *Alternative 3 — sacred-tradition: 'ABHA Torus'.* Names after Bahá'í-tradition word. Marko's choice. *Honors origin; introduces tradition-engagement requirement.*
>
> *Alternative 4 — combination: 'Rodin's ABHA Torus'.* Both eponymy and sacred-tradition. *Used sometimes; longer; more explicit.*
>
> *Each alternative is legitimate; Marko chose Tradition 5.*
>
> *[pause]*
>
> *Three structural similarities to other Tradition-5 namings.*
>
> *Similarity one — origin acknowledgment.* Sacred-tradition naming *acknowledges origin* in a way descriptive or eponymous naming doesn't. *VBM acknowledges Bahá'í origin; Eightfold Way acknowledges Buddhist origin; both are honest.*
>
> *Similarity two — symbolic richness.* Sacred-tradition names *carry meaning beyond mere labeling*. *Eightfold Way* carries Buddhist resonance; *ABHA Torus* carries Bahá'í resonance. *Names do work beyond identification.*
>
> *Similarity three — audience-shaping.* Sacred-tradition names *partly determine audience*. *Eightfold Way's name didn't prevent mainstream adoption; ABHA Torus's name has shaped VBM's audience similarly.*
>
> *[pause]*
>
> *What the comparison reveals.*
>
> *Reveal 1 — VBM's naming isn't unprecedented.* Sacred-tradition naming exists in mainstream science. *Not categorically problematic.*
>
> *Reveal 2 — naming choice is engineering decision.* Marko could have chosen any of the alternatives. *He chose ABHA; the choice is examinable; trade-offs are visible.*
>
> *Reveal 3 — substitution is usable.* In *contexts where sacred-tradition naming is friction*, alternatives are available. *Math is invariant; names are flexibly substitutable.*
>
> *[pause]*
>
> Useful framing. *Scientific naming follows several traditions: eponymy, descriptive, mythological, institutional, and (rarely) sacred-tradition. VBM's ABHA Torus fits in the sacred-tradition naming tradition, with precedent in Murray Gell-Mann's Eightfold Way. The choice is unusual in modern science but not unprecedented; it acknowledges origin honestly while requiring tradition-engagement; alternatives (eponymy, descriptive) are available for contexts where sacred-tradition naming is friction.*
>
> Practical takeaway. *VBM's naming choice has scientific precedent.* Sacred-tradition naming exists; Eightfold Way is the closest parallel; alternatives are available. *Engineering communication can use canonical names or substitutes per audience.*"
