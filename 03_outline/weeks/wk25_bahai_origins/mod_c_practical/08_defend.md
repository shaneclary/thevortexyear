## 0. Authoring notes
Seventy-fifth Defend. Builders are free to use the math under any name. Practical-side defence of math/name separability with builder accommodation.

## 1. Outcome
Student can defend the framework's accommodation of builders who substitute names while preserving the math.

## 2. Hook (0:00–0:30)
*[A builder at a workbench, looking up the canonical VBM name and substituting it in their technical document — operating freely.]*

> "*Can builders use the math under any name?* Six minutes."

## 3. Setup (0:30–1:00)
> "Lens 8 of 9. Defend builder freedom; preserve math."

## 4. Body (1:00–4:30)

> "The objection. *VBM names its central torus 'ABHA Torus' — a Bahá'í-sacred word. If a secular builder wants to use VBM's mathematical content in their engineering work, do they have to use 'ABHA' too? Doesn't this name choice gatekeep the framework's audience?*
>
> *The objection is sincere; engineers ask whether the naming is a religious test.*
>
> *[pause]*
>
> *The honest answer.*
>
> *No. Builders are free to use the math under any name. The naming gates audience but not capability.*
>
> Three things this answer affirms.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> *Affirm 1 — math is name-invariant.*
>
> The mod-9 modular arithmetic, the doubling cycle, the toroidal structure, the helix, the Symbol of Enlightenment — *all this math works under any name*. *Calling the torus 'Modular Torus' or 'Helical Torus' or 'X-Torus' doesn't change a single equation*. The math is invariant under name substitution.
>
> *Affirm 2 — engineers may publish under substitute names.*
>
> An engineer publishing a paper using VBM's mathematical content *isn't required to use canonical VBM-tradition names*. *Substitute names are acceptable* in technical communication. The math is the contribution; the names are flexible.
>
> *Affirm 3 — builders honor the tradition by using the math well.*
>
> *Doing the math right* honors the tradition that gave it. *The framework's value to the world is its math content; engineers who use the math well — under any name — extend the framework's value*. *Builder activity honors source.*
>
> *[pause]*
>
> *What can be defended.*
>
> *Defend 1.* Math is mainstream and name-invariant.
>
> *Defend 2.* Builders may substitute names per audience.
>
> *Defend 3.* Engineering value is in correct math, not in name fidelity.
>
> *Defend 4.* Tradition is honored by good engineering using the math, regardless of which names appear in the engineering documents.
>
> *Defend 5.* Acknowledging origin (e.g., one-line provenance note) is good practice but doesn't require canonical-naming.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> *What can't be defended.*
>
> *Can't 1.* That builders should *erase* the framework's Bahá'í origin. *Erasing origin is dishonest; provenance acknowledgment is good practice.*
>
> *Can't 2.* That canonical names are *interchangeable* with all substitutes in *all contexts*. *Within VBM-tradition contexts, canonical names are appropriate; the canonical/substitute choice is audience-dependent.*
>
> *Honest acknowledgment.*
>
> *[pause]*
>
> *Honest articulation to a builder.*
>
> *Articulation.* 'Yes, you can use VBM's mathematical content in your engineering work under any name. The math — modular arithmetic, doubling cycle, toroidal geometry — is mainstream and name-invariant. Substituting names like "Modular Torus" for "ABHA Torus" doesn't change the math; it changes the audience and the cultural-acknowledgment level. For technical engineering work where the Bahá'í-tradition context is irrelevant, substitute names are appropriate; for VBM-tradition or course contexts, canonical names are appropriate. Acknowledging origin in a provenance note is good practice but doesn't require canonical naming. The framework gives you the math; how you name what you build with it is your engineering choice. Building well honors the source regardless of which names appear in your documents.'
>
> Around 130 words.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> *Why this honest acknowledgment is the right defence.*
>
> *Reason 1 — engineering accessibility.* Affirming substitute-naming *opens the framework to all engineers*. *Religious-test gatekeeping would shrink the audience.*
>
> *Reason 2 — math integrity.* Affirming name-invariance *preserves math's universal applicability*. *Math doesn't belong to a religious tradition; it belongs to whoever does it correctly.*
>
> *Reason 3 — tradition respect.* Affirming origin-acknowledgment *honors tradition without requiring adoption*. *Provenance notes are minimal acknowledgment; they're sufficient.*
>
> *[pause]*
>
> *Calibrated answer.*
>
> *Yes, builders are free to use VBM's mathematical content under any name. The math is mainstream and name-invariant; substitute names are appropriate in technical contexts where the Bahá'í-tradition context is irrelevant; canonical names are appropriate in VBM-tradition or course contexts; provenance acknowledgment is good practice but doesn't require canonical naming. The framework's value is in its math; engineers who do the math well honor the source regardless of naming choices. Builder freedom and tradition respect coexist.*
>
> Around 95 words.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> *The principle restated.* *Builders are free; math is invariant; provenance acknowledgment is sufficient.* Three claims; defendable; consistent.
>
> Practical takeaway. *Use the math under any name in your work.* The framework gives you mathematical content; *naming is your engineering choice per audience*. Honor the source through good math; provenance notes if appropriate; substitute names as needed."
