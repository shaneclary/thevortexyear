## 0. Authoring notes
Naming as commitment. Contemplative on what it means to commit to a name — to use it consistently, to defend it, to live with its consequences.

## 1. Outcome
Student has spent quiet time with naming as ongoing commitment rather than one-time decision.

## 2. Hook (0:00–0:30)
*[A name being signed at the bottom of a long-running document; the same name being used over decades; commitment visualised across time.]*

> *(slow)* "Naming as commitment. Across time. Sit with it."

## 3. Setup (0:30–1:00)
> "Lens 5 of 9. Six minutes, slower."

## 4. Body (1:00–4:30) — *contemplative*

> *(slow)* "*Naming is commitment.* Once a thing is named, *the name carries forward*. Documents, conversations, citations, devices — *each use of the name extends the commitment*.
>
> *VBM's commitment to 'ABHA Torus' is decades-old.* Every paper, every workshop, every transmission carries the name. *The commitment compounds.*
>
> *[long pause — 12 seconds]*
>
> Sit with naming as compounding commitment.
>
> *[long pause]*
>
> *Three contemplations on commitment.*
>
> *Contemplation one — names accumulate weight.* A name used once carries little weight. *A name used over decades carries the weight of all those uses.* ABHA Torus, by the time you encounter it, has been named thousands of times. *Each previous use deepens the commitment.*
>
> *Contemplation two — commitment shapes audience.* The accumulated commitment to a Bahá'í-rooted name has *shaped VBM's audience over decades*. *Bahá'í practitioners welcomed; secular engineers required to acknowledge tradition.* The framework's audience is partly the consequence of its naming commitment.
>
> *Contemplation three — commitment can be honored, can be revisited.* Most commitments aren't permanent in a strict sense; they're *honored as long as the reasoning supporting them holds*. *VBM could rename the torus; it hasn't.* The continued non-renaming is itself a re-commitment.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> *What does engaging naming as commitment feel like?*
>
> Some students notice *gravity*. *Names carry weight; ours, others'.* Naming a thing isn't casual; *it's a commitment to use the name forward*.
>
> Some students notice *responsibility*. *If you adopt VBM's vocabulary*, you take on partial commitment to its naming. *Choose deliberately; substitute when useful; don't adopt casually.*
>
> Some students notice *interest*. *The commitment dynamic is itself interesting.* Why some names persist; why others fade; what makes a name carry decades.
>
> All three are honest.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> *A practical observation.*
>
> The course's *use of canonical VBM names* — ABHA Torus, Symbol of Enlightenment, Decoqubit, Sanctified Mirrors, Holographic Hypersphere — *is itself a commitment*. *We've named these objects with these names across many lessons.* By the time the course is complete, the commitment will be substantial.
>
> *Subsequent students using the course will inherit the commitment*. *Renaming would require explanation.* The commitment compounds course-internally as well as community-externally.
>
> *Awareness of this commitment is part of teaching with care.*
>
> *[pause]*
>
> *One more contemplation.*
>
> *The commitment to names is a kind of fidelity.* Fidelity to Marko's choices; to the tradition that supplied the names; to the students who learned the framework under these names.
>
> *Fidelity is reciprocal.* If we commit to use the names *with care* — neither casually nor zealously — *the names commit to us*. They become reliable handles for the framework's content. *Fidelity returns continuity.*
>
> *Whether or not we adhere to Bahá'í tradition, fidelity to the names honors the framework's character.*
>
> *[long pause — 12 seconds]*
>
> *Hold naming as commitment.* Compounding across time; shaping audience; reciprocal fidelity. *Notice the weight.* Continue."
