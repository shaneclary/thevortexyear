## 0. Authoring notes
The depth of the choice. Contemplative on the depth of naming a mathematical framework after a Bahá'í sacred word.

## 1. Outcome
Student has spent quiet time with the depth of the naming choice and what it means about VBM's character.

## 2. Hook (0:00–0:30)
*[The calligraphic ABHA fading slowly into the ABHA Torus diagram — visual transition from sacred name to mathematical object.]*

> *(slow)* "The depth of the choice. Sit with it."

## 3. Setup (0:30–1:00)
> "Lens 5 of 9. Six minutes, slower."

## 4. Body (1:00–4:30) — *contemplative*

> *(slow)* "Mathematical objects have names. *Most names are mundane* — labels for convenience. *Some names are weighted.*
>
> *VBM's choice to name its central torus 'ABHA' — the Most Great Name — is a weighted choice.*
>
> *[long pause — 12 seconds]*
>
> Sit with the weight.
>
> *[long pause]*
>
> *Three contemplations on the depth.*
>
> *Contemplation one — what naming declares.* Names declare *what a thing is meant to be*. Calling a torus 'ABHA' declares: *this object is, in its tradition-relative significance, the most glorious of mathematical objects we propose*. *Aspirational naming; large declaration.*
>
> *Contemplation two — risk of naming.* Sacred names *can be misused*. *Borrowing a Bahá'í-sacred word for a mathematical object risks trivialising the sacred or overclaiming for the math.* Marko's choice is intentional; whether the choice is well-judged is a separate question.
>
> *Contemplation three — naming as commitment.* When you name a thing, you commit to it. *Marko committed VBM to a Bahá'í-tradition lineage by choosing 'ABHA'.* That commitment shapes the framework's reception, vocabulary, and audience.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> *What does sitting with the depth feel like?*
>
> Some students feel *gravity*. *The naming choice is weighty.* Mathematical work doesn't usually carry sacred-name weight; this work does.
>
> Some students feel *uncertainty*. *Should mathematical work carry sacred-name weight?* Different students answer this differently.
>
> Some students feel *interest*. *The sacred-name weight invites questions about what mathematical work can be.*
>
> All three are honest.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> *A reflection on tradition.*
>
> Bahá'í tradition takes ABHA seriously — *liturgically, theologically, calligraphically*. Borrowing it means *engaging the tradition's seriousness*, even at one remove. *VBM's audience inherits this engagement.*
>
> *Some practitioners welcome the engagement.* For them, the sacred dimension is part of the framework's appeal.
>
> *Some practitioners find it awkward.* They want math without religious overlay; the naming requires acknowledging the overlay.
>
> *Both responses are legitimate.*
>
> *[pause]*
>
> *The course's posture.*
>
> *Engage the depth honestly.* Names matter; this name matters; we acknowledge that.
>
> *Maintain math/naming separation.* The mathematics is engageable independently of the names. *Engineers may use the math under any name.*
>
> *Hold the choice as Marko's.* *He chose this name, knowing what it carried.* The choice is biographically grounded and traditionally rooted. *We engage the choice without ourselves being required to adopt the tradition.*
>
> *[long pause — 12 seconds]*
>
> *One more contemplation. The depth of naming is itself part of VBM's character.* A framework that named its torus 'CompactToroidalMod9' would be a different framework. *The Bahá'í-traditional naming is part of what VBM is.*
>
> *Whether or not students adhere to Bahá'í tradition, engaging the framework includes engaging the names.* *Names are doors; what they open varies by who walks through.*
>
> *[pause]*
>
> Hold the depth as depth. *Naming as commitment, as risk, as character.* Notice the weight. Continue."
