> *What ABHA means in Bahá'í tradition.*
>
> *Translation.* From Arabic *al-Abhá* — 'the Most Glorious'. *A superlative; intensifier of the root B-H-Á (glory, splendour).*
>
> *Liturgical position.* In Bahá'í tradition, ABHA is *the Most Great Name* — invoked in prayer, written in calligraphy, central to devotional practice.
>
> *Traditional usage.* Often appears in formulas like *Yá Bahá'u'l-Abhá* — 'O Glory of the Most Glorious'. *Foundational to Bahá'í liturgy.*
>
> *[pause]*
>
> *What ABHA does in VBM.*
>
> *VBM uses 'ABHA' as a name for objects in its mathematical framework.* The *ABHA Torus* (Wk 18) is the primary example. *The naming is deliberate.*
>
> *The naming choice does three things:*
>
> *Thing one — historical placement.* Names VBM as growing out of Bahá'í tradition. *The framework wears its origin.*
>
> *Thing two — superlative resonance.* *'Most Glorious'* points at the framework's largest claims — the cosmological, the universal. *Aspirational language.*
>
> *Thing three — numerological connection.* In Bahá'í Abjad numerology, the letters of *Bahá* sum to 9. *Wk 25 will work through this calculation.* *The framework's centring on 9 has Bahá'í numerological roots.*

> *Three observations on the calligraphy.*

> *Observation one — sacred craft.* Bahá'í calligraphy is *centuries-old craft*. Specific calligraphers (Mishkín-Qalam, others) developed canonical forms. *The word has visual weight from tradition.*

> *Observation two — visual symmetry.* The word ABHA in Arabic calligraphy often has *visual symmetry properties* — vertical mirror, balance. *This is intentional in the calligraphy and resonates with VBM's interest in symbolic geometry.*
>
> *Observation three — VBM's reception.* VBM uses Bahá'í-tradition names for its objects. *Whether or not students engage Bahá'í tradition, the names carry tradition weight.*
