## 0. Authoring notes
Cut the printed Symbol into a strip. Physical reproduction of the unfolding move.

## 1. Outcome
Student has cut a printed Symbol of Enlightenment into a strip and pasted it into the build log.

## 2. Hook (0:00–0:30)
*[A printed Symbol about to be cut, scissors hovering at the gap between 9 and 1.]*

> "Today: cut the circle. By hand."

## 3. Setup (0:30–1:00)
> "Lens 3 of 9. The unfolding, made physical."

## 4. Body (1:00–4:30)

> "Today's build is small but symbolic. You will physically cut a printed Symbol of Enlightenment and convert it from a circle into a strip.
>
> The point isn't a precise artefact. The point is the *act of cutting*. The unfolding move you saw in Lens 1 becomes a thing you actually did.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> *Materials.*
>
> A printed Symbol of Enlightenment. The course-issued PDF, printed at any reasonable size — about 10 to 15 cm in diameter is fine. If you don't have a printout, hand-draw a Symbol on paper.
>
> Scissors. A sharp pair if you have one.
>
> Tape or glue.
>
> Your build log, open to a fresh page reserved for Wk 14.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> *Step 1 — orient the Symbol.* Place it flat in front of you, with 9 at the top and 1 to 8 clockwise.
>
> *Step 2 — find the cut line.* The cut goes between 9 and 1. Picture a straight line from the centre of the circle outward, passing exactly between the 9 at the top and the 1 just clockwise of it. That's where you cut.
>
> *Step 3 — cut radially.* Cut from the outer edge of the circle, along the line, *to the centre*. Don't cut beyond the centre. Stop at the central point.
>
> *Step 4 — cut around the perimeter.* From the centre — without lifting the scissors if you're skilled with them, or in a fresh cut otherwise — cut around the inside of the numerals, just inside the circle, leaving a thin ring of paper around the centre. You're freeing the circle from its background.
>
> *Step 5 — straighten.* Take the freed paper ring and *straighten it*. The numbers will lie along the resulting strip, 1 at one end and 9 at the other. The strip will be *curved* if you printed the Symbol on stiff paper — that's fine, just lay it flat.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> If the cutting goes wrong — and for many students it does on the first try — print a second Symbol and try again. The first attempt is for learning; the second usually works.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> *Step 6 — paste into your build log.* Tape or glue the strip into your Wk 14 page. Title the page *Wk 14 — From Circle to Strip — physical artefact*.
>
> Caption it: *Symbol of Enlightenment, unfolded into a strip. The cut is between 9 and 1; the implicit closure is now between the two ends of the strip.*
>
> *[pause]*
>
> What you've done. *You've physically performed the geometric unfolding* you watched in the Hook. Cutting and pasting are not advanced technique; the *concept* is what matters. The same operation will be applied conceptually to higher-dimensional structures throughout Q2 — a torus is a strip with both ends joined, a hypersphere is a similar joining at higher dimension. The *cutting and joining* moves repeat.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> Three observations on the act.
>
> *Observation one.* The cut destroys the circle's symmetry. The strip has two distinct ends. A circle had no ends. *Cutting introduces orientation.*
>
> *Observation two.* The strip can be *re-joined* by taping the two ends together. The original circle returns. *Unfolding and re-folding are inverse operations.*
>
> *Observation three.* You can also *not* re-join the ends and instead extend the strip — paste another strip onto one end. The strip can grow to any length. *The strip is the natural extension of the circle once we want room to extend.*"
