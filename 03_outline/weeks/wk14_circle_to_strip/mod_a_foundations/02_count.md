## 0. Authoring notes
Count cells in a 1×6 strip, then 2×6, then preview the 6×24. Introduces the two-axis structure.

## 1. Outcome
Student can count cells in increasingly tall strips and see the second dimension scaling.

## 4. Body (1:00–4:30)

> "Today we count cells. Cell counting is a small operation, but it scales fast as we add the second dimension.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> *Strip one — the 1×6 doubling row.* A strip showing only the doubling-circuit positions: 1, 2, 4, 8, 7, 5. Six cells. One row.
>
> *Cells: 6.*
>
> *Note.* The 1×6 strip is what the *units* of (ℤ/9ℤ)* trace under doubling. The non-units (3, 6, 9) aren't on this strip. The strip captures only the doubling-relevant positions.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> *Strip two — the 2×6 doubling-and-halving rows.* Stack the doubling row and the halving row. Top row: 1, 2, 4, 8, 7, 5. Bottom row: 1, 5, 7, 8, 4, 2. Two rows × six columns.
>
> *Cells: 12.*
>
> *Note.* Doubling and halving are inverse operations on the same six positions. The two rows show the same six numbers in opposite traversal order. Reading the columns: column 1 is (1, 1), column 2 is (2, 5), column 3 is (4, 7), column 4 is (8, 8), column 5 is (7, 4), column 6 is (5, 2). The columns *pair* numbers that are multiplicative inverses mod 9.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> *Strip three — the 6×24 Rodin Number Map preview.* Six rows. Twenty-four columns. Each cell will hold a digit-root of (2ʳ × 10ᶜ) for row r and column c. Wk 15 derives this carefully; today we just count cells.
>
> *Cells: 6 × 24 = 144.*
>
> *Note.* 144 is also 12² — sometimes called the *gross* in old commercial usage. Sometimes called *Fibonacci index 12* — F(12) = 144. The number 144 is the cell count of the basic Rodin Number Map. The *Pisano period 24* you met in Wk 11 is the column count.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> Three cell-count observations.
>
> *Observation one — the column count comes from order.* The doubling cycle has order 6 (Wk 4). That's the row count. The Pisano period mod 9 is 24 (Wk 11). That's the column count. Both numbers come from Q1 mathematics. The grid isn't arbitrary; its dimensions are *fixed by the underlying group structure*.
>
> *Observation two — the cells will repeat.* When the grid is laid out, each cell holds a digital root from {1, 2, 3, 4, 5, 6, 7, 8, 9}. With 144 cells and 9 possible values, many cells will share values. The *pattern* of repetition is the structure we're after.
>
> *Observation three — the count is small.* 144 isn't a large number. The full Rodin Number Map fits on one A4 page if drawn neatly. You'll draw it next week, by hand, in approximately twenty minutes.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> Practical takeaway. *Memorise three numbers: 6, 24, 144.* Six is the doubling order. Twenty-four is the Pisano period. One hundred and forty-four is their product, the cell count of the basic grid.
>
> These three numbers will keep appearing across Q2."
