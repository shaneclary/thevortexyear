## 4. Body (1:00–4:30)

> "Today's application is small and immediate. We turn the strip you cut yesterday into a *working bookmark* — something you carry in a book or notebook every day for the rest of Q2.
>
> The point isn't decoration. The point is *exposure*. Seeing the strip every time you open the book burns the structure into long-term memory.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> *Materials.*
>
> The strip you cut in Wk 14 Lens 3 — or a fresh one if that one's pasted into your build log already.
>
> A book or notebook you reach for daily. Could be your build log, your work notebook, a current reading book, anything.
>
> *Optional:* clear plastic adhesive sheets or thin laminate, to protect the bookmark over time.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> *Step 1 — make a fresh strip if needed.* If the cut strip is already pasted into your build log, print or hand-draw a second copy. Cut it the same way.
>
> *Step 2 — annotate the strip.* On the back of the strip, write three things in small print:
>
> Top: *Doubling: 1 → 2 → 4 → 8 → 7 → 5 → 1.*
>
> Middle: *Polar pairs: (1,8) (2,7) (4,5) — sum to 9.*
>
> Bottom: *Families: {1,4,7} {2,5,8} {3,6,9}.*
>
> The annotations make the strip useful even when the front is hidden.
>
> *Step 3 — laminate or sleeve, optional.* If you have laminate, protect the strip. It will be handled often.
>
> *Step 4 — place it in your daily book.* Use it as a bookmark.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> *How to use it.* Two practices.
>
> *Practice one — passive exposure.* Each time you open the book, the strip is in your hand. Don't force study. Just see it. Repetition does the work.
>
> *Practice two — active recall.* Once a day or so, glance at the strip and silently recite: *the doubling circuit*, *the polar pairs*, *the families*. Don't read the annotations. Try to recall, then check.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> *Why this works.* Three reasons.
>
> *Reason one — distributed practice.* Five seconds of recall once a day beats five minutes of cramming once a month for long-term retention. The strip enables distributed practice automatically.
>
> *Reason two — context-free exposure.* Seeing the structure outside the course videos generalises the memory. The strip becomes part of your *background environment*, not just course content.
>
> *Reason three — physical artefact.* A paper strip you carry has a different memory affordance than a digital flashcard. Physical objects integrate into bodily memory differently.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> *Practical observations.*
>
> The bookmark gets *handled*. After a few weeks it will be slightly worn, slightly grimy, slightly creased. That's fine; that's evidence of use.
>
> The bookmark *travels*. It moves with the book, into bags, onto tables. It accidentally becomes a quiet ambient teacher.
>
> Other people will *notice it*. Coworkers, friends, family members will ask. You now have a thirty-second answer prepared — the calibrated paragraph from Wk 13's Defend lesson.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> One observation. This is the *first* persistent physical artefact from Q2. By Q4 you will have the integrated Symbol on the wall, the bookmark in the book, the build log on the shelf, the mini-build on display, and (after Q3) a coil somewhere visible. The course gradually populates your environment with reminders. *Each artefact is small. Together they form a context that supports continued attention.*"
