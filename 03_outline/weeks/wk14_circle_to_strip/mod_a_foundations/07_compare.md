## 0. Authoring notes
Strip vs. flat circle — what each visualisation does well and what it leaves out.

## 1. Outcome
Student can articulate when to reach for the circle visualisation and when to reach for the strip.

## 2. Hook (0:00–0:30)
*[Side-by-side: circle Symbol on the left, strip Symbol on the right, doubling circuit traced on each.]*

> "Same math, two visualisations. Six minutes."

## 3. Setup (0:30–1:00)
> "Lens 7 of 9. Compare the circle and the strip as carriers of the same arithmetic."

## 4. Body (1:00–4:30)

> "Both the circle and the strip carry the same nine numbers and the same modular arithmetic. They differ in *what they make easy to see*.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> Three things the *circle* makes easy.
>
> *Circle-easy one — closure.* The wrap from 9 back to 1 is *visible*. You see the loop. The cyclic nature of the group is on the page.
>
> *Circle-easy two — symmetry.* All nine positions are *equivalent* under rotation by 40°. The circle's geometric symmetry mirrors the group's algebraic symmetry. The 3-6-9 axis becomes an actual axis of symmetry.
>
> *Circle-easy three — polar pair geometry.* The pairs (1,8), (2,7), (4,5) sit on parallel chords across the centre. The geometry of the pairs *is* their algebraic property.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> Three things the *strip* makes easy.
>
> *Strip-easy one — extension.* The strip can grow. Add more numbers along its length and the strip extends naturally; the circle can't extend without losing its shape.
>
> *Strip-easy two — second axis.* The strip has width. A second arithmetic axis (powers of 10, coming next week) goes *across* the width. The circle has no perpendicular direction.
>
> *Strip-easy three — comparison.* Multiple strips can be stacked vertically and compared row-by-row. Doubling circuit on top, halving on bottom. The strip's geometry supports stacking; the circle doesn't.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> Three things both *make hard*.
>
> *Hard for both — the 3D lift.* Neither the circle nor the strip is yet 3D. Both are flat. The torus you'll build in Wk 16 is what genuinely lifts to 3D.
>
> *Hard for both — the engineering bridge.* Neither directly tells you how to wind a coil. The strip is closer (it's the unrolled coil substrate), but the *winding* needs the toroidal closure plus the helical path.
>
> *Hard for both — physics interpretation.* Neither visualisation makes claims about energy, fields, or propulsion. Both are pure geometric/arithmetic representations.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> When to reach for *which*.
>
> *Reach for the circle when:* you want to emphasise cyclic structure, group-theoretic symmetry, or the visual identity of the Symbol of Enlightenment.
>
> *Reach for the strip when:* you want to extend, add a second axis, compare stacked rows, or lay out a coil winding.
>
> *Use both when:* you want to teach. Showing the same arithmetic on two visualisations makes the underlying structure visible *in itself*, distinct from any one diagram. The math is what's preserved when you change the diagram.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> Useful framing. *The circle and the strip are not competing visualisations. They are two cross-sections of the same mathematical content. Q2 will introduce more cross-sections — the grid, the torus, the helical winding. Each is a tool. None is the truth.*
>
> Practical takeaway. *Don't replace the circle with the strip.* Keep the integrated Symbol on the wall. Add the strip to your bookmark. By Wk 16 you'll add a torus model. By Q3 you'll add a coil. *Each visualisation pulls forward different features of the same underlying math.* A working VBM practitioner uses several visualisations fluently, not one.
>
> The circle teaches symmetry. The strip teaches extension. The torus will teach closure-in-3D. The coil will teach winding."
