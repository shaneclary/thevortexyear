## 4. Body (1:00–4:30)

> "*Frame.*
>
> The clip you're about to hear is Marko Rodin describing the moment in his work when the circular Symbol stopped being enough and the strip — and later, the grid — became necessary. The original 1973 diagram had everything on a circle. By the early 1980s, Marko was working with extended 2D layouts.
>
> The clip is approximately fifty seconds. **`[ORG-CLIP]`**.
>
> What to listen for. Three things.
>
> *One — the inadequacy of the circle.* Marko will likely describe a specific question or pattern that the circle couldn't accommodate. The circle is closed; once you've laid out all nine positions, there's no room for additional structure on the same diagram.
>
> *Two — the role of base 10.* The strip's second axis comes from powers of 10. Marko will probably mention this — the connection between scale invariance (the 10ᵏ ≡ 1 mod 9 result you proved in Wk 9) and the need for a column dimension.
>
> *Three — the moment of seeing 'the grid'.* Marko occasionally describes the recognition that the strip's empty width *should* be filled with a second axis. The recognition was visual, not purely arithmetic. Listen for the visual framing.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> *[Clip plays — approximately 50 seconds. **`[ORG-CLIP]`** — to be selected from foundation archive.]*
>
> *[pause]*
>
> *Contextualise.*
>
> What you just heard tells you several things about how the move from circle to strip happened historically.
>
> *Move-thing one.* The move was *driven by a specific limitation*, not by aesthetic preference. The circle ran out of room. Specific arithmetic relationships — the way doubling and powers-of-10 *interact* — couldn't be drawn on a closed loop.
>
> *Move-thing two.* The two axes have *different mathematical roles*. The doubling axis is the *operation*: applying multiplication by 2 repeatedly. The powers-of-10 axis is the *scale*: applying base-10 shifts repeatedly. *Two distinct operations*, each with cyclic structure, displayed on perpendicular axes.
>
> *Move-thing three.* The strip is a *bridge* artefact. It's not Marko's destination. The destination is the toroidal grid (Wk 15-16). The strip is the intermediate object that demonstrates *why* the next move (wrapping into a torus) is needed.
>
> *Move-thing four.* The move was hand-drawn, not computer-generated. Marko has noted that the patterns he found weren't predicted; they emerged when the grid was actually drawn. *Hand-drawing is part of the discovery method*, even at this stage of the work.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> Practical takeaway. *Hand-draw the strip yourself before next week.* Tomorrow's lesson is contemplative; the day after that begins the body of the strip work. You'll spend more time with the strip if it's already in your hand and on your page.
>
> One more practical note. Marko's tone in clips on this material is often slower and more careful than his discovery-story clips. The strip work is *technical*; he wants the student to follow the steps. Mirror the pace. Don't try to absorb the full structure today; the week has eight more lessons to layer it in."
