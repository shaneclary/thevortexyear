## 4. Body (1:00–4:30)

> "Today's Mod C looks ahead. The 6×24 strip you've been studying is also — *prophetically* — the unrolled surface of a Rodin Coil. When Q3 arrives and you wind copper, this same strip becomes the chart on your bench.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> *Why the strip works as a wiring chart.* Three reasons.
>
> *Reason one — cells correspond to winding positions.* Each cell of the strip will eventually correspond to *a specific position on the coil's surface*. The doubling order (vertical) determines which strand passes through that position. The scale axis (horizontal) determines where along the coil's length that position sits.
>
> *Reason two — the cycle structure becomes the wind path.* The doubling cycle (1 → 2 → 4 → 8 → 7 → 5 → 1) determines the *order* in which winding positions are visited as you wind. Reading the strip cell-by-cell tells you which position to wind to next.
>
> *Reason three — the periodicity matches the coil's geometry.* Six rows × twenty-four columns = 144 cells. A canonical Rodin Coil has 144 winding positions before the pattern repeats. The arithmetic anticipates the engineering.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> *What the strip will look like as a wiring chart.* Three features.
>
> *Feature one — annotated cells.* In Q3, each cell will gain annotations: which strand passes through (1 of 6 typically), the angular position on the torus, the longitudinal position. The strip becomes dense with engineering metadata.
>
> *Feature two — colour-coding by strand.* Multi-strand coils have multiple wires winding simultaneously. Each strand gets a colour. The strip gains coloured highlights showing which cells belong to which strand.
>
> *Feature three — a sequencing arrow.* The wind path is *sequential*. The strip will have an arrow tracing 'wind here first, then here, then here'. The arrow follows the doubling order and the column progression in a specific pattern.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> *What today's blank strip is missing for that role.* Honest preview.
>
> *Missing one — the strand assignments.* Q3 will introduce strand counts. The strip's rows can be re-labelled by strand once you know how many strands you're winding.
>
> *Missing two — the physical dimensions.* Q3's coils have specific diameters and pitches. The strip's geometry stays the same; the *physical mapping* gets layered on.
>
> *Missing three — the termination points.* Real coils have leads — wires that come out at specific positions for connection. The strip will gain start/end markers in Q3.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> So today's strip is the *empty template*. It carries the topology — the position structure — but not the engineering. Q2 fills in the topology; Q3 layers the engineering on top.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> Three observations.
>
> *Observation one.* The strip is *both* a piece of pure mathematics (Mod B) and *a future engineering blueprint* (this lens). The same object serves both roles. This is unusual; mathematics and engineering blueprints rarely coincide so directly.
>
> *Observation two.* The decision to use the doubling structure as the winding path *isn't aesthetic*. The doubling produces a winding that has specific electromagnetic properties (Q3 will measure them). The math chose the wind path for reasons; we'll evaluate those reasons later.
>
> *Observation three.* The strip is *reusable*. The same template applies to single-strand coils, six-strand coils, multi-strand phased coils. The Q3 builds vary; the strip stays.
>
> Tape today's strip into your build log next to last week's first-coil paper plan. They are the same object at different stages of becoming."
