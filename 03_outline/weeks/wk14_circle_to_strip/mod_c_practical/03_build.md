## 0. Authoring notes
First paper strip on a former. Take a paper strip and curl it around a small cylinder to physically experience how the strip becomes a winding substrate.

## 1. Outcome
Student has wrapped a paper strip around a cylindrical former and observed how cells become winding positions.

> "Today: paper strip on a former. Watch the geometry transform."

## 3. Setup (0:30–1:00)

## 4. Body (1:00–4:30)

> "Today's build is hands-on but cheap. We wrap a paper strip around a cylindrical former and observe how the strip's flat geometry becomes the winding geometry of a coil.
>
> The point isn't precision. The point is *physical intuition* for the coil-as-wrapped-strip.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> *Materials.*
>
> A paper strip from this week's build (the one with row labels). If you've already pasted that into your build log, cut another from the empty grid sheet you scaffolded yesterday.
>
> A cylindrical former. Anything sturdy and roughly the size of a soda can: a cardboard tube from paper-towel rolls, an empty pasta container, a glass jar. The exact diameter doesn't matter for this exercise.
>
> Tape (clear or paper).
>
> Optional: coloured pens for marking the wrap path.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> *Step 1 — Orient the strip.* Place the strip flat with rows running horizontally and columns running vertically. (For this build, we'll wrap around the cylinder so columns run *around* and rows run *along the cylinder's length*.)
>
> *Step 2 — Begin the wrap.* Tape one end of the strip to the cylinder, with the strip's first column aligned vertically. The strip should curve around the cylinder's circumference.
>
> *Step 3 — Wrap.* Without overlap or gap, wrap the strip around the cylinder. If the strip is too long, wrap multiple times; if too short, wrap less than once. Either is fine.
>
> *Step 4 — Tape the end.* Secure the wrap.
>
> *Step 5 — Observe.*
>
> The columns now wrap *around* the cylinder. Column 0 is at one position; column 6 is a quarter-turn around (if you've been generous with column count); column 24 (if your strip has all twenty-four) wraps fully back to column 0.
>
> The rows now run *along the length* of the cylinder. Row 0 is at one position along the length; row 5 is at another.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> *Step 6 — Trace the doubling cycle.*
>
> Pick a coloured pen. On the wrapped strip, trace the doubling cycle (1, 2, 4, 8, 7, 5) by going from the cell labelled 1 to the cell labelled 2 to 4, etc. The trace will move *both around the cylinder and along its length* simultaneously.
>
> The trace looks like a *helix* on the cylinder.
>
> *That helix is the basic wind pattern of a Rodin Coil*, scaled to your paper-strip-on-tube experiment.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> What you've just demonstrated. *The flat strip becomes a helical wind path when wrapped around a cylinder.* The doubling cycle, which was a closed figure-eight on the original Symbol, is now a 3D helix. Q2 will refine this picture (the cylinder is ultimately a *torus*, not just a tube), but the helical-wind idea is already visible.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> Three observations.
>
> *Observation one.* The wrap converts *flat 2D structure* into *curved 3D structure*. The math doesn't change; the embedding does.
>
> *Observation two.* The cylinder is *almost* a torus, but not quite. A torus has the additional closure of the cylinder's length into a loop. Wk 16 will make the additional closure.
>
> *Observation three.* This is the *first time* you have seen a doubling-circuit-as-helix in 3D. By Q3 you will have wound copper helically on torus formers; today's paper version is the precursor.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> Photograph or sketch the wrapped strip into your build log. Caption it: *Doubling helix on a cylindrical former, paper version.*"
