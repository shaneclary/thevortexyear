> *Contextualise.*
>
> What you just heard tells you several things about how the strip operates in practice.
>
> *Practice-thing one.* The strip is *physical reference material* in Powell's workshop. It's pinned to the wall, taped to the bench, photographed on his phone. It's *available* during winding, not consulted before and put away.
>
> *Practice-thing two.* The strip *prevents errors*. Without it, multi-strand winding is easy to mess up — strands cross each other, positions get confused, and the resulting coil is no longer a clean Rodin pattern. The strip is the error-prevention tool.
>
> *Practice-thing three.* The strip is *teaching material* as well as building material. When Powell teaches new builders, he hands them the strip first and walks through it. The teaching workflow is *strip-first*, *coil-second*.
>
> *Practice-thing four.* The strip's authority *is its mathematical correctness*. Powell trusts the strip because the math is right. If a builder tries to deviate from the strip's pattern — to 'simplify' — the resulting coil isn't a Rodin Coil; it's something else. The mathematical structure is the structure of the build, not a guideline.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> Practical takeaway. *Treat the strip as a working tool from the start.* Don't store it in your build log only; print or copy a working version that lives on your bench. By Q3 you will have a *bench-strip* that's grimy, marked up with notes, and clearly different from the clean version in the build log.
>
> One more practical note. Powell occasionally distinguishes *the strip as concept* from *the strip as printed chart*. The concept is mathematical; the printed chart is the working tool. Both matter. The chart without the concept is a piece of paper; the concept without the chart is theory you can't act on. Have both."
