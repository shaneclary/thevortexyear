## 4. Body (1:00–4:30)

> "Today's apply produces a *sketch* — not a build. The point is to translate what the strip says into a coil drawing, so when Q3 introduces real winding, the translation is already practiced.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> *Materials.*
>
> Your build log, fresh page.
>
> A pencil. Eraser. Optional coloured pens.
>
> The strip you wrapped around the cylinder in Lens 3 — keep it nearby for reference.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> *The sketch you'll produce.* A torus seen from a quarter-turn angle, with the doubling helix traced on its surface in a single line. The torus and the helix together represent a single-strand single-traversal Rodin Coil.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> *Step 1 — draw the torus.* Sketch a torus using two ellipses: a large outer ellipse and a smaller inner one, with the inside of the doughnut hole shown. The torus should occupy most of the page.
>
> *Step 2 — divide the torus into 24 segments.* Using the strip's column count, mark 24 evenly-spaced positions around the torus's outer edge. These correspond to the strip's columns.
>
> *Step 3 — divide the cross-section into 6 positions.* At one column position, draw a small cross-section of the torus (a circle showing the doughnut's tube cut). Mark 6 positions around this small circle. These correspond to the strip's rows.
>
> *Step 4 — trace the doubling helix.* Starting at column 0, row 0 (which corresponds to value 1 on the doubling cycle), trace a line that:
>
> moves *forward* one column (column 0 → column 1) and
>
> moves *vertically* through the rows in the doubling order (row 0, value 1 → row 1, value 2 → row 2, value 4 → row 3, value 8 → row 4, value 7 → row 5, value 5 → wraps to row 0).
>
> The line spirals around the torus, advancing by one column per row, and after 24 columns and 4 traversals returns to the starting position.
>
> *Don't try to make the sketch geometrically perfect.* The point is to *suggest* the helical pattern, not to produce CAD-quality drawings.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> *Step 5 — annotate the sketch.* Label:
>
> The torus.
>
> One column position (e.g., column 12) for orientation.
>
> One cross-section showing the six row positions.
>
> The starting point of the helix at column 0, row 0.
>
> The helix's direction with an arrow.
>
> *Step 6 — caption.* Below the sketch, write *Rodin Coil — single strand, one traversal, drawn from the strip*.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> What you've sketched. *A torus with a helix wound on its surface.* The math you've been doing on a 2D strip *lifts* into 3D when wrapped around the torus's surface. The cells of the strip become positions on the surface; the doubling cycle becomes the helical traversal.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> Three observations.
>
> *Observation one.* This is the *same coil* Marko built in 1987. Single strand, single traversal, on a torus. The simplicity is intentional. Q3's canonical builds are six-strand four-traversal — denser, but built on the same skeleton.
>
> *Observation two.* The sketch is *not yet engineering*. There are no dimensions, no wire gauges, no termination points. Q3 layers those on. Today's sketch is the *topology* of the coil — its abstract shape — not its physical realisation.
>
> *Observation three.* The sketch makes the strip's role *visible*. Without the strip, you'd be drawing a coil from intuition. With the strip, you're drawing a coil *from a chart*. The chart is the structure; the coil is the manifestation.
>
> Sketch and caption. The strip is now a translator into 3D space."
