## 0. Authoring notes
Holding the strip feels different. Contemplative on the embodied difference between Q1's circle and Q2's strip.

## 1. Outcome
Student has spent quiet time with the embodied experience of holding the strip versus holding the circular Symbol.

## 2. Hook (0:00–0:30)
*[A circular Symbol in one hand, a paper strip in the other; slow alternation.]*

> *(slow)* "Hold them both. Notice the difference."

## 3. Setup (0:30–1:00)
> "Lens 5 of 9. Six minutes, slower."

## 4. Body (1:00–4:30) — *contemplative*

> *(slow)* "Take the circular Symbol of Enlightenment in one hand. Take a paper strip in the other. Hold them both. Compare what each *feels* like.
>
> *[long pause — 12 seconds]*
>
> The circle has a centre. Your eye returns to it. The structure is *gathered*. There's a felt sense of inward-orientation.
>
> The strip has no centre. Your eye runs along its length. The structure is *extended*. There's a felt sense of forward-orientation.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> A circle is a *closed* artefact. You can put it on a wall and it sits whole, complete in itself. A strip is an *open* artefact. You sense, even unconsciously, that it could be longer. The width could be filled. The ends could be joined.
>
> *Each artefact pulls its viewer differently.*
>
> *[long pause]*
>
> Two contemplations on this difference.
>
> *Contemplation one — closed forms invite stillness.* The circular Symbol on your wall asks for *being-with*. You sit with it. You return to it. Its content is dense; you find new things in re-looking, but the shape doesn't suggest motion outward.
>
> The strip on your wall asks for *moving-along*. The eye traces. The hand reaches toward the empty width. The shape suggests continuation.
>
> *Contemplation two — both are needed.* The mistake is to think one is *better* than the other. Q1 used closed forms; Q2 uses open forms. Different stages of the same long inquiry. The contemplative practitioner uses both.
>
> Closed-form practice: *being still with what is.* The circular Symbol on the wall.
>
> Open-form practice: *moving along with what becomes.* The strip extending across the bench.
>
> *[long pause — 12 seconds]*
>
> What does *holding* the strip feel like?
>
> Some students notice the strip is *light* — a thin paper rectangle. The circle is more substantial; it has a centre that anchors it. The strip blows in a slight breeze.
>
> Some students notice the strip is *manipulable* in ways the circle isn't. You can curl it, fold it, wrap it around a tube (which Lens 3 had you do). The circle resists this — bend a circular Symbol and you destroy the symmetry. The strip *invites* manipulation.
>
> Some students notice the strip is *less impressive*. The circle has visual gravity — a complete object. The strip looks unfinished. *That's the point.* The strip is the medium; the work happens *to* it.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> Three feelings practitioners report at this stage of the curriculum.
>
> *Feeling one — re-orientation.* The closed Symbol was familiar. The open strip is novel. There's a small disorientation that fades over a few weeks of looking at the strip.
>
> *Feeling two — possibility.* The strip's emptiness *promises*. Wk 15's filling will be felt as *arrival*, even though the structure itself is just arithmetic.
>
> *Feeling three — flatness.* The strip is currently flatter than the circle in significance. It hasn't yet earned the depth Q1 gave the Symbol. By the end of Q2, the strip — and its torus closure — will feel as full as the Symbol did. It just takes time.
>
> *[long pause]*
>
> The hand learns differently from the eye. By holding the strip, by wrapping it around a tube, by tracing the doubling on its surface, the hand begins to know the strip in ways the eye alone wouldn't. *Embodied knowledge accumulates over weeks of contact.*
>
> Sit with the strip in your hand for the rest of this lesson. Don't analyse it. Just hold it.
>
> *[long pause]*"
