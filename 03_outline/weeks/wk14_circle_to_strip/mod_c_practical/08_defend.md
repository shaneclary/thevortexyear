## 0. Authoring notes
Forty-second Defend. Why builders should embrace the strip view rather than winding from intuition.

## 1. Outcome

## 3. Setup (0:30–1:00)
> "Lens 8 of 9. Defend strip-first building."

## 4. Body (1:00–4:30)

> "The objection — coming from experienced builders. *I've wound coils for twenty years. I can wind a Rodin Coil from the photo of one. I don't need a chart; my hands know what to do.*
>
> The objection is honest. Experienced hands really can wind from a photo. But the strip-first practice is still the better default for three reasons.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> *Reason one — accuracy.* Multi-strand Rodin Coils have specific wind orderings. Winding from a photo, you can produce something that *looks like* a Rodin Coil but is actually a different topology. The differences may not show up until you try to measure or compare with another build. The strip prevents this; *the strip-correct wind is the Rodin-correct wind, by definition.*
>
> *Reason two — repeatability.* When you wind your second Rodin Coil six months after the first, you'll want it to be *the same*. Memory of how you wound the first is unreliable. The strip is a written record of the wind pattern, available across builds and across years. Even an experienced builder benefits from a written record.
>
> *Reason three — teaching.* You will, eventually, teach someone else to wind a Rodin Coil — even if just a friend who's curious. The strip is the teaching artefact. *Without a strip, the only way to teach is to wind alongside the learner.* With a strip, the learner can wind on their own with the strip as guide.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> *A counter-acknowledgement.* The strip *isn't perfect*. Three honest limitations.
>
> *Limitation one.* The strip captures topology but not workmanship. Wire tension, soldering quality, terminations — none of these are on the strip. The strip is the *plan*; workmanship is *execution*. A perfectly strip-correct wind with bad workmanship is still a bad coil.
>
> *Limitation two.* The strip is *2D*; the coil is 3D. Translation requires a small mental rotation. Some builders never quite internalise the translation; they need the wrapped strip (Lens 3's exercise) to bridge the gap.
>
> *Limitation three.* The strip *doesn't tell you about scale*. A 2-cm coil and a 20-cm coil have the same strip. Scale information is supplementary.
>
> The strip is the *necessary* but not *sufficient* tool for Rodin Coil building. It *plus* good workmanship *plus* careful scale considerations *plus* measurement gives a complete build.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> So the calibrated answer to *why use the strip*:
>
> *The strip ensures the wind is topologically a Rodin Coil rather than something that resembles one. It enables repeatability across builds and across years. It supports teaching the wind to others. Experienced builders may sometimes wind without it, but the strip-first habit prevents the kind of subtle errors that look fine but produce a non-Rodin topology. The strip is necessary; workmanship and scale are also required for a complete build.*
>
> Around eighty words.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> Practical takeaway. *Pin the strip to the wall above your bench when you wind.* Even if you don't think you need it, glance at it. The strip is cheap insurance against the kind of mistake you only catch by checking against the canonical pattern.
>
> One more practical note. The Rodin Foundation has emphasised, over the years, that *the strip is the standard of correctness* for Rodin Coil builds. A coil wound to a different pattern, however well-built, is not a Rodin Coil. The standard is mathematical; the strip is its working expression. Builders who skip the strip can produce excellent toroidal coils — but those coils may not be the *Rodin* coil that the framework's claims are about.
>
> Use the strip. It costs nothing and prevents real errors."
