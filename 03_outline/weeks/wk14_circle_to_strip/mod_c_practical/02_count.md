## 4. Body (1:00–4:30)

> "Today we count what each column of the strip will represent in coil hardware. The arithmetic is small; the implication is significant.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> *One column of the strip — six cells.* The cells correspond to six winding positions: the doubling cycle (1, 2, 4, 8, 7, 5).
>
> If a Rodin Coil has six strands wound simultaneously, *one column corresponds to one passage of all six strands across one slice of the coil's surface*. Six strands × six rows = thirty-six wire segments per column? No — six strands, *one row each*. Six wire segments per column.
>
> *Six segments per column × twenty-four columns = 144 wire segments total.* That's the canonical count for a six-strand single-pass Rodin Coil with one full traversal.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> *Most canonical builds use four traversals.* Four passes around the torus per strand, each pass covering all 24 columns. So the wire count becomes:
>
> Six strands × twenty-four columns × four traversals = *576 wire segments per coil*.
>
> Or, expressed differently: each strand traverses 24 columns per pass × 4 passes = 96 turns. Six strands × 96 turns = 576 segments. The same number.
>
> Note: 576 = 24 × 24 = 24². The Pisano period squared. The number isn't arbitrary; it's algebraic.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> *What a column gives you, practically.*
>
> *Practical one — slice information.* If you're holding a Rodin Coil and you cut it perpendicular to its axis at one column position, you'd see six wire cross-sections — one per strand. *Six dots in a vertical line.* The strip's column captures that cross-section.
>
> *Practical two — wind sequencing.* The order in which wires occupy a column position tells you the *winding sequence*. For column 0, strand 1 occupies row 0, strand 2 occupies row 1, etc. As columns advance, the strand-row mapping shifts in a regular pattern — that's what the doubling cycle determines.
>
> *Practical three — antipodal pairing.* Polar pairs (Wk 6) appear in columns. Cell at row 0 (value 1) and cell at row 3 (value 8) are polar. Cell at row 1 (value 2) and cell at row 4 (value 7) are polar. Cell at row 2 (value 4) and cell at row 5 (value 5) are polar. *Each column contains all three polar pairs, vertically stacked.*
>
> *[pause]*
>
> Three numbers to remember from Mod C of this week.
>
> *Six.* Winding positions per column.
>
> *144.* Total winding positions per single-traversal coil (six strands × twenty-four columns).
>
> *576.* Total wire segments in a four-traversal six-strand coil. Equal to 24².
>
> These numbers will recur throughout Q3.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> One observation. The numbers aren't yet *physical*. They are *combinatorial counts of cells in a grid*. Q3 will translate them into wire lengths, resistance values, and field calculations. Today's count is preparation.
>
> Practical takeaway. *Memorise six, 144, and 576.* Annotate your bookmark strip with these numbers on the back, below the doubling-circuit recitation."
