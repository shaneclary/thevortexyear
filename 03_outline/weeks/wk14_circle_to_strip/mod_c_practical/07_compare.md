## 4. Body (1:00–4:30)

> "*Mainstream electronics has a small zoo of coil topologies.* Three are most common; the Rodin Coil is a fourth.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> *Coil one — the solenoid.* A wire wound in a tight helical pattern around a straight cylindrical former. The simplest coil. The wind is *uniform*: every turn is geometrically equivalent to every other turn.
>
> Used for: electromagnets, inductors, electromechanical actuators.
>
> Strip representation: a *single column* of cells, one cell per turn.
>
> *Coil two — the toroidal inductor.* A wire wound around a doughnut-shaped (toroidal) former. Each turn passes through the doughnut hole. The wind is again uniform but the geometry is closed: no leakage at the ends as with a solenoid.
>
> Used for: power-supply inductors, RF chokes, electrical isolation.
>
> Strip representation: similar to a solenoid but with the implicit closure that the strip's left and right edges identify.
>
> *Coil three — multi-strand transformer windings.* Multiple wires wound simultaneously, often with phase relationships. The wires interact electromagnetically.
>
> Used for: transformers, three-phase electrical equipment, signal isolators.
>
> Strip representation: multiple parallel rows on the strip, one per strand.
>
> *Coil four — the Rodin Coil.* A specific multi-strand winding pattern on a torus, where the wind path follows the doubling cycle. *The wind is non-uniform*: turns are not all geometrically equivalent. The strip's structure is *encoded* in the wind path.
>
> Used for: research and experimental work on electromagnetic field structure; some claims about novel field configurations are made by Marko and Powell. Q3 will engage these claims carefully.
>
> Strip representation: the full 6×24 grid with the doubling cycle traced.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> Three structural similarities across all four.
>
> *Similarity one — wire on a former.* All four are 'wire wound on a substrate'. The basic engineering is the same.
>
> *Similarity two — strip-representable.* All four can be represented on a flat strip with rows = positions and columns = turns. The strip is *general enough* to encode any of them.
>
> *Similarity three — measurable with standard instruments.* Resistance, inductance, magnetic field — all measurable with ordinary equipment, regardless of which topology.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> Three differences that distinguish the Rodin Coil.
>
> *Difference one — non-uniform wind.* In a solenoid, every turn is the same. In a Rodin Coil, the *position* of each turn is determined by the doubling cycle, which is non-uniform around the torus.
>
> *Difference two — encoded structure.* The strip *isn't auxiliary* to the Rodin Coil; it *is* the structure. Removing the strip pattern changes the coil's identity. For other coils, the strip is just a wiring diagram.
>
> *Difference three — claimed novel behaviour.* Marko and Powell make specific claims about Rodin Coils having properties (field shape, efficiency) that distinguish them from other toroidal coils. The claims are tested in Q3. Mainstream toroidal coils make no such claims.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> Useful framing. *The Rodin Coil is one species in the genus 'wire wound on a former'. What distinguishes it is the specific wind pattern — the doubling cycle on a torus — and the claims attached to that pattern.*
>
> Practical takeaway. *If you have an electronics background, you already know how to build a coil.* The Rodin variant uses your existing skills with a different wind path. Q3's hardware work won't be alien to you.
>
> *If you don't have an electronics background*, the Rodin Coil is your *first* coil — and the strip is your roadmap. Q3 is hands-on building from scratch.
>
> Both starting points are accommodated. The strip works for both."
