## 0. Authoring notes
Forty-first Defend. Why the strip is well-defined topologically — addressing the concern that VBM's strip is informal or hand-wavy.

## 1. Outcome
Student can defend the VBM strip as a well-defined mathematical object — a fundamental domain of a specific group action.

## 4. Body (1:00–4:30)

> "The objection. *VBM presents the strip as if it were a precise mathematical object, but the description is informal. Where are the topology, the group action, the boundary identifications? Without these, the strip is a suggestive picture, not a defined object.*
>
> The objection is fair to the *level of detail* in many presentations. It is wrong about the *underlying object*. The strip *is* well-defined; the formal definition exists; it just doesn't always appear in introductory VBM material.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> *The formal definition.*
>
> The strip is the *fundamental domain* of the action of the group (ℤ/6ℤ × ℤ/24ℤ) on the torus T² = ℝ²/ℤ², parametrised so that the rows carry residues 2ʳ mod 9 and columns carry residues 10ᶜ mod 9.
>
> Equivalently: the strip is the quotient (ℝ × ℝ) / (6ℤ × 24ℤ) restricted to one fundamental domain (e.g., [0, 6) × [0, 24)), with cells indexed by integer pairs (r, c) and cell (r, c) holding the residue (2ʳ × 10ᶜ) mod 9.
>
> Equivalently again: the strip is a 2D discrete grid carrying the *Cayley graph* of the abelian group ⟨2, 10⟩ acting on ℤ/9ℤ, with the proviso that 10 acts trivially.
>
> *Three formal statements, all consistent.* The strip is well-defined under each.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> *What 'well-defined' means here.* Three properties:
>
> *Property one — uniqueness of cell content.* For each (r, c), the cell content (2ʳ × 10ᶜ) mod 9 is a uniquely determined residue in {0, 1, …, 8}. No ambiguity, no choice required.
>
> *Property two — periodicity.* The grid is periodic in both directions: row 6 = row 0, column 24 = column 0. The fundamental domain captures every orbit exactly once.
>
> *Property three — torus closure.* When the boundaries are identified (left edge to right edge, top edge to bottom edge), the strip becomes the surface of a torus. The closure operation is canonical.
>
> All three are checkable from the formal definition. The VBM strip passes.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> *Why the informal presentations work.* In introductory teaching, the formal apparatus would obscure the intuition. The strip is *introduced* as a visual unfolding because that's how Marko discovered it and how students best learn it. The *formal* underpinning is available for verification when needed.
>
> This is a common pedagogy in mathematics. Calculus is introduced via slopes and areas before ε-δ definitions. Group theory is introduced via symmetries before Cayley tables. *Intuition first, formalisation as needed.*
>
> *[pause]*
>
> So the calibrated answer to *is the strip well-defined topologically*:
>
> *The strip is the fundamental domain of the action of (ℤ/6ℤ × ℤ/24ℤ) on a torus, with cells indexed by integer pairs (r, c) and cell content (2ʳ × 10ᶜ) mod 9. It is well-defined under multiple equivalent formal definitions. Introductory VBM presentations emphasise the visual unfolding rather than the formal apparatus, but the formal underpinning is available and can be checked. The strip is a precise mathematical object whose precision is rarely foregrounded.*
>
> Around eighty words.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> Practical takeaway. *When asked about formality, point to the fundamental-domain framing.* It connects VBM to standard topology in a way that mathematicians recognise immediately. The strip stops looking like an arts-and-crafts project; it starts looking like the discrete analogue of a torus fundamental domain — which is what it is.
>
> Hold the formal definition lightly. You don't need to lead with it. But know it's there."
