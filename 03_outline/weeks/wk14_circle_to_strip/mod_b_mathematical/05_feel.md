## 4. Body (1:00–4:30) — *contemplative*

> *(slow)* "Mathematics has dimensions. Not just *physical* dimensions — *conceptual* ones. A function of one variable lives in a different mental space than a function of two.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> Q1's mathematics was almost entirely 1D. The Symbol of Enlightenment is a 1D object — a closed curve. The doubling circuit traces a path *along* the circle. The polar pairs are chords *across* a 1D circle. Even the 'family groups' partition a 1D set.
>
> Q2 begins with a 2D move. The strip's two axes turn 1D arithmetic into 2D structure.
>
> *[long pause — 12 seconds]*
>
> Sit with the dimensional jump.
>
> *[long pause]*
>
> A 1D mind asks: *what comes next in the sequence?* It traces forward and backward along a single thread.
>
> A 2D mind asks: *what's at this position, and what's at that one, and how do they relate?* It moves not only forward and backward but also up and down. It compares horizontally adjacent values to vertically adjacent ones. It sees patterns that 1D thinking can't see.
>
> Plotting the doubling cycle on a 2D grid reveals features the 1D circle hid. *Some columns repeat the same value.* *Some rows match other rows shifted.* *Diagonals carry information.* None of these structures exist on a 1D circle.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> What does the dimensional jump *feel* like?
>
> Some students feel *expansion*. The math has more room to breathe. Patterns visible in 2D were always *implicit* in the 1D version, but invisible. Now they appear.
>
> Some students feel *vertigo*. Two axes is one more thing to track. The mind's eye has to hold position, direction, and value simultaneously. The cognitive load is higher.
>
> Both are honest. Dimensional jumps are real shifts in mental geometry.
>
> *[long pause — 12 seconds]*
>
> A practical observation. *Most insight comes from dimensional jumps.* A problem that resists 1D thinking often yields when raised to 2D. A 2D problem that resists often yields in 3D.
>
> Mathematics has done this many times historically. Cartesian coordinates lifted geometry from 1D constructions to 2D algebra. Tensors lifted 2D matrices to higher-rank objects. Hilbert spaces lifted finite-dimensional spaces to infinite-dimensional ones. Each lift opened structures the previous level couldn't see.
>
> Q2's lift from circle to strip is small by these standards. *One dimension up.* But it is real, and it follows the same pattern: structures that were hiding in the 1D version become visible in the 2D version.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> Some students at this point feel *prepared*. They've done dimensional jumps before in other contexts — physics, programming, design — and recognise the move. They are ready to inhabit the strip's 2D structure.
>
> Some students feel *new*. This is their first explicit dimensional jump. They're going to need a few weeks to feel comfortable in 2D mathematical territory.
>
> Some students feel *suspicious*. *Why two axes? Why not three? Why not five?* Suspicion is healthy. The framework will eventually use higher dimensions (the torus is a 2D surface in 3D space; the Decoqubit suggests higher again). Today's jump is the *first* in a sequence.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> One more contemplation. The strip you cut yesterday, with its empty width, *invites* the second axis. The width isn't *waiting* — width doesn't have intentions. But you, looking at it, may feel *invited*. The empty space pulls forward. *Something belongs there.*
>
> Sit with the invitation. Continue."
