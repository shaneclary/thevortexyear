## 4. Body (1:00–4:30)

> "*Mainstream topology — unrolling a cylinder.* A cylinder is a 2D surface — the lateral surface of a tube. *Unrolling* the cylinder is the operation of cutting it along a vertical line and laying it flat. The result is a rectangle. Width: the circumference of the original cylinder. Height: the length of the cylinder. The unrolled rectangle is a *fundamental domain* — every point on the cylinder corresponds to exactly one point on the rectangle, with the proviso that the *left and right edges of the rectangle* are identified (taped together to recover the cylinder).
>
> *VBM strip.* A 6-row × 24-column grid. The vertical (rows) carries the doubling cycle, period 6. The horizontal (columns) will carry the Pisano-mod-9 cycle, period 24. The strip is a *fundamental domain* of the doubling+Pisano structure on the torus that Wk 16 will construct.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> Three structural similarities.
>
> *Similarity one — fundamental domain.* Both the cylinder unrolling and the VBM strip are *fundamental domains* of a periodic structure. Each cell appears exactly once; the boundaries are identified (will be identified, in the VBM case, when Wk 16 closes the strip into a torus).
>
> *Similarity two — periodicity becomes spatial.* In the cylinder, the angular periodicity (going around once and returning) becomes the *width* of the rectangle. In the VBM strip, the algebraic periodicity (the doubling cycle's period 6 and Pisano period 24) becomes the *height* and *width*. The same conceptual move — *exposing periodicity by cutting and laying flat*.
>
> *Similarity three — torus closure.* The cylinder, when its top and bottom are also identified, becomes a torus. The VBM strip, when both axes are identified, also becomes a torus. Both structures *target a torus* through the unrolling+identification pattern.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> Three structural differences.
>
> *Difference one — geometric vs. algebraic origin.* The mainstream cylinder unrolling starts from a *physical surface* and produces a flat representation. The VBM strip starts from an *algebraic structure* (group orders) and produces a flat representation that *anticipates* a future torus. The cylinder pre-exists; the VBM torus is constructed afterward.
>
> *Difference two — what the cells contain.* Mainstream unrolling cells are points on a continuous surface; values are continuous (positions, function values, etc.). VBM strip cells contain *discrete residues mod 9*. The strip is *discrete*; the unrolled cylinder is *continuous*.
>
> *Difference three — what's being identified at the boundaries.* Mainstream cylinder unrolling identifies *opposite vertical edges* (the cut). The VBM strip eventually identifies *both pairs of opposite edges* (vertical and horizontal) to form a torus. The mainstream cylinder is intermediate; the VBM strip is the target.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> Useful framing. *The VBM strip is a discrete-arithmetic analogue of a topological fundamental domain. The unrolling-and-identifying conceptual machinery is the same; the underlying mathematics is different (algebra vs. continuous geometry).*
>
> Practical takeaway. *If you're comfortable with topology, the strip is recognisable.* The strip is a discrete fundamental domain of the action of (ℤ/6ℤ × ℤ/24ℤ) on a torus. Mathematicians familiar with covering spaces or fundamental domains can read the strip immediately.
>
> *If you're not coming from topology*, the strip is its own object. You don't need topology to use it. But knowing the analogy is available means you can connect to mainstream literature when needed.
>
> A good cross-reference: any introductory text on covering spaces or on the topology of tori. The fundamental-domain framing translates directly to VBM."
