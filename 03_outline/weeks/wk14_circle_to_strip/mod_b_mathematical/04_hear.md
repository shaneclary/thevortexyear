The recognition wasn't immediate; the strip with only its vertical axis was on his page for some time before the horizontal axis — and the Fibonacci/Pisano structure that fills it — became visible to him.

> What to listen for. Three things.
>
> *One — the recognition that doubling and scaling are different.* Marko will likely note that 'multiplying by 2' and 'multiplying by 10' produce different effects in mod 9 — even though both are multiplications. Doubling cycles through six values; multiplying by 10 cycles through one. Different operations, different visual demands.
>
> *Two — the role of base 10 specifically.* The horizontal axis is base-dependent. Marko has emphasised this many times: base 10 is the natural fit for the strip because 10 ≡ 1 mod 9. In other bases, the horizontal axis would not be trivial in the same way.
>

> *Contextualise.*
>
> What you just heard tells you several things about how the two-axis insight came together.
>
> *Insight-thing one.* The two-axis structure was *layered into existence over time*, not designed up front. First the doubling cycle (one axis), then the recognition that scale (the second axis) needed its own direction, then the Fibonacci structure that *uses* both axes simultaneously. The framework grew by addition, not by master plan.
>
> *Insight-thing two.* The orthogonality between the axes is *meaningful*. Doubling and scaling commute (multiplication by 2 and multiplication by 10 commute) and are independent generators of *different* operations. Putting them on perpendicular axes mirrors their algebraic independence.
>
> *Insight-thing three.* Marko emphasises that the *visual* discovery preceded the algebraic systematisation. He drew the strip; he saw patterns; he then verified them with arithmetic. The hand and eye lead; the algebra confirms.
>
> *Insight-thing four.* The two-axis strip is a *necessary* foundation for the toroidal lift. Once the strip has both axes, joining one pair of opposite edges gives a cylinder, and joining the other pair gives a torus. The torus needs a 2D substrate; the strip provides it.
>
> *[pause]*

> Practical takeaway. *Don't try to grasp the full strip on first encounter.* The two-axis structure unfolds across Wk 14, 15, and 16. Today is the orientation; the body of the work is yet to come.
>
> One more practical note. Marko's tone in this clip is often slower than his discovery-story clips. The strip work is technical — careful, patient, derivation-heavy. Match his pace. Don't try to leap ahead to the torus."
