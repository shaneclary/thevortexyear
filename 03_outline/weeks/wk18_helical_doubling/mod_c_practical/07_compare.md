## 4. Body (1:00–4:30)

> "Rodin Coils, RF inductors, and transformers all use *wire wound around a former*. The differences in *winding pattern* are what distinguish them.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> *RF inductor (toroidal).* Wire wound around a toroidal core in a uniform helical pattern. Each turn passes through the core's hole and wraps around the body. Used for: RF chokes, signal inductors, EMI suppression.
>
> Wind pattern: *uniform helical*. Every turn is geometrically equivalent. Easy to compute inductance.
>
> *Transformer winding.* Multiple windings on a common core. Primary and secondary windings are wound separately, often in different directions. Used for: voltage transformation, isolation, impedance matching.
>
> Wind pattern: *separate uniform helices*, often layered. Geometrically simple but counted carefully for turn ratios.
>
> *Rodin Coil.* Wire wound around a torus former following the doubling helix path with (p, q) = (1, 4) winding numbers. Used for: research and experimental work; some claimed unconventional EM properties.
>
> Wind pattern: *non-uniform toroidal helix*. Turns aren't all geometrically equivalent. Specific structural pattern from mod-9 arithmetic.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> Three structural similarities.
>
> *Similarity one — toroidal core.* All three use toroidal formers (versus straight-cylinder solenoids). Closed magnetic circuits.
>
> *Similarity two — wound wire.* All three are wire wound on a former. Same basic engineering.
>
> *Similarity three — measurable EM properties.* All three have inductance, resistance, capacitance that can be measured with standard equipment.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> Three structural differences.
>
> *Difference one — uniformity.* RF inductor and transformer windings are *uniform* — every turn is geometrically equivalent. Rodin Coil is *non-uniform* — turn positions follow the doubling cycle structure.
>
> *Difference two — symmetry.* Uniform windings have *cylindrical symmetry* (rotation about the toroidal axis). Rodin Coils have *cyclic symmetry* (rotation by 15° increments) but not full cylindrical symmetry.
>
> *Difference three — claimed behaviour.* Mainstream inductor designs *predict their EM behaviour* from standard EM theory; behaviour matches predictions to engineering tolerance. Rodin Coils have *some claims* of unconventional behaviour; mainstream EM predictions partly cover them but some claims remain contested.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> *What an RF engineer would notice immediately about a Rodin Coil.*
>
> *Notice 1 — non-uniform spacing.* The wire turns aren't equally spaced around the major axis. *They follow the doubling pattern's cyclic order, not uniform increments.*
>
> *Notice 2 — apparent strand crossings.* Multi-strand Rodin Coils have wire crossings that don't appear in transformers. *These are intentional structural features, not winding mistakes.*
>
> *Notice 3 — coplanar entry/exit.* Rodin Coils typically have terminals close together (both at column 0 region). RF inductors and transformers often have terminals on opposite sides.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> *What an RF engineer would test first.*
>
> *Test 1 — basic inductance.* Measure with an LCR meter. Should match prediction from L = N²μA/ℓ formula or its toroidal variant.
>
> *Test 2 — Q factor.* Measure at various frequencies. Compare to typical toroidal inductor Q factors.
>
> *Test 3 — magnetic field shape.* Map with a probe. Compare to standard toroidal field shape.
>
> *Test 4 — efficiency / energy claims.* Test specific claims about energy density, field strength, etc. Most rigorous step.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> Useful framing. *The Rodin Coil is a non-uniform toroidal helix coil. Most of its bulk EM behaviour is predicted by standard theory — same as RF inductors. Specific claims about novel behaviour require careful measurement and aren't yet conclusively settled.*
>
> Practical takeaway. *When discussing the Rodin Coil with engineers, don't claim unconventional behaviour up front.* Lead with the *non-uniform toroidal helix* description; engineers can then ask about the structural details. *Strong claims are testable, not assertable.*"
