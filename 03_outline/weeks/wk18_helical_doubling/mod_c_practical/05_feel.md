## 4. Body (1:00–4:30) — *contemplative*

> *(slow)* "Fifteen weeks from now, your hand will hold a torus former with wire in your fingers. You'll wind the same helix you've traced this week — but with copper instead of pencil.
>
> *[long pause — 12 seconds]*
>
> Sit with the future moment.
>
> *[long pause]*
>
> Anticipation is a real practice. *Imagining what's coming, with specificity*, is part of preparation. The build that's fifteen weeks away is *already partly happening* in your imagination.
>
> Some students dismiss anticipation as daydreaming. *Specific anticipation*, with concrete details — the wire's gauge, the former's weight, the bench's position — is something different. It's *prefiguration*.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> Three contemplations on prefiguration.
>
> *Contemplation one — prefiguration shapes preparation.* When you imagine winding the helix in detail, you notice gaps in your preparation. *Do I have the right wire? Have I marked the former? Do I know the cell sequence?* Specific imagined moments reveal what's not yet ready.
>
> *Contemplation two — prefiguration recruits the future self.* The you of fifteen weeks from now will be the one winding. By imagining their work specifically, you're *negotiating with that future self* about what they'll need. *Today's preparations are gifts to that future self.*
>
> *Contemplation three — prefiguration is mathematical-feeling.* The math of the helix is *abstract* — winding numbers, cell coordinates, parametrisations. The build is *embodied* — fingers, copper, time. Prefiguring the build *connects the two*. The math becomes physical in advance.
>
> *[long pause — 12 seconds]*
>
> What does prefiguring the wind feel like?
>
> Some students notice *eagerness*. The build is coming; the preparation feels purposeful. Each lesson is one step closer.
>
> Some students notice *daunted anticipation*. The build is *long*. Five hours of focused winding is a real commitment. The thought of it can feel heavy.
>
> Some students notice *steady patience*. The build is far enough that there's no urgency. The preparation will continue for weeks; the build will arrive when it does.
>
> All three are honest.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> A practical observation. *Most builders find the wait between learning the helix and actually winding it is the hardest stretch.* Q2's remaining lessons (Wks 19-26) and Q3's first weeks (Wks 27-32) all build on the helix without yet asking you to wind. *The patience period is structural*; it's not a delay — it's the time the framework gives you to absorb the math before doing the engineering.
>
> Some students try to wind early. The course doesn't forbid this — wind a single-strand single-traversal coil now if you have materials. But *the patience period reduces winding errors* by giving the math time to settle.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> One more contemplation. *In fifteen weeks, this lesson will feel like a memory.* You'll be winding copper, and you'll remember tracing the helix in pencil weeks earlier. The memory will help — *the path will feel familiar* even though you've never wound it before.
>
> Hold the marked, traced torus. Imagine the future moment. Feel the copper that's not yet in your fingers. The lesson is doing prefiguration work even now.
>
> Continue. The wind is coming."
