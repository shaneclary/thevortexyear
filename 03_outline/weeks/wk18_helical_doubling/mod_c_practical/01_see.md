## 4. Body (1:00–4:30)

> "The helix you traced on your paper torus this week is *not a metaphor* for the coil winding. It *is* the coil winding, ahead of time. When Q3's Wk 33 wires copper, the wire follows exactly this path.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> *Path identity.* The path you've traced has these properties:
>
> *Property 1.* 24 cells visited, in the sequence (0, 0), (1, 1), (2, 2), …, (23, 5).
>
> *Property 2.* (1, 4) winding numbers — one major-axis circuit, four minor-axis circuits per traversal.
>
> *Property 3.* Closed at the end of the 24th cell — the wire returns to its starting position.
>
> *Property 4.* Single continuous curve — no breaks, no gaps.
>
> All four properties carry over to the wire path:
>
> The wire passes through 24 angular positions on the torus former.
>
> The wire wraps once around the major axis and four times around the minor axis per traversal.
>
> The wire ends at its starting position (or near it — slight offsets allow for terminations).
>
> The wire is one continuous piece — no joins or splices.
>
> *Path identity is exact*; the trace and the wire are *the same shape* in different materials.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> *What changes from trace to wire.*
>
> *Material.* Pencil mark on paper → copper wire on plastic former.
>
> *Diameter.* Trace line ~0.5 mm wide → wire ~0.5 to 0.7 mm in diameter (AWG 22-24).
>
> *Permanence.* Trace fades; wire stays.
>
> *Electrical properties.* Trace has none; wire has resistance (~0.5 Ω per metre) and inductance (depending on winding).
>
> *[pause]*
>
> *What stays.*
>
> Path geometry. Cell-by-cell positions. Winding numbers. Closure. Continuity.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> *Why this matters.*
>
> Practical: *the trace is your wind plan*. When you wind copper in Wk 33, you'll follow the trace cell-by-cell. The trace tells the wire where to go.
>
> Pedagogical: *seeing the trace is rehearsal for winding*. The hand learns the path with pencil before learning it with wire. Errors during winding are reduced because the path is already familiar.
>
> Mathematical: *the trace and the wire are the same homotopy class*. Topologically, they're indistinguishable. The same wave function, current distribution, and field pattern apply to both — except the trace doesn't carry current.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> *Three observations.*
>
> *Observation one.* Most students at this point feel a small *recognition* — the abstract math of the helix has converged on the practical wire of a coil. The convergence is genuinely happening.
>
> *Observation two.* The helix is *direction-specific*. The wire follows the trace in the same direction as your finger. Reversing the direction gives a *different* coil (the halving helix), with potentially different electromagnetic properties.
>
> *Observation three.* The traced helix is also *handedness-specific*. Right-handed and left-handed traces produce mirror-image coils. The foundation tradition specifies one handedness; using the other produces a coil that doesn't match published measurements.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> Practical takeaway. *Look at the traced helix and remember: this is what you'll wind.* When Q3 arrives, the trace becomes the chart on the bench. The chart is the wire path. The wire path is the coil. *Three identifications, one shape.*"
