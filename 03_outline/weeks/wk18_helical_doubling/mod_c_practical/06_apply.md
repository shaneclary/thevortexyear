## 4. Body (1:00–4:30)

> "Today's apply produces a *complete written wind plan* for the Q3 single-strand build. The plan is what you'll bring to the bench when you actually wind.
>
> Open your build log to a fresh page. Title it *Q3 Wind Plan — Single Strand Coil*.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> *Section 1 — Materials.*
>
> 3D-printed torus former (10 cm major × 2.5 cm minor, marked with helix line and 5 essential marks).
>
> 1 m AWG 24 enamelled magnet wire (single conductor).
>
> 2 small terminal posts (or alternative: insulated through-holes).
>
> Soldering iron, rosin-core solder, flux.
>
> Wire strippers, fine-point pliers.
>
> Multimeter for resistance check.
>
> *Section 2 — Bench setup.*
>
> Clean bench at elbow height.
>
> Good lighting overhead.
>
> Wire spool within arm's reach.
>
> Soldering iron heated, on a heat-resistant pad.
>
> The marked torus former on a small stand or held in a soft clamp.
>
> *Section 3 — Procedure.*
>
> *Step 1.* Cut wire to ~1 m length. Strip 1 cm of enamel from each end.
>
> *Step 2.* Solder one wire-end to terminal post A. Position terminal A at column 0, row 0 (the helix start).
>
> *Step 3.* Begin winding. Place wire at cell (0, 0). Advance to (1, 1). Place wire. Advance to (2, 2). Place wire. Continue diagonally.
>
> *Step 4.* At cell (5, 5), wrap wire around the minor circumference back to row 0. Continue: (6, 0), (7, 1), …, (11, 5).
>
> *Step 5.* Repeat the row-wrap for (12, 0), (13, 1), …, (17, 5).
>
> *Step 6.* Repeat for (18, 0), (19, 1), …, (23, 5).
>
> *Step 7.* From (23, 5), the wire returns to (0, 0). Solder this end to terminal post B (very close to terminal A, since both are at column 0).
>
> *Step 8.* Verify visually that the helix line on the marked former is *fully covered* by wire. Any uncovered segments indicate an error; rewind.
>
> *Step 9.* Cut excess wire. Smooth solder joints.
>
> *Section 4 — Verification.*
>
> Resistance from terminal A to terminal B: should be ~1.5 Ω (for 3 m of AWG 24 wire). If much higher: a short or break exists. If much lower: a short circuit.
>
> Continuity check: probe terminal A while touching terminal B. The multimeter should read continuity.
>
> Visual: 24 wire-cell positions visible around the major circumference, in 6 row-positions around the minor circumference.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> *Section 5 — Common errors.*
>
> *Error 1 — wrong row sequence.* If you skip a row at any wrap, the helix is broken. Resolve by unwinding and re-doing.
>
> *Error 2 — wire crossing itself.* The single-strand helix shouldn't cross itself anywhere. Crossings indicate a wrong wrap.
>
> *Error 3 — over-tight wire.* Tight wire pulls the former out of round. Loosen and rewind.
>
> *Error 4 — bad solder joints.* Cold solder joints cause continuity issues. Reflow or redo.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> What you've produced. *A working wind plan in five sections totalling about 200 words.* When Wk 33 arrives, this plan tells you everything you need at the bench.
>
> Three observations.
>
> *Observation one.* The plan is *boring on paper*. That's correct. Wind plans should be procedural, not exciting.
>
> *Observation two.* The plan is *individual to your hardware*. Different former dimensions or wire gauges would change the materials list and resistance prediction.
>
> *Observation three.* The plan will be *revised at the bench*. Some steps will reveal practical issues you didn't anticipate. Update the plan as you build; it's a living document.
>
> Practical takeaway. *Save the wind plan now.* When Q3 arrives, you'll have a 15-week-old plan ready to use. Other students will be writing theirs at the bench; you won't be."
