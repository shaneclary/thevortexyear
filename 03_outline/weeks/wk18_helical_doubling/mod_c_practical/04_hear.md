> What you just heard tells you several things about hand-experience of winding.
>
> *Hand-thing one.* Winding is *paced*. Powell typically winds 1-2 turns per minute in careful work. Faster than this risks errors; slower wastes time. *Find your own pace and stay consistent.*
>
> *Hand-thing two.* The hand develops its own *sense of progress*. By turn 12 (halfway through a single traversal), Powell knows by feel that he's halfway. *The hand learns the rhythm.*
>
> *Hand-thing three.* Mistakes are *catchable mid-wind*. If the wire suddenly feels wrong — too taut, too slack, off-position — pause and check. *Most errors are correctable* if caught within 1-2 turns.
>
> *Hand-thing four.* The marked former is *essential* for first-time winders. Powell winds without checking the marks for short stretches because his hands know the pattern; new builders need to check every turn.
>

> *Practical takeaways for your future winding.*
>
> *Takeaway 1 — wind in good light.* The marks should be clearly visible. Squinting leads to errors.
>
> *Takeaway 2 — sit comfortably.* You'll be in this position for hours. A bench at elbow height with the former easily reachable.
>
> *Takeaway 3 — keep the wire spool nearby.* Stretching to reach the wire breaks rhythm. *Set up the workspace to support the wind.*
>
> *Takeaway 4 — talk through the first few turns.* Saying *column 0 row 0; column 1 row 1; column 2 row 2* aloud helps embed the pattern. Once embedded, you can wind silently.
>
> *Takeaway 5 — break every 30 minutes.* Hand fatigue accumulates. *Step away briefly to rest the wrists and eyes.*
