## 0. Authoring notes
Helix vs solenoid vs spiral. Three families of curves; comparison clarifies what makes the toroidal helix specific.

## 4. Body (1:00–4:30)

> "*Solenoid helix.* A wire wound around a straight cylindrical axis. Each turn is geometrically identical to every other — same diameter, same pitch. The wire follows a constant-radius helix in 3D.
>
> Common examples: solenoid coils, screw threads, spring coils.
>
> Mathematical: parametrised by (cos t, sin t, ct) for parameter t and pitch constant c.
>
> *Archimedean spiral.* A curve in a flat plane that traces a constant-pitch spiral outward from a centre. Each turn is one unit (or one constant) further from the centre than the previous.
>
> Common examples: snail shells (approximately), some galaxy arms, some clockwork springs.
>
> Mathematical: parametrised by r = a + b·t (for r = distance from centre, t = angle).
>
> *Toroidal helix.* A wire wound around a *toroidal* axis (not a straight one). The wire winds simultaneously around the major axis and the minor axis. The helix has *two periods* — one for each axis.
>
> Common examples: Rodin Coil winding, toroidal inductor windings (single-strand simple version).
>
> Mathematical: parametrised by (R cos φ + r cos(qφ) cos φ, R sin φ + r cos(qφ) sin φ, r sin(qφ)) for major angle φ, with q = minor turns per major turn.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> Three structural similarities.
>
> *Similarity one — all are 'wound' curves.* Each has a wire-like physical analogue. All are 1D curves embedded in 3D.
>
> *Similarity two — all have periodic structure.* Each has a periodicity (turn-frequency, pitch, etc.) that captures how often the curve repeats.
>
> *Similarity three — all closed under rotation.* Rotating the curve about its axis gives the same curve. (Sort of — modulo handedness.)
>
> *[pause]*
>
> Three structural differences.
>
> *Difference one — axis topology.* Solenoid: straight axis (a line). Spiral: point axis (a 2D centre). Toroidal helix: closed-loop axis (a circle).
>
> *Difference two — closure.* Solenoid: open at both ends, doesn't close. Spiral: open at the centre, doesn't close at the outside. Toroidal helix: closed.
>
> *Difference three — complexity.* Solenoid has *one* parameter (pitch). Spiral has *one* parameter (rate). Toroidal helix has *two* parameters (major and minor pitch, or equivalently the (p, q) winding numbers). The toroidal helix is the *richest* of the three.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> *What each is best for.*
>
> *Solenoid.* Best for generating *uniform magnetic fields along an axis*. The straight axis means the field inside is parallel to the axis. Used in electromagnets, MRI coils, particle accelerators.
>
> *Spiral.* Best for *2D layouts* where you want to *fit a long path into a finite area*. Used in clock springs, decorative motifs, some antenna designs.
>
> *Toroidal helix.* Best for *closed magnetic circuits* and for *combining two cyclic structures*. Used in toroidal inductors, transformers, and Rodin Coils.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> *What's specific about the doubling helix.*
>
> The doubling helix is a toroidal helix with *(p, q) = (1, 4)* and *closure after 24 surface-cells*. Among toroidal helices, this specific (p, q) ratio gives:
>
> *Spec 1 — coprime winding numbers.* gcd(1, 4) = 1. The helix is a *simple* (non-self-intersecting) closed curve on the torus.
>
> *Spec 2 — short closure.* The helix closes after just 24 cells, which is the LCM of the two axis periods (mathematically: 24 = LCM(24, 6 × 4)). For other (p, q) ratios, closure could take much longer.
>
> *Spec 3 — base-10 alignment.* The 6 minor turns and 24 major positions correspond to the doubling-period 6 and Pisano period 24 in mod-9 arithmetic. *The helix's parameters are forced by the underlying mod-9 structure.*
>
> *[pause]*
>
> Useful framing. *The doubling helix is one specific toroidal helix — chosen by mod-9 doubling, not by aesthetic preference.* Among the family of all toroidal helices, this one has particular VBM-significance.
>
> Practical takeaway. *When discussing the helix with engineers, position it as a toroidal helix with (p, q) = (1, 4).* This places it in a recognisable family. The VBM-specific significance is that the (p, q) parameters come from arithmetic, not engineering choice."
