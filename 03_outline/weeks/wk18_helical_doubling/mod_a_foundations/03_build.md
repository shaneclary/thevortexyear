## 4. Body (1:00–4:30)

> "Today's build makes the doubling helix *visible* on the marked paper torus from Wk 17. The helix is then permanent (within the torus's lifespan) and serves as the central visual reference for Q2's remaining weeks.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> *Materials.*
>
> Your marked paper torus from Wk 17 (5 essential marks already applied, cells labelled).
>
> A coloured pen or thin marker (red, blue, or green works well).
>
> Optional: coloured thread or yarn (more durable than pen marks for long-term use).
>
> *[pause]*
>
> *Step 1 — locate the start.* Find cell (0, 0) on your torus — the cell with the column-0 triangle and the value '1'.
>
> *Step 2 — begin the trace.* Place your pen at the centre of cell (0, 0). Draw a small dot.
>
> *Step 3 — trace the diagonal.* Move the pen to the centre of cell (1, 1) — one column right, one row down. Draw a line segment connecting (0, 0) to (1, 1).
>
> *Step 4 — continue.* Trace through (2, 2), (3, 3), (4, 4), (5, 5). The first six cells of the helix are now visible — a short diagonal staircase across the torus surface.
>
> *Step 5 — cross the row wrap.* The next cell after (5, 5) is (6, 0) — column 6, row 0 (because row 5 + 1 = 6, which wraps to row 0). On the physical torus, this means *the line crosses from the row-5 side to the row-0 side around the minor circumference*.
>
> Draw the line continuing from (5, 5) around the minor circumference back to (6, 0). The line wraps around the small circle of the tube.
>
> *Step 6 — continue through (6, 0) → (7, 1) → … → (11, 5).* Same diagonal pattern.
>
> *Step 7 — cross another row wrap.* From (11, 5), wrap minor again to (12, 0).
>
> *Step 8 — continue through (12, 0) → … → (17, 5).* Diagonal.
>
> *Step 9 — cross another wrap.* (17, 5) → (18, 0).
>
> *Step 10 — continue (18, 0) → (19, 1) → … → (23, 5).* Final diagonal segment.
>
> *Step 11 — close the loop.* From (23, 5), the next cell would be (24, 6) which wraps to (0, 0). Draw the line from (23, 5) back to (0, 0). *The helix is now closed.*
>
> *[pause]*
>
> What you've traced. *The complete doubling helix on the torus surface.* One full traversal: 24 cells, 1 major-axis turn, 4 minor-axis turns, closed helical loop.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> *Step 12 — inspect.* Look at your torus from multiple angles.
>
> View 1 — looking down the major axis (top view). You see the helix wrapping around the major circumference once.
>
> View 2 — looking along the minor axis (side view). You see the helix wrapping the minor circumference four times.
>
> View 3 — quarter-rotated. You see the helix as a compound 3D path with both windings visible.
>
> *Step 13 — photograph.* Photograph the helix from multiple angles. Tape into your build log.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> Three observations.
>
> *Observation one.* The helix is *one continuous closed loop*. Despite traversing two different axes, it's *one curve* without breaks. This is what makes the Rodin Coil's wire *one continuous piece*.
>
> *Observation two.* The helix has *no preferred starting point*. Cell (0, 0) is just where we chose to begin. Any cell could be the 'start'; the helix would close at the same point regardless.
>
> *Observation three.* The trace is *a rehearsal for Q3's winding*. When you wind copper in Wk 33, you'll follow the same path with wire instead of pen. *The trace is the dress rehearsal.*"
