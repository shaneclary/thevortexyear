## 4. Body (1:00–4:30)

> "The doubling cycle has had several visual forms. Today it acquires its definitive 3D form: *a helix on the torus surface*.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> *Form 1 — Q1 figure-eight on the circle.* The original Symbol of Enlightenment with the doubling circuit traced. 1 → 2 → 4 → 8 → 7 → 5 → 1 drawn as a closed figure-eight on a flat circle. *Six positions, one closed loop, on a flat 2D circle.*
>
> *Form 2 — Q2 strip diagonal.* The same arithmetic on the unrolled 6×24 strip. The cycle traces a *diagonal staircase* — one column right, one row down per step. After 24 steps, returns to the start. *Six row-positions traversed four times across 24 columns.*
>
> *Form 3 — Q2 torus helix.* The same diagonal, now traced on the torus surface (after the strip wrapped). The diagonal becomes a *helical path* — winding around the torus's major axis (the long way) while simultaneously winding around the minor axis (the short way). *One closed helical loop on the torus surface.*
>
> *[pause]*
>
> *What changes between forms.* The arithmetic stays. The visualisation surface changes.
>
> *Flat circle.* 1D loop in 2D space. Figure-eight visual.
>
> *Flat strip.* 2D rectangle in 2D space. Diagonal-staircase visual.
>
> *Torus.* 2D surface in 3D space. Helix visual.
>
> Each form preserves the cycle. Each form makes different features apparent.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> *What the helix shows.*
>
> *Show 1 — closure.* The helix closes after one full cycle. Starting at (0, 0), traversing 24 cells, returning to (0, 0). The cycle is a *closed curve on the torus*.
>
> *Show 2 — winding numbers.* The helix winds *p times around the major axis* and *q times around the minor axis* during one closure. For the basic doubling helix: *p = 1 (one major-axis circuit), q = 4 (four minor-axis circuits).* The (p, q) winding numbers characterise the helix.
>
> *Show 3 — handedness.* The helix has a *direction* — clockwise or counterclockwise from a chosen vantage. Reversing the direction gives the *halving* helix. Doubling and halving are *opposite-handed helices on the same torus*.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> Three observations.
>
> *Observation one.* The helix is *the wind path of a Rodin Coil*. When Q3's first copper is wound (Wk 33), the wire follows this helix. *The 3D form of the doubling cycle is the engineering specification of the coil.*
>
> *Observation two.* The helix is *the same on every torus with the same dimensions*. The helix isn't unique to *your* torus; it's a structural feature of any (6, 24)-dimensioned torus with the doubling structure.
>
> *Observation three.* The helix is *visually unfamiliar at first*. Most students take a few hours of contact with the torus and the helix before the 3D shape clicks. Be patient.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> Practical takeaway. *The helix you'll see in Q2 is the same helix you'll wind in Q3.* What you trace with a finger this week, you'll trace with copper later.
>
> Hold this picture: the figure-eight from Q1, the strip diagonal from Q2's first half, the torus helix from this week. *Three faces of one cycle.*"
