## 4. Body (1:00–4:30)

> "DNA's structure — discovered by Watson, Crick, and Franklin in 1953 — is a *double helix*. Two strands wound around each other in a coiled pattern. Today we compare DNA's helical geometry to the doubling helix on the torus.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> *DNA's helical structure.*
>
> Two strands of nucleotides wound in opposite handedness — one right-handed, one left-handed (or, more precisely, both right-handed but anti-parallel). The strands are connected by hydrogen bonds at each base pair.
>
> Pitch (rise per turn): about 3.4 nm.
>
> Diameter: about 2 nm.
>
> Number of base pairs per turn: about 10.
>
> Mathematical: parametrised as two helices on a *straight cylindrical axis* (not a torus). Each helix is a 3D curve r(t) = (cos t, sin t, ct).
>
> *[pause]*
>
> *Doubling helix structure.*
>
> One helix on a *toroidal axis*. Wound with (p, q) = (1, 4). Closed after 24 steps.
>
> Pitch: 24 cells per major turn; 6 cells per minor turn.
>
> Diameter: minor radius / major radius set by torus geometry; for canonical Rodin Coil, 2.5 cm / 10 cm.
>
> Mathematical: parametrised as a single helix on a torus surface (not a straight cylinder).
>
> *[pause]*
>
> Three structural similarities.
>
> *Similarity one — both are helices.* Both are closed curves with helical winding. Both can be parametrised as continuous 3D curves.
>
> *Similarity two — both have winding numbers.* DNA's strands have specific pitches. The doubling helix has (p, q) = (1, 4). Each helix is characterised by its winding parameters.
>
> *Similarity three — both close (or are intended to).* DNA forms closed loops in some contexts (circular DNA in bacteria, mitochondrial DNA). The doubling helix is always closed. *Closure is meaningful for both.*
>
> *[pause]*
>
> Three structural differences.
>
> *Difference one — axis topology.* DNA winds on a *straight cylindrical* axis. The doubling helix winds on a *toroidal* axis. The topology is different — DNA is genus 0 (sphere-like) at its endpoints, doubling helix is genus 1 (torus).
>
> *Difference two — strand count.* DNA is a *double* helix (two strands). The doubling helix is a *single* helix. Multi-strand Rodin Coils (six strands) get closer to DNA's strand count, but with toroidal axis.
>
> *Difference three — biological vs mathematical origin.* DNA's helical structure was *empirically discovered* in 1953. The doubling helix is *mathematically derived* from VBM's mod-9 arithmetic. Different epistemic paths to similar geometry.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> *Why the comparison is interesting.*
>
> *Reason 1 — pattern recurrence.* Helical structures appear repeatedly in nature (DNA, vine tendrils, galaxy arms, etc.) and in engineered designs (springs, screws, coils). The doubling helix joins this family.
>
> *Reason 2 — toroidal helices specifically.* Some biological structures (e.g., some viral capsids) have toroidal helical features. Mathematical analysis of toroidal helices has biological applications.
>
> *Reason 3 — speculative connections.* Marko has occasionally suggested deeper connections between VBM helices and biological helices. *These are speculative*; the math doesn't establish them. Hold them as suggestive, not proven.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> Useful framing. *The doubling helix is one specific helix in a large family of helical structures across mathematics, engineering, and biology.* It's not unique; it's distinguished by its toroidal axis and (p, q) = (1, 4) parameters.
>
> Practical takeaway. *Compare the doubling helix to other helices when you want to place it in a larger context.* DNA is a recognisable comparison point for general audiences. Toroidal inductors are a recognisable comparison point for engineers. The doubling helix is distinct from both but related to both.
>
> A note on overclaiming. Don't say *the doubling helix is DNA*. They share *helical structure*; they differ in many other ways. *Pattern recognition is useful; over-identification is not.* Hold the comparison loosely."
