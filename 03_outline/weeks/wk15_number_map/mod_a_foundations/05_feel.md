## 0. Authoring notes
The grid as a window onto pattern. Contemplative on what it feels like to look at structure rather than at examples.

## 1. Outcome
Student has spent quiet time with the grid as an object that lets pattern be seen rather than computed.

## 2. Hook (0:00–0:30)
*[Slow zoom from a single cell out to the full coloured grid, then back in.]*

> *(slow)* "The grid is a window. Sit with what it shows."

## 3. Setup (0:30–1:00)
> "Lens 5 of 9. Six minutes, slower."

## 4. Body (1:00–4:30) — *contemplative*

> *(slow)* "Until now you have computed individual values. *2² mod 9 = 4.* *7 + 8 mod 9 = 6.* One operation at a time. One value at a time.
>
> The grid does something different. It *displays the pattern of values* simultaneously. You don't compute; you *look*.
>
> *[long pause — 12 seconds]*
>
> Sit with the difference.
>
> *[long pause]*
>
> Computing is *sequential*. The mind moves step by step. Each step takes attention. The pattern, if there is one, has to be *inferred* from the sequence of computed values.
>
> Looking is *parallel*. The eye takes in the whole field at once. Patterns appear *immediately*. The structure is *present* rather than constructed.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> A practical example. *Without the grid*, asking 'what is the digital root of 2¹³ × 10⁵?' requires computing: 2¹³ = 8192; 8192 × 100000 = 819,200,000; digital root of 819,200,000 = ... and so on. A minute of arithmetic.
>
> *With the grid*, asking the same question takes seconds. 2¹³: row 13 mod 6 = row 1 = value 2. Column 5: any column has multiplier 1, so no change. Answer: 2. Three seconds.
>
> The grid *makes computation a lookup*.
>
> *[long pause]*
>
> But the deeper feature is what the grid does *contemplatively*. It lets you *see structure* rather than *compute examples*.
>
> An example is a single instance. Structure is the pattern across instances. *Examples are the dataset; structure is the map.* Mathematics is the practice of moving from data to map.
>
> The grid is one such map. The Symbol of Enlightenment was another. The proof tree at Q1 synthesis was a third. *Each map gives the eye something the case-by-case computation cannot.*
>
> *[pause]*
>
> What does it *feel* like to look at structure?
>
> Some students feel *peace*. The randomness of individual values resolves into the regularity of the pattern. The mind that was computing relaxes; it can now just see.
>
> Some students feel *suspicion*. The structure looks too neat. *Did Marko cherry-pick? Is the apparent regularity contrived?* The suspicion is healthy and answerable: hand-fill the grid yourself, and verify the regularity emerges from straightforward arithmetic.
>
> Some students feel *gratitude*. They had been doing modular arithmetic on faith for weeks; the grid shows them why the arithmetic produces the patterns it does.
>
> All three are honest. Looking at structure is genuinely a different kind of mental activity than computing examples.
>
> *[long pause — 12 seconds]*
>
> A meditation. *Pick one cell. Look at it. Then expand attention to the row that contains it. Notice all 24 cells in that row hold the same value. Then expand to the column. Notice all 6 cells in the column contain the doubling cycle in order. Then take in the whole grid. Notice the alternating-family stripes.*
>
> *Three scales of attention. The cell. The row-and-column. The whole grid. Each reveals different structure.*
>
> *[pause]*
>
> One observation. The grid will *change* as Q2 develops. Wk 16 wraps it; Wk 17 traces a helix on the wrap; Wk 18 introduces antipodal pairs. The grid you fill today is the *static* form. The dynamic forms come later.
>
> But the *contemplative move* — looking at structure rather than computing examples — is the move you'll keep making throughout Q2 and beyond.
>
> Sit with the grid. Don't analyse it further. Just look."
