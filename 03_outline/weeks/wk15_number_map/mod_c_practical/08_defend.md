## 4. Body (1:00–4:30)

> "The objection — coming from practical-minded builders. *The doubling cycle has period 6. So the wind pattern repeats every 6 cells. Why not use a 6-column chart instead of a 24-column one? The 24 just adds visual clutter.*
>
> The objection is reasonable. The answer requires unpacking what *traversal* means in a Rodin Coil context.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> *The key fact.* A canonical Rodin Coil has *four traversals*. Each traversal covers all 24 column-positions. After 24 columns × 4 traversals, the wind closes — the wire returns to its starting position.
>
> If you used only 6 columns, you'd have *6 column-positions × ? traversals* = ? winds. To match the canonical 96-turn-per-strand count, you'd need 16 traversals. The compression seems possible.
>
> *But.* Each *physical* turn around the torus corresponds to *one column*, not to one row-cycle. So 6 columns gives you 6 turns per traversal. To get 96 turns per strand, you need 16 traversals at 6 columns each. *That's 16 different starting angular positions per strand* — much more complex to keep track of than 4 traversals at 24 columns.
>
> The *full 24-column chart* is simpler to *use* than a 6-column chart, even though it has more cells.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> *Three reasons builders need the full 24.*
>
> *Reason one — angular position is what matters physically.* The torus has 24 angular positions (15° apart). To wind the coil, you need to know which angular position to be at for each turn. The 24-column chart maps this directly. The 6-column chart would *factor out* angular position, requiring builders to track it separately.
>
> *Reason two — the traversal count is determined by the wind structure.* The canonical Rodin Coil has 4 traversals because of how the doubling cycle interacts with the angular positions. The number 4 = 24 / 6 *emerges from the math*, not from a design choice. A 6-column chart would obscure why 4 traversals.
>
> *Reason three — multi-strand coils require column tracking.* Each strand starts at a different column position offset from the others. With 6 columns, the offsets are constrained to 6 values; with 24, they're constrained to 24 values. The 24-column chart accommodates the standard six-strand offset (0°, 60°, 120°, 180°, 240°, 300° — corresponding to columns 0, 4, 8, 12, 16, 20). The 6-column chart cannot.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> So the calibrated answer to *why builders need the full 24 columns*:
>
> *The 24 columns correspond to physical angular positions on the torus, which builders need to track during winding. While the doubling cycle has period 6, the angular position has period 24. Compressing to 6 columns would require builders to mentally separate the doubling cycle from the angular position, increasing cognitive load. The 24-column chart presents both simultaneously and matches the canonical four-traversal six-strand wind pattern. The full grid is operationally simpler even though it has more cells.*
>
> Around eighty words.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> Practical takeaway. *Use the full 24-column grid when winding.* Compressed versions are useful for *teaching the doubling cycle in isolation* (Q1's purpose) but not for actual building (Q3's purpose). The chart and the build are matched in dimensionality.
>
> One more practical note. The Rodin Foundation's distributed coil-building documentation uses the 24-column grid as standard. If you're collaborating with other builders, having the same chart-format prevents misunderstandings about position and traversal numbering. *Standardisation matters across collaborators*, even if a non-standard simpler chart would suffice for solo work."
