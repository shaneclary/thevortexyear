## 4. Body (1:00–4:30)

> "Today's build is logistical. The grid leaves the build log and becomes a *fixture* on your workspace wall, where it can serve as a daily reference.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> *Materials.*
>
> A printer, or a copy shop. Or hand-redrawn copy if no printer is available.
>
> The course's PDF of the 6×24 grid (or your hand-drawn version, photographed and printed at A3 if your printer can).
>
> Pins, tape, or magnets — whatever attaches paper to your wall.
>
> A workspace wall — your office, your bench area, your home study space. Anywhere visible during build work.
>
> Optional: clear plastic sheet or laminate to protect the grid over time.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> *Step 1 — print the grid.* The course PDF is sized for A3 / tabloid printing. If you only have A4 / letter printing, print at that size; you can always print again larger when you reach Q3.
>
> If you don't have a printer: take your hand-drawn version to a copy shop. Or hand-draw a wall-sized version directly on butcher paper or large drawing paper.
>
> *Step 2 — choose the location.* The grid should be:
>
> Visible from where you sit when working.
>
> Not behind a screen or above your eye level (you want to glance at it, not crane).
>
> Near (but not on) your build log if possible.
>
> If you have an office at work: discuss with whomever shares the space before pinning. The grid can look unusual to people who don't know what it is.
>
> *Step 3 — pin or tape.* Attach the grid securely. It will be looked at often; flapping or peeling will distract.
>
> *Step 4 — annotate around the grid.* Use sticky notes or a small whiteboard area beside the grid for *running notes*:
>
> The doubling cycle reminder: *1, 2, 4, 8, 7, 5, 1*.
>
> The polar pairs: *(1, 8), (2, 7), (4, 5)*.
>
> The numbers from Lens 2: *144, 576, 96*.
>
> Other reminders as they come up.
>
> *Step 5 — photograph for your build log.* Take a photo of the wall display. Tape or insert the photo into your build log on a dedicated *Workspace fixtures* page.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> What you've done. *The grid has become a piece of your environment.* You no longer need to open the build log to glance at it. Daily exposure is automatic.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> Three observations.
>
> *Observation one — Cumulative environment.* The integrated Symbol from Wk 13 is already on your wall. The grid joins it. Q2 will add a paper torus next week, and Q3 will add an actual coil. *Each artefact accumulates*; the workspace becomes a memory aid you don't have to consult deliberately.
>
> *Observation two — Visible commitment.* The grid is visible to anyone who enters your workspace. This is *cheap social signalling* — your engagement with the framework is now public, at least in your immediate environment. Some students find this strengthens their commitment; others find it adds light pressure to keep working.
>
> *Observation three — The print is workable, not precious.* The grid will get marked, scrubbed, scribbled on. That's fine. If you want a *clean* grid, keep one in the build log; the wall version is for *use*.
>
> Practical observation. The grid will *change function* across quarters. In Q2 it's a study aid. In Q3 it becomes the bench-side wiring chart, and you may move it from the wall to the bench. The grid follows the work."
