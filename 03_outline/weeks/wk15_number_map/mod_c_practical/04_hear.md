## 4. Body (1:00–4:30)

> "*Frame.*
>
> The clip you're about to hear is Randy Powell standing next to a wall-pinned 6×24 grid in his workshop, narrating how the grid functions when actually winding a Rodin Coil. Powell's clips on the grid tend to be *operational* — he describes what to look at, in what order, when the wire is in his hand.
>
> The clip is approximately fifty seconds. **`[ORG-CLIP]`**.
>
> What to listen for. Three things.
>
> *One — the start position.* Powell will name where on the grid winding begins. Typically it's column 0, row 0 — the *upper left cell*. The convention is consistent across his teaching.
>
> *Two — the read direction.* As winding proceeds, Powell's eye and finger move *down and to the right* on the grid. Down because the next strand position in the doubling cycle is one row below; right because the next column is one column right. Listen for whether he describes this *staircase* read pattern.
>
> *Three — the cycle close.* Every six rows, the wire returns to its starting strand position. Every twenty-four columns, the wire returns to its starting angular position. Powell will likely note when the cycles close on the grid as he wraps around.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> *[Clip plays — approximately 50 seconds. **`[ORG-CLIP]`** — to be selected from foundation archive.]*
>
> *[pause]*
>
> *Contextualise.*
>
> What you just heard tells you several things about the grid in operation.
>
> *Operational-thing one.* The grid is *read sequentially*, not surveyed all at once. Winding is a step-by-step process; the grid supports it cell-by-cell. *You do not memorise the whole grid*; you read the next cell when you're ready for the next wind.
>
> *Operational-thing two.* The reading direction follows the *doubling staircase*. Diagonal motion through the grid corresponds to the natural progression of the wind. The grid is, in this sense, a *map of the wind path*.
>
> *Operational-thing three.* Mistakes are catchable on the grid. If your wire is at column 5 and the next position should be column 6 row (current+1), but the wire actually went to column 6 row (current+2), the grid catches the error. *Without the grid, the same error is invisible until the entire coil is built.*
>
> *Operational-thing four.* Powell often refers to the grid as a 'safety net'. Even experienced builders use it. The grid makes mid-wind verification fast.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> Practical takeaway. *Read the grid the way Powell does.* Start at column 0, row 0. Move diagonally — down and right. Glance at the grid before each wind. Verify before you commit.
>
> One more practical note. Powell occasionally distinguishes *grid-as-design* from *grid-as-bench-tool*. The design version is clean, pristine, mathematically correct. The bench version gets marked, scribbled, taped over. Both are useful; they're different artefacts of the same underlying object.
>
> When you reach Q3, you'll have a clean version (in the build log) and a bench version (on the wall, increasingly marked). Both serve different purposes."
