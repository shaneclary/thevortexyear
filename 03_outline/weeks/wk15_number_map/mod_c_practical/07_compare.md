## 4. Body (1:00–4:30)

> "*PCB schematic.* A standard electronics-engineering document. Components (resistors, capacitors, ICs) drawn as symbols. Lines connect their pins. The schematic represents the *electrical relationships* between components, not their physical arrangement.
>
> *Rodin Number Map (used as wiring chart).* A 6×24 grid showing the *winding path* of a Rodin Coil. Each cell is a position on the torus surface; lines (or sequence numbers) trace the wind through positions.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> Three structural similarities.
>
> *Similarity one — both are working documents.* Both live on workbenches and are referenced during builds. Both can be marked up, photocopied, taped to walls.
>
> *Similarity two — both encode physical arrangement implicitly.* Neither shows the actual physical layout directly. The PCB schematic doesn't show where on the board components physically sit; the Number Map doesn't show the curvature of the torus. Both abstract the *connectivity* without committing to *geometry*.
>
> *Similarity three — both have standard conventions.* Electronics has IEEE/IEC schematic conventions; the Rodin Foundation has its own (less formal but consistent) grid annotation conventions.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> Three structural differences.
>
> *Difference one — what's represented.* PCB schematic represents *components* and *connections*. Number Map represents *positions* and *path-through-positions*. Different primitives.
>
> *Difference two — directionality.* PCB schematics are usually direction-agnostic — current flows determined by analysis, not the schematic. Number Map is direction-specific — the wind has a definite start and end, traversed in order.
>
> *Difference three — scale information.* PCB schematics are typically dimensionless. Number Maps are sometimes *annotated with angular positions* (15° steps for 24 columns), making them dimensional in a constrained way.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> What PCB schematics are *better at.* Multi-component circuits with complex topology. Standardised parts catalogues. Universal across electronics design.
>
> What Number Maps are *better at.* Coils with specific wind patterns where the sequence and topology are critical. Toroidal layouts. The single specific case of Rodin Coil engineering.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> *A second comparison — the wiring harness diagram.* Common in automotive and aerospace engineering. Shows wire routing through a complex 3D space, with connectors, splices, and terminations marked.
>
> *Closer match to the Number Map than PCB schematics are.* Both wiring harnesses and Number Maps are *path-specifying* documents — they tell you which wire goes where in what order. The Number Map is, in effect, a *wiring harness diagram for one Rodin Coil*, with the doubling cycle as the route specification.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> Useful framing. *The Number Map is a wiring chart of a particular kind. PCB schematics are about circuits; wiring harness diagrams are about routing; the Number Map is closest to a routing diagram, specialised for a single coil topology with mathematical structure.*
>
> Practical takeaway. *If you have an electronics or wiring background, the Number Map is recognisable.* Don't expect it to be a PCB schematic; expect it to be a routing diagram. The conventions are similar but not identical; the underlying purpose is path specification.
>
> *If you have no engineering background*, the Number Map is your *first wiring document*. Q3 will introduce more. Today's grid is the simpler one — single-strand single-traversal — that complicated forms expand from."
