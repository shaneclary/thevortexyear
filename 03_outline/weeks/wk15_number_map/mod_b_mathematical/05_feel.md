## 4. Body (1:00–4:30) — *contemplative*

> *(slow)* "There are infinitely many integers. The grid has 144 cells. Yet the grid contains *all the digital-root information about every product of a power of 2 and a power of 10*. Compression on this scale is unusual.
>
> *[long pause — 12 seconds]*
>
> Sit with the compression.
>
> *[long pause]*
>
> The grid records a *quotient*. Every integer of form 2ʳ × 10ᶜ — and there are infinitely many such — is sorted into one of 144 cells. The infinite source collapses to a finite image. *The cells are equivalence classes of the integers under digital-root identification.*
>
> Mathematics often does this. *Quotients* — taking infinite or large structures and collapsing them by an equivalence relation — are how we make working-sized objects from unwieldy ones.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> Two contemplations on compression.
>
> *Contemplation one — what's preserved and what's lost.* The grid preserves digital-root information. It loses the original integer's value. From cell (3, 5) you cannot recover that the integer was 800,000 specifically; you can only recover that *some* 2³ × 10⁵ falls there.
>
> The compression is *informationally lossy*. But the *structural information* — what digital-root family the integer belongs to, how it relates to other integers — is preserved.
>
> Some students find this unsettling. *Where did the rest of the information go?* Answer: it was discarded as irrelevant for digital-root analysis. The compression keeps what's needed for the question; throws out what isn't.
>
> *Contemplation two — the cost of compression.* Compression isn't free. The grid's pattern is *only valid for 2ʳ × 10ᶜ products*. Other integers — say, 3ʳ — would need a different grid. *Each operation gets its own grid*; the compression is operation-specific.
>
> The grid is therefore *a tool*, not a universal compression. It serves a particular purpose; using it for other purposes requires construction of further grids.
>
> *[long pause]*
>
> What does compression *feel* like?
>
> Some students feel *power*. They can answer questions about infinite-sized integer products by glancing at a small finite grid. The grid is a kind of magnifying glass turned inside-out — vast input, small output.
>
> Some students feel *suspicion*. *Surely the grid is hiding something.* Suspicion is honest. The grid does hide things — the original integer's value, for instance — and you should know what it hides as well as what it reveals.
>
> Some students feel *appreciation* of the algebraic structure. The grid reveals that 2ʳ × 10ᶜ products have a quotient structure that's *much* simpler than the products themselves. *The quotient is the interesting object.*
>
> All three are honest. Compression is genuinely a different mode of mathematical engagement than direct computation.
>
> *[long pause — 12 seconds]*
>
> A practical observation. *Most useful mathematics is compressed.* Multiplication tables, calendars, periodic-element tables, abstract algebra — each compresses an infinite domain into a finite working object. Working mathematics is the practice of using compression appropriately.
>
> The Number Map is one such compressed working object. Q1's Symbol was another (the cyclic structure of mod 9 collapsed to nine positions on a circle). Q2 will introduce more — the torus, the Decoqubit, eventually the holographic hypersphere. *Each is a compression*.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> One more contemplation. The compression *preserves the structure that matters*. This is the artist's claim about the framework: *what we've kept is what's worth keeping*. Whether you accept that claim depends on whether you find the preserved structure useful. Q1 has prepared you to evaluate. The remaining year will continue the evaluation.
>
> Sit with the grid. The compression is real. The grid is small. The structure it carries is not."
