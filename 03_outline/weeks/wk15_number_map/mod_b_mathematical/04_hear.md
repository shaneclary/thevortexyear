> *Three — the role of base 10 in selecting 9 as modulus.* The Pisano period varies with modulus. Mod 9 happens to give 24, which is a 'nice' number — a multiple of the doubling cycle, an hour-count, a divisor of 360 degrees. Marko occasionally notes these convergences.

> What you just heard tells you several things about the column count.
>
> *Column-thing one.* The 24 is *forced*, not chosen. Marko is consistent on this — the grid couldn't be 12 columns or 36 or any other number; the Fibonacci structure requires exactly 24.
>
> *Column-thing two.* The 24 is *base-10-specific*. In base 7 with mod 7 modulus, the Pisano period is 16, and the analogous grid would be 3 × 16. In base 12 with mod 12 modulus, different again. The grid's specific dimensions match base 10's particular Pisano period.

>
> *Column-thing four.* The 24 is *a Fibonacci number itself* in the sense that 24 = F(3) × F(4) = 2 × 3 × 4 (or, F(4) + F(5) + F(6) = 3 + 5 + 8 = 16, no that's 16; 24 = F(8) − F(0) = 21 − 0 + 3?). The arithmetic relations to Fibonacci numbers are looser than the Pisano-period relation; treat them as motifs rather than load-bearing claims.

The forced-ness is what makes the grid's structure non-arbitrary, and that non-arbitrariness is what defends VBM against the *isn't this just numerology* line of objection."
