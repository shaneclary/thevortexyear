## 0. Authoring notes
The 2D becomes 3D in your hands. Contemplative on the felt experience of dimensional ascent through a hand-folded artefact.

## 1. Outcome
Student has spent quiet time with the experience of holding a 3D object derived from a 2D process.

## 2. Hook (0:00–0:30)
*[The paper torus rotating slowly in hand, light catching its surface.]*

> *(slow)* "Hold the torus you made. Sit with what changed."

## 3. Setup (0:30–1:00)
> "Lens 5 of 9. Six minutes, slower."

## 4. Body (1:00–4:30) — *contemplative*

> *(slow)* "The strip in your hand last week was *flat*. The grid you filled was *flat*. The chart pinned to your wall is *flat*.
>
> The torus you folded yesterday is *not flat*. It's a 3D object, however imperfect.
>
> *[long pause — 12 seconds]*
>
> Sit with the difference.
>
> *[long pause]*
>
> Flat objects can be looked at *from one direction*. You hold them in front of your face, you see one side. To see the other side, you turn them. *Their structure presents itself sequentially*.
>
> Three-dimensional objects can be looked at *from any direction*. You rotate them and the structure presents itself *in the round*. The object has a here-side and a there-side simultaneously; both exist whether you're looking or not.
>
> *Three-dimensional objects have presence in a way two-dimensional objects do not.*
>
> *[pause]*
>
> Rotate your paper torus slowly. Notice:
>
> The doughnut hole *passes through* the torus. There is *literally nothing there* in the middle. A flat artefact has no equivalent of a hole.
>
> The outer surface curves *both ways* simultaneously. Around the major axis (the long way around) it curves one way. Around the minor axis (the short way around) it curves the other way. *The curvature is bidirectional*.
>
> Some cells of the grid are now *on the inside* of the doughnut hole. They face inward. Other cells are on the outside.
>
> *[long pause — 12 seconds]*
>
> What does it *feel* like to hold this?
>
> Some students feel *transformation accomplished*. The math was on paper for fourteen weeks; it's now an object. The transition feels significant — *the abstract became real*.
>
> Some students feel *unease*. The paper torus is *crinkled, imperfect, tape-marked*. The platonic torus they imagined isn't quite this. The gap between the imagined ideal and the physical artefact may feel disappointing.
>
> Some students feel *appreciation*. The torus, however imperfect, is the *first 3D object the course has produced*. It has been earned over fifteen weeks of work.
>
> All three are honest. The first dimensional ascent is real and brings real feelings.
>
> *[long pause]*
>
> Three contemplations on the dimensional ascent.
>
> *Contemplation one — the ascent is small.* Going from 2D to 3D adds *one dimension*. By the standards of higher mathematics, this is a small step. Q3's full Decoqubit will involve more dimensions. *But you've made the first step.*
>
> *Contemplation two — the ascent costs nothing reversible.* You can flatten the torus back into a strip if you cut it carefully. The 3D object hasn't *destroyed* the 2D source; it has *dressed* it. The ascent is reversible.
>
> *Contemplation three — the ascent reveals what was hidden.* On the flat grid, you could not see the doubling cycle as a *helix*. On the torus surface, the cycle is a 3D path that winds through the surface. *The 3D embedding made visible something the 2D grid could only suggest.*
>
> *[pause]*
>
> A practical observation. *Most insight comes from dimensional ascents.* Algebra to geometry. Geometry to topology. Topology to higher categories. Each ascent makes structure visible that was hiding in the previous level.
>
> The Q2 ascent is *one specific case* of this general pattern. The cyclic groups of mod 9 became *visible as a helix on a torus* once the dimensional ascent occurred.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> One more contemplation. Hold the torus and notice that *you made it*. Your hands wrapped the strip into a cylinder. Your hands bent the cylinder into a torus. This wasn't a 3D model rendered by a computer; it was *paper folded by you*.
>
> The relationship between the math and the physical world *passes through your hands*. This will become more pronounced in Q3 when you wind copper. The torus is the practice run.
>
> Sit with the torus. Notice you made it. Notice the math now has a body."
