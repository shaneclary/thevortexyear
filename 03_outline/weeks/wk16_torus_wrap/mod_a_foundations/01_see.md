## 4. Body (1:00–4:30)

> "The 6×24 grid lifts into a torus through two wraps performed in sequence. Today the wrap is *seen*; Mod B will *prove* it; Mod C will *fold* it.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> *Stage 1 — flat strip.* The grid as it lives on paper. 6 rows tall, 24 columns wide. Two pairs of opposite edges:
>
> *Vertical pair* — left edge (column 0) and right edge (column 23+1 = 24, which we identify with column 0).
>
> *Horizontal pair* — top edge (row 0) and bottom edge (row 5+1 = 6, identified with row 0).
>
> Currently, neither pair is joined. The grid is *open*.
>
> *Stage 2 — cylinder.* The first wrap. Roll the strip so its left and right edges meet. Tape them together. The 24 columns now form a closed *circle* of 24 angular positions. The 6 rows still run along the *length* of the cylinder.
>
> The grid is now half-closed. One pair of edges joined; one pair still open.
>
> *Stage 3 — torus.* The second wrap. Bend the cylinder so its top and bottom edges meet. Connect them. The 6 rows now form a closed *circle* of 6 cross-section positions. The 24 columns are still the larger circle around the torus's main axis.
>
> The grid is now *fully closed*. Both pairs of edges joined. The flat 2D substrate has become the *2D surface of a 3D torus*.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> Three things to see in the three stages.
>
> *Thing one — the geometry shifts.* The flat strip is *2D in 2D*. The cylinder is *2D in 3D* — the surface is 2D but it's now embedded in 3D space. The torus is also 2D in 3D, but with a different topology than the cylinder.
>
> *Thing two — what's preserved.* The cells. The 144 cells exist throughout. On the strip they're in a flat grid; on the cylinder they're on a curved surface; on the torus they're on a doubly-curved surface. *The cells maintain their identity*; the surface they live on changes.
>
> *Thing three — what's gained.* Closure. The torus is *closed* — there are no edges. Every cell is in the interior of the surface. The cylinder still has top and bottom edges (the boundaries); the torus has none.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> *Why this matters.* The flat grid had implicit structures (column-constancy, doubling cycles) that needed *interpretation* to relate them to physical reality. The torus *is* a physical reality — a doughnut shape in 3D space. Once the grid is on a torus, the structures are *literally on a surface in space*.
>
> Doubling cycles become *helices on the torus surface*.
>
> Polar pairs become *antipodal points* (opposite sides of the torus).
>
> Family groups become *coloured regions* on the surface.
>
> The 3-6-9 axis becomes *a real spatial axis* in 3D.
>
> Each Q1 abstraction becomes a Q2 geometric structure on the torus.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> Three observations.
>
> *Observation one.* The wrap is *deterministic*. Given the flat grid and the convention of identifying opposite edges, the resulting torus is unique up to scale.
>
> *Observation two.* The wrap is *reversible*. Cut the torus along its two natural circles, and you recover the flat grid. The wrap and the unwrap are inverse operations.
>
> *Observation three.* The wrap is *natural*. The grid's two cyclic structures (period 6 vertically, period 24 horizontally) *want* to close. Forcing the grid to remain flat would obscure the closure that's mathematically already there.
>
> Hold the three frames in mind. Mod B will derive each stage formally; Mod C will fold a paper version with your hands."
