> "Today: paper torus. Six minutes."

## 3. Setup (0:30–1:00)

## 4. Body (1:00–4:30)

> "Today's build is the wrap performed by hand. The flat grid becomes a paper torus that you can hold, rotate, and look at from any angle.
>
> The result is approximate — the geometry is forgiving — but the *experience* of bending a flat 2D surface into a 3D toroidal one is exactly what Q2's geometric turn is about.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> *Materials.*
>
> A second printout of the 6×24 grid (the wall-pinned one stays on the wall).
>
> Scissors.
>
> Tape (clear is best).
>
> A pencil.
>
> About fifteen minutes.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> *Step 1 — orient the printed grid.* Place the printed grid flat with rows running horizontally (top to bottom: rows 0 to 5) and columns running vertically along the wider direction (left to right: columns 0 to 23). The grid should be roughly four times wider than it is tall.
>
> *Step 2 — first wrap (cylinder).* Roll the grid so the left edge meets the right edge. The columns 0 and 23 should be adjacent; column 24 is identified with column 0.
>
> Tape the seam carefully. Use clear tape so the cells stay readable. The grid is now a *paper cylinder*.
>
> Inspect. Looking at the cylinder from one end, you see a cross-section of 6 rows. Looking at the side, you see all 24 columns wrapping around.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> *Step 3 — second wrap (torus).* This is the harder bend. You need to bend the cylinder so its two open ends meet, forming a torus.
>
> *Practical caveat.* Paper does not bend smoothly into a torus shape. Real toroidal forms are made of flexible material like fabric, or they're 3D-printed. For paper, the bend is approximate; the result is more *donut-shaped paper sculpture* than a precise torus.
>
> *Method one — direct bend.* Bend the cylinder in a U shape and try to bring the two open ends together. The paper will resist; some crinkling is unavoidable. Tape the ends together where they meet.
>
> *Method two — segment-and-tape.* Cut the cylinder into 6 short segments (each 4 columns wide) and bend each segment slightly. Tape the segments end-to-end with each next segment angled relative to the previous. The result is a polygonal-prismatic torus, not a smooth one, but the topology is correct.
>
> *Method three — accept and proceed.* The torus shape is approximate; that's fine. The pedagogical point is the *transformation*, not the precision.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> *Step 4 — caption and document.* Title the page in your build log *Paper torus from the 6×24 grid*. Photograph the torus from two angles (top-down and side). Tape both photos into the build log.
>
> *Step 5 — display.* Hang the paper torus where it's visible — by a thread, on a hook, or on a stand. The torus joins the integrated Symbol and the 6×24 grid as workspace fixtures.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> *What you've built.* A 3D approximation of the toroidal surface that the grid implicitly defines. Crude, but real. The paper torus is the *first 3D object in the course*.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> Three observations.
>
> *Observation one.* The paper torus is *frustratingly imperfect*. Smooth tori require materials that paper isn't. The imperfection teaches that mathematical objects can exceed what physical materials easily support — the math is cleaner than the physics.
>
> *Observation two.* Despite imperfection, the *topology* is correct. The torus has no edges; the cells wrap correctly; the doubling cycle (when traced) appears as a 3D helix.
>
> *Observation three.* This is a *single-use* artefact for some students. Once you've built the paper torus, you can move to better materials (fabric, 3D-printed plastic) for further study. *But the act of building it once* is what produces the embodied understanding."
