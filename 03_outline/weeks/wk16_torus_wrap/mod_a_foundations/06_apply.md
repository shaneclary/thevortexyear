## 4. Body (1:00–4:30)

> "Today's apply uses the paper torus you folded as a tool. Q1's polar pairs (1, 8), (2, 7), (4, 5) become *physical antipodes* on the torus surface. Reading them off requires touching the torus.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> *Definition.* Two points on a torus are *antipodal* if they're as far apart as possible on the surface. For the doubly-cyclic 6×24 torus, antipodal points are exactly 12 columns apart (half of 24) AND 3 rows apart (half of 6).
>
> *Cell-pair antipodes.* Cells (r, c) and (r+3 mod 6, c+12 mod 24) are antipodal.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> *Worked examples.*
>
> *Pair 1.* Cell (0, 0) holds value 1. Antipode: cell (3, 12). What does cell (3, 12) hold?
>
> r = 3 → value 2³ mod 9 = 8.
>
> So cells (0, 0) and (3, 12) hold values 1 and 8. *The polar pair (1, 8).*
>
> *Pair 2.* Cell (1, 0) holds value 2. Antipode: cell (4, 12). What does cell (4, 12) hold?
>
> r = 4 → value 2⁴ mod 9 = 7.
>
> Values 2 and 7. *The polar pair (2, 7).*
>
> *Pair 3.* Cell (2, 0) holds value 4. Antipode: cell (5, 12). What does cell (5, 12) hold?
>
> r = 5 → value 2⁵ mod 9 = 5.
>
> Values 4 and 5. *The polar pair (4, 5).*
>
> *[pause]*
>
> *On the paper torus.* Each pair of antipodal cells is *physically opposite each other through the torus*. If you stick a long pin through the centre of cell (0, 0) and continue through the torus's body, the pin emerges at cell (3, 12).
>
> The polar pairs from Wk 6 are now *literal geometric opposites* on the 3D surface.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> *Step-by-step procedure for the build.*
>
> *Step 1.* On your paper torus, locate cell (0, 0). Mark it with a small dot or sticker.
>
> *Step 2.* Counting columns around the major circumference, find column 12 (180° around).
>
> *Step 3.* From column 12, move to row 3 (halfway around the minor circumference).
>
> *Step 4.* Mark cell (3, 12). The two marks are antipodal.
>
> *Step 5.* Repeat for the other two pairs.
>
> *Step 6.* Note in your build log: *the three polar pairs of mod-9 are antipodal point-pairs on the torus surface*.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> Three observations.
>
> *Observation one.* The geometric realisation of polar pairs as antipodes is *not coincidence*. It's a structural consequence of how the wrap was performed. Specifically: the wrap identified opposite edges, and the doubling-cycle's involution (multiplication by −1 mod 9, which equals multiplication by 8) maps each unit to its polar pair. The geometric antipode-correspondence is the geometric realisation of this involution.
>
> *Observation two.* The antipode property generalises to *all* of Q1's structures.
>
> Family groups become *coloured regions* on the torus surface (Wk 17 will work this out).
>
> The 3-6-9 axis becomes *the orthogonal direction* through the torus's centre (Wk 19-20).
>
> Doubling cycles become *helical paths* on the surface (Wk 17-18).
>
> Each Q1 structure has a Q2 geometric realisation.
>
> *Observation three.* The paper torus is *useful as a tool*, not just an artefact. You can read off structure from it that the flat grid hides.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> Practical takeaway. *Use the paper torus actively.* Pin it where you can pick it up; rotate it when thinking about polar pairs or antipodes. It is more than a souvenir; it is a *physical model* that supports geometric reasoning."
