## 4. Body (1:00–4:30)

> "Three closed-or-bounded surfaces appear repeatedly in mathematics and physics: the sphere, the cylinder, and the torus. Each has different topological properties; each suits different purposes.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> *Sphere (S²).* The surface of a ball. Closed (no edges). Curvature *positive everywhere* — the surface curves outward in all directions from any point. Genus 0 — no holes. Euler characteristic 2.
>
> Used for: planetary models, galactic models, atomic-orbital surfaces, soap bubbles.
>
> *Cylinder.* The surface of a tube. Closed if you add caps; otherwise has two circular boundaries. Curvature: *positive in one direction* (around the tube) but *zero in the other* (along the tube's length). Genus depends on whether ends are capped or not.
>
> Used for: pipes, tubes, columns. Most common in engineering.
>
> *Torus (T² or S¹ × S¹).* The surface of a doughnut. Closed (no edges). Curvature varies: *positive on the outer surface* of the doughnut, *negative on the inner* (the hole side), *zero on the top and bottom*. Genus 1 — one hole. Euler characteristic 0.
>
> Used for: anywhere two cyclic structures interact. Tori are common in physics (phase spaces, energy landscapes), in engineering (toroidal inductors, wedding rings), in pure mathematics (elliptic curves, abelian varieties).
>
> *[pause]*
>
> Three structural similarities.
>
> *Similarity one — all are 2D surfaces.* All three are 2-manifolds, embeddable in 3D space.
>
> *Similarity two — all carry coordinates.* You can parametrise points on each surface with two coordinates (latitude/longitude on a sphere; angle/length on a cylinder; major-angle/minor-angle on a torus).
>
> *Similarity three — all support continuous functions.* Heat distributions, electromagnetic fields, etc., can live on any of the three.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> Three structural differences.
>
> *Difference one — Euler characteristics.* Sphere: 2. Cylinder (capped): 2. Cylinder (open): 0. Torus: 0. The Euler characteristic captures the topological *type*. Tori and open cylinders share χ = 0 but differ in *genus* (number of holes): cylinder has 0, torus has 1.
>
> *Difference two — closed vs. open.* The sphere is *closed without holes*. The torus is *closed with one hole*. The cylinder is either *open (uncapped)* or *closed-with-caps* — but topologically the closed-with-caps cylinder is a sphere.
>
> *Difference three — symmetry.* The sphere has *3D rotational symmetry* (any rotation of 3D space takes the sphere to itself). The cylinder has *axial symmetry* (rotations about its axis). The torus has *biaxial cyclic symmetry* (rotations in either of two directions, but with different periods generally).
>
> *[pause]*
>
> *Why the torus and not the sphere or cylinder for VBM?*
>
> The Number Map has *two cyclic axes* (rows of period 6, columns of period 24). The natural surface for *two* cyclic structures is the torus, because the torus = S¹ × S¹ — the Cartesian product of two circles.
>
> A sphere has *one* angular direction (latitude) plus a non-cyclic direction (longitude actually wraps but only over 0-360°). It accommodates *one* cyclic structure cleanly.
>
> A cylinder has *one* cyclic direction (around the tube) plus a *linear* direction (along the length). It accommodates one cyclic plus one linear structure.
>
> The torus is *the right surface* for two-cyclic-structure objects. The Number Map is exactly such an object. The match is forced.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> Useful framing. *Different cyclic-structure counts force different surfaces. One cyclic structure → sphere or cylinder. Two cyclic structures → torus. Three or more → higher-genus surfaces or higher-dimensional manifolds.*
>
> Practical takeaway. *VBM's torus isn't aesthetic; it's forced.* When you encounter the torus throughout the rest of the year, remember it's the unique closed surface that hosts two cyclic structures naturally. The sphere and cylinder *cannot* host the doubling-and-Pisano structure of the Number Map without distortion."
