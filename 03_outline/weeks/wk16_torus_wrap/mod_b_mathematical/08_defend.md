## 4. Body (1:00–4:30)

> "Mod A's defence engaged the metaphysical objection (universe-as-doughnut). Mod B's defence engages a more technical objection: *why this specific surface and not another with similar topology?*
>
> *[pause]*
>
> The objection (technical version). *Many surfaces have non-trivial fundamental groups. The torus is one. Higher-genus surfaces have more loops. Klein bottles, projective planes, and Möbius strips have non-orientable cousins. Why specifically T² rather than these alternatives?*
>
> The answer requires unpacking what *the Number Map's structure forces* about the underlying surface.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> *Theorem (Surface Selection).* Among compact, closed, orientable, genus-≤1 surfaces, only T² supports the algebraic structure of the Number Map.
>
> *Proof sketch.*
>
> *Step 1.* The Number Map has two independent cyclic generators: doubling (period 6) and Fibonacci (period 24, to be added in Wk 16+). Each generator must traverse a non-contractible loop on the surface.
>
> *Step 2.* The number of independent non-contractible loops on a compact orientable surface is *2g*, where g is the genus. For g = 0 (sphere): 0 loops. For g = 1 (torus): 2 loops. For g = 2 (double torus): 4 loops.
>
> *Step 3.* The Number Map needs exactly 2 loops. Therefore g = 1. *The torus is the unique genus-1 compact closed orientable surface*; surfaces of genus 0 or 2+ don't fit.
>
> *Q.E.D. (sketch).*
>
> *[pause]*
>
> *What about non-orientable alternatives?*
>
> *Klein bottle.* Has 2 independent loops but is non-orientable. *Could host the Number Map* in some sense, but introduces orientation reversal — a wind path traced once would return reversed. This *changes the algebraic structure* of the action and is incompatible with VBM's specific generators.
>
> *Real projective plane.* Has 1 non-contractible loop. *Wrong number.*
>
> *Möbius strip.* Has 1 non-contractible loop and is open (has boundary). *Wrong number and wrong topology.*
>
> *None of these fit.*
>
> *[pause]*
>
> *What about higher-genus surfaces?*
>
> *Double torus.* Has 4 non-contractible loops. The Number Map would only use 2 of them, leaving 2 *unused* loops. This *over-fits* — the surface carries extra structure that doesn't correspond to anything in the Number Map. The double torus is mathematically possible but *not minimal*; the torus is preferable for parsimony.
>
> *Higher-genus surfaces.* Same problem, more pronounced. Over-fitting at higher genus.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> *What about R²*? *
>
> ℝ² (the plane) has *no non-contractible loops* (it's simply connected). The Number Map's cycles would have to be drawn separately rather than traced as loops. *Wrong topology for cyclic structure.*
>
> *[pause]*
>
> So the calibrated answer to *why specifically T²*:
>
> *The Number Map has two independent cyclic generators, requiring a surface with at least two non-contractible loops. Among compact closed orientable surfaces, the unique surface with exactly two such loops is the torus T² of genus 1. Higher-genus surfaces over-fit; lower-genus surfaces don't have enough loops; non-orientable alternatives introduce orientation reversal incompatible with VBM's generators. T² is therefore the unique minimal-fitting surface for VBM's structure.*
>
> Around eighty words.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> Practical takeaway. *The torus's selection is a topological theorem.* Stating the theorem is the cleanest defence. Mathematicians familiar with surface classification will recognise the argument immediately; non-mathematicians can follow the loop-counting reasoning.
>
> One more note. *The course doesn't yet engage non-compact alternatives (like ℝ × S¹ or non-Hausdorff weirdness).* These technically exist but aren't relevant for finite-cell objects like the Number Map. The compactness assumption is justified by the *finite cell count* of the Map."
