## 4. Body (1:00–4:30) — *contemplative*

> *(slow)* "Topology is a branch of mathematics that studies *what stays the same when shapes are stretched and bent*. A coffee cup and a doughnut are 'topologically the same' because each can be deformed into the other without tearing or gluing.
>
> *[long pause — 12 seconds]*
>
> Hold your paper torus. Notice that it is *topologically* a torus, even though physically it is a crinkled imperfect paper artefact. The crinkles don't matter to the topology. The number of holes does. The closure of the surface does. The fact that any walk on the surface returns to start does.
>
> *[long pause]*
>
> Sit with what topology *cares about* and what it *ignores*.
>
> *[long pause]*
>
> Topology cares about: holes, boundaries, closures, connectivity.
>
> Topology ignores: angles, distances, areas, exact shapes.
>
> The paper torus could be perfectly smooth or could be crumpled. Both have the same topology. Topology is what *survives* the deformation.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> Three contemplations on topology felt.
>
> *Contemplation one — topology is robust.* You can crinkle the paper torus, fold it slightly, dent it. As long as you don't tear or glue, the topology is preserved. *This is unusual robustness in mathematics.* Most mathematical structures are fragile to small perturbations. Topology is not.
>
> *Contemplation two — topology is global.* You can't determine whether a surface is a torus by looking at any small patch. Local geometry doesn't reveal global topology. *You need to walk the whole surface — or wrap your hands around it — to know what topology you're holding.*
>
> *Contemplation three — topology is body-scaled.* The torus's defining features (the hole, the closure, the two cycles) are felt at hand-scale. You can put a finger through the doughnut hole. You can trace a closed loop with your fingertip. The topology is *available to direct embodied perception*.
>
> Most mathematics is not body-scaled. Linear algebra, differential equations, abstract algebra — these live in the head, not the hands. Topology is unusual in being directly perceivable.
>
> *[long pause — 12 seconds]*
>
> What does it feel like to hold a topology?
>
> Some students feel *concreteness*. The math has a body; you can touch it. The abstraction is no longer floating; it has settled into a paper artefact you can rotate.
>
> Some students feel *modesty*. The paper torus is so simple — just a crinkled bagel of paper. Yet it carries genuine topological structure. The simplicity feels disproportionate to the structure it encodes.
>
> Some students feel *gratitude*. They can now *look at* a torus when they think about it, rather than relying entirely on imagination. The artefact is a reference point.
>
> All three are honest.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> A practical observation. *Topology is one of the few mathematical fields that benefits from physical models.* Sphere models, torus models, knot models — these aren't toys; they're working tools for topologists. Many topology textbooks suggest making physical models. Marko's torus tradition fits this convention.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> One more contemplation. The torus you hold is *the same topological object* as a smooth ideal torus, as a sphere with a handle, as the surface of a doughnut, as the configuration space of two angles. *All of these are the torus*; your paper version is one realisation of the equivalence class.
>
> When you think about Q2's torus from now on, you can hold *this* torus in mind. The physical artefact is now a reference for the abstract object. Embodied topology.
>
> Sit with the torus. Don't compute. Notice it is *one shape*, of which the math is one description and the paper is another."
