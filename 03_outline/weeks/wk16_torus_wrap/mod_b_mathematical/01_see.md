## 4. Body (1:00–4:30)

> "Mod A introduced the wrap visually. Mod B writes it formally. The torus is defined by two *equivalence relations* on the strip's points.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> *Setup.* Consider the points of the strip as pairs (r, c) where r ∈ ℝ ranges over [0, 6] and c ∈ ℝ ranges over [0, 24]. (Continuous, not just integer cells, for a moment — we'll discretise back.)
>
> *Equivalence relation 1 — horizontal closure.* For all r and all c:
>
> *(r, 0) ~ (r, 24)*
>
> Read aloud: 'point (r, 0) is equivalent to point (r, 24)'. The left edge and the right edge of the strip are identified. Points on the left edge become the *same point* as their counterparts on the right edge.
>
> Continuing the equivalence: *(r, c) ~ (r, c + 24)*. The horizontal coordinate is taken mod 24. *Adding 24 to the column index doesn't change the equivalence class*.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> *Equivalence relation 2 — vertical closure.* For all r and all c:
>
> *(0, c) ~ (6, c)*
>
> The top edge and the bottom edge are identified. Continuing: *(r, c) ~ (r + 6, c)*. The row coordinate is taken mod 6.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> *Combined equivalence.* Two points (r₁, c₁) and (r₂, c₂) on the strip are *equivalent* if and only if:
>
> r₁ ≡ r₂ (mod 6) AND c₁ ≡ c₂ (mod 24).
>
> Each equivalence class corresponds to *one point* on the resulting torus. The torus is the set of equivalence classes.
>
> *Formal definition.* Torus T² := ℝ² / (6ℤ × 24ℤ). Read: ℝ² modulo the lattice generated by (6, 0) and (0, 24).
>
> Or, restricted to the integer cells: T² := (ℤ × ℤ) / (6ℤ × 24ℤ) ≅ ℤ/6ℤ × ℤ/24ℤ.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> *Three things this formalisation gives us.*
>
> *Thing one — well-definedness.* The torus is a precise mathematical object. It's a quotient of ℝ² by a discrete subgroup. Every point on the torus corresponds to exactly one equivalence class. There's no ambiguity.
>
> *Thing two — coordinates.* Points on the torus have *coordinates* — pairs (r mod 6, c mod 24). You can compute on the torus by computing on coordinates and reducing. The coordinates form an abelian group under coordinate-wise addition.
>
> *Thing three — connection to (ℤ/6ℤ × ℤ/24ℤ).* The discrete torus (the cells, not the continuous surface) is *isomorphic* to the abelian group ℤ/6ℤ × ℤ/24ℤ. Every Q1 group-theoretic insight about cyclic groups applies to the torus structure.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> *Why this matters for VBM.*
>
> *VBM matter one.* The torus is now a *standard topological object* with a precise definition. Mainstream mathematicians can engage with it directly.
>
> *VBM matter two.* The torus's coordinates *include* the row index (which carries the doubling cycle) and the column index (which carries the scale axis, becoming Fibonacci-mod-9 next week). The torus carries both Q1 structures simultaneously.
>
> *VBM matter three.* The wrap *preserves all the cell content* of the grid. The 144 cells from the strip become the 144 equivalence classes on the torus. Nothing is lost; structure is reorganised.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> Memorise the two relations. *(r, c) ~ (r mod 6, c mod 24)*. Three lines including the formal definition.
>
> Mod A's wrap is now Mod B's quotient. Both are correct; the formal version makes the structure explicit."
