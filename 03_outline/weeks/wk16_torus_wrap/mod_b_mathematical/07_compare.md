## 4. Body (1:00–4:30)

> "Mod A's compare lens compared the surfaces visually. Mod B compares them *formally* — using their topological definitions as products and quotients.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> *Torus.* Formally: T² = S¹ × S¹.
>
> Read: 'the torus is the Cartesian product of the circle with itself'. Each point on the torus is specified by *two angles*, one for each circle factor.
>
> Topology: closed (no boundary). Genus 1 (one hole). Euler characteristic 0. Homology H₁(T²) = ℤ × ℤ.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> *Sphere.* Formally: S² = boundary of the unit ball in ℝ³. Or: S² = compactification of ℝ² by a single point at infinity.
>
> Read: the 2-sphere is the surface of a 3-ball. Each point is specified by *two angles* (latitude and longitude), but the parametrisation is *singular* at the poles (latitude ±90° has undefined longitude).
>
> Topology: closed (no boundary). Genus 0 (no hole). Euler characteristic 2. Homology H₁(S²) = 0.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> *Open cylinder.* Formally: S¹ × ℝ.
>
> Read: 'the cylinder is the product of a circle with the real line'. Each point is specified by *one angle* and *one real number*.
>
> Topology: open (has two boundary circles when restricted to a finite-length cylinder; for the infinite cylinder, no boundary but unbounded). Genus 0. Euler characteristic 0 (when treated as a surface with boundary). Homology H₁ = ℤ.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> *Compact closed surfaces of genus g.* Σ_g. Genus 0 = sphere. Genus 1 = torus. Genus 2 = double-torus (two-holed surface). And so on.
>
> The torus and the sphere are the *only* compact closed surfaces with at most one hole. The cylinder is *not compact* (infinite or boundary-having); the closed disk-with-hole is its compact analogue but topologically different.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> Three formal differences.
>
> *Difference one — homotopy structure.* The torus has *two non-contractible loops* — meridian and longitude — both fundamental in H₁. The sphere has *no non-contractible loops* — every loop on a sphere can be shrunk to a point. The cylinder has *one non-contractible loop* — the meridian.
>
> *Difference two — fundamental group.* π₁(T²) = ℤ × ℤ. π₁(S²) = 0. π₁(S¹ × ℝ) = ℤ.
>
> *Difference three — universal cover.* The torus's universal cover is ℝ². The sphere is its own universal cover (simply connected). The cylinder's universal cover is ℝ × ℝ.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> *Why this matters for VBM.*
>
> The Number Map's structure has *two cycles* (period 6 vertically, period 24 horizontally). To support both cycles topologically, the underlying surface must have at least *two non-contractible loops*. Of S², T², and S¹ × ℝ, only T² has two such loops. *Therefore T² is the unique compact closed surface compatible with the Number Map's structure.*
>
> *[pause]*
>
> Useful framing. *The torus's status as the natural surface for VBM is a topological theorem, not a design preference. The sphere has no non-contractible loops; the cylinder has only one. The torus has exactly two — matching the Number Map's two cyclic axes.*
>
> Practical takeaway. *When asked why the torus*, give the topological argument. The torus is *the unique compact closed orientable surface with two independent non-contractible loops*. Among small surfaces, it is the right one.
>
> *Higher-genus surfaces* — double tori, etc. — would have *more* loops and would over-fit the Number Map's structure. *The torus is minimal-fitting*; it has exactly the loops needed, no extras.
>
> The torus isn't chosen aesthetically. It's the unique solution to a topological constraint."
