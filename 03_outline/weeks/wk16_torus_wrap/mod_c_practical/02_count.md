## 0. Authoring notes
Count physical seams on the paper torus. Each method has a different seam count; the count tells you what to expect when handling the artefact.

## 1. Outcome
Student can count the seams on their paper torus and predict the seam count for alternative methods.

## 2. Hook (0:00–0:30)
*[A torus with seams traced in red — direct-bend method has 2 seams; segment-and-tape has 6.]*

> "Two seams or six. Count."

## 3. Setup (0:30–1:00)
> "Lens 2 of 9. Counting physical seams on the torus."

## 4. Body (1:00–4:30)

> "Today we count physical seams on the paper torus. The seam count varies by method; knowing the count helps with handling, repair, and visual identification.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> *Method 1 — direct bend. Seam count: 2.*
>
> First seam: where left edge (column 0) meets right edge (column 23). This is the cylinder's seam — a straight line running the full length of the cylinder.
>
> Second seam: where the cylinder's two open ends meet to close into the torus. This is a curved seam following the cross-section circumference at the joining point.
>
> Total: 2 seams, one straight and one curved.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> *Method 2 — segment-and-tape. Seam count: 6.*
>
> Each of the 6 segments has *one* seam: where its left and right edges (column 0 and column 23 of that segment's portion of the grid) meet to form a short cylinder.
>
> The segments are taped *end-to-end* — the segment-junctions are not strictly seams in the same sense; they're more like flexible joints. *If we count them as seams,* we'd add 6 segment-junction seams = 12 seams total.
>
> If we count only the cylinder seams (one per segment): 6 seams.
>
> If we count cylinder seams + segment junctions: 12 seams.
>
> The exact number depends on what you classify as a 'seam'. *Six is the conservative count*; twelve is the maximalist count.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> *Method 3 — cardstock reinforced. Seam count: 2 (same as Method 1).*
>
> The reinforcement doesn't change the topology of how the paper joins; it only stiffens the paper. The seams are in the same places as Method 1.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> *Method (pivot) — fabric sewn. Seam count: 2 sewn seams.*
>
> Same topology as Methods 1 and 3, but the seams are *sewn* rather than taped. Sewn seams hold longer and look cleaner. Still two of them.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> *Method (pivot) — 3D printed. Seam count: 0.*
>
> The 3D-printed torus is *one continuous piece of plastic*. No seams. The grid annotation is applied to the surface — drawn, painted, or printed as a wrap — but the substrate has no seams.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> *Why seams matter.*
>
> *Matter one — handling fragility.* Seams are the weak points. A seam-heavy torus (segment-and-tape with 12 seams) tears at junctions before the paper itself fails. A seam-light torus (3D-printed, zero seams) is essentially indestructible.
>
> *Matter two — visual reading.* Seams interrupt the cell pattern. If you trace a doubling cycle on the torus surface, the cycle crosses seams; at each crossing, you have to *interpret* whether the seam matters. *Topologically* it doesn't — the cells continue across the seam — but *visually* it can be confusing.
>
> *Matter three — repair and replacement.* Seams can be re-taped or re-sewn. *Failures on the seam are repairable*; failures on the paper itself usually aren't. This favours methods with predictable, accessible seam locations.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> Practical observation. *Seam count is a quality indicator*. Fewer seams = more durable. More seams = more crafted. Choose based on whether you want a permanent reference (few seams) or an explicit teaching artefact (more seams visible as construction).
>
> Practical takeaway. *Count the seams on your torus and note the count in the build log.* Future you will thank you when you're looking at a damaged torus six months from now and trying to remember whether the failure is on a seam or on the paper.
>
> Most students have a Method 1 torus from Mod A: 2 seams. Some have Method 2 from this week's redo: 6 to 12 seams. A few have Method 3 (3D printed): 0 seams. Each is fine for its purpose."
