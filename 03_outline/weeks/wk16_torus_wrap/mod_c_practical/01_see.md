## 4. Body (1:00–4:30)

> "Mod A's build folded a paper torus quickly. Mod C lays out *three* folding methods and the trade-offs each makes.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> *Method 1 — direct bend.* Roll the strip into a cylinder. Bend the cylinder into a U. Bring the two open ends together; tape.
>
> *Pros.* Fast — three to five minutes. Single piece of paper. No cutting beyond the original strip.
>
> *Cons.* The paper resists the bend; crinkling is unavoidable. The shape is approximately toroidal but not smooth. The doughnut hole is irregular.
>
> *Best for.* Quick demos, first-pass exploration, when smoothness doesn't matter.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> *Method 2 — segment-and-tape.* Cut the cylinder into 6 short equal-length segments (each 4 columns wide). Bend each segment slightly so its end-rims angle inward. Tape each segment's end to the next segment's start, with each successive segment angled relative to the previous. The result is a polygonal-prismatic torus made of six trapezoidal segments.
>
> *Pros.* Cleaner geometry than the direct bend. Each segment is a roughly cylindrical piece, easier to handle. The polygonal approximation is closer to a smooth torus than a single-piece bend.
>
> *Cons.* More cutting and taping required. Total time: 15-25 minutes. The segment joins are visible.
>
> *Best for.* Display copies, teaching aids, demonstrations where you want a recognisable torus shape.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> *Method 3 — cardstock reinforcement.* Print the grid on regular paper, then back the print with thin cardstock or cereal-box paper. The combined sheet is stiffer and holds shape better. Then either direct-bend or segment-and-tape.
>
> *Pros.* The torus holds its shape over weeks of handling. Suitable for a permanent workspace fixture.
>
> *Cons.* Stiffer paper resists smooth curves more than thin paper. Pre-creasing along the bend lines is recommended. Total time: 30-45 minutes.
>
> *Best for.* Long-term reference torus that lives on the shelf for the rest of the year.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> *Three pivot options.*
>
> *Pivot 1 — fabric.* Print the grid on fabric (iron-transfer or fabric-printed paper). Sew the seams. Result: smooth, durable, very flexible torus. Time: 1-2 hours including sewing. Best for someone with sewing skills.
>
> *Pivot 2 — 3D printed plastic.* Skip paper entirely. Use the STL file (Lens 3 will introduce) and print a torus former. Apply the grid as a printed wrap or hand-drawn surface marking. Time: 3-8 hours of print time but mostly unattended. Best for a Q3-ready hardware former.
>
> *Pivot 3 — bought torus.* Foam toruses are sold for floral arrangements and pool floats. Buy one and apply the grid to it (paint, marker, or tape). Time: 30 minutes. Best for someone without makers' tools but with retail access.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> Three observations.
>
> *Observation one.* Each method *trades time for quality*. A 5-minute torus and a 5-hour torus serve different purposes. Pick the method that matches what you'll do with the result.
>
> *Observation two.* The *topology is the same* across methods. The crinkled paper torus and the 3D-printed plastic torus have identical topology. Method choice is about *workmanship*, not about *what kind of torus* you're making.
>
> *Observation three.* Most students at this point have *one* paper torus from Mod A's quick build. Mod C is permission to make a *better* one if you want — or to stay with what you have.
>
> Practical takeaway. *If your Mod-A torus is unsatisfactory, this is the week to redo it.* Pick a method appropriate to your skill level and material availability. The torus is a *core artefact* for the rest of Q2 and Q3; investing one to four hours in a better one pays off."
