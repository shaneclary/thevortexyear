## 0. Authoring notes
The doughnut feels different than the strip. Contemplative on the embodied difference between a 2D paper artefact and the 3D torus.

## 1. Outcome
Student has spent quiet time with the embodied difference between holding a flat strip and holding a 3D torus.

## 2. Hook (0:00–0:30)
*[A flat strip in one hand, a paper torus in the other; slow alternation between the two.]*

> *(slow)* "Strip. Torus. Notice the difference in your hands."

## 3. Setup (0:30–1:00)
> "Lens 5 of 9. Six minutes, slower."

## 4. Body (1:00–4:30) — *contemplative*

> *(slow)* "Two weeks ago you held a paper strip. Last week you filled a grid on a piece of paper. This week you hold a paper torus.
>
> *Each artefact has a different feel.* Notice the differences.
>
> *[long pause — 12 seconds]*
>
> The strip is *flat*. It lies on the desk. It's two-sided — you can flip it over. The two faces are visually identical (or might have annotations on the back). *The strip is like a piece of paper, because it is one.*
>
> The torus is *not flat*. It can't lie on the desk in a stable way; it rolls or wobbles. It's *one-faced* — there's no flip, no front-and-back, no top-and-bottom. Wherever you turn it, the surface continues.
>
> *[long pause]*
>
> Three contemplations on this difference.
>
> *Contemplation one — orientation.* The strip has a clear orientation. The torus does not. You can name the strip's *front*; you cannot name the torus's *front*. The torus is *isotropic in a topological sense* — every direction on its surface is locally equivalent.
>
> Some traditions privilege orientation; others don't. Architecture privileges orientation (front, side, back). Sculpture often doesn't (a sculpture should look interesting from any angle). The torus is closer to sculpture in this respect.
>
> *Contemplation two — the hole.* The torus has a hole. The strip doesn't. You can pass a finger through the doughnut hole; nothing passes through a strip.
>
> The hole is *the structural feature* that makes the torus topologically distinct from the sphere. Without the hole, you'd have a sphere or a disk. *The hole is what the torus is.*
>
> *Contemplation three — handling.* The strip can be folded, rolled, written on without much consequence. The torus is more delicate; it has structure that resists deformation. You handle it differently. *The torus asks for slower, more careful holding* than the strip.
>
> *[long pause — 12 seconds]*
>
> What does it *feel* like to alternate between the two?
>
> Some students notice their *hands change behaviour*. Holding the strip, the hand is loose, casual. Holding the torus, the hand cradles, supports. *Different artefacts elicit different physical postures.*
>
> Some students notice the *attention shift*. Looking at the strip, the eye scans rows. Looking at the torus, the eye traces around the doughnut. *Different artefacts elicit different reading patterns.*
>
> Some students notice the *time relationship*. The strip feels like *a stage*; the torus feels like *a destination*. The arc Q2 has been on — strip → grid → torus — feels like a journey arriving at the torus, with the strip and grid as way-stations.
>
> All three are honest. The artefacts are *companions* on the same arc.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> A practical observation. *Most builders keep both* — the strip on the wall as a 2D reference, the torus in hand as a 3D model. They serve different purposes that don't replace each other.
>
> When you wind a coil in Q3, you'll have *the strip* (for verifying the wind pattern) *and the torus former* (for actually winding around). Both are necessary; neither is sufficient alone.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> One more contemplation. *The torus is closer to a real coil than the strip is.* Q3's actual coil will be a 3D object with a wind path traced on it. The torus is the *geometric simulation* of that future coil — the substrate before the wire.
>
> Holding the paper torus is rehearsal. By Q3 you'll be holding a wound coil that has the same shape but is heavier, denser, more loaded with materials. The paper torus is the lightweight version; the wound coil is the full version.
>
> Sit with the difference. The journey from strip to torus is real. So is the journey to come."
