## 4. Body (1:00–4:30)

> "The objection — coming from builders considering shortcuts. *Q3 is months away. Why commit to a torus former now? Maybe a simpler shape — a square loop, a flat coil, a simple solenoid — would let me start sooner and cheaper.*
>
> The objection is reasonable for *exploration* but misguided for *Rodin-specific work*. The torus former is required for Rodin Coil engineering, not optional.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> *Three reasons the torus former is the right starting point.*
>
> *Reason one — the math forces it.* Wks 14-16 have established that the natural surface for the Number Map is a torus (Mod B's Surface Selection theorem). Building on a non-torus former *changes the structure* and stops being a Rodin Coil. *If you want a Rodin Coil, you need a torus.* Non-Rodin coils are fine engineering objects but they aren't what this course teaches.
>
> *Reason two — the foundation tradition standardises on torus formers.* The Rodin Foundation's published builds, measurements, and comparisons all use torus formers with specific dimensions (10 cm major × 2.5 cm minor as canonical). Builders working with the same standard can compare results, share measurements, troubleshoot together. *Standardisation is a community feature*, not a constraint.
>
> *Reason three — the torus is reusable.* Once you have a torus former, you can wind, unwind, rewind, and rewind for years. The same former supports single-strand builds, six-strand canonical builds, phased multi-strand experiments, biomimetic variations, and so on. *One former enables many builds.*
>
> *[pause]*
>
> *Counter-options and their problems.*
>
> *Square loop.* Topologically a circle, not a torus. Doesn't accommodate the strand cycle. Some 'square Rodin Coils' exist as variants, but they're a separate research line, not the canonical build.
>
> *Flat (planar) coil.* Topologically a disk. Doesn't accommodate cyclic structure. The doubling pattern would terminate after one cycle; you couldn't sustain four traversals.
>
> *Solenoid.* Topologically a cylinder. Accommodates partial Rodin patterns (single-strand single-traversal helically wound) but not the full structure. Most lacking: no antipodal pairing.
>
> *Sphere.* Topologically too simple (genus 0). Mod B established that the sphere has no non-contractible loops. Wouldn't host the Number Map structure.
>
> *No former (winding in air).* Free-air windings are unstable; the geometry shifts under tension. Not a serious option for measurement-grade work.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> So the calibrated answer to *why the torus former is the right starting point*:
>
> *The torus former is required by the math: VBM's two-cyclic-structure content forces a torus surface. The Rodin Foundation tradition standardises on this former for reproducibility across builders. The former is reusable across years and builds. Alternative starting points (cylinder, sphere, square loop, flat coil) are simpler but accommodate only partial Rodin patterns or none. The torus former enables the full canonical Rodin Coil and its tradition's comparisons.*
>
> Around eighty words.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> *A pragmatic concession.* If you really cannot acquire a torus former by Q3 — printing not available, services too expensive, no time to wait — the course offers two fallbacks:
>
> *Fallback one.* A wooden torus blank (5-30 USD; available from craft and woodworking suppliers).
>
> *Fallback two.* A foam torus from floral or pool-toy retail (5-15 USD; less precise but functional for first builds).
>
> Neither fallback is ideal, but both are *better than skipping Q3 entirely*. The torus is the substrate; alternatives degrade the build but don't disqualify it.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> Practical takeaway. *Acquire a torus former — printed, machined, or bought.* Begin the acquisition planning now. Q3's first hardware week (Wk 32) is sixteen weeks from now; allow time for shipping, printing queues, and craft-store hunts."
