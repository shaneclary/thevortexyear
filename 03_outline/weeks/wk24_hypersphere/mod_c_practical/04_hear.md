> *Three engineering principles for holographic claims.*
>
> *Principle one — specify the encoding.* Before any engineering claim, *specify what the encoding-and-reconstruction scheme actually is*. Mainstream Reed-Solomon is fully specified; VBM-Hypersphere-encoding currently isn't. *Specification is the prerequisite to engineering.*
>
> *Principle two — prototype the substrate.* Build a physical or computational prototype that uses the specified encoding. *Stand-alone prototype, then integration.* Without prototype, performance can't be measured.
>
> *Principle three — measure against benchmarks.* Compare prototype performance to mainstream benchmarks. *Reed-Solomon (n,k) achievable rates; AdS/CFT bulk-boundary mapping fidelity; quantum surface-code thresholds.* Numbers must compete on equal terms.
>
> *Each principle is gated; you can't skip steps.*
>
> *[pause]*
>
> *What Powell would advise on the Hypersphere claim.*
>
> *Advice 1.* Specifications come first. *Until VBM specifies the encoding scheme rigorously, there's no engineering work to do.* Mathematical underspecification (Mod B) blocks engineering progress.
>
> *Advice 2.* Don't oversell. *Don't market 'holographic engineering using the Hypersphere structure' until prototypes exist and measurements have been taken.* Marketing ahead of engineering damages credibility.
>
> *Advice 3.* Find research collaborators with information theory and coding theory backgrounds. *They can help bridge VBM specifications to mainstream-comparable engineering.* Don't reinvent.
>
> *[pause]*
>
> *Three things engineering discipline brings to the holographic claim.*
>
> *Thing one — testability.* Engineering claims are *testable*. Either the prototype hits the benchmark or it doesn't. *Cuts through ambiguity.*
>
> *Thing two — gradation.* Engineering allows for *gradations of success*. *Maybe the prototype achieves 60% of mainstream rate; that's still informative.* Honest gradation beats binary success/failure.

> *[pause]*
